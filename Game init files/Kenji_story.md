# KENJI

## *Day One — A World Without Maps*

---

The first thing I notice is the smell.

Woodsmoke. Wet earth. Something faintly animal underneath it all — hay, or livestock, or both. The cold comes next, seeping through my clothes like it has been waiting for me specifically. Then the sound of wind moving through trees that are enormous in a way trees don't usually get to be anymore. Ancient. Cathedral-column enormous.

I am face down in the mud. Something hums at the base of my skull — faint, warm, like a coal that hasn't decided whether to catch. I notice it the way you notice a sound in a dream: real, but not yet important.

I push myself up slowly — palms flat against the cold earth, testing my weight — and get to my knees. Then my feet. Everything works. Nothing broken. I stand at the edge of a dirt road that cuts through a forest so dense the sky above it is more suggestion than fact, and I take a slow breath, and I try to understand where I am.

I can't.

The road runs two directions. North and south, roughly. To the east, through a gap in the treeline, smoke threads upward above the canopy — more than one fire. A settlement. The sound of something metallic drifts from that direction on the wind. A hammer.

I have nothing in my pockets. Nothing on my belt. The clothes on my back are wrong for this weather and already damp at the knees.

I start walking toward the smoke.

---

The forest gives me things if I look properly. Within the first hundred yards I find a fallen branch — dense, dry, roughly my height, the right weight when I swing it once. I take it. Sharp rocks at the road's edge next — flint fragments thrown there by years of cart wheels. I take the best two and pocket them carefully.

The herbs take longer. I don't know their names here but certain things translate — the sharp antiseptic smell of a low-growing cluster of dark serrated leaves near a mossy log tells me something useful even without a label. I don't know why I know this. The knowledge is just there, seated deep, like muscle memory for a body I don't remember training. I wrap a handful in a broad flat leaf and tuck it away. Further along, a pale fibrous root half-exposed from the soil at the base of an oak comes free easily. Dry it out and it'll hold a flame.

By the time the trees begin to thin I have a walking staff, two cutting tools, a small bundle of medicinal herbs, and tinder material. In a world where I arrived with nothing, that feels like wealth.

The village reveals itself gradually. Twenty buildings maybe. Timber-framed, thatched, practical. A stone well at the centre. Chickens pecking between structures. A blacksmith's forge glowing orange at the far end, the hammering from earlier now loud and rhythmic and real.

A boy — ten years old at most — is the first to see me emerge from the treeline. He stops dead. Stares. Then bolts toward the nearest building shouting something too fast to catch.

I keep walking.

---

The door the boy disappeared into opens. A broad-shouldered woman steps out wiping her hands on a coarse linen apron. Mid-fifties. Weathered face, sharp eyes, the particular calm of someone who has dealt with worse things than strangers before breakfast. She stops and looks me up and down slowly.

"Forest spat you out, did it."

It isn't a question.

My eyes are already moving past her — sweeping the village, taking inventory. Tools against barn walls. A hand axe wedged into a splitting log near the woodpile. At the far end the forge, and in front of it a large man in a leather apron who has stopped pretending not to watch.

"Aye?" the woman says, when I don't respond. Her tone has cooled considerably -"Most folk say good morning first. But you do as you like, stranger."

She turns and walks back to her door with the unhurried confidence of someone on their own land.

I head for the forge.

---

The smith doesn't move to meet me. He stands at the forge entrance with arms folded and a hammer across his shoulder and waits to see what kind of man walks toward him. Up close he's even larger than he looked from across the village. Grey-streaked beard. Forearms like knotted rope.

My eyes move past him into the forge and find what I came for almost immediately. Against the far wall, hanging from a peg — a longsword. Clean steel. Real balance. I can tell from across the room.-

"That one's spoken for." he says. He caught me looking. Of course he did. "Everything else is priced fair. If you've got coin."-

He lets that last part land deliberately.

I look at him and smile. Not the careful kind. The other kind.

Something shifts. The hard line of his mouth loosens. Just fractionally.

"Aldric," he says finally. "Aldric Voss."

"Kenji," I say.

He doesn't offer a hand but the hammer shifts off his shoulder. Progress.

"You're not from Duskfen," he says. Then: "Not from anywhere near Duskfen."

He leads me into the forge without being asked. Rummages in a heavy wooden chest and surfaces with something wrapped in burlap. Sets it on the workbench with a dull clank.

"Made that eight months ago," he says, unwrapping it. "Balance was off. Blade pulls left on the draw — not enough to matter in a real fight, most likely; but enough to bother me.."-

A shortsword. Single edged. Plain. Real steel.

"Won't sell it. Won't throw it out." He looks at it the way men look at things they've failed. "It sits."

He rewraps it and looks at me directly for the first time.

"As for hunting—" He nods toward the treeline to the north. "Old mill north of the village. Something's been wrong out that way past few weeks. Animals coming back strange — or not coming back. Lost two good hunting dogs. Whatever's out there — if you put it down and bring me proof — that sword's yours."

I look at the sword. Then at him.

"Let me have the sword now," I tell him. "I'll head out and find the threat right away."

Aldric's expression closes like a shutter.

"Aye, that's not how it works."

He picks up the burlap bundle and tucks it back into the chest, latching it with a finality that echoes off the stone walls.

"You come back with proof — I hand it over. That's the deal."

He turns back to his workbench. Pulls the cloth off a half-finished blade. Picks up his hammer.

Then pauses without turning around.

"There's one thing I'll give you free of charge."

He nods toward a bucket near the forge entrance. Inside — a hand axe. Handle worn, head slightly rusted along the flat, but the edge still serviceable. The kind of thing a smith keeps for splitting kindling and forgets about.

"Don't go into those woods with a stick."

He begins hammering. The conversation is over.

---

I find a private clearing first — a fallen oak creating a natural wall at my back — and crouch with the hand axe across my knees and go still. The longsword is still on Aldric's wall. What I have is a borrowed axe, a walking staff, two rocks, and whatever is burning quietly at the base of my skull.

The warmth is stronger now. I focus on it the way you focus on a sound you're not sure you actually heard.

I try to build ice. Cold. Something that slows and freezes. The ember doesn't respond to logic or architecture. But when I stop trying to direct it — when I just hold the axe and think about the weight of it in my hand, the intention behind it — something happens. A current, running from my chest down through my arm into the hand wrapped around the grip.

Silver-white energy crawls along the axe head like heat haze in reverse. The clearing fills briefly with a sound like a struck piano wire.

Then it fades. Ten seconds. Maybe twelve.

I stare at the axe. Plain iron again. Rust and all.

Not what I asked for. But something.

I try for a shield next — a sustained purple shell, something that stays. The magic blooms immediately, purple light threading up from my fingertips across my chest and shoulders, humming and real. For one extraordinary moment I stand in a forest clearing in another world wrapped in violet luminescence.

Then it collapses inward. Not gone — compressed. Coiled beneath the surface like a held breath. I tap my own forearm. Nothing. I swing the axe handle against my own shoulder.

A ghost of purple light flares at the point of impact and absorbs it.

Not the sustained shell I designed. Something that waits and answers when it's needed. The magic has its own opinions about how it works and they don't entirely match mine. I'm beginning to understand that arguing with it is less productive than learning its language.

I take out my last sharp rock. Aim at a tree. Focus on it expanding mid-flight, growing dense and crushing at velocity.

The ember looks at the request and loses interest completely.

I throw the rock anyway. My whole body into it — shoulder dropping, hips rotating, arm whipping through in one clean chain. The flint crosses the clearing in a flat line and hits the oak dead centre with a crack sharp enough to startle birds from the canopy twenty feet up.

No magic. Just a man who can throw hard.

I collect what I've learned and head north with a borrowed axe and two rocks and a purple aura coiled under my skin.

---

From a limestone shelf jutting from the hillside I see the mill below. The stream. The pond filmed grey and iridescent with something that has killed every fish in it. And in the mill's shadow — something moving. Low. The size of a large horse but the shape entirely wrong. Too many joints. The head swinging in slow arcs like it was tasting the air.

I climb down. Find a natural choke point between two oaks sixty yards from the mill. And I dig.

Thirty minutes of hard work. The hand axe for the surface, my hands for everything else, the loose earth piled carefully where it won't be visible from the mill direction. Four feet deep. Three feet wide. I lay branches across the opening in a loose lattice and cover them with leaves and soil scraped from the surrounding ground. From ten feet away it is invisible.

Then I go back to my vantage point, take out my last sharp rock, and throw it at the stone wall six inches above the creature's head.

The sound it makes — that sub-frequency vibration that lives in the back teeth before the ears catch it — is the sound of something that has never been threatened by anything smaller than itself.

It comes for me in a straight line.

And hits the pit like a verdict.

The ground shakes. The creature screams every wrong sound at once. I'm already at the edge — the hand axe raised, silver-white energy crawling along the blade — and I drop in and bring it down into the junction between the creature's first and second shoulders. The magic detonates on contact. The creature shrieks and thrashes.

On my second pass I take three of its six eyes with a lateral sweep that fills the pit with light. On my third pass I look at it — really look, with everything that survival makes sharp — and I find it. Beneath the jaw. Where the neck meets the skull. A soft darker membrane pulsing visibly with each agonised heartbeat.

I leap.

Both feet leave the ground. The axe comes two-handed above my head, the silver fire screaming along the blade, and I drive it down through that membrane with everything my body has — weight, velocity, intention — and the magic detonates so violently it blows loose earth from the pit walls in a ring.

The creature's scream begins and ends in the same instant.

Then silence. Complete and absolute.

I stand in the pit with shaking arms and a hand axe and a creature the size of a horse completely, utterly still beneath me.

A borrowed tool. A hole in the ground. That's what it took.

---

I work methodically before the adrenaline fades. Claws. A fluid sac near its midsection, dark and iridescent, tied off carefully. A section of thick hide. Then I take the hand axe to its neck with patient precision until the head comes free.

I wrap it in the harvested hide and tuck it under my arm. Then I climb out of the pit and walk back through a forest that feels fractionally different than the one I walked into.

---

Aldric doesn't speak when I set the head on his workbench. He stares at it — six empty eyes, the fishhook teeth, that too-wide skull — for a long moment that has weight in it.

Then he opens the chest. Lifts out the burlap-wrapped shortsword and sets it on the workbench.

"It pulls left," he says quietly. "You'll learn it."

He looks at the head again. Then at me.

"There was only the one?"

I look at the shortsword on the workbench. Then I slide it back across toward him.

"The monster was tough," I tell him. "I had the advantage of seeing it first and setting a trap, and I got lucky — and it still nearly wasn't enough. By all rights I should be what that thing leaves behind." I meet his eyes. "New deal. I give you this proof, the head as a trophy, and in return you equip me properly — longsword, leather armour. I'll seek out additional help in town and raise a party to find out where these things are coming from."

Aldric stares at me for a long moment. His eyes move to the head on the workbench. To the ichor still dark on my hands. To the hand axe — his hand axe — that I set beside the head.

He killed it, those eyes are doing the math. With that. And he's already planning what comes next.

"The longsword on the wall," he says finally, "belongs to a merchant who ordered it four months ago and hasn't come to collect. Man's three months late. Paid half upfront." A pause long enough to mean something. "I'll call the debt settled."

He lifts the longsword from its peg. Sets it on the workbench. Disappears into the back of the forge and returns with leather armour — worn but well-maintained, the chest piece stiffened with boiled hide layered three deep.

"My son's," he says flatly, setting it beside the sword. He doesn't elaborate. His face makes clear he won't.

I pull the armour on piece by piece. Buckle the straps. Settle the longsword on my hip. Roll my shoulders once.

Different. Better. The weight of something earned.

"I appreciate you, Aldric," I tell him. "I'll have an update as soon as possible."

He raises one hand without turning from the anvil.

"Aldric. Are there any other important people in this town worth talking to? A warrior, a healer. I dabble in magic myself — badly — but I'd take someone who knows what they're doing over someone who doesn't."

He sets his hammer down. Scratches his beard. Seems to decide I've earned at least this much.

"Three people worth talking to." He holds up a thick finger. "Maren Ashby. The woman you walked past this morning without a word." A pointed look. "Village elder. Knows everything that happens within ten miles of Duskfen. Tough as the timbers in her house and twice as old. If anyone's noticed a pattern in what's been happening — dead animals, strange sounds, the mill pond going grey — it's her. She won't volunteer it to a stranger who ignores her."

Second finger.

"Priest named Edwyn. Tends the chapel at the south end. Healer — good one. Quiet man. He's been treating farmhands for what he calls nervous exhaustion. Men who went near the northern field and came back wrong. He doesn't talk about it publicly but he's worried."

Third finger.

"As for a fighter—" He pauses. Something shifts in his expression. Careful word-choosing. "There's a woman staying at the inn. Been here four days. Calls herself Sera. Doesn't talk about where she came from or where she's going. Eats alone. Sits with her back to the wall." He picks up his hammer. "She has a sword that's seen real use and eyes that miss nothing. I don't know if she'll help. But she's the only one in this village besides yourself who doesn't look afraid."

He turns back to the anvil. Then, without looking around:

"Start with Maren. Apologise first. She remembers everything."

The inn's common room is dim and low-ceilinged. Four tables, a stone hearth, an old man behind the bar. Two farmhands in their drinks. And in the far corner — back to the wall, exactly as Aldric described — a woman who was watching me from the moment the door opened even though her eyes haven't visibly moved.

Her gaze drops briefly to the longsword. To the leather armour. To the creature ichor still dark on my hands.

Then back to my face.

I cross the room and stop at her table.

She doesn't move. Doesn't reach for her drink or her sword. Just waits with the particular stillness of someone who has learned that patience is its own kind of weapon.

I wipe the last of the ichor off my hands onto my trousers and hold one out.

"Kenji," I say. "Mind if I sit?"

Her eyes drop to the offered hand for exactly one second. Then she takes it — firm, brief, the handshake of someone who was taught properly.

"Sera," she says. "And no."

She kicks the chair across from her out with one boot. An invitation that manages to feel like it might be revoked at any moment.

I sit.

She watches me settle. Then her eyes move to my hands again. To the dark iridescent staining still worked into the creases of my knuckles that the ichor-wipe didn't fully clear.

"That's not animal blood," she says. Flat. A test, not a statement.

"No," I agree. "Something with six legs and eyes in two rows. North of the village at the old mill."

Something shifts in her expression. Microscopic. Gone before it fully arrived.

"Aldric pointed me to you," I tell her. "Said you were the only other person in this village who doesn't look afraid."

She picks up her drink. Considers that.

"Aldric talks too much," she says.

But she doesn't leave.

I look past her. Behind the bar, a young woman — early twenties, auburn hair pinned up with a cooking skewer. Bright-eyed. She catches me looking and doesn't look away immediately.

I raise one hand.

"You there."

She looks over. I meet her eyes and smile.

"Come hear something worth listening to."

She hesitates exactly one second. Then drifts toward the table with studied casualness.

"I'm working," she says.

"You're walking," I say pleasantly. "Close enough."

I lean forward with both forearms on the table and begin. Not with the creature — with waking up in the mud. The wrong sky. The cold. The forest that felt like it was deciding something. My voice stays low enough that she has to lean in slightly to catch every word, and she does, after the first sentence, without noticing she's doing it.

Then the tracks. Six-legged. Wrong. The mill. The water that wasn't right. And here I pause — just half a breath — *the size of a horse, six eyes in two rows, and a sound that lives in your back teeth before your ears know it's there.*

Her eyes have gone wide. Her hands grip the table edge.

I describe the pit trap with the self-deprecating honesty of a man who knows that admitting I dug a hole with my hands is funnier than pretending I didn't. The hand axe Aldric gave me — this close to a stick, really, if we're being honest. I describe the fall, the earth shaking through my boot soles, the creature's shriek. Then the leap — slow and deliberate, the silver light, the falling weight, the committed decision of a man who understood that hesitation was its own kind of death. I bring my hand flat down on the table like the axe hitting home.

She flinches. Then laughs at herself for flinching.

"And then," I say, sitting back, "it was quiet."

She disappears. Returns thirty seconds later with a full cup of something amber and local and sets it in front of me with a small decisive thud.

"On the house," she says. Then quieter: "My name's Pip."

She retreats to the bar, glancing back once.

In the corner of my eye — the woman. Still as carved stone. Untouched drink. Her jaw has shifted approximately three degrees to one side.

I pick up the ale and take a long sip. Then, quiet enough that only the two of us exist in the radius of it:

"And that's how you sing for your supper."

A single exhale through her nose. The architectural suggestion of a laugh.

"Cute," she says. The word landing with the precise flatness of someone choosing it instead of something warmer.

A beat.

"You dug a hole with your bare hands, fell into a pit with a six-limbed apex predator, and your plan was one hand axe and optimism."

She looks at me with eyes that have genuinely reassessed something.

"And it worked."

She turns her cup once on the table. A small thoughtful rotation.

"I've been watching this village for four days waiting for whoever was going to deal with that thing." Her voice is low and even. "I had a list of what that person would probably look like."

Her eyes move across me. The armour. The longsword. The ale someone gave me because I talked well.

"You were not on the list."

She leans forward.

"But you killed it. And you came back. And you're already building a party instead of taking the sword and walking south."

She looks at me with the full weight of attention she'd been rationing since the door opened.

"So. Ask me what you actually came here to ask me, Kenji."

I look her in the eyes.

"Isn't it obvious," I say. "I need your help killing the remaining monsters and figuring out where they came from."

Something happens that hasn't happened in four days of careful stillness in this corner. She smiles. Small. Controlled. The kind that escapes rather than gets offered. Gone almost before it arrives. But real.

She reaches into her travelling coat and slides a folded piece of parchment across the table with two fingers.

A map. Hand-drawn in precise careful strokes. The forest north of Duskfen. The mill marked. Three other locations spreading outward like a slow constellation. At the map's edge — *The Delve.*

"There isn't just one," she says quietly. "There are never just one. They're called Hexcrawlers — six limbed, pack hunters, directed by something else. Something that organises them."

She taps The Delve.

"They come from there. An old excavation site two days northeast. Something woke up inside it roughly six weeks ago and started sending them out in expanding patrol patterns. I was hired to assess the situation. Report back. Not engage alone." A pause. "Then I saw what they did to the animals at the forest edge and decided reporting back could wait."

She extends her hand across the table.

"We'll need the healer too. Priest Edwyn. I've seen his work."

I look at her extended hand.

The smart move is to shake it and talk strategy. I know this even as I hear myself say something else.

"Any chance you'd want company tonight that isn't tactical?"

It lands wrong. I can feel it land wrong the moment the air touches it.

The hand comes back. Slowly. With the unhurried precision of someone returning a weapon to its sheath. Whatever warmth had begun quietly occupying the corners of her expression closes with a soft precise click.

She picks up her drink. Takes a measured sip. Sets it down.

"You were doing so well," she says, with the calm tone of someone discussing a failed experiment. "Genuinely. Right up until that."

A beat.

"You killed one Hexcrawler. With a hole in the ground and a hand axe. There are at least twelve more between here and The Delve."

She stands. Drops a coin on the table for her untouched drink.

"Impress me out there."

She heads for the stairs. Stops at the bottom post without turning.

"The long rest is a good idea though. Get a room, Kenji." Her voice carries back perfectly. "You smell like Hexcrawler and poor decisions."

She goes upstairs.

From behind the bar Pip makes absolutely no effort to hide her grin.

"She's been like that for four days," she offers cheerfully.

I look over at her.

"Pip," I say — and some part of me notes that I'm doing this again, that whatever impulse just crashed and burned with Sera is apparently unkillable, except this time I know exactly what I'm doing — "how about you let me take a hot bath, and afterward I'll tell you the rest of that story in your room. The parts I left out. The parts that would have made your hands do more than grip the table."

She stops wiping the bar.

Looks at me. Travels the full entertaining distance from surprise to amusement to something warmer that she doesn't bother reining in.

She sets the cloth down.

"My room," she repeats slowly. Not a question. Tasting the shape of it.

"Your room," I confirm. "I'm told I'm an exceptional storyteller. The kind that keeps you up all night."

She holds my gaze for a beat longer than casual. Then she looks at the stairs. Then at the kitchen where her father's sounds suggest he's occupied.

She reaches under the bar and produces a large iron key. Slides it toward me with one finger.

"There's a copper tub in the back room," she says. "I'll heat the water. Twenty minutes — then room three. Top of the stairs, second door on the left."

She picks up her cloth. Resumes wiping the bar with studied composure that fools absolutely no one.

"Kenji."

I'm already moving. I look back.

Her eyes are bright and entirely too knowing for someone her age.

"The stories better be *very* good."

---

The copper tub is real and the hot water is the finest thing I have encountered since arriving in this world. The creature's dark iridescent blood lifts off my skin and sinks to the bottom. My hands stop aching. My shoulders unclench for the first time since I woke up in the mud.

I lie back in the water and look at the ceiling.

This morning I woke up in the dirt with nothing. No name anyone here recognised. No memory of how I arrived. Tonight I'm sitting in a copper tub in a village I've decided to protect, wearing a dead man's armour and carrying a sword meant for someone who never came back for it. I killed something that shouldn't exist with a borrowed hand axe and a hole I dug with my bare hands. I have magic I don't understand, skills I can't explain, and a mouth that runs faster than my judgement in every direction at once.

Sera saw through me in four seconds flat. Pip saw through me too — the difference is she liked what she saw underneath.

Tomorrow — Maren Ashby, who deserves an apology. Priest Edwyn, who is quietly worried about things he hasn't said aloud yet. Twelve Hexcrawlers between here and The Delve. Something that woke up six weeks ago inside an old excavation site two days northeast.

Tonight — hot water. A key to a room that isn't mine. And stories I intend to tell very, very slowly.

I close my eyes.

The ember at the base of my skull burns steadily in the dark.

*Good,* I think. *Stay lit.*

Chapter 2

Morning light through a window that isn't in room four.

I come downstairs to a common room that smells like fresh bread and woodsmoke. Sera is already at her corner table, map spread flat, eating in silence. She looks up when I hit the bottom stair and her eyes do a single precise sweep — my face, my clothes, the door I came out of — and she returns to her breakfast without comment. "You look rested," she says flatly.

"I had an excellent night's sleep."

"I'm sure you did." She stands. "Maren Ashby first. Then Edwyn. We leave tomorrow at dawn." She looks at me with eyes that miss absolutely nothing. "Try to stay focused today, Kenji."

She walks out.

From the kitchen, the unmistakable sound of Pip laughing.

## *Day Two — The Things We Carry Into the Dark*

---

Morning light through a window that isn't in room four.

The ember notices before I do. It pulses once — warm, steady, the quiet acknowledgment of a body that rested well in a place that felt safe. Pip's room is small and smells like lavender and bread flour and something else that belongs entirely to her. The blankets are still warm where she was. The pillow holds the shape of her. She's already gone — downstairs, working, being the girl who runs the inn while her father pretends he still does.

I pull on my clothes. The leather armour. The longsword on my hip, still strange in its weight, still not mine in the way things become yours after years. Two days. Everything I own in this world I've had for two days.

I come downstairs.

---

The common room smells like fresh bread and woodsmoke. Sera is already at her corner table, map spread flat, eating in silence with the mechanical efficiency of someone who views food as ammunition. She looks up when I hit the bottom stair and her eyes do a single precise sweep — my face, my clothes, the door I came out of — and she returns to her breakfast without comment.

The faintest twitch at the corner of her mouth.

Pip is behind the bar. She sets a plate in front of me before I sit down — eggs, bread, thick bacon, a cup of something hot that smells like the best decision anyone has ever made. More food than the two farmhands at the next table got combined.

"On the house," she says. The same words as last night's ale, delivered with the same easy tone, except this time there's a warmth behind it that wasn't there before. Small. Private. The kind that exists between two people who've shared something they haven't told anyone else about.

She goes back to the kitchen. Glances over her shoulder once.

I eat well. Best meal I've had in this world, though I admit my sample size is limited.

Across the room, Sera folds her map. Stands.

"Maren Ashby first. Then Edwyn. We leave tomorrow at dawn." She looks at me with eyes that miss absolutely nothing. "Try to stay focused today, Kenji."

She walks out into the grey morning light.

From the kitchen, the unmistakable sound of Pip laughing.

---

I catch up to Sera at the well. She's filling a waterskin and doesn't turn until it's capped and stowed.

"Already?" Eyes flat. "I said Maren first."

I lay it out — gather the team first, approach the elder together. Show up with a fighter and a healer flanking me and it says something different than showing up alone. It says *this is being handled*.

She's quiet for half a second. Tactical math running behind her eyes. Then: "Fine. Edwyn first. Maren second. Together."

She says it like she's filing a revised itinerary, not conceding a point. Caps her waterskin. Hooks it to her belt. Starts walking.

I'm not done. The longsword on my hip — I killed a Hexcrawler with a hand axe and a pit. Whatever instinct lives in my body isn't training, and I know the difference. There are at least twelve more of those things between here and The Delve.

"I need an hour with someone who knows steel," I tell her. "Longsword fundamentals. You're the only person in this village who qualifies."

She stops walking. Turns. Looks at me, then at the sword, then back at me.

"No."

She says it clean. No malice. The way you decline something that isn't unreasonable but isn't happening.

"You killed a Hexcrawler alone with a hand axe and a hole. Your instincts aren't the problem." She looks toward the chapel. "Time is. We've got a priest to convince, an elder who already thinks you're rude, and twelve of those things between us and The Delve. I'm not spending an hour playing instructor when there's work to do."

She holds my gaze for one beat longer than necessary.

"Survive the next fight. Then we'll talk about training."

She starts walking south. The conversation is over. I file it where I file things that aren't finished yet and follow.

---

The chapel sits at the village's southern edge. Small. Stone-built. A carved symbol above the door — a cupped hand holding a single flame. The kind of building that looks like it's been here longer than the village and simply allowed the rest of Duskfen to grow around it.

The door is open. Inside, the light is amber and quiet. Wooden pews for maybe twenty. Dried herbs hanging from the rafters — rosemary, sage, something sharper I can't name. At the far end, a man kneels before a simple altar arranging small clay jars with the unhurried attention of someone who believes the order of things matters.

He turns before I speak. Mid-forties. Thin-faced. Kind eyes sitting above dark circles that say he hasn't been sleeping well. His hands are clean but stained yellow at the fingertips — years of poultices and tinctures.

"Ah," he says, rising slowly. His voice is soft, measured — the voice of a man who has spent years choosing his words with the care of someone dispensing medicine. "You must be the one who came out of the forest yesterday. Aldric's been... animated this morning."

Behind me, Sera leans against the doorframe with her arms crossed. She gives Edwyn a short nod. He returns it — they've met before, apparently.

His eyes settle on me. Patient. Waiting.

"Hi Edwyn," I say. "It's a long story. Sera here's actually been scouting the situation for four days. We'd like to ask you to join us in getting rid of the threat to the village. But before that — let's compare notes. See if you know something we don't."

Something in his expression shifts — not suspicion, but the careful relief of a man who has been carrying something alone and isn't sure yet if the people offering to help can bear the weight.

"Compare notes," he repeats quietly. "Yes. I think that would be wise."

He moves to a side table and opens a heavy leather-bound ledger. Pages filled with small careful handwriting.

"Seven men in the last five weeks. Farmhands. Woodcutters. All working the northern fields or the forest edge." He runs a finger down the page. "Same symptoms. Fatigue beyond what labour explains. Night terrors. A feeling they describe as... *thinning*. As though something essential were being slowly drawn out of them."

He turns a page. More names. More notes.

"The symptoms do not respond to treatment. I've tried herbal remedies. Rest. Prayer." A pause that carries the weight of professional failure. "They come back. Every time. Whatever is happening to these men is not illness."

He closes the ledger gently and looks at me.

"It is *exposure*."

From the doorframe, Sera shifts her weight. A slight creak of leather. She's listening harder than she was thirty seconds ago.

Edwyn sets his hand flat on the ledger.

"I don't know what these creatures are. But I know what they leave behind in people. And it's getting worse."

His eyes settle on me again. Patient. Waiting. A man who's been carrying this alone, wondering if someone would finally ask the right questions.

"I have a condition," he says. "If we find the source of this — whatever it is — I need to understand it. Not just destroy it. If we kill the source without learning how it works, I may never be able to treat the men it's already touched."

He meets my eyes with a quiet firmness that says this isn't negotiable.

"Deal," I say.

Something in his shoulders eases.

We cross the village square three abreast — a fighter, a healer, and whatever I am. The few villagers out at this hour stop what they're doing to watch. A woman with a feed bucket pauses mid-scatter. The two farmhands from the inn stand in a doorway, mugs in hand, tracking our movement with the wary attention of people who know something is being decided on their behalf.

Maren Ashby's house. Timber-framed. Solid. Chimney smoke curling steady. The same door the boy disappeared into yesterday. The same door the broad-shouldered woman came out of, looked me up and down, and told me most folk say good morning first.

Sera glances at me sideways. Says nothing. The glance says enough.

Edwyn knocks twice. The door opens.

Maren fills the frame the way a woman fills the frame of a house she built with her own decisions. Mid-fifties. Weathered face. Sharp eyes. The coarse linen apron is gone — replaced by a heavy wool shawl and the expression of someone who already knows why we're here and is deciding how much of her morning we deserve.

Her eyes move across the three of us. Sera gets a short nod. Edwyn gets something warmer. Then her gaze lands on me and stays there.

"Ah," she says. "The one who walked past me without a word yesterday and went straight for the forge."

She leans against the doorframe. Arms folded.

"You've brought company this time. That's something."

I bow. It catches her off guard — I can see it, a micro-shift in her posture, the arms uncrossing half an inch before they resettle.

"I apologise for ignoring you, Maren. If you have a moment, I'd like to share the details about the threat we've gathered so far and listen to any insight or wisdom you can give us on dealing with it as quickly as possible."

She studies me for a long moment. The kind of look that goes through skin and checks what's underneath.

"Hm," she says. The sound carries more information than most people fit into a full sentence.

She steps back and holds the door open.

"Inside. All of you. I'm not having this conversation where the Hargrove boy can eavesdrop from behind the woodpile."

Somewhere behind us, the faint sound of a child retreating at speed.

---

The inside of Maren's house is warm, clean, and organised the way a military supply room is organised — everything in its place because the woman who put it there does not tolerate alternatives. A stone hearth. A heavy oak table with four chairs. Dried herbs and smoked meat hanging from ceiling beams. Maps and almanacs stacked neatly on a shelf beside the window.

She sets a kettle over the fire without asking if anyone wants tea. There will be tea.

We lay it out. The Hexcrawler at the mill. Six legs, six eyes, the iridescent fluid. Sera's four days of scouting — the patrol patterns, the map, The Delve two days northeast. Edwyn's ledger — seven men, five weeks, symptoms that don't respond to treatment.

Maren listens the way a stone wall listens to rain. Absorbing everything. Giving nothing back until it's ready.

When we finish, the kettle screams. She rises, pours four cups without ceremony, and sits back down.

"Six weeks," she says. "That's when it started. You're right about that."

She wraps both hands around her cup.

"But it didn't start at the mill. It started at the old Brennick farm. North-northeast. Closer to the forest edge than any homestead should be — Harlan Brennick was a stubborn man." A pause. "Was."

Her eyes are steady.

"Harlan went into his root cellar six weeks ago because he heard sounds underneath it. *Underneath* a cellar he dug himself into solid clay twenty years prior." She takes a slow sip. "His wife came to me the next morning. Said he'd come back up different. Quiet. Staring at nothing. Wouldn't eat. Kept saying the ground was *breathing*."

The fire pops. Nobody moves.

"Three days later he walked into the northern forest before dawn and didn't come back. Search party found his boots at the tree line. Nothing else."

She sets the cup down.

"That's when the animals started changing. Then the pond. Then Edwyn's farmhands." She looks at the priest. "You thought it was five weeks. It's six. Harlan was the first and nobody connected it because Harlan was always odd."

Sera has gone very still at the end of the table. Her hand rests flat on the wood — the one that isn't near her sword.

Maren looks back at me.

"There's one more thing. The Brennick farm — it sits on the old trade road that runs northeast. Same road that leads to The Delve."

She lets that settle.

"Whatever woke up out there, it didn't just send creatures south. It reached into Harlan Brennick's root cellar and *called* him."

The table is quiet. Steam curls from four cups. The weight of what she's just said sits on the oak surface between us like something with physical mass.

I think about what I came here to do. I thought I'd be running up and stabbing the problem. A cellar. A stone door. Something that *calls people by name*. This isn't a monster hunt. This is something else entirely.

"That's a long time," I say. "No telling what the endgame is for whatever's brewing down in that cellar. I think we need to attempt to head to the source and cut it off — may not be enough time left to chase after the monsters currently roaming." I look around the table. "I'd like to hear the rest of the party's suggestions."

Maren nods slowly. Opens her hand — *speak*.

Sera goes first. "Straight to The Delve is two days northeast on foot. The old trade road." She taps the table twice. "Problem is, my scouting puts at least three Hexcrawler patrol zones between here and there. We don't chase them — fine. But they're not going to step aside because we're in a hurry. We walk into a patrol zone at night without seeing them first, it won't be one in a pit. It'll be three or four in the dark."

She looks at me.

"I'm not saying don't go to the source. I'm saying the road there has teeth."

Edwyn has been quiet. His hands are wrapped around his tea like it's the only warm thing left in the world.

"The Brennick farm. It's on the way — half a day's walk, maybe less. If the cellar is where this started... that may be where we learn what we're walking into. Going straight to The Delve without understanding what called Harlan—" He shakes his head. "We'd be walking in blind."

Maren sets her cup down with a decisive *clack* and unfurls a hand-drawn map — older and more detailed than Sera's. The trade road. The Brennick farm. Landmarks, water sources, and a faded notation at the eastern edge that reads *The Delve — condemned*.

"Harlan's wife, Bessa, is staying with her sister at the south end of the village," Maren says. "She hasn't spoken about what happened to anyone but me. If you want to know exactly what Harlan said when he came back up from that cellar, she's the one to ask."

She looks at me with weight behind it.

"But you'll need to be gentle. That woman is barely holding together."

I divide the labour. Sera — rations, camp gear, a light crossbow if Aldric has one. Edwyn — medical supplies for the road, anything he's got for poisons and healing. I ask about heavy armor and a shield for Sera. She tells me with absolute clarity that she's a scout, not a wall, and if I want a tank I need a different person. Fair enough.

The crossbow question lands next. If Sera's scouting and I'm in melee, someone needs a ranged option. Sera looks at Edwyn. Edwyn looks at his own hands like they've betrayed him.

"I... can point one in the correct direction," he offers carefully.

"We'll work on that," Sera says flatly.

Maren snorts into her tea.

I ask if anyone on the team can pick locks — cellars mean doors, and I don't want to get to our objective and spend the day looking for a key.

Nobody can.

Maren leans back. Her eyes narrow — sorting through decades of mental filing.

"There's a boy. Pip's age, roughly. Name's Ryn. Came through the village about two weeks ago heading east on the trade road. Fixes things. Hinges, latches, mechanisms." She gives me a weighted look. "He has *clever hands*. Whether he'd go with you into danger is another matter entirely. He's jumpy. Thin. Not a fighter."

She takes a slow sip.

"He's at the inn."

The blue door is easy to find. Small house, well-kept, a window box with herbs someone is still tending out of habit. A cat watches me from a fence post with the boneless suspicion of its kind.

Sera stayed behind at Maren's to plan the supply run. Edwyn offered to come — "She knows me. I've been checking on her." He stands a step behind me, satchel over his shoulder, hands folded.

He knocks. Soft. The knock of someone who has done this before and learned how hard is too hard.

The door opens a crack. A woman's face — not Bessa. Older. Tight-lipped. Eyes that go to Edwyn first and relax one degree, then to me and harden.

"Father Edwyn." Her voice is clipped. Protective. "She's having a difficult morning."

"I understand, Anneth," Edwyn says gently. "This is Kenji. He's looking into what's been happening in the northern forest. We thought Bessa might—"

"I know what you thought." Anneth's jaw sets. She looks at me the way a door looks at a boot. "My sister has answered enough questions. Every time someone asks her to *go back to that night* she doesn't sleep for three days."

The door hasn't opened further. Behind Anneth, somewhere deeper in the house, the faint sound of a chair creaking. Someone is sitting very still and listening.

I don't push. I don't ask to come in.

"I'm going to the cellar," I tell her. "I just thought she should know. And if she has any requests, I'm willing to listen before I head out."

Something shifts. Anneth's grip on the door loosens — just slightly. I didn't ask to interrogate her sister. I told her I'm going regardless. The offer to listen put the power in Bessa's hands instead of taking it.

A voice from inside. Thin. Quiet.

"Let him in, Anneth."

---

The room is small and warm. A low fire. Two chairs near the hearth. The smell of chamomile and something sharper — a sleeping draught, maybe, untouched on the side table.

Bessa Brennick sits in the chair nearest the fire. She's younger than I expected — late thirties, maybe — but her face carries the particular exhaustion of someone who hasn't truly rested in six weeks. Thin hands in her lap. Dark hair pulled back. Eyes that land on me and stay there with a steadiness that surprises me.

She doesn't look broken. She looks like someone holding every piece of herself in place through sheer will.

"You're going to the cellar," she says. Not a question.

A pause. Her fingers tighten in her lap.

"Then you should know what my husband said when he came back up."

The fire pops. The room holds its breath.

"He was down there for two hours. I called to him. Many times. No answer. When he finally came up the stairs his hands were bleeding — fingernails torn — and his eyes were..." She stops. Swallows. Continues. "Wrong. Like he was looking at something behind everything. Behind the walls. Behind me."

Her voice is steady. Barely.

"He said the ground was breathing. He said there was a door that shouldn't be there. Stone. Old. Older than the cellar, older than the farm, older than *Duskfen*." Her hands are white-knuckled now. "He said he opened it. And something on the other side... *knew his name*."

The fire is the only sound for a long moment.

"Three days. He sat in that chair and stared at nothing for three days. Wouldn't eat. Wouldn't sleep. Just kept whispering that it was *waiting*. That it was *patient*."

She looks at the fire.

"Then he got up before dawn and walked into the forest. I watched him go. He didn't take his boots off at the tree line — I don't know why the search party said that. He walked in wearing them." Her voice finally cracks, just at the edge. "He walked in like he'd been *called home*."

Silence.

She looks back at me. Her eyes are wet but her jaw is set.

"I have one request. If you find him down there — or what's left of him — don't leave him. Bring him back to me. Whatever state he's in. I need to bury my husband."

I cross the room and open my arms. Bessa looks up at me — this stranger with purple dreadlocks and borrowed armour who just promised to walk into the thing that took her husband — and for a moment she doesn't move.

Then something in her releases. She stands and takes the hug like someone who hasn't been held in six weeks because everyone around her has been too careful, too gentle, too afraid of making it worse. Her hands grip the back of my leather armour and she shakes once — hard — and then goes still.

"I will find him," I say.

She nods against my shoulder. Says nothing. Behind me, Edwyn looks at the floor. Anneth's arms have uncrossed.

Bessa steps back. Wipes her eyes. Sits down with the composure of someone who just spent the last of something precious and is already rebuilding.

"Go," she says quietly. "Before I ask you questions you can't answer yet."

The blue door closes behind us. The street is quiet. The cat hasn't moved.

Edwyn walks beside me in silence. Then: "That was good. What you did in there. Most people try to *say* the right thing. You just... showed up."

He adjusts his satchel strap. Then the healer's mind engages — I can see it happen, the distracted urgency giving way to something sharper.

I stop him before he reaches the chapel.

"Wait. We learned something important back there. Something around that cellar is able to cause physical damage — like an illness. Bessa's description of Harlan — torn fingernails, bleeding hands, two hours underground. Have you heard of those symptoms? What can we do to ward the effects on ourselves?"

Edwyn stops. Sets his satchel on the low stone wall beside the chapel path and opens it. Pulls out his ledger.

"Physical damage..." he murmurs. "Torn fingernails. Bleeding hands. But she didn't say he was *digging*. He was down there for two hours." He flips pages. "My patients — fatigue, night terrors, *thinning*. But none of them went underground. None of them opened a door."

His eyes narrow. Something connecting.

"Two different degrees of the same thing," he says slowly. "My farmhands — they were *exposed*. At a distance. Low doses over weeks. Like standing near a fire and getting warm. But Harlan..." He closes the ledger. "Harlan opened the door and put his hand *in* the fire."

He looks at me.

"This isn't a disease, Kenji. Diseases spread between people. None of the wives or children are affected — only the men who worked those northern fields. Only the ones who were *close to the source*. What I'm describing is something more like..." He searches for the word. Finds it. Doesn't like it. "...a *presence*. Something that radiates. And the closer you get, the stronger it pulls."

His hand moves to the flame symbol around his neck. Unconscious. Then conscious.

"There are old texts. Very old. About places where the boundary between our world and... other things... wears thin." His voice drops. "The symptoms — the *thinning*, the compulsion, the sense of being called — those are described in the scripture of the Wandering Flame as signs of divine bleed. When something with a will far larger than mortal pushes against the skin of the world from the other side."

He meets my eyes.

"Not a demon. Not a ghost. Something that simply *is*, in the way a storm *is*. Patient. Vast. And very, very aware."

I feel the ember pulse at the base of my skull. Faintly. As though it heard something it recognised.

"As for protection," Edwyn says, the healer taking over from the theologian, "I can do three things."

Willowbark and silverleaf tea — dulls the mind's receptivity. Like putting cloth over your ears in a windstorm. He has enough for six doses.

Consecrated binding wraps — cloth soaked in blessed water, wound around the wrists. Old practice. Won't hold against a direct assault, but for ambient exposure, they should buy time.

And the most important one: awareness. The compulsion works best on people who don't realise it's happening.

I tell him what I've been thinking. That I was expecting to run up and stab the problem, but you can't stab a plague. Or a curse. Two hours is what it took to break Harlan — so we risk one hour maximum. Go in, identify what's down there, get out with our wits. Multiple solutions plotted before we set foot on those stairs. And if we can't destroy it — trapping it, quarantining the area, bottling it behind a threshold — that's just as good as a kill if it buys us time.

Edwyn reaches into his satchel and pulls out something wrapped in undyed linen — a small iron disc etched with the cupped flame of his order. He holds it carefully.

"A Seal of the Wandering Flame. I have two." He turns it in his fingers. "They're not weapons. They're *boundaries*. Place one on either side of an opening, speak the rite, and for a time... nothing that radiates spiritual corruption can pass through."

He wraps it back up.

"If there's a stone door down there, and something behind it that radiates — two seals on the doorframe and the rite could bottle it. Temporarily. Long enough to get people clear and plan a permanent solution." A pause. "It won't work if the presence is already *through* the door and spread into the cellar. The seals contain what's behind a threshold — they can't push something back that's already crossed."

I ask if he has anything else. Religious relics. Holy weapons. Anything from his order that might give us an edge against something divine.

He frowns. Rubs his temple. Something he's reaching for that isn't there.

"Beyond the seals, I..." He shakes his head. "My order is small, Kenji. Village healers and road priests. We're not exorcists. What I have is what I've shown you — the tea, the wraps, the seals, and prayers that were written for *comforting* the dying, not *fighting* what kills them."

He's honest about the gap. No pretence.

"If we need more than that — something to destroy or permanently seal whatever's down there — we'd need knowledge I don't have. The Academy in Varenholm might. The archives there go back centuries." A careful pause. "But that's a long way from here and we don't have that kind of time."

He picks up the satchel. 

"Plan A — we go down, identify it, get out in under an hour. Plan B — if it's still behind the door, we seal the threshold. Plan C—" He shakes his head. "I don't have one yet."

He turns toward the chapel. Stops.

"You think like a strategist, Kenji. Not a swordsman." Mild surprise in his voice. "Hold onto that down there. The thing that took Harlan didn't take him by force. It took him by *fascination*."

He walks into the chapel and disappears.

The stable smells like hay, horse, and iron filings. At the far end, crouched beside the last stall door with a handful of small tools spread on a rag, a young man works a latch mechanism with the focused intensity of a surgeon.

Early twenties. Wiry. Sandy hair that looks like it was cut with a knife and not recently. Quick hands — Maren was right about that. His fingers move inside the latch housing with the unconscious fluency of someone who speaks a language most people don't know exists.

He doesn't hear me until my shadow crosses the stall door. Then he *flinches* — full body, tools scattering, one hand going to a short knife at his belt before his eyes catch up to his reflexes.

He sees me. The armour. The longsword. The purple dreadlocks.

The knife hand drops. Slowly.

"I — sorry. I don't—" He straightens up. Wipes his hands on his trousers. A full head shorter than me and trying hard to look like that doesn't bother him. "Can I help you?"

I lay it out. Adventuring party heading northeast. Two days on the trade road, probably more. I need someone to carry gear, set up camp, keep eyes open, and most importantly — open things that don't want to be opened. Doors. Locks. Mechanisms. Problem-solving when steel won't do it. I ask him what the going rate is for that kind of work.

His eyes widen at *adventuring party*. Then narrow at *open things*. He's doing math — not the coin kind. The survival kind.

"Look, I — that's..." He picks up a latch pin. Turns it in his fingers. Nervous habit. "I fix *stall doors*. I'm not — I don't *adventure*. I've never been in a fight. I don't even own armour."

He swallows.

"And the trade road northeast — that's toward the forest where those *things* are. The ones that took the hunting dogs." He shakes his head. "I heard what you brought back to the blacksmith yesterday. Six eyes. I don't — I can't—"

He's scared. Not of me — of what I'm asking him to walk toward. But underneath the fear his eyes keep flicking back to the tools on the rag. The part of him that wants to matter fighting the part that wants to run.

He steadies himself. Looks at me directly for the first time.

"What exactly is out there? And who else is going?"

Fair enough. I respect the honesty. And the fear is real — I'm not going to push a man toward something that'll get him killed just because he's useful.

"Dangerous creatures," I tell him. "I respect your decision to stay here in safety. How about this instead — you give me a two-hour course on lockpicking, and in return I'll bring you any neat curiosities I find out there."

The relief is immediate. Visible. His shoulders drop. The fear drains out of his posture and something else fills the space — interest. Professional interest.

"Curiosities?" He turns the latch pin. "What kind of curiosities?"

He catches himself. Reels it back.

"Two hours is a lot. And lockpicking isn't — it's not just *poking metal into a hole*. There's feel to it. Tension. Feedback." He's talking faster now. The only subject that makes him feel tall. "Most people don't have the hands for it."

He looks at mine. The sword calluses. The size of them.

"...You might not have the hands for it," he admits.

"But I can show you the principles."

He crouches and unrolls a small leather wrap from his pack. Six thin metal instruments — picks, rakes, tension wrenches — each slotted into its own pocket. Clean. Cared for. The way Sera cares for her sword.

"First pick of whatever you bring back," he says. "Not the valuable stuff necessarily — just the *interesting* stuff. Mechanisms. Components. Things that work in ways they shouldn't."

"Deal."

He starts with the stall latch. Pulls it apart and lays the components across the rag.

"Every lock is a conversation," he says. "The pins are asking a question. The pick is your answer. The tension wrench is what holds the answer in place while you move to the next one."

He demonstrates on the stall lock — pick sliding in, wrench applying pressure, a series of tiny precise movements that look like nothing. The lock opens in four seconds.

"Now you."

My hands are too big for this. I know it immediately. The pick feels like a splinter between my fingers. But somewhere between the third and fourth attempt something translates — not the fine dexterity Ryn has, but a different kind of feel. I learn to listen through the metal. The pin sets with a click I feel in my back teeth more than my fingertips.

The stall lock opens. Ugly. Slow. But open.

"Huh," Ryn says. He wasn't expecting that.

The padlock is harder. Four pins. Tighter tolerances. Nothing. My fingers cramp. The pick slips. Three failed attempts in silence.

"You're *fighting* it," he says. "A sword pushes through resistance. A lock gives you the answer if you stop demanding it."

He takes it back. Not unkindly.

"Simple mechanisms — stall latches, cellar bolts, basic tumbler locks — you'll get through those. Anything more complex, you'll need more practice. Or you'll need me."

He hands me a small rolled leather wrap — not his main set. Spares. Three tools. A hook pick, a rake, a tension wrench.

"Keep them," he says. "Bring me something interesting, Kenji."

The land around Duskfen isn't generous this time of year. The cold has stripped most of the colour from the fields and the forest edge is all brown bracken and bare branches. But I know how to look — or something in me does.

Along the stream south of the village, where the water keeps the soil warmer, I find pale blue wildflowers growing stubborn and low against the bank. Delicate. Five-petaled. Further along — deep violet blooms tucked under an overhang where the frost hasn't reached. The colour is close enough to my hair that I notice and almost laugh.

I pick carefully. Enough for a decent handful. Blues and violets, stems long enough to hold together.

The inn's common room is warm and half-empty. Late afternoon light slants through the windows in long amber columns. Pip is behind the bar sorting clay mugs with the easy rhythm of someone who's done it ten thousand times.

She sees me come in. The flowers register before the words do — her hands stop and her eyes go from the wildflowers to my face.

I cross the room and set them on the bar.

"You look exceptionally beautiful this afternoon."

Colour hits her cheeks. Fast. She picks up the flowers and turns them once, fingertips on the petals, a small involuntary smile she doesn't bother hiding.

"You went *picking flowers*," she says. "You. The man who killed a six-legged monster yesterday with a hand axe."

She brings them to her nose. Breathes in. Sets them down gently.

I lean on the bar and lay out the rest. I know she'll get in trouble letting me stay another night for free. But I heard a rumour there's a very handsome bandit in town. If she hears a rustle at her door, and it magically opens, he may come bearing gifts and an excellent foot massage. If she lets him stay the night.

She stares at me. The smile breaks wide open — the kind that takes over a whole face and doesn't ask permission.

"A *bandit*," she repeats. "A bandit. With *foot massages*."

She glances toward the kitchen. Her father's voice — something about grain stores. She looks back at me.

"Room three is unoccupied tonight. If someone were to happen across it after closing... I suppose I couldn't be held responsible for unlocked doors."

She finds a clay cup behind the bar, fills it with water, and sets the flowers in it. Places it on the shelf behind her where she'll see it all evening.

Then she leans across the bar. Close.

"The stories better be even better than last night." Her eyes are bright and entirely certain. "And my feet *really* hurt."

She straightens up. Picks up her cloth. Returns to wiping the bar with studied composure that fools absolutely no one.

I find the clearing. The fallen oak at my back. The canopy overhead thinning toward evening. Private. Quiet.

The ember is warm. After two days it feels less like a foreign object and more like an organ I was born with and am only now learning to use. I draw the longsword and start with what I know.

Arcane Edge first. The current runs from my chest down through my arm into the steel. Silver-white energy crawling along the edge. That struck-wire hum filling the clearing.

Yesterday it lasted ten seconds. I stop *pushing* and start *feeding*. The difference is everything. Pushing forces energy like water through a pipe. Feeding lets the ember breathe at its own pace — a slow steady exhale instead of a shove. The silver light stabilises. Holds. The longsword hums in my grip like a living thing.

Thirty seconds. Forty. A full minute.

I swing. The infused blade cuts air with trailing luminescence — silver arcs hanging for a heartbeat before they dissolve. The activation becomes near-instant by the fifth repetition. Intention to ignition in under a second. The ember knows what I want before my arm moves.

Blade Ward is harder. It *reacts*. I can't practise a reaction without something to react to. I throw rocks at my own face. The first one hits my shoulder. Hard. No ward. The second clips my forearm — a ghost of purple flickers and dies. The third I flinch from, and the magic doesn't bother showing up for a flinch.

The problem is clear. Blade Ward triggers on *commitment*. When the hand axe handle hit my shoulder yesterday, I wasn't afraid — I was testing. The rocks trigger the animal brain that says *move*, and moving tells the magic the threat isn't real.

I drop a rock onto my upturned palm. Purple light flares and swallows the impact. But that's a parlour trick. Not combat.

Then I stop asking and listen.

I sit cross-legged on the cold earth with the longsword across my knees. The ember is warm. I've been hitting it with requests all afternoon and it's responded to some and ignored others.

This time I don't ask it anything. I just listen.

And for the first time, it answers.

Not in words. In *feeling*. A pulse that starts at the base of my skull and radiates outward — through my jaw, my collarbones, down my arms into my fingertips. The longsword resonates. The ground beneath me hums. For one crystalline moment I feel the ember not as a thing inside me but as a frequency I'm *tuned to*. Like a string on an instrument that's been vibrating my whole life and I only just now pressed my ear to the wood.

I feel its edges. Its shape. It's bigger than I thought. Much bigger. What I've been using — the infusion, the ward — is the surface. The shallow water. Underneath...

Something vast. Patient. Mine.

Not ready. Not yet. But *there*.

The moment passes. The hum fades. I'm sitting in a dark clearing with numb fingers and a sword across my knees and the absolute certainty that what lives inside me has no ceiling I've found yet.

The ember settles back to its quiet burn. But warmer than before. Like it's pleased I finally listened.

---

I try fire next. The clearing is dark now. Stars through the canopy. I want something that burns — light, heat, damage.

The ember responds. A heat building in my chest, crawling down my arm. For one flickering instant the edge of the longsword glows orange at the base, a wavering tongue of heat that throws my shadow against the fallen oak—

Then it snaps back. Recoils up my arm like a startled animal. My palm stings. The ember retreats to the base of my skull and sits there pulsing with what I can only describe as frustration.

The power is there. I felt it. But the shaping — the architecture that turns raw force into a specific element — I can't hold it. Not tonight. Close. Closer than it should be.

I try for night vision. Pushing the ember upward into my eyes.

The ember doesn't even acknowledge the request. Flat refusal. The same disinterest it showed when I tried to make a rock expand mid-flight. Whatever lives inside me has opinions about its purpose, and *seeing in the dark* is not on the list. It is a weapon. It enhances weapons. It protects from weapons. It does not do utility work.

I sit in the dark and accept that the thing inside me has a personality. And it isn't changing tonight.

---

I practise with the flint stones next. The thought is simple — Arcane Edge rides a blade, rides an axe, rides anything with a cutting edge. A sharp flint has an edge.

The first stone is smooth. Round. I focus. The ember reaches, pools in my palm, and stays there. Confused. A smooth stone has no edge. No direction. The magic has nowhere to drain.

I throw it anyway. Crack against the oak. No magic. Just a man who can throw hard.

The second stone is a flint fragment. Sharp. An edge.

The ember catches. Silver-white light crawls across the stone like frost on glass. Not as bright as on the longsword. Not as stable. But *there*.

I throw.

The flint hits the oak dead centre. The silver light detonates on impact — a sharp crack that sends bark fragments spinning and leaves a scorched divot two inches deep.

I stare at the tree. Then at my empty hand.

It works on *edges*. Give the ember a cutting surface and it will infuse anything I throw.

---

The forest is cold. Dark. The moon is up and the things that hunt do it now because the dark is theirs. I should be heading back. I should be heading to Pip's door and the promised foot massage and a warm bed.

Instead I follow the stream north. Looking for something that will try to kill me.

Tracks within twenty minutes. Paw prints in the mud. Two sets. Large. Canine. Wolves — pushed south by something, probably the Hexcrawlers disrupting the northern territory.

The stream bends at a rocky depression. Moonlight cuts through a gap in the canopy and lights the hollow in pale silver.

Two wolves. Grey-furred. Lean. Hungry. One drinking from the stream. The other sitting on the far bank with its ears turning like dishes.

The scanner finds me first.

The sound it makes isn't a growl. It's a *statement*. The drinker's head snaps up. Both sets of amber eyes fix on me with the flat assessment of animals that have already decided I'm either food or a threat.

They separate. One left. One right. Pack tactics.

The left wolf lunges first — low, fast, teeth bared. It closes the distance in a heartbeat and snaps at my sword arm. I see the teeth coming and the purple light flares on instinct — Blade Ward answering the commitment of real teeth heading for real flesh. The bite hits the ward and skids. Blunted to pressure instead of a tear. I keep my feet.

The second wolf comes from my right while I'm processing the first. It finds the gap below my knee guard and *bites*. This one I feel — pressure, bruising, teeth that should be deeper than they are but are deep enough. I wrench my leg free. Stay standing. Barely.

My turn.

I kick dirt. My boot drags the stream bank and sends a spray of wet gravel and silt directly into the left wolf's face. Full recoil — head twisting, pawing at its eyes. Blind.

I'm already turning. The ember ignites down my arm — silver-white fire along the longsword, instant, hungry. The right wolf is still at my knee, jaws releasing, head coming up—

The longsword comes down in a single arc. The silver-white blade takes the wolf through the neck clean. The force detonation blows fur and mist in a ring. The head rolls into the stream.

The ember surges. Something new — a pulse of momentum, of *more*. The kill didn't end the movement. It *accelerated* it. My body is already turning, the longsword coming around, and the ember is pushing energy into the rotation like fuel into a fire.

The blinded wolf is right there. Pawing at its face. I bring the blade low — a vicious lateral aimed at its hind leg. The steel bites deep into the haunch. Blood dark on grey fur. The wolf screams. Its hind leg buckles.

I step back. Two paces. Stream at my side, rock at my back. Longsword up. Waiting.

The wolf thrashes. Lunges toward where I was, not where I am — guided by sound and fury. Teeth snap on empty air.

I step forward. The pirouette happens without thought — weight shifting, hips driving the rotation the way they've always known how, the longsword extending outward in a silver arc aimed at the wolf's collapsed side.

The blade catches the wolf across the ribs and the force detonates so hard the body lifts off the ground. It hits the stream bank and doesn't move.

Silence.

The stream runs. Moonlight on dark water. Two wolves dead. I stand between them, chest heaving, blood running from my leg into the water.

A borrowed sword. Dirt in its eyes. That's what it took.

---

I find a sheltered spot where the rock overhang meets the stream bank — back covered, sightlines in two directions. I peel the leather armour away from the leg bite and wince. The teeth went deeper than it felt in the moment. I unwrap the medicinal herb bundle — those dark serrated leaves I foraged on my first day in this world — and press them against the wound. The antiseptic sting is sharp enough to make my eyes water. The bleeding slows almost immediately. I bind it with a strip torn from the hem of my undershirt and do the same for the forearm where the ward caught the first bite.

Then I sit. One hour. Back against cold stone, longsword across my knees, and the forest settling around me. Distant owl. Wind through the canopy. The stream's quiet voice over stone. Nothing approaching. Nothing hunting. Not right now.

My body does what bodies do when you finally stop moving. The aches find me one at a time — the leg, the forearm, the shoulders, the deep exhaustion of muscles that have been running on the ember's fuel and are only now presenting the bill. But the rest helps. By the time the hour passes, the leg is stiff but functional. The forearm throbs but grips. Something essential has knit back together in the quiet.

I gather sharp flint stones from the stream bed while I rest. Twelve good fragments — sharp edges, good weight, flat enough to throw clean. I wrap them in wolf hide and tuck the bundle where I can reach it fast.

I also take what I can from the wolves. Two pelts — grey, thick winter fur. Four canines, pried free with the hand axe. Not pretty work, but serviceable. Could be trade goods. Could be proof.

---

The cougar almost gets me.

I find its tracks thirty minutes later. Further north. Deeper forest. Massive paw prints. Deep claw marks. Something that drops from above.

I spot it before it spots me — barely. A shape on a thick oak branch fifteen feet up. Tawny fur catching moonlight. Yellow eyes open and fixed directly on me. It was waiting for me to walk beneath it.

I pull a sharp flint from my belt. The ember catches the edge. Silver-white light in my palm. I throw.

Long range. The stone streaks silver through the dark and cracks into the branch six inches left of the cat's shoulder. Bark explodes. Miss.

The cat drops from the branch — fifteen feet, silent, hitting the ground in a crouch that absorbs the fall like water absorbs a stone. Then it *runs*. At me. Low and fast.

I backtrack hard. Pull another flint. Throw. This one connects — catches the cougar in the shoulder and the force detonation punches through muscle. The cat screams. Its front leg buckles. Blood matting tawny fur.

It stares at me with one compromised shoulder and the survival mathematics of a predator. I can see it calculating — the distance, the damage, the strange silver light that hits harder than any stone should.

It turns. Three-legged. Fast even wounded. Gone into the undergrowth heading east.

I let it go. The fight was mine from the moment I saw it first. That's the lesson — see them before they see you, open at range, and they're the ones making bad decisions in the dark.

---

I lean against a tree. Breathing hard. The cougar fight was fast — faster than the wolves — and I'm buzzing with the particular energy of a body that expected to get hit and didn't. But the fight replays behind my eyes and I don't like what I see. I spotted it first. Opened at range. Put a stone into it. Did everything right. And it *still* closed thirty-five feet in a single bound before I could blink.

If that had been a Hexcrawler — faster, tougher, six legs — the range game buys me one throw. Maybe two. Then it's in my face.

I need to be faster.

I stop thinking. That's the key. That's what I realise — every time the ember has worked, it wasn't because I *understood* it. It was because I *wanted* it. Arcane Edge didn't happen because I studied infusion theory. It happened because I held a weapon and *intended to kill something with it*. Blade Ward didn't happen because I designed a shield. It happened because something was about to hit me and I *refused to take it*.

The ember doesn't speak intellect. It speaks *me*. Force of personality. Raw stubborn will.

So forget the meditation. Forget trying to architect something elegant.

I run.

Full sprint. Flat out. Not graceful, not tactical. Just a man running as fast as his body allows and *wanting more*.

*Gotta go fast. Gotta go fast. Gotta go FAST—*

It happens on the sixth stride.

Silver-white light detonates under my foot. Not a sustained glow — an *impact*. Each footfall triggering a burst of force against the ground. Not enough to crater the earth. Just enough to *launch*. Each step kicks harder than my legs alone can manage. The force rebounds upward through my boots, my ankles, my knees — and suddenly each stride covers twice the distance it should.

The forest blurs. The stream becomes a silver line. The wind hits my face like a wall and the ember *sings* down my legs in pulses — strike, launch, strike, launch.

Then it gutters. My next step hits plain dirt and the deceleration nearly sends me face-first into a thornbush. I stagger, catch a tree, and stand there panting with my hand on bark and my heart trying to exit through my teeth.

I look back at the trail. Footprints glowing silver in the mud, already fading. Each one deeper than it should be. Spaced further apart than any human stride.

I start laughing. Alone in a dark forest with torn-up legs and a magic coal in my skull that just learned to be rocket boots because I was too stubborn to ask nicely.

---

The bear is the last hunt of the night.

I find it in the deep forest. North-northeast. Past the wolves' hollow, past the cougar's tree, into old growth that doesn't want me here. The tracks are massive. Five-toed. Deep-set. Territorial markings scored into hardwood at shoulder height. Fresh sap still wet.

The den is a shallow depression at the base of a granite outcrop. The smell hits first — musk, rotten meat. A dark brown mass the size of a small horse. Shoulders hunched. Head down. Sleeping.

I creep close. Twenty-five feet. Pull a sharp flint. The ember catches it instantly.

Two spell slots. Both at once. One for the new trick — the stride. One for Thunderous Strike, loaded into the longsword. The ember splits and I feel it fork — one channel pouring down through my legs, the other coiling along my sword arm.

I run.

The silver detonations kick off the frozen earth. Three strides. Four. The trees blur. The bear's sleeping shape grows large and real and close. My hand finds the longsword mid-sprint and draws it in a single motion.

I aim for the hind leg. The thick joint where power meets ground. Take that away and five hundred pounds of muscle can't chase me.

The blade connects mid-stride. The longsword bites deep into the bear's rear haunch — and then the thunder detonates. The sound is enormous. A concussive *boom* that flattens undergrowth and sends birds screaming from the canopy for a hundred yards. The bear's body lifts off the ground and slams into the granite outcrop.

It roars. The sound shakes my ribs.

I'm already gone. The Arcane Stride carries me back — thirty-five feet of silver-kicked sprint. The bear is surprised, confused, hurt. Its hind leg is a ruin of torn muscle and thunder-scorched fur.

The momentum catches me. My last stride hits a root and my legs tangle — I pitch forward, hit the ground shoulder-first, roll twice through frozen bracken, and slam into a tree trunk with my back.

Prone. On the ground. Forty feet from a very angry, very hurt bear.

It heaves itself upright. The hind leg barely holds. It swings its massive head toward me and the roar it unleashes is not a threat. It's a *declaration*.

But it can't reach me. Not yet. Forty feet of frozen ground between us, and half its legs aren't working. It stands there swaying, bleeding, staring at me with black eyes that have already made every decision they're ever going to make.

I shove myself upright. The tree at my back helps. My legs are shaking — Arcane Stride afterburn, cold, adrenaline — but they hold. I pull a flint from the bundle. The ember catches the edge. Forty feet. Long range. I throw.

The stone sails wide. Silver streak arcing past the bear's shoulder and detonating against the granite behind it in a shower of sparks. Nothing. Miss.

I step back. Fifteen feet. Every foot I've got after standing.

The bear comes. Three-legged. That horrible dragging charge. Blood trailing behind it in a dark smear. One good eye locked on me. It closes thirty feet and stops. Panting. Twenty-five feet away.

Not close enough. Not yet. But the math is becoming clear — its movement matches my backtrack almost exactly. I can't outpace it. I can't close to accurate range without it closing on me. And at long range, the throws aren't landing.

I throw again. Twenty-five feet. The flint streaks silver and goes wide — cracking into a tree trunk to the bear's left. The detonation showers bark. The bear doesn't flinch. It's past flinching.

I backtrack. Thirty feet this time. Fast as my legs allow.

The bear comes again. Thirty feet of three-legged commitment. Same distance. Same result. Twenty-five feet. Panting. Swaying. The growl is weaker now — wet-sounding. Something broken inside. But it won't stop. It won't turn. Whatever is keeping this animal upright isn't survival instinct anymore. It's something older and simpler.

We're locked. The same distance. The same pattern. It can't catch me and I can't hit it. I could do this all night — except my flints are finite and every throw at this range is a coin flip I keep losing.

The stones won't come to me. So I go to them.

Ten feet forward. Deliberate. The bear sees me step closer and something in its posture shifts — the shoulders bunch, the good eye widens. It thinks I'm charging.

I'm not. I stop. Fifteen feet. Normal range. Flint in hand. Ember catches. I throw with every ounce of will I have left.

The stone hits the bear dead centre in the chest. The silver-white force punches inward and detonates against the ribcage. Something cracks — deep, structural, final. The bear's front legs buckle. Its head drops. One good eye finds me one last time.

It doesn't roar. It just stops.

Five hundred pounds fold sideways onto the frozen earth. One long exhale — fog hanging in the moonlight — and then nothing.

I stand there with flint dust on my fingers and the ember fading from my palm. The longsword never left its sheath for the killing blow.

My hands are shaking. Not fear. Not cold. The after-vibration of a body that just pushed itself somewhere new and is quietly recalculating what it's capable of.

The ember burns. Steady. Warm. Not smug this time.

*Proud.*

---

I harvest everything. The bear pelt — thick, dark brown, winter-heavy. Four claws. Two canines the length of my thumb. Two haunches of meat wrapped in the wolf pelts I took earlier.

I head south. Heavy. Aching. Loaded down with the spoils of a night that started with two wolves and ended with a bear and in between I learned to throw lightning in a stone and turn my feet into weapons.

The village is dead asleep. I stash the pelts and meat in the stable, slip into the inn like smoke, and steal a heel of bread, a wedge of hard cheese, and two strips of dried meat from the kitchen. The bandit's code — take what you need, not what you can carry.

I eat standing in the dark. Then I look at the stairs.

Pip's room. The door handle. The lock.

I kneel. Pull Ryn's spare picks from my belt. Close my eyes.

*Every lock is a conversation. The pins are asking a question. Lighter than you think. Always lighter.*

I've been fighting all night. My hands have killed two wolves and a bear and thrown flint hard enough to crack bone. But the lock doesn't want force. It wants patience.

I ease the tension wrench in. Light pressure. The hook pick finds the first pin. I feel the set in my back teeth. The second pin is stiffer. I ease off the wrench — *lighter, lighter* — and the pick finds the angle.

A tiny click. The wrench turns.

The lock opens.

I kneel there grinning in the dark like an idiot. Ryn just earned his first curiosity and doesn't know it yet.

---

Candlelight. Low. Almost gone. The wildflowers — pale blue and deep violet — sit on the windowsill in their clay cup, catching the last of the flame. Pip is curled on her side, hair loose on the pillow. She tried to stay awake. The candle says she tried for a long time.

She stirs when the floorboard shifts. Blinks. Sees me.

"You're late." Then her eyes narrow. "You smell like... is that *blood*?"

"Bear."

A long pause.

"You fought a *bear*?"

"I promised a foot massage. Getting killed by a bear wasn't going to get in the way of that."

She laughs — soft, sleepy, real — and pulls the blanket back.

"Get that armor off. You're not getting blood on my sheets."

She sees the bandaged arm and the wrapped leg. Makes me sit. Unwraps the forearm with careful hands and rewraps it tighter, better.

"You went hunting wolves and bears in the dark. Alone. The night before you leave for something worse."

She finishes the wrap. Holds my arm for a beat longer than medical.

"You're insane, Kenji."

She says it the way people say things they've decided to accept.

Her feet are cold when I take them in my hands. She flinches — then settles. The massage is slow, deliberate, and exactly as promised. She watches me from the pillow with eyes too awake now for sleep.

"Tell me a story," she says. "A real one. Not the monster-killing ones you tell for ale."

The candle burns down. The flowers lose their colour in the dark. The stories I tell her are quiet ones — not about monsters or magic, but about the strange uncertain feeling of waking up in a world you don't recognise and choosing to stay anyway. She listens with her hand on my arm. At some point the words give way to silence and the silence gives way to everything else.

The ember burns low. The night passes.

I close my eyes with her warmth against my chest and the steady pulse of something vast and patient humming at the base of my skull, and the last thing I think is that tomorrow I'm walking toward the thing that called a man by his name and took him into the dark.

And the ember answers that thought the way it always does.

It burns brighter.

## *Day Three — What Walks Toward Us*

---

Morning light through Pip's window. Third sunrise in this world. The ember wakes before I do — a slow pulse at the base of my skull, warmer than yesterday, deeper. Like something that spent the night growing roots.

Pip is still asleep beside me. Her breathing is slow and even. Her hair fans across the pillow in auburn tangles and one hand rests on the space where my arm was. I lie still and let the thoughts come.

Six weeks. That's what Maren said. Whatever woke up in The Delve — whatever reached into Harlan Brennick's cellar and called him by name — it stirred six weeks ago. The Hexcrawlers started appearing. The farmhands started thinning. The pond went grey.

And then — roughly six weeks later — I woke up face down in the mud on a dirt road outside Duskfen with nothing in my pockets and an ember burning at the base of my skull.

Coincidence.

I turn the word over in the dark of Pip's room and it doesn't fit. It sits wrong in my mouth the way a lie sits wrong. Six weeks. The same stretch of time. The same forest. Whatever is out there pushed against the skin of the world — Edwyn's words, *divine bleed* — and I showed up on the other side of it with magic that isn't learned. Magic that rides weapons. That enhances steel. That responds to *will* rather than study.

The ember pulses. Not an answer. An acknowledgment.

Who am I?

I don't know my past. I know I can throw hard enough to crack bone. I know I can dig a pit trap by instinct. I know the weight of a blade before I've been taught to hold one. I know how to read a room and talk a blacksmith out of his best steel and talk a barmaid out of her better judgement. These things live in my body like muscle memory for a life I can't access.

And last night — the ember listened to me for the first time. Really listened. What I felt underneath the surface wasn't small. It was vast and patient and *mine*. A weapon-force with no ceiling I've found yet. I turned my feet into weapons through sheer stubbornness. I fought a bear with rocks and won. I picked a lock and delivered a foot massage.

Something brought me here. Something that knew what was waking up in The Delve and dropped a Blade Channeler on its doorstep.

I don't know what that means yet. But I'm going to find out.

The thought doesn't get to settle. My stomach clenches — hard, sudden, the kind of hunger that doesn't politely request attention but *demands* it. The magic that lets me heal like a furnace burns fuel like one too. Four hours since the stolen bread and cheese. My body is already running a deficit.

I need food. Now.

---

I ease out of bed without waking Pip. Armour on, sword on hip, down the stairs. The common room is pre-dawn grey. The fire is banked. Nobody up yet except the sound of Pip's father moving in the kitchen — pots clanking, the heavy rhythm of a man who starts his day before the sun does.

I push through the kitchen door.

The innkeeper — broad, weathered, flour to his elbows — turns and looks at me with the expression of a man who knows exactly which room I didn't sleep in and has decided that this conversation isn't happening before breakfast.

"Eggs," he says flatly. "Sit down."

He feeds me without small talk — eggs, bread, thick bacon, a cup of hot broth that tastes like someone's grandmother knew what she was doing. I eat fast and without dignity. The hunger eases. The ember settles.

I finish the plate. Push it back. Now business.

"I've got something for you. Two haunches of bear meat, fresh from last night. And I've got wolf meat if you want it. Enough to feed the inn for days. What's that worth to you?"

He stops wiping his hands. Bear meat. In a village where the northern forest has been too dangerous to hunt for weeks. His eyes do the math — supply, demand, the fact that his stores are running thinner than he'd admit to Pip.

"Bear," he says. "Fresh."

"Killed it myself. Six hours ago."

He looks at me. Re-evaluates something. Then nods once.

"I'll take both haunches and whatever wolf meat you've got. Three gold for the bear. One for the wolf. Fair price for fresh game in a village that hasn't had any in a month."

Four gold pieces hit the counter.

"And your breakfast is still free," he adds. "On account of my daughter being happier than I've seen her in a year. Which we are *not* discussing."

He goes back to his pots.

---

Aldric's forge is already hot. The hammer stopped when he saw me coming — he's standing in the doorway with his arms folded, watching me cross the square with the same measuring look he gave me two days ago. Except now I'm carrying wolf pelts and a bear hide over my shoulder instead of a stick and two rocks.

"Morning, Aldric."

His eyes go to the pelts. To the bear hide. Stay there.

"That's a brown bear," he says. Not a question. "Full winter pelt."

"Killed it last night. Also two wolves." I set the bundle on his workbench. "I need gold for the road and I need better armour. What can you do for me?"

Aldric unwraps the bear hide first. Runs his hand across it — checking thickness, checking cuts, checking whether the man who skinned it knew what he was doing. He grunts. Professional assessment.

"Fieldwork," he says. "Not a tanner's job. But the hide is intact where it counts. Thick. Winter grade." He folds it. Sets it aside. Examines the wolf pelts. The canines. The bear claws.

He leans against his workbench and looks at me.

"Two gold for the wolf pelts. Three for the bear hide. One gold for the canines and claws combined — they're component material, not finished goods." He holds up a hand before I speak. "That's generous. I know what these are worth and so do you."

He reaches for the bear hide again. Turns it in his hands. The craftsman replacing the trader.

"As for armour—" He taps the leather on my chest. His son's armour. "This is protection for a farmhand in a bar fight. Not for a man walking toward Hexcrawlers."

He sets the bear hide flat on the workbench.

"Bear leather, boiled and layered properly, is twice as hard as what you're wearing. I can reinforce your chest piece, add layered bear-hide plating on the shoulders and forearms. Won't slow you down like metal would — you're fast, I can tell that much — but it'll turn a claw that would've opened you up."

He looks at me.

"I can have it done before you leave if I start now. Cost you nothing — the bear hide *is* the payment." A pause. "But if you want the *good* version — hardened, oil-cured, riveted at the joints — I need something I don't have."

He reaches under his workbench and pulls out a cracked, empty glass vial with a faint amber residue inside.

"Ironwood sap. Comes from a tree that only grows in deep old forest — old growth, the kind near ley lines. The sap hardens leather to near-steel strength without the weight. There used to be ironwood stands north of Duskfen." He sets the vial down. "That was before whatever's out there poisoned the forest. If you find one on your way to the Brennick farm — or beyond — bring me back a vial of sap and I'll make you armour that'll last longer than you will."

I tell him the plan. Hold off on the basic version. I want the best he can make. I'll find the ironwood sap on the road and send a runner back with it. He holds the hide. The runner picks up the finished armour and delivers it to me.

"You've got someone who'll run through Hexcrawler territory to deliver armour," he says.

"I'm working on it."

"Hm." He folds his arms. "I'll hold the hide and keep your current leathers in working shape for now. You find the sap, get it to me, and I'll need half a day from the moment it's in my hands."

He picks up his hammer. Pauses.

"Ironwood trees have silver-white bark. Smooth. Distinctive — you won't mistake it for anything else. The sap runs amber and smells like hot copper. Cut a notch at chest height and it bleeds. Fill the vial." He pulls a second empty vial from under his bench. "Take this. Don't let the sap touch bare skin — it irritates before it cures."

---

The stable. Early morning light through the slats. Ryn is already up — sitting cross-legged on a hay bale with his lockpick set open, working a padlock with the focused silence of a man doing his morning prayers.

He sees me and the pick freezes mid-pin.

"Kenji." Cautious. The look of someone who remembers saying no and is wondering if the question is coming back around.

It is. But different this time.

I lay it out. Not a fighter. Not an adventurer. A porter. Carry the party's camp gear on the road. Stay at camp, away from combat, away from danger. And one specific job — when I find ironwood sap on the trade road, he runs it back to Aldric in Duskfen and picks up the finished armour. Delivers it to me wherever we've made camp. A straight supply run on a road he already knows.

His eyes narrow.

"So I'm... a delivery boy."

"You're the team's logistics. Gear, supplies, camp setup. And one round trip on a road you walked to get here in the first place. You stay behind the line. The party handles anything with claws. You handle everything that doesn't want to kill us."

He's quiet for a long moment. The padlock hangs forgotten in his hands. His eyes move to his pack — tightly rolled, ready to move at short notice.

"The road to the Brennick farm," he says slowly. "Half a day. And I'd be with a group. A fighter, a healer, and..." He looks at me. At the longsword. At the leather armour that still has wolf blood in the creases. "...whatever you are."

"I'm the man who brings you interesting curiosities. This is how I get close enough to find them."

Something shifts. The fear is still there — it lives in his shoulders. But underneath it, the same thing I saw yesterday. The part of him that wants to matter.

"I have conditions," he says.

"Name them."

"One. I don't fight. I don't hold a weapon. I don't stand anywhere near anything with more than four legs."

"Done."

"Two. I get first pick of curiosities. Not second. Not *after* the valuable stuff. First."

"Already agreed."

"Three." He hesitates. "If it gets bad — really bad — and you tell me to run... I run. No guilt. No *where were you when we needed you*. I run and nobody holds it against me."

He's looking at me with the raw honesty of someone who knows exactly who they are and isn't pretending otherwise.

I look him in the eye. Steady.

"If it gets bad and I tell you to run — you run. No guilt. No judgement. That's a promise. No harm will come to you."

Ryn holds my gaze for a moment longer than he's held anyone's since arriving in Duskfen. Then he nods. Once. Sets the padlock down.

"I'll pack light. When do we leave?"

"Dawn departure was the plan. We've still got an hour. Be at the village gate with your pack."

He's already rolling his bedroll before I reach the stable door.

---

I find them at the well. Together for once — Sera checking the straps on a heavy canvas pack stuffed with rations and camp gear, a light crossbow and a quiver of bolts leaned against the stone beside her. She did her part. Edwyn stands nearby with his satchel, freshly packed, the clay jars inside clinking softly when he shifts his weight.

Sera sees me first. Her eyes do what they always do — the sweep. Face, posture, hands, sword, boots. But this time the sweep snags on something. She looks again. Slower.

The bandaged forearm under my bracer. The slightly different way I'm favouring my left leg. The fact that my leather armour has new stains that weren't there yesterday evening.

She straightens up.

"You went out last night."

Not a question. Not an accusation. A reading.

Edwyn looks between us. Adjusts his satchel strap. Says nothing.

Sera's eyes haven't left mine.

"What did you kill."

"Two wolves, a tie against a cougar, and a bear."

She doesn't move. But something behind her eyes recalculates.

"A bear," she repeats.

"Brown bear. Full grown. Den near a granite outcrop north-northeast. The wolves were a warm-up. The cougar ran after I put a stone into its shoulder at range. The bear—"

"You fought a *bear*. Alone. At night. In a forest full of Hexcrawlers." She says each word like she's setting them on a table. "With a longsword you've never been trained to use."

"I didn't use the longsword on the bear. I used rocks."

Silence.

Edwyn is staring at me with the expression of a healer who's been told his patient performed surgery on himself in the dark.

Sera looks at my bandaged forearm. At my leg. At Aldric's forge across the square where a bear hide is spread on a workbench.

"That's the hide," she says.

"That's the hide."

"You're an idiot," she says.

A beat.

"But you're an idiot who killed a bear with rocks and walked back." She folds her arms — less wall, more assessment. "How."

I lay it out. Arcane Edge on sharp flint — the ember infuses anything with a cutting edge. Opening at distance. Forcing predators to close on my terms. The wolves went down in melee after I blinded one with dirt. The cougar ran when the math stopped working. The bear — Thunderous Strike on the opening charge, Arcane Stride to get in and out, then kiting at range until I stepped closer and put a stone through its ribs.

She listens without interrupting. Her jaw shifted three degrees to one side.

"Arcane Stride," she says. "You can move at double speed."

"Twelve hours on one spell slot. Learned it last night."

"And the rock-throwing. You infuse flint with force magic and throw it."

"If it has a sharp edge. Smooth stones don't hold."

Something in her expression reorganizes — the assessment from two days ago being rewritten in real time.

"Your instincts aren't the problem," she says slowly. "I told you that. I was right. But instinct without foundation gets you killed the moment something is faster than your reaction time. You fought a bear and won because bears are strong and stupid. Hexcrawlers are strong and *coordinated*. Three of them will flank you in the dark and the range game won't save you because one will circle behind while the other two hold your attention."

She picks up the crossbow. Holds it out to Edwyn without looking.

"Father. Take this. Go to the field behind the chapel. Set up against the hay bales and put thirty bolts into them. I don't care about accuracy right now — I care about loading speed. You need to be able to reload without looking down. Go."

Edwyn takes the crossbow with the careful reverence of a man receiving a holy relic he's not qualified to hold.

"*Go.*"

He goes.

Sera turns back to me. Draws her arming sword. Unhurried. Professional.

"One hour. That's what you asked for two days ago. I told you to survive the next fight. You survived three. So here's what happens now."

Her eyes are sharp and completely focused.

"I can't teach you instinct. You don't need it. What I can teach you is what to do when something fast gets inside your range and the rocks are gone and the magic is spent and all you have is three feet of steel and the space between your feet."

She shifts her weight. The guard tightens.

"Defensive swordwork. How to not die when everything goes wrong. Draw your sword, Kenji."

---

She comes at me without warning. Three fast cuts — diagonal, lateral, thrust. I catch the first on instinct, redirect the second, and the third stops with the point at my throat.

"Dead," she says. "But better dead than yesterday would have been. Your guard is raw but it holds under pressure. Stop dropping your elbow on the redirect — you're opening your ribs."

She resets. Comes again. This time I keep the elbow tucked. The redirect is tighter. Her thrust slides past my shoulder instead of finding my neck.

"Better."

She teaches me the thing that matters — what she calls *the cage*. A defensive framework built around three principles: keep the blade between you and the threat, move your feet instead of reaching with your arms, and when steel meets steel, feel which way their weight commits and give them the empty space to fall into.

The cage clicks. Not perfectly — my muscle memory keeps trying to turn defence into offence, to counter when she wants me to *absorb* — but the framework seats itself in my footwork. Blade between. Feet moving. Let them fall past.

She speeds up. Testing. Her blade comes from angles I didn't know existed and the cage holds for three exchanges, four, five — and then she feints high and sweeps low and I'm on my back looking at the sky.

"Dead again." She offers her hand. "But you lasted five. Yesterday would have been one."

The final spar is harder. She comes at full speed — the real Sera, not the instructor — and the cage breaks in four exchanges. My elbow drops. My feet plant. She puts me on the ground twice in thirty seconds and the second time the flat of her blade taps my chest with a finality that says *this lesson is over*.

She sheathes her sword.

"Your shoulder still telegraphs the rising cut. Your elbow still drops under pressure. And you plant your feet when you're scared instead of moving them." She picks up her waterskin. "But the cage is in there now. Under pressure, your body will find it."

She looks at me with something that wasn't there two days ago.

"You killed a bear with rocks, Kenji. That's not nothing. But out there—" She nods northeast. "—the things we're walking toward don't care about impressive. They care about weaknesses. And yours is that you don't know what you don't know."

She takes a drink.

"So stay behind me when it gets tight. Use the range game when you can. And when you can't—"

She taps her own chest where the flat of the blade landed on mine.

"Cage. Feet. Blade between."

From the field behind the chapel, the distant *thwack... thwack... thwack* of crossbow bolts hitting hay bales. Edwyn is practising. The intervals are getting shorter.

---

I stop at the village gate. Sera and Edwyn are ahead — Sera checking the crossbow bolts in Edwyn's quiver with the expression of someone who doesn't trust anyone else's packing. Edwyn endures it with priestly patience. Ryn lingers a few steps behind them, pack on his back, looking like a man who's made a decision and is trying not to unmake it.

The ember pulses. Different than before.

I go still. Something new — sitting in the space the ember grew into last night. A shape I can't see but can *feel*. Like reaching into a dark room and knowing there's a handle before your fingers close around it.

Blades. Small ones. Not steel — something darker. Shadow and force compressed into an edge. I can feel them waiting to be called, five of them, hovering at the edge of existence like words on the tip of a tongue. The ember shows me in sensation, not language. The throw. The impact. And then... something coming *back*. Warmth returning through the same channel the blade left by. Life drawn from the wound and fed back into me.

A weapon that heals me when it hurts something else.

My hand twitches. The ember is eager. But a spell slot is a spell slot, and the road ahead has teeth. I close my fist. Hold the feeling where it is.

*Not yet. But soon.*

The party forms up at the gate. Sera on point. Edwyn behind her. Ryn behind Edwyn, eyes already scanning the tree line with the nervous attention of a rabbit in open ground. Me at the rear.

Pip is in the doorway of the inn. She doesn't wave. She just watches — arms folded, one shoulder against the frame — and when I look back she holds my gaze for three seconds before she goes inside.

Maren is at her window. A hand raised. Brief. Then the curtain falls.

Aldric's hammer starts up behind us. The sound follows us to the tree line and then the forest swallows it.

---

The trade road is older than Duskfen. You can feel it under your boots — packed earth worn by decades of cart wheels and foot traffic, grooved and rutted and slowly being reclaimed by the forest on both sides. Once this was a proper road. Now it's a suggestion the trees are considering overruling.

Sera sets the pace. Not fast, not slow — the measured stride of someone who knows that speed means noise and noise means things with six legs know where you are.

Thirty minutes in, she holds up a fist. Everyone stops.

"Tracks," she says quietly. She crouches. Points.

Six-legged prints in the soft earth at the road's edge. Wide. Deep. The gait pattern spreading outward into the undergrowth to the east.

"Hexcrawler patrol. Two of them. Heading east." She touches the edge of the nearest print. "Eight hours old. Maybe ten. They crossed the road during the night and kept moving."

She stands. Pulls out her map. "We're here. This patrol crossed between zones one and two. If they're running on schedule, they won't loop back for another six hours."

She folds the map.

"We move. Eyes open."

---

The forest changes as we walk. Slowly at first — a tree with bark darker than it should be. A patch of undergrowth that's grey instead of green. A stream crossing where the water runs clear but the stones underneath have a faint iridescent sheen I recognise from the old mill pond.

I fall into step beside Edwyn and tell him what to watch for. Ironwood tree — silver-white bark, smooth, grows near ley lines. Aldric needs the sap for the armour. I've got the vial.

Edwyn nods without breaking stride. "I know ironwood. Rare but not unheard of. The Temple archives mention them as markers — they grow where the boundary between our world and the divine runs thin. Ley line anchors." He glances at the corrupted undergrowth. "If the corruption hasn't killed them. We'll be passing through deeper old growth near the Brennick farm — that area sits close to the same ley line the old mill tapped. If there's ironwood anywhere between here and The Delve, it'll be there."

He looks at me. "I'll watch for it."

I scan the tree line as we walk. Dark-barked oak and ash and twisted elm. No silver-white bark. Not in this stretch. Maybe further northeast. I keep the vial in my pack and my eyes open.

Then I notice it. Not with my eyes. With the silence.

For the first hour of walking the forest was alive. Birds. Insects. Small things rustling in the undergrowth. Normal forest noise.

It stopped. I can't pinpoint when, but the air around us is silent now in a way that has texture. The trees are taller here. Older. The canopy thicker. And the sounds that should fill the space between them have packed up and left.

"The forest is quiet," I say.

Sera nods without turning. "Has been for the last ten minutes. You're the first to say it."

Edwyn has gone pale. His hand is on the flame symbol at his neck.

"It's the *thinning*," he says quietly. "The same thing my farmhands described. We're closer to the source. The ambient presence is stronger here — not dangerous yet, but strong enough to push wildlife out." He looks at me. "If it starts affecting us — fatigue, whispers, the feeling of being watched — we say it out loud. Immediately."

Ryn hasn't spoken since the village gate. His knuckles are white on his pack straps.

---

Another hour. The trade road narrows. The trees press in. The grey corruption is more visible now — patches of bark that look bleached, leaves curling inward, a wrongness in the colour of things that's hard to name but easy to feel.

My stomach tightens. Three hours since breakfast. The ember burns hot and the body that houses it doesn't care about Hexcrawler patrols when the fuel gauge hits empty.

"We need to eat. Soon."

Sera points. A rocky clearing just off the road — a fallen tree creating a natural windbreak, sightlines in three directions.

"Ten minutes. Eat fast."

The party pulls off the road. Sera takes a position facing northeast, chewing dried meat without looking at it, watching the tree line. Edwyn distributes rations — hard bread, cheese, dried fruit. Ryn sits on a rock with his pack between his knees and eats in small nervous bites.

I eat. The hunger settles. The ember calms.

Then Sera goes still mid-chew. Her hand moves to her sword.

"Nobody move," she says quietly. "Nobody speak."

Her eyes are fixed on a point sixty feet northeast through the trees. I follow her gaze.

Movement. Low. The size of a large dog but the shape entirely wrong. Too many joints. A head swinging in slow arcs — tasting the air.

Then a second shape. Flanking wide to the right. Same size. Same wrong geometry. Moving in coordinated silence.

Two Hexcrawlers. Moving toward the road. Toward us.

They haven't seen the party yet. The fallen tree and the rocky clearing are providing cover. But they're closing. Sixty feet and shrinking.

Sera's voice is barely a breath.

"Two. Coming from the northeast. Sixty feet. They're hunting — sweeping pattern. They'll hit our scent in about thirty seconds."

She draws her sword without a sound. Edwyn's hands are shaking but he's reaching for the crossbow. Ryn has gone absolutely white and frozen.

Sera is looking at me. Waiting. She follows the plan of whoever's calling the shots in the field. Right now that's me.

"Sera — go for the one fifty feet east-northeast. Edwyn — back her up with bolts but keep your distance. I'll take the one sixty feet out."

I grab a sharp flint. The ember catches the edge — instant, hungry, silver-white light blooming in my palm. I break from cover and sprint twenty-five feet toward my target.

Thirty-five feet. Long range. But the Hexcrawler doesn't know I'm here. I throw.

The flint crosses the gap in a silver streak and hits the Hexcrawler square in the flank. The force detonation blows chitin fragments in a ring — the creature shrieks, that sub-frequency vibration I remember from the mill, the one that lives in your back teeth. It staggers sideways. Six legs scrambling. Six eyes snapping to my position.

It sees me. All six eyes lock on.

From the east — the sound of Sera's blade finding its target. A lateral cut that takes the second Hexcrawler across two of its front legs. Chitin splits. Dark iridescent fluid sprays across the bracken. The creature screams in harmony with the first — the same awful song in different keys.

Then a crossbow bolt punches through the second creature's rear membrane. Edwyn. Ugly release, wobbly flight — but it hit. The Hexcrawler bucks.

The surprise round is spent. Both creatures are wounded. Alerted. And I can see them reorganizing — the coordinated intelligence Sera warned me about. My Hexcrawler isn't charging blindly. It's *circling*. Moving laterally, keeping distance, looking for a flank angle. The second one is backing up — drawing Sera forward. Toward the gap where the first one could loop around behind her.

They're not panicking. They're *coordinating*.

The ember forks down my legs. Silver-white light detonates under my first footfall and the world *lurches*.

Arcane Stride.

Fifty feet of forest disappears in four strides. Each step kicking force off the earth. The trees blur. Sera's back appears ahead of me. The wounded Hexcrawler beyond her — leaking fluid, bolt in its flank — doesn't see me coming because nothing in this forest moves this fast.

I blow past Sera on her left. The longsword clears the scabbard mid-stride. No Arcane Edge — the ember is busy being rocket boots — just naked steel and momentum.

The slash comes from the sprint. Every ounce of speed channeled into a diagonal cut aimed at the creature's wounded flank.

The blade bites through cracked chitin and sinks into the meat underneath. The Hexcrawler shrieks — and drops. Mid-shriek. The sound cuts off like someone closed a door on it. Six legs fold inward and the body hits the forest floor with a wet crunch.

One hit. One kill.

I'm already past it. The momentum carries me ten feet beyond the corpse. I dig my boots into the soft earth and stop clean — weight centered, sword up, no tree required.

I turn. The Hexcrawler is a heap of chitin and dark fluid. Sera is standing over it with her sword raised for a strike that's no longer needed.

"I had that," she says.

She doesn't waste time. Her head snaps toward the first Hexcrawler — the one that was circling to flank her. It's forty feet away now, six eyes locked on the spot where its partner just died.

Sera sprints. Closes the distance. Her blade catches the creature across the front two legs as it tries to pivot. Chitin cracks.

Edwyn's second bolt sails wide and thunks into a tree. He winces.

The creature is hurt. Badly. Its partner is dead. The flanking maneuver is broken. It's surrounded — Sera in front, me behind with silver light fading from my boots.

It runs. Six legs churning earth, the wounded side dragging, crashing through undergrowth heading northeast at full speed. In three seconds it's forty feet away. In five it's lost in the tree line.

Toward The Delve. Toward whatever sent it.

Sera watches it go.

"It's running home," she says quietly. "Which means whatever is at home now knows we're coming."

---

Silence. The forest holds its breath. Iridescent fluid pools under the dead Hexcrawler.

Ryn's head appears above the rocks. He stares at the corpse. Then at me. Then at the silver-fading footprints carved into the earth.

"What... *are* you?" he whispers.

Sera sheathes her sword. Walks to the dead Hexcrawler. Crouches. Examines it with clinical precision.

"You crossed fifty feet in about two seconds," she says without looking up. "And killed it in one swing."

She stands. Looks at me.

"You're not a swordsman, Kenji. You're a *striker*. You hit once, hard, and you're gone before anything touches you."

She looks northeast.

"That one will reach The Delve before we reach the Brennick farm. If there's anything intelligent directing these patrols, it knows our numbers, our direction, and that one of us moves faster than anything in this forest."

She picks up her pack.

"We move. Now. Fast pace. No more stops until the farm."

---

The trade road narrows further. The corruption deepens — trees leaning at wrong angles, patches of earth that look exhausted, a smell underneath everything like old pennies left in standing water.

Then through a gap in the canopy — the angular shape of a roofline. Timber-framed. Sagging slightly to the east. A stone chimney with nothing coming out of it.

The Brennick farm.

The clearing opens around us. Fifty feet of open ground between the tree line and the farmhouse. The grass is dead. Not autumn dead — *wrong* dead. Grey and brittle, crunching under our boots like old paper. A vegetable garden to the left has collapsed into itself, the plants blackened and curled inward.

The farmhouse is intact but abandoned. The front door is closed. One window shutter hangs loose, swaying in a wind I can't feel. A woodpile beside the house still has an axe wedged in the splitting log — untouched for six weeks.

I see them. Boot prints in the dead grass leading from the front door to the tree line north. One set. Deep. Evenly spaced. Walking, not running. Harlan's last walk.

No return prints.

And then I feel it.

Not with my eyes. Not with my ears. With the ember.

A *pull*. Faint. Steady. Coming from below the farmhouse. Like standing at the edge of deep water and feeling the current tug at your ankles. The ember reacts — not with fear, not with eagerness. With *recognition*. It knows what's down there. Or it knows the *kind* of thing that's down there.

Edwyn has stopped walking. His hand is on his flame symbol. His face is grey.

"I feel it," he says quietly. "The bleed. It's stronger here than anywhere in Duskfen. Much stronger."

The cellar doors. Wooden double doors set into the ground at the side of the building. One slightly ajar. Dark underneath.

Sera looks at me.

"This is your call, Kenji. We talked about the plan — one hour maximum, seals ready, everyone watches for symptoms. How do you want to approach?"

"We may be able to do multiple tries," I tell her. "Let's do what we can in the first hour and make it back to camp with Ryn to discuss what we learned."

Sera nods. "Smart. Reconnaissance, not commitment."

Ryn is at the tree line. I tell him to set up camp in the trees, off the road, concealed. One hour. If we're not back in ninety minutes—

"I run. I know." He says it quietly. Without shame. "Ninety minutes. Then I'm gone."

He disappears into the tree line. Good. Safe. Out of it.

---

Edwyn prepares us. The silverleaf tea — pale green with a faint shimmer, brewed in an iron flask, poured into clay travel cups.

"Drink all of it. It dulls receptivity — you'll still feel the presence, but the *voice* won't get through as clearly. Lasts roughly two hours."

I drink. It tastes like wet leaves and copper. Something settles behind my eyes. A slight muffling, like someone draped a cloth over a part of my mind I didn't know was exposed.

The ember notices. Pulses once. Doesn't fight it.

The binding wraps come next. Consecrated cloth wound around our wrists. Cool and faintly damp. Blessed water.

"Ambient protection," Edwyn says. "Not a shield — a filter."

He pulls out the two Seal of the Wandering Flame discs. Iron. Etched with the cupped flame. Heavy for their size.

"If we find the door and the presence is still behind it — I place these on the frame and speak the rite. Thirty seconds." He looks between us. "Don't let anything kill me for thirty seconds."

Sera almost smiles.

"Sera takes point," I say. "Edwyn in the middle. I'll cover our rear. My speed won't work down there — our best bet is to see threats before they see us."

---

The cellar doors open to stone steps descending into darkness. Sera goes first. Sword in her right hand, left hand trailing the stone wall. Edwyn follows — and the light comes with him.

His flame symbol glows. Soft amber. The warm radius of a campfire, maybe fifteen feet. Enough to see walls and stairs and shapes. Not enough to see the far end of a large room.

"The Wandering Flame," he says. "Minor invocation. Lasts as long as my concentration holds. If I take a hit, it goes out."

So Edwyn is the light source. He takes a hit, we're all blind.

"Stay between us," Sera tells him. "If something comes from the front, I handle it. If something comes from behind—"

She looks at me.

"That's mine."

---

Eight steps. The air changes. Colder, damper, the smell of old earth thickening. The stone walls are rough-cut. Hand-dug. Twenty years of Harlan Brennick's stubbornness carved into clay and rock.

The cellar opens. A root cellar — fifteen feet by twenty. Low ceiling. Shelves along the left wall holding clay jars of preserved food, most still sealed. A workbench against the right wall with tools — hand saw, chisels, measuring cord. Harlan was expanding the cellar when he heard the sounds.

And at the far end.

The stone door.

It doesn't belong. Everything in this cellar is clay and timber and human hands. The door is *other*. Smooth grey stone, no mortar, no frame. The surface covered in carved symbols worn almost flat by time that dwarfs the farm, the village, everything built by human hands in this region. The symbols catch Edwyn's amber light and seem to drink it.

The door is six inches ajar.

The pull hits me through the silverleaf like a wave hitting a seawall. Muffled but *present*. Coming through that six-inch gap with the steady patience of water finding a crack. The ember responds — not with fear, not with eagerness. With the taut attention of something that has met its own kind and isn't sure yet if it's friend or predator.

Sera hasn't moved. Her sword is up. Her knuckles are white.

"That's not human construction," she says. "That's not even close."

Edwyn's light flickers — not from wind, from his hands trembling. He steadies them. His eyes are locked on the symbols.

"Old," he whispers. "Older than the Wandering Flame. Older than any scripture I've read. These symbols — I don't know the language. But the *structure*... it's a threshold ward. This door was meant to stay closed."

Scratch marks on the floor. Deep gouges in the packed earth leading from the stairs to the door. Ten fingernails' worth of desperate clawing — but going the wrong direction. *Toward* the door, not away from it.

Harlan didn't claw his way out. He clawed his way *in*.

We need to secure the door open — a known route out. I grab both chisels from the workbench. Sera sheathes her sword and we put our hands on the stone. The surface is cold — not cellar cold. *Deep earth* cold. The symbols under my palms feel like they're vibrating.

We push. The door resists — not mechanically. It resists the way deep water resists a hand pushing through it. I plant my feet and push with everything my legs and shoulders have and Sera grunts beside me and the stone grinds against the floor — a sound that vibrates through my molars.

One foot. Two feet. Three.

The pull *surges*. Something pushes against the inside of my mind like a hand pressing on a window. The ember flares in response — a pulse of heat that feels like a door being bolted from the inside.

I jam both chisels under the door's base. They bite and hold. Sera tests it. The door doesn't move.

"It'll hold unless something stronger than us pushes from the other side," she says. "So we work fast."

---

I look at the three-foot gap. The darkness beyond. Even Edwyn's amber glow stops at the threshold — not fades, *stops*. The dark on the other side isn't shadow. It's *substance*.

"That's not natural darkness," Edwyn says. "That's the presence itself. It fills the space."

We need more than a candle and a prayer. We need something the darkness is afraid of.

I hold my sword up high. The ember has refused utility work. Night vision — flat no. But this isn't a request for utility. The darkness isn't empty air — it's the *enemy*. It occupies this space the way an army occupies a battlefield. And the sword in my hand is raised against it.

"We need a weapon of light!" I shout at the ceiling, grinning like a man who's either brilliant or insane.

The ember hears me. And for the first time since I've known it — it doesn't hesitate.

It *detonates*.

Upward. Through my arm, through my wrist, through my grip and into the steel. The longsword ignites. Not silver-white. Not the familiar force infusion.

Gold.

Blazing, furious, *radiant* gold. The light erupts from the blade like sunrise compressed into three feet of steel. It doesn't flicker. It doesn't waver. It *burns* — and the darkness *recoils*. Ten feet. Twenty. Thirty.

Thirty feet of clear space. The passage walls revealed in sharp gold detail — the organic curves of the stone, the moisture on the floor, Harlan's boot prints glowing wet like a road map into the deep.

Edwyn drops his concentration on the Wandering Flame. He doesn't need it anymore.

"That's not arcane light," he whispers. "That's *radiant*. That's the kind of light the old texts describe as *anathema* to entropy. The light that exists specifically to push back against the unraveling of things."

"What *are* you, Kenji?"

Sera hasn't spoken. She's looking at the passage ahead and her expression says she'll process the theological implications later.

"Questions later. Move now. He just rang the doorbell."

She's right. Something below has changed. Something is *attentive*.

I tell them we've got about forty minutes. Double time. Eyes open for anything that looks important.

First find — an alcove in the wall. Waist-height. Inside, a clay oil lamp, ancient, cracked but intact. Symbols on its base matching the door. Beside it — a small iron ring set with a dull black stone that catches my golden light and *holds* it. The radiance enters the stone and doesn't come back out.

"Don't touch it. That ring is an *anchor*. See how the light enters the stone and doesn't reflect? It's designed to *absorb*. This is how the presence feeds — objects like this placed at intervals, drawing in ambient energy. Magical, spiritual, life force."

Edwyn stops dead.

"Wait. *Wait.*"

And these people worshipped it because endings are *part of the cycle*."

He steps back.

"It was dying even then. The worshippers fade across the panels — fewer and fewer — and the god's symbol gets smaller. It was being forgotten. And a forgotten god is a desperate god."

Sera's voice from ahead. Tight.

"Kenji. Edwyn. Come look at this."

The passage ends. Opens into a chamber that the golden light fills completely and still feels too dark.

Circular. Forty feet across. The ceiling vaulted high enough that the light barely reaches it. The walls covered in symbols — thousands of them, layered, overlapping, carved and recarved over centuries. The black veins in the rock are thicker here. Pulsing. Slowly. Like a heartbeat you can see.

In the centre — a stone platform. Low. Altar-shaped.

And on the altar — a body.

Harlan Brennick.

Lying on his back. Arms at his sides. Eyes closed. Work shirt, heavy trousers, boots still on. His face is peaceful. Not decayed. Not injured. He looks like a man sleeping.

Except for the black veins.

They run from the altar surface up through his clothing and into his skin — his hands, his neck, his face. Dark threads spreading under the flesh like roots of a plant that's using him as soil.

Edwyn makes a sound. Small. Professional. Horrified.

"He's alive," the priest says. "I can see his chest moving. He's *alive*."

Sera is scanning the room. Sword up. Turning in slow circles.

"Where's the thing that walked beside him," she says. "The drag marks. Where did it go."

She's right. Harlan's boot prints end at the altar. The drag marks end at the altar. But the chamber is empty.

Then the vibration stops.

Not fades. *Stops*. The constant low hum that's been in the floor since we entered goes silent.

The black veins on the walls pulse once. Hard. My sword flickers — not dims, *flickers* — as if something pushed against the light from outside.

**I KNOW YOU.**

The ember ignites. White-hot. Defensive. Like a dog with its hackles up.

**NOT YOU. THE THING INSIDE YOU. I KNOW WHAT IT IS.**

The pressure shifts. Focuses. On me specifically. Sera and Edwyn might as well not exist.

**YOU ARE NOT FROM HERE. AND NEITHER IS YOUR FIRE.**

The amber glow of his faith flares against the black threads and they *recoil* — pulling back into the stone like worms retreating into soil. Harlan's body shudders. A gasp. His eyes don't open but his breathing changes.

She nods. "Clock's running."

The word lands like a stone dropped into still water.

The black veins pulse. Slow. Heavy.

The murals seem to shift in the golden light. The figures with raised hands. The circle with radiating lines.

The pressure shifts. Closer.

**THE DARKNESS YOU SEE IS NOT MALICE. IT IS HUNGER. I AM STARVING, LITTLE FIRE. I HAVE BEEN STARVING FOR LONGER THAN YOUR WORLD REMEMBERS I EXIST. AND STARVING THINGS DO DESPERATE THINGS.**

The ember flares. The presence notices.

**YOUR FIRE. IT IS LIKE ME AND UNLIKE ME. IT BURNS WHERE I FADE. IT BUILDS WHERE I UNRAVEL. IT CAME FROM SOMEWHERE ELSE — THE SAME SOMEWHERE YOU CAME FROM.**

A beat. Precise. Intimate. Aimed directly at the question I asked myself this morning in Pip's room.

The presence hesitates. The first hesitation since it spoke.

The chamber is silent except for Harlan's breathing and the drip of moisture on ancient stone.

**YOU ASKED ME TO STOP. I WILL TELL YOU WHAT STOPPING LOOKS LIKE. IT LOOKS LIKE DYING. THE SPREAD IS NOT A CHOICE — IT IS THE INVOLUNTARY GASP OF A DROWNING THING. SEAL THIS DOOR AND I SUFFOCATE SLOWER. DESTROY THIS CHAMBER AND I END.**

A pause. Then quieter.

**UNLESS YOU CAN OFFER ME WHAT I ACTUALLY NEED. NOT STOLEN LIFE. NOT A MAN ON AN ALTAR. I NEED ESSENCE GIVEN FREELY. WILLINGLY. ENOUGH TO SUSTAIN WITHOUT CONSUMING. YOUR FIRE COULD DO THIS. CREATION CAN FEED ENTROPY WITHOUT BEING DESTROYED BY IT — THE WAY A RIVER FEEDS THE OCEAN WITHOUT THE RIVER DISAPPEARING.**

The pressure withdraws. Waiting.

I turn to face my party. The golden light catches the chamber walls and the slow pulse of the black veins that look less menacing now.

"I figured out what it is," I say. "It's a god. The god of entropy — endings, things coming apart. It used to be worshipped here, in this chamber, centuries before Duskfen existed. Its people forgot it. And a god without worshippers starves."

Edwyn's face goes white. Then something else — the look of a man whose worst theological suspicion just got confirmed.

"The divine bleed," he whispers. "It wasn't pushing through. It was *gasping*."

"The spread. The Hexcrawlers. The thinning. All of it — involuntary. The drowning reflex of something that's been dying for centuries." I look at Harlan's unconscious body over Edwyn's shoulder. "It kept him alive. Took what it could reach to survive."

"Three options. We destroy the altar — the god dies. Permanently. Or we seal the door — it suffocates slower, buys time. Or I can touch it. Make a sacrifice. Give it what it needs — essence offered freely. I think it would restore the god. And I think I'll be ok." I look between them. "I don't think it wants to hurt us."

Sera speaks first.

"You *think* you'll be ok. You *think* the god doesn't want to hurt us. A god that's been draining farmhands for six weeks and put a man in a coma on a stone altar." She points at Harlan. "That man has black veins under his skin, Kenji. Whatever *it* wants, what it *does* is consume people."

She steps closer. Her voice drops.

"What exactly happens when you touch it and you're *wrong*?"

Her eyes are hard. Not angry. *Scared*. Sera doesn't do scared.

"I need more than *I think I'll be ok*."

Edwyn speaks. Quieter.

"My condition was to understand the source. Not just destroy it. If this is a divine entity in its death cycle, destroying the altar isn't justice. It's execution." He meets my eyes. "But Sera isn't wrong. If you do this — if you touch it and offer freely — what guarantee do we have that it takes only what's offered?"

I close my eyes. Reach for the ember.

*Talk to me*, I think. *Hot if we can save it and I survive. Cold if I should destroy the altar. Warm if we shut it in and walk away.*

The ember listens. Weighs.

A flash of searing heat — there and gone in half a heartbeat. A pulse of cold so deep it locks my jaw. Then warmth — steady, sustained, filling my chest.

And with the warmth — a vision. Two forces. One gold, one black. Not fighting. *Orbiting*. Circling each other the way day circles night. The gold pours into the black and the black pours into the gold and where they meet there's a hum. The sound of a cycle working correctly.

The warmth stays. The others fade.

*Purpose*.

The ember wasn't sent here to destroy entropy. It wasn't sent here to observe. It was sent here to *restore a cycle*. Creation feeding entropy. Entropy making space for creation. The river and the ocean.

And underneath — a certainty that isn't mine. It belongs to the ember.

*You will survive this. This is what you are for.*

My eyes open. The golden light from my sword is brighter. The ember is *eager*. For the first time since I've known it, the thing inside me isn't just willing or ready.

It's *eager*.

I look over at Edwyn. Smile.

"Make sure Harlan gets home to his wife."

Then I turn to Sera. She's already moving — she can read the intention on my face.

I stare her in the eyes.

"So you *do* worry about me."

Her fingers close on air.

"Guess I'm more than just cute."

I stick my tongue out at her.

And dive.

"KENJI—"

My hand hits the altar.

The world *ends*.

Not dies. Not breaks. *Ends*. The way a sentence ends. The way a breath ends. I feel the god's nature pour into me through my palm and it is every final moment that ever existed — the last heartbeat, the last ember, the last star going dark in an empty sky.

My body tries to die. Every cell receives the message *it is time to stop* and for one horrifying instant I feel myself beginning to unravel — threads of who I am pulling loose at the edges like fabric caught in a machine—

The ember *detonates*.

Not outward. Not upward. *Against*. It meets the entropy at the point of contact and *refuses*. Gold light erupts from my hand — not the gentle radiance of the sword, something deeper, something primal — the light of the first thing that ever existed pushing back against the last thing that will ever exist.

The collision is *visible*. Gold and black meeting at my palm in a border that screams with energy. The chamber shakes. Dust falls from the vaulted ceiling. The symbols on the walls blaze — every single one, all at once, lit from within by something that remembers what they meant.

**IT HURTS.**

The presence. Not attacking. Not taking. *Receiving*.

**DON'T STOP.**

I wasn't planning to.

The ember opens. Not the surface — the *depth*. The vast, patient, limitless thing I felt in the clearing last night. It rises through me like a tide and pours through my palm into the altar and the altar drinks and drinks and drinks—

My vision whites out. My knees hit stone. I can't feel my hand anymore — it's just a point of light connected to a point of dark. Something essential is leaving me — not stolen, *given* — and the giving costs more than I expected.

Somewhere behind me Sera is screaming my name. Edwyn is praying — the real kind, the kind that sounds like a man bargaining with the universe.

The drain continues. I feel myself dimming. The edges of who I am growing soft. Thin.

And then — between one heartbeat and the next — the border *stabilizes*.

The screaming energy goes quiet. The gold and the black stop fighting and start *flowing*. I feel it happen — the moment the river finds the ocean and realizes they were always the same water. The creation pours into the entropy and the entropy *makes space* for the creation and where they meet there is a hum. The sound of a cycle working correctly for the first time in centuries.

The black veins in the walls stop pulsing. They *settle*. Like a heartbeat finding its rhythm. The thick darkness thins — not gone, but no longer hungry. It becomes what darkness is supposed to be. Just the absence of light. Nothing more.

**OH.**

Different now. Not pressure. Not weight. Wonder.

**I REMEMBER THIS. I REMEMBER WHAT THIS FELT LIKE.**

The symbols glow steadily. Gold and black intertwined. The murals are fully visible for the first time in centuries — the worshippers with raised hands, the circle with radiating lines. And beneath them, painted so faintly it was invisible before — a second symbol. A circle with lines radiating *inward*. Creation. The companion piece.

They were always meant to be together.

The drain stops.

My hand comes free. I fall backward. Hit stone. The ceiling swims above me — vaulted stone and ancient symbols and golden light from a sword lying on the ground still blazing like a small sun.

Everything hurts. My hand is numb. My arms are shaking. My vision pulses dark at the edges. The ember is still there — not gone, not diminished, but *exhausted*. Like a muscle after lifting something at the very limit of what it can bear.

Sera is on her knees beside me. Her hands on my shoulders. Her face is — I've never seen that expression. Fury and relief and something else braided so tight she can't separate them.

"You *idiot*," she says. Her voice cracks on the word. "You absolute — you *stupid* —"

She can't finish. Her hands are shaking.

Quieter now. Smaller. Not weaker — *contained*. A fire given a hearth after burning wild for centuries.

**THE CREATURES WERE BORN FROM MY DESPERATION. THEY WILL NOT STOP IMMEDIATELY — THEY ARE ALREADY ALIVE, ALREADY DIRECTED. BUT THE INTELLIGENCE DRIVING THEM WILL FADE. WITHOUT MY DESPERATE NEED PUSHING THROUGH THE BOUNDARY, THEY BECOME ANIMALS. DANGEROUS BUT NO LONGER COORDINATED. NO LONGER EXPANDING.**

Another pause. Longer.

**YOU ASKED WHO YOU ARE. I STILL CANNOT TELL YOU. BUT I CAN TELL YOU THIS: WHATEVER SENT YOU CHOSE WELL. YOU TOUCHED A DYING GOD AND GAVE FREELY AND DID NOT BREAK.**

**MOST WOULD HAVE BROKEN.**

The presence withdraws. Resting. Breathing — really breathing, for the first time in centuries, in a rhythm that doesn't sound like drowning.

The stone door. The chisels holding. We squeeze through one at a time.

The cellar. Harlan's shelves. His tools. The stairs.

Daylight.

The grey afternoon sky hits my eyes like a fist. I'm on my knees in the dead grass beside the cellar doors, sword blazing gold, blinking against a world that seems too bright.

Sera says my name. I look where she's pointing.

The dead grass nearest the cellar doors. The brittle grey blades.

There's green at the roots. Faint. Barely visible. But *there*. The first green thing on the Brennick farm. Pushing up through six weeks of grey death like it just remembered how.

The cycle. Already working.

---

Ryn nearly falls out of a tree when we emerge. He sees Edwyn carrying a body. Sees Sera half-dragging me. Sees the blazing sword. Sees the blood on my hand.

"Is that — is he—"

"Alive," Edwyn says. Sets Harlan down on the bedroll Ryn laid out. "Unconscious. Stable. The veins are fading."

"Food. Now. *Please.*"

"I'll take first watch," she says to no one. "All night if I have to."

Then, quieter, without looking at me:

"Don't ever do that again."

A pause.

"Idiot."

Ryn has the camp together in twenty minutes. Bedrolls. A small fire — concealed behind the fallen tree, smoke dispersed through the canopy. Harlan is stable, breathing steady, the black veins almost invisible now. Edwyn checks him every fifteen minutes.

I lie on my back and look at the sky through the trees. The ember is there — quiet, exhausted, but *warm*. Not the desperate warmth of a fire fighting to stay lit. The settled warmth of something that did what it was meant to do.

*Good*, I think. *Stay lit.*

The ember answers. Faint. Barely a whisper.

But warm.

Tomorrow I'm carrying a man home to his wife. The Hexcrawlers are still out there — uncoordinated now, but alive and dangerous. The Delve is still a day and a half northeast. The cycle needs tending. And somewhere in this world, something sent me here on purpose — dropped a weapon of creation into the path of a dying god — and I still don't know who I am or why.

But I know what I'm for.

I close my eyes. Pip's face behind my eyelids for a moment. Then Sera's — the expression I've never seen before, the one she couldn't separate into its parts. Then the vision — gold and black orbiting each other, day and night, the cycle turning.

The ember burns steady in the dark.

The last thing I hear before sleep takes me is the sound of Sera sharpening her sword at the edge of the firelight, and the slow, peaceful breathing of a god that isn't drowning anymore.

The golden light pushes into the shaft and the darkness retreats — ordinary darkness, not the supernatural resistance from the cellar. Just a mine nobody's lit in years.

The main shaft descends steep. Timber supports every eight feet — most intact, some bowed, two snapped with the ceiling sagging. The Hexcrawler's mental map overlays what I'm seeing. The angle matches. The spacing matches. We're on the right path.

Sera marks every junction. Quick precise slashes. Her breathing controlled. Her footsteps silent on the damp stone.

"Thirty feet down. Air's changing. Wetter. The timber's worse here."

She's right. The supports are rotting from the bottom up. The iridescent fluid has been seeping upward — faint traces on the walls, a thin sheen on the floor getting thicker with every step. Warm through the leather of my boots in a way leather shouldn't conduct.

"Stop." Sera's hand finds my arm. Iron grip. "Left wall. Six feet ahead."

I hold the sword forward. The golden light finds it.

A shape. Half-embedded in a pool of fluid against the left wall. Three chitinous legs growing from the fluid like stems from soil. No body. No head. Just legs, twitching in slow rhythmic pulses. The chitin translucent — not yet hardened. Fluid moving inside like blood in veins.

The spawning process. Right here in the corridor.

"It's growing," Sera says. Very quiet. "How long until those have a body."

"Mark it. Keep moving."

---

The shaft opens.

The golden light floods forward into space and the change hits before I see it — the ceiling vanishing upward, the walls pulling apart, the air going from corridor-tight to cavern-vast. The sound changes. Echo. Drip. And underneath — a wet, constant churning. Like a slow river made of something thicker than water.

The natural cavern. The Hexcrawler's memory didn't do it justice.

Sixty feet across. Ceiling thirty feet up. Stalactites. The walls raw limestone, broken where the excavation punched through. And the floor —

A lake.

Iridescent fluid covers everything. Not ankle-deep — knee-deep now in the centre. Shimmering in the golden light — rainbow slicks of entropy given liquid form, moving in slow lazy currents despite no wind.

And in the fluid — shapes. Dozens of them. At various stages of *becoming*.

Near the edges — raw material. Chitin plates floating like lily pads, accreting, finding each other, fusing. Further in — partial forms. A torso with two legs and no head, twitching. A skull-shape with four of six eye sockets formed. And near the centre — a nearly complete Hexcrawler. Five legs attached, the sixth growing from a nub, floating motionless with its eyes closed.

None of them moving independently. All growing. Silently. The factory running on autopilot.

"Gods," Sera whispers. Counting. "Fourteen. Various stages. The one near the centre — five legs — that's days from finishing. Maybe less."

She looks at me. In the golden light her face is hard and certain.

"We can't wait for Edwyn. These pools need to be destroyed today. If that five-legged one finishes and starts moving, it'll finish the others."

Her eyes move to the far wall. "Kenji. Two o'clock."

An opening in the far wall. Not a mine shaft — a natural crack in the limestone, widened by time. Roughly four feet wide. The fluid doesn't flow into it — pulls away from it. A thin margin of dry stone around the crack like the fluid is avoiding it.

Even from sixty feet I can feel it. Not the entropy god's pull — that was hunger, desperation. This is *still*. Patient in a way that makes the entropy god's patience feel like fidgeting. Something very old and very quiet.

The ember goes respectful. The way a campfire might feel near a volcano.

Sera's hand is white on her sword.

"That's the deeper thing. The one your Hexcrawler wouldn't go near."

I want to know. I pull a plain flint — no ember, just a rock — and hurl it past the spawning lake, into the crack.

*Clack.* First bounce. Close. *Clack.* Second. Further. Descending. *Clack.* Third. Fainter.

Then silence.

Five seconds. Ten. Fifteen. Nothing answers.

But the echo changed. The acoustic signature shifted — the passage suddenly got much, much larger. The last clack didn't fade into distance. It *opened up*. Into a space big enough to swallow the sound whole.

And one more thing. In the half-second after the sound opened up — a change in the air from the crack. The temperature dropped. A single pulse of cold that touched my face and retreated. Like something very far away exhaled once and went back to sleep.

"Something is there," I tell Sera. "But it's more like a giant... really giant space. Something to file away. Let's stay on task."

She nods. Hard. "Agreed."

---

We clear the cavern.

The near-complete Hexcrawler first. Sera circles left along the dry margin. I come straight in. The fluid climbs to my shins. Warm. Wrong-warm.

We converge on the five-legged form. It floats on its side, soft chitin, translucent at the joints. Not yet alive. Not yet not alive.

I bring the Radiant Edge down. The golden blade punches through the soft chitin and radiant light detonates inside — gold flaring through the translucent shell, lighting up every unfinished organ. The body convulses once and collapses inward. Sera's sword takes the head simultaneously. Clean. Done.

The rest is work. Not combat. Fifteen minutes of wading through knee-deep fluid, swords rising and falling. Each partial form gets the same treatment. The torso folds. The skull-shape cracks under a boot heel. The chitin plates scatter when the sword breaks their clusters.

I take the deeper sections. The Radiant Edge makes it efficient — the radiant damage *unravels* the proto-Hexcrawlers faster than steel alone. Where the golden blade strikes a forming creature, the surrounding fluid loses its sheen. Goes grey. Goes clear. Goes still.

"Kenji." Sera is watching it. Standing in a patch of fluid gone transparent. "The fluid is dying. Every one we destroy — the fluid around it stops producing."

She's right. The spawning medium is one substance — the entropy god's bleed, pooled and concentrated. Each forming creature was a node drawing from the fluid. Destroy the node, the fluid goes inert.

By the time I cut apart the last cluster — six free-floating legs near the far wall — the lake has changed. Knee-deep iridescent entropy fluid is now knee-deep grey-clear water. Still warm. Still wrong-smelling. But dead. The factory is shut down.

I scan the cavern one final time. Golden light reaching every corner. Nothing twitching. Nothing growing. Fourteen proto-Hexcrawlers destroyed.

We give the crack in the far wall a wide berth. Neither of us needs to discuss it.

---

Daylight. The bowl. Fresh air that tastes like paradise.

Sera leans against a boulder. Wipes her sword. Her boots are stained iridescent — the colour might be permanent.

"Fourteen destroyed. Spawning fluid neutralised." She says it like she's filing a report. "The Delve is clear of active threats. The deeper passage remains unexplored and should be classified as a separate hazard."

She sheathes her sword. Looks at me.

"You mentioned my superiors."

Not a question. A door opening.

"I was hired by the Varenholm Mage Council. Contracted through the Academy to assess the situation in the Duskfen region." She folds her arms. "I report to a woman named Instructor Seraphine Ash. She's the one who flagged the Duskfen reports as worth investigating when everyone else dismissed them."

She looks at me.

"Varenholm is roughly three days west of Duskfen. The Academy is the largest magical institution in the region. If anyone can explain what's in that deeper passage, or what your ember actually is, or how a god of entropy ended up starving under a farmhouse — they're in Varenholm."

A pause.

"I should have reported back days ago. I was contracted to assess, not engage. I stayed because—" She stops. Reorganises. "—the situation escalated beyond assessment parameters and leaving would have been tactically irresponsible."

She says it like she rehearsed it.

"If you're serious about going to Varenholm — I can get you in front of the Council. And Seraphine will want to hear everything."

"But first — we go back. Duskfen. Harlan to his wife. Supplies. Rest." A beat. "And I want a real bed and a meal that isn't boar."

---

We head southwest. The trade road unspools. The corruption softens mile by mile. Edwyn was right — the ironwood is out here. Ninety minutes in, he spots it. Through the undergrowth, rising from a mossy depression — a tree with bark like polished bone. Silver-white. Smooth. Luminous.

I'm getting ahead of myself. I haven't told you about Edwyn and Ryn yet.

---

Mid-afternoon. Two hours from Duskfen. Sera holds up a fist.

"Voices. Ahead."

Two voices. One low and measured. One higher, faster. The cadence of a man who talks when he's nervous.

We round the bend. Fifty feet ahead — Edwyn in front, satchel on his shoulder, a crossbow on his back. Ryn behind him, pack heavy, something long and wrapped in oilcloth strapped across the top.

No Harlan.

Edwyn's face breaks into open relief. "Kenji! Sera!"

They close the distance. Edwyn reads our faces, our posture, the iridescent stains on our boots.

"The Delve?"

"Cleared," Sera says. "Spawning pools destroyed. Fourteen proto-Hexcrawlers neutralised. The fluid is inert."

Edwyn closes his eyes. Exhales. The kind that carries days.

"And the deeper passage?"

"Unexplored. Classified as a separate hazard."

He nods. Opens his eyes.

"Harlan is awake."

---

The words sit in the afternoon air.

"He woke on the road. Yesterday evening, about an hour from Duskfen. Confused. Weak. The last thing he remembers is a voice in the cellar telling him his name."

He adjusts his satchel.

"I got him to Bessa. She—" He stops. Swallows. "I've administered healing rites to dying men and held the hands of women in childbirth. I was not prepared for what happened when Bessa opened that door."

He doesn't elaborate.

"The veins are gone. Completely. He's lucid. Eating. Talking. He asked who brought him home. He wants to meet you."

Ryn steps forward. Produces the oilcloth package with careful urgency.

"From Aldric."

He unwraps it.

---

The armor is beautiful.

Not in the way jewelry is beautiful — in the way a well-made weapon is beautiful. Functional beauty. The bear hide transformed — boiled, oil-cured, hardened to a deep rich brown nearly black at the edges. The chest piece layered three deep, the ironwood-treated leather stiff enough to turn a blade but shaped to move with the body. Shoulder guards — reinforced curves of hardened bear leather. Forearm bracers that buckle tight. Iron rivets, small, flush.

And the ironwood. A faint amber sheen on the hardened surfaces. Where Aldric cured the leather with the sap, the material has a quality that's hard to describe. Not quite metal. Not quite leather. I tap the chest piece and the sound is *wrong* for leather. Deeper. Harder.

"He worked through the night," Ryn says. "Kept saying *the boy earned this*."

A folded piece of paper. Aldric's handwriting.

*Kenji. Ironwood-cured bear leather. AC 14. Won't slow you down. Won't let you down. Don't die in it — my son never got to wear the last set I made for someone who deserved it. — Aldric*

I strip off the old leather. Pull on the new set. Buckle the bracers. Settle the chest piece. Roll my shoulders.

Different. The weight distributed better. The movement unrestricted. The shoulders don't catch when I swing. The bracers don't bind when I flex. And when I tap the chest piece that *wrong* sound rings out — the sound of something that will stop a claw that would have opened me up in the old set.

Sera looks me up and down.

"Better," she says.

Ryn produces one more thing. A small wrapped bundle. "From Pip."

Three honey cakes. Still faintly warm. Wrapped in a cloth that smells like lavender. A note.

*Come back. — P*

---

I give my old armor and all the sellable loot to Ryn — the Hexcrawler claws, the iridescent fluid sac, the boar tusks. Everything.

"No curiosities found yet, Ryn. But there's gold to be made. Take this, head back to town, sell it. Keep fifty percent of the profit. Give my half to Pip." I meet his eyes. "Tell her it's from the bandit."

Something crosses his face. Not quite a smile.

"Varenholm," he says, testing the word. "I've never been. Heard there are locks there that would take me a *week*." Something sparks. "I might find my way there. Eventually. If someone needed a door opened."

"Thanks for letting me run," he says. He says my name differently than before. Like it means something now.

He walks south. The old leather armor strapped to his pack. The first real thing anyone gave me in this world, passing to the next person who needs it.

---

We don't go back to Duskfen. No reason. Ryn's handling the village. We cut south to the main crossroads and pick up the western highway heading west toward Varenholm.

I tell Sera to scout ahead for opportunities — game trails, trouble, anything unusual worth stopping for. I tell Edwyn to watch for herbs. I take some of Edwyn's load — the bedrolls and tarp — so he can walk at full speed instead of hunched under the camp pack.

Edwyn's gratitude is immediate and dignified. He rolls his shoulders. Stands straighter. Starts scanning the roadside undergrowth with professional interest.

Sera ranges ahead a quarter mile. Owl call if she finds something. Two owl calls if it's dangerous.

Edwyn catalogs plants as we walk. He stops twice in the first hour — once for a cluster of yarrow along a stream bank, once for something he calls feverfew that he tucks into his satchel with quiet satisfaction. Further on, brightcap mushrooms growing in a rotted stump — pale caps that glow faintly when he cups his hand over them.

"Dried and brewed into tea, these are a mild stimulant," he says. "Sharper than coffee, gentler than alchemy. Useful on long watches."

He's talking faster now. The particular energy of a man on the one subject that makes him feel indispensable.

"This close to Varenholm, near the ley line network that feeds the Academy, we'll start seeing rarer things. Moonpetal — a luminescent flower that grows at ley line intersections. The petals can be brewed into a draught that temporarily sharpens magical focus. If you're going to be casting spells in front of the Mage Council, one dose before the audience would be worth its weight."

He ducks under a branch.

"Ironroot — not to be confused with ironwood — a ground-cover vine whose berries provide sustained energy without the crash of stimulants. You eat every four hours because your metabolism demands it. Ironroot berries could stretch that to six, maybe seven. They're not common but they grow in magical runoff near the Academy."

He glances at the undergrowth with professional hunger.

"And bittershade. Dark-leafed shrub that grows in magical runoff. Toxic raw — will make you violently ill. But properly prepared, it's the base for the most effective antitoxin in the eastern provinces. My two doses came from a bittershade preparation. If we find a stand of it, I can make a dozen more."

He pulls out his ledger and scribbles notes without breaking stride.

"If we gather enough of the right materials, I can prepare field medicines on the road. And anything surplus sells well at the Academy — Varenholm scholars pay premium prices for raw alchemical components."

The western highway is a real road. Wider. Maintained. Cart ruts deep and layered. Settled country — less monsters, more people.

---

The ironwood tree was yesterday — Edwyn spotted it on the road before we reached the Brennick farm. The sap. The vial. Ryn carrying it south. By now Aldric has it. By now the armor on my back exists because a priest noticed a tree with silver bark and a kid with clever hands ran a vial through Hexcrawler territory. The chain of small decisions that led to me wearing ironwood-cured bear leather instead of a dead man's hand-me-downs is longer than it has any right to be.

---

Second hour on the western highway. Sera's owl call.

One call. *Come. Quiet.*

I find her at a bend in the road. Crouched at the tree line. She points through the trees without speaking.

Fifty feet off the road. A clearing — not natural, *cleared*. Stumps cut low. A cold firepit. Three lean-tos of branches and stolen canvas. Rope with dried meat hanging. A pile of goods under oilcloth — crates, sacks, a rolled carpet.

Boot prints. Five, maybe six people. Heavy prints. Armored boots.

"Bandit camp," Sera breathes. "Active. Five or six. They've been hitting crossroads traffic." She points at the lean-tos. "Nobody's home. Fire's cold — six hours. They're out working."

She looks toward the crossroads.

"Working the crossroads. Right now."

She gives me the assessment. Three options. Raid the empty camp. Push to the crossroads and see if they're actively robbing someone. Or do both.

"Six bandits against three of us — one of whom is a priest with a crossbow he's fired maybe thirty times. Those aren't bad odds for you and me. But bandits are humans. They have ranged weapons." She looks at me. "This isn't a Hexcrawler. These things shoot back."

I tell Sera I plan on soloing them. But I'd like her help with a plan to deal with the ranged combatants.

"Solo." She says it flat. "Six bandits. With bows. Solo."

She needs to understand why. I tell her — I need to know what I'm capable of against enemies that think and coordinate and shoot back. If I can't handle people, Varenholm is going to eat me alive.

She chews on it. Doesn't like it. Accepts it.

"Fine. You solo. I plan. But I'm positioning myself with a line of sight. If you go down, I'm not sitting in a bush watching you die."

---

Fifteen minutes of scouting later she draws in the dirt.

The crossroads. Two archers in a blind on the ridge — longbows, overwatch. Two flankers in the tree line — hand crossbows, short swords. Two at a log barricade — the talkers. One with a mace and chainmail, one with a longsword and studded leather. Classic hammer and anvil. The archers pin, the flankers close, the barricade collects.

She builds my approach. East through the forest, not the road. Hit the archer blind from behind — it's open at the back. Drop both before anyone below knows. The ten-second confusion window. Then the flankers. Then the barricade.

"The whole plan lives or dies on the first five seconds."

I decide something in my head that I don't tell Sera. First time killing people. Except I'm not going to kill anyone unless I have to. The flat of the axe, not the edge. Knock them out. Disarm them.

Not the wisest tactical decision I've ever made. But I've never been the wisest fighter. Kind of a softy, now that I think about it.

---

The archer blind. I come from the east. Pine needles underfoot. Silent. Two shapes inside — one chewing bread and watching the road, the other asleep with a cloak over his head.

The hand axe. Flat side. Not the edge.

The awake archer first. The flat connects with the base of his skull. A dull heavy *thock*. His head snaps forward. He slumps — but doesn't go down. His hands catch the rock. His mouth opens to shout—

I bring it down again before the sound forms. Across the temple. His eyes roll white. He folds sideways. Out. Breathing. Unconscious.

The sleeping archer stirs. His hand reaches for the longbow. I don't have time for elegant.

The flat catches him across the jaw through the cloak. His head snaps sideways. Wide frightened eyes — young, barely twenty, scraggly beard — finding mine for one half-second of absolute confusion.

I clamp my hand over his mouth. Pin his shoulder to the ground. He thrashes. Weak. Disoriented. I shake my head once.

*Quiet.*

He goes quiet. Not brave. Terrified. The man kneeling on his chest with a hand axe and hazel eyes is the most terrifying thing he's ever woken up to.

One more tap. Clean. He goes limp. Breathing. Done.

I strip both archers. Longbows, arrows, anything usable — scattered into the blind where they can't reach. Two down. No alarm.

---

I hear the barricade pair start climbing the ridge to check on their archers. Perfect. I slip out the back of the blind and circle east, then south, looping toward the flanker sitting against a tree. The one called Wes. Picking his nails. Hand crossbow in his lap. Back to me.

The voice from the ridge — "They're not sleeping. They're *out*. Cold. Someone *hit* them."

Wes stands. Watches the ridge. His back is fully turned. Thirty feet. Twenty. Fifteen.

The flat of the axe. Behind the right ear. His knees buckle. The hand crossbow drops into pine needles. One more tap on the way down. He folds into the undergrowth without a sound.

Three down. One flanker, two archers. All breathing. All disarmed.

---

"*Wes! Report!*" The mace fighter's voice from the ridge.

No answer.

"*Tam! West position! Report!*"

The west flanker — Tam — calls back. "I'm here! Wes isn't answering!"

"Fall back to the barricade. Now. We're being picked off."

Tam breaks cover. Moving across open ground toward the barricade. Hand crossbow up. Sixty feet from me.

The ember detonates under my feet. Arcane Stride. The forest blurs.

Sixty feet in three strides. Tam hears the silver-bell sound. His head snaps left. Eyes wide.

He raises the crossbow. Pulls the trigger.

The bolt whips past my ear. One shot. Panic spent it.

I don't swing. I go low. The last stride becomes a slide — boots kicking out, sweeping into his legs at full momentum.

His feet leave the ground before his brain understands why. The hand crossbow flies from his grip. He hits dirt face-first with the graceless impact of a man who was not expecting the ground.

The axe flat comes down. Twice. He goes limp.

Four down.

---

I stand up. Slowly. Deliberately. The silver light fading from my last footprint. The hand axe resting casually on my shoulder.

I look up at the ridge. Thirty feet above. The mace fighter in chainmail and the longsword fighter in studded leather staring down at me.

They just watched a man cross sixty feet in two seconds, trip their flanker at full speed, and knock him unconscious with two hits before the body finished bouncing.

I grin. Ear to ear.

The longsword fighter takes a step backward. Involuntary.

The mace fighter doesn't step back. But his eyes are doing math. Four men down. No blood. No screams. Just silence and then *this*.

"Who the *hell* are you," he says. The mace head trembling in his grip.

"I am Kenji. I'm just traveling through the area and saw your setup here. How much are you charging people to get through?"

---

The conversation that follows is the strangest negotiation I've had in two worlds.

Garrett — that's the mace fighter's name — tells me about the toll. Two silver per person. Five per cart. Ten per merchant wagon. They don't hurt anyone who pays. His voice has the flat honesty of a man who knows the only card he has left is the truth.

Sev — the longsword fighter — doesn't want to talk. He wants to run. I can see it in every muscle.

I let Sev leave. Start a new life, I tell him. Or report back to your employer and explain what happened. His choice.

He doesn't need to be told twice. He walks. Then walks faster. Then he's gone, heading west at a pace that will become a run.

I call the rest of my party with an owl noise. Sera emerges from the trees. Edwyn behind her. They take positions flanking me. Garrett watches them arrive with the resigned assessment of a man at a job interview he didn't apply for.

"A fighter, a priest, and whatever you are."

I make him a deal. All the gold in the lockbox — sixty-three gold, six weeks of collections, all his. One month as my contractor. I'll deal with his employer, a man called Voss, on the way.

"You are not a slave," I tell him. "I don't do slaves. And I hate slave owners." I let that sit. "Think of yourself as a contractor. You seem professional — I expect you won't take the gold and run. I need your skills and at the end of the contract you can do as you like."

I hold his gaze.

"I'm not a criminal. At least not yet. As long as you maintain a neutral or lawful alignment, we're good. No going off doing anything too crazy to get me in trouble. I don't care if you're evil or good." A beat. "But if you cross me, be prepared to fight."

Garrett listens. His expression shifts through my words — *not a slave* gets a micro-nod, *hate slave owners* gets a longer look, *contractor* settles something behind his eyes. When I mention alignment his scarred jaw tightens. Not offended. Assessed.

"I've done things I'm not proud of on this road," he says. Flat. Honest. "But I've never killed a traveler who couldn't fight back. And I've never taken a job that involved women or children. Voss offered both." A pause. "I turned both down. That's how I ended up running the *eastern* crew instead of the central one." He looks at the road west and something dark crosses his face. "The central crew doesn't have my rules."

Something raw and wanting surfaces behind his eyes when I mention dealing with Voss.

"One month. Contractor. I keep the box. We deal with Voss." He extends a thick hand. "You've got yourself a mace, Kenji."

Sera steps forward. The interrogation is professional and thorough. Garrett gives her everything — Aldren Voss, the Broken Antler tavern, three toll crews, Captain Dunmore's corruption, and Brindle at the river crossing. Especially Brindle.

"She enjoys the work," Garrett says. "The part that involves people being afraid. The toll is the same but if someone can't pay—" He doesn't finish. "Voss keeps her on because she's effective. I keep my crew away from hers because my boys don't need to learn what she teaches."

Edwyn speaks for the first time. Quietly. "The river crossing. Travelers who can't pay. What happens to them, Garrett?"

Garrett looks at the priest. Something in his expression flinches.

"They don't come out of the river crossing, Father."

---

We leave the crossroads behind. Garrett strips the camp of everything useful. The four unconscious bandits get tied up with water in reach. They'll work free in hours. Without weapons, without the operation, they're just four men on a road with nothing.

Garrett hoists the supply pack onto his back like it's a Tuesday. Chainmail, mace, lockbox, and a full camp's worth of gear. The man is built for carrying.

The western highway stretches west. A real road. Cart ruts. Drainage ditches. Civilization — or it was, before Voss started taxing it.

Garrett walks beside me and delivers intel like a debrief. Brindle's bridge. The Ashford river crossing. He lays out the positions with the precision of a man who's studied a colleague's operation and found it distasteful.

"The bridge is twenty feet across and sixty feet long. Stone pillars, wooden deck. Brindle keeps two guards on the near side — the greeting committee. Two more on the far side — the door closers. And Brindle herself sits in the middle with her enforcer. Heavy crossbow. Bastard sword. She watches from the center of the bridge like a spider in a web."

He ticks off the rest. "Archers on both banks. When you step onto the bridge, there's nowhere to go. The archers pin you. The guards at both ends close. Brindle in the middle decides whether you pay or you don't come out the other side."

He glances at me.

"Your speed trick. The one that turned Tam into a mudstain. How does it work over a bridge?"

Good question. Sixty feet of open bridge with no obstacles, no terrain, no trees to use. Just wood planking and a woman with a heavy crossbow at the halfway point. The Stride would cross it in seconds — but there's nowhere to dodge on a twenty-foot-wide bridge.

"Two of her crew are ex-military," Garrett continues. "Actual soldiers who deserted. They know formations, they know discipline, they know how to hold a line. The other three are muscle — big, dumb, loyal to Brindle because she feeds them and scares them more than anything else does."

He looks at the road ahead. Then at me.

"People who can't pay don't come out of the river crossing. That's not rumor. That's a number I stopped counting."

Edwyn makes stew at camp that night from rations and wild herbs. Garrett eats three bowls. I eat four. The Fast Metabolism doesn't care about dignity.

Sera takes second watch. I take third. Garrett takes first — he's fresh and knows the road. Edwyn sleeps through.

I close my eyes on a bedroll stolen from the men I knocked unconscious, wearing armor forged from a bear I killed with rocks, with honey cakes from a girl who doesn't know what I've become in her chest pocket, and the ember burns deep and steady and ready for whatever tomorrow puts on the road.

The river crossing. Brindle. The bridge. People who can't pay don't come out the other side.

I'm coming. And I'm bringing the flat of an axe and a very persuasive smile.

Room twelve. Not a grand office. A study — cramped, lived-in, buried under paper. Books stacked on the floor. A desk somewhere underneath scrolls and inkwells. The walls are shelves and the shelves are full.

At the desk — Instructor Seraphine Ash.

Late thirties. Sharp-featured. Dark hair in a losing battle with escaped strands. Dark quick eyes that miss nothing. Ink-stained fingers. Both hands. Permanently. The stains of someone who writes more than she speaks.

She finishes a line before looking up. Those dark eyes sweep the four of us in a single pass.

"Sera. You're late. By five days."

"The situation escalated beyond assessment parameters."

"Clearly."

She catalogues Edwyn in two seconds. Garrett in one. Then me.

The sweep stalls. Her gaze moves from the armor to the sword to the dreadlocks to my eyes. Something behind her expression shifts. Not recognition. *Resonance*.

"And you're the one Sera wrote about. The Blade Channeler."

---

Sera reports. Standing. Military posture. Duskfen. The Hexcrawlers. My arrival. The party. The Brennick cellar. The entropy god.

"Stop," Seraphine says. "A divine entity. Beneath a farmhouse. You're certain."

"I was present, Instructor."

"Continue."

The altar. The channeling. The cycle restored. The green grass. The Delve. The spawning pools. The deeper passage.

Seraphine hasn't blinked in thirty seconds.

"Are you telling me that this man channeled raw creation energy into a dying god of entropy and partially restored a broken cosmic cycle?"

"Yes, Instructor."

Silence.

"Edwyn. Your documentation. Everything. On my desk. Now."

Edwyn opens his satchel. The spawning fluid sample. The ledger. Pages of careful observations. Seraphine speed-reads with the hunger of a scholar finding primary source material in the dark.

"This is exceptional fieldwork, Father."

She turns to me.

"Kenji. Sera's report describes abilities that don't fit any classification in our records. Weapon infusion. Force projection. Radiant manifestation. Sensory enhancement. And the ability to channel raw creation energy into a divine entity through physical contact."

She leans forward.

"What you did in that cellar shouldn't be possible. Not for a trained archmage. Certainly not for an unschooled stranger who arrived in this world six weeks after the entity began bleeding through the boundary."

Her eyes are sharp. Hungry.

"I need to understand what you are."

"Heh. I see Sera did a good job spilling the beans on all my abilities." I grin. Wink at Sera. "When you put it that way, it's almost as if I dropped out of heaven."

Sera's color rises. She doesn't look at Seraphine to check if she noticed.

Seraphine noticed.

"I'll accept that as *I don't know yet* rather than *I won't tell you*." Not a request. A schedule. "We'll return to the subject."

---

The highway evidence. I set Voss's ledgers on the desk. Lay out the full operation — three crews, the shakedown, the disappearances at the river crossing.

"These are his financial records. And bribe receipts for Captain Dunmore. Regular payments. Dates and amounts."

Seraphine opens the ledger. Speed-reads. Administrative fury replacing the scholar's hunger.

"Dunmore. The Council has received complaints about reduced patrol coverage for two years. His reports blamed budget constraints." She taps a page. "Twenty gold per month. He wasn't short on budget."

Garrett steps forward. Gives his statement without embellishment or excuse. Two years. Eastern crew. He didn't run the river crossing. The violence was Brindle's operation under Voss's orders.

"And now you work for Kenji," Seraphine says. She studies him. Three seconds. "You deserted a criminal operation and switched sides in the space of a conversation."

"I switched sides when a better commanding officer showed up. That's not desertion. That's professional development."

Something happens at the corner of Seraphine's mouth. She doesn't let it develop.

"He dismantled the operation, recruited Brindle, and turned the Broken Antler into a mercenary guild," Garrett continues. "In one day. On a short rest. I was there."

I set the nighthollow vial on the desk.

Seraphine picks it up. Color drains from her face by one shade.

"Refined nighthollow. This concentration requires equipment that doesn't exist outside of three locations. One pharmaceutical house in the capital. One private laboratory in the north."

She sets it down.

"The third is here. In this Academy. Sub-basement level two."

The room goes still.

"Are you saying someone in the Academy supplied Voss?" Sera asks.

"I'm saying this vial raises questions I've been trying to find evidence to ask for a very long time."

She looks at me. The hunger different now. A woman who's been pulling at a thread alone in the dark finding someone holding the other end.

---

"How much time do you have, Kenji?"

"I have one week. For the information we've gathered and for solving your bandit problem, I'd like a week of training here at the Academy. If you have questions or research to do, you can do it during that time."

I continue.

"I also now have a mercenary guild. We won't work for free. The payments going to Voss should be redirected to Brindle under a contract for protecting the roads. Garrett here serves as my liaison, able to take on missions using guild resources during my down time."

Seraphine processes. Multiple proposals simultaneously.

"One week. Training. For a man with no formal magical education, no enrollment history, and abilities that don't appear in any classification text."

She picks up Edwyn's ledger. The spawning fluid sample. The nighthollow vial.

"Normally I'd say no. The Chancellor controls enrollment and he's — particular — about who studies here."

The hesitation on *particular* is brief. The predator's eyes catch it.

"However. This documentation is primary source evidence our theological department has theorized about for a century. This specimen is something our research wing would commit minor crimes to study. And this—" The nighthollow. "—is evidence of internal corruption I need time to investigate."

She paces. Two steps one way. Two steps back.

"I can justify a week as a research residency. You demonstrate abilities under controlled conditions. In exchange — training grounds, library, and select instructors."

She sits.

"The guild. Captain Dunmore was receiving twenty from Voss. The Council was paying Dunmore's garrison forty to protect the highway. Sixty total for a road that was killing travelers."

She writes on Academy letterhead.

"I'll draft a provisional contract. The Broken Antler Guild assumes highway security. Forty gold per month. Paid quarterly. Three-month trial. Subject to performance review."

"And Dunmore?"

"Arrested within the week."

She signs the contract.

"Training. Two instructors. Master Aldwin — combat magic integration. Seventy years old. Half-deaf. The most dangerous man in this building. Don't mistake age for weakness."

She meets my eyes.

"The second instructor is me. Magical theory. You cast by instinct and will. That's gotten you this far. But instinct without understanding is a blade without a handle."

She assigns Edwyn a formal presentation to the theological department in two days. Sera files her report tomorrow, then a private meeting. Garrett has free run of the city.

She pauses at the window.

"The entity beneath the Brennick farm. It told you that you were sent deliberately. That something felt it waking and answered with you."

A pause.

"I believe it. And that frightens me more than anything else you've told me today."

She turns back.

"Welcome to the Academy."

---

Before I leave I ask where to find a magical items shop and a blacksmith.

Seraphine gives me three options. Hargrove's Forge on Ironmonger's Row — best mundane smith in Varenholm. The Gilt Lens on the corner of Academy Way and Thornwell Street — licensed magical items, run by Maren Holt. Expensive, blunt, honest. And the Academy's own requisition vault — enchanted items and specialized equipment, available on day two of my residency.

"The vault is where the interesting things are."

---

The Gilt Lens. A narrow storefront between a bookbinder and a tailor. The window display — a floating crystal orb, gloves with blue runes, a dagger made of frozen smoke. A sign: *If you have to ask if it's real, you can't afford it.*

Maren Holt. Fifties. Short grey hair. Reading spectacles. Examining a ring under a magnifying glass. She doesn't look up.

"Browsing or buying."

"Buying. If you've got what I need."

She looks up. The spectacles magnify her eyes. "Maren Holt. You're the one Seraphine sent. She said you'd be interesting."

I lay out what I need. Storage. Stat-enhancing boots. Throwing weapons with return enchantment and non-lethal poison.

"Three categories," she says. "Let me show you what I have."

She sets a brown leather satchel on the counter. Plain. Unremarkable. She opens the flap and inside is darkness. Not shadow. *Depth*.

"Satchel of Holding. Fifty pounds regardless of bulk. Weighs two pounds. Food doesn't spoil. Worn cross-body — doesn't drop in combat. Sixty gold."

Windrunner Boots next. Dark leather with a band of silver thread around the ankle. "Plus two to Acrobatics checks. Won't make you faster. Will make you surer. Thirty-five gold."

Throwing knives last. Plain blackened steel — four gold for a set. Somnus toxin — sleeping agent, Constitution save or unconscious for one minute, eight gold for six doses. And the return enchantment — twenty gold per knife.

She chalks the totals. The satchel alone costs more than everything in my purse. The full package is a fantasy.

But I'm not buying with gold alone.

"Maren. Let me reframe this. I'm the man Seraphine Ash just granted a research residency to. The Academy vault opens for me in two days. I could wait."

Her eyes narrow.

"But I'm here. In your shop. Because Seraphine recommended you by name."

I set my gold purse on the counter.

"The Satchel of Holding, one returning knife with somnus toxin, and the Windrunner Boots. For fifty-two gold — everything I've got."

Her mouth opens. I keep going.

"But that's not the deal. I just founded a mercenary guild on the western highway. Every enchanted item, every magical accessory, every artifact my people pull out of a ruin or strip off a bounty — it comes here. Exclusively. Thirty percent below market."

She doesn't move for five seconds. Behind the spectacles, arithmetic at a speed that would impress a banker.

A mercenary guild clearing the highway. Recovering loot. Funneling every magical find to a single shop. The volume alone worth more in a year than a hundred sales. She'd become the primary supplier of recovered enchanted goods in Varenholm.

"Fifty-two gold. The satchel, the boots, and one returning knife with two somnus doses."

She holds up a finger.

"Two doses. Not six. You want more, come back with guild loot."

She drafts the exclusivity contract. Standard terms. Right to refuse cursed items and stolen property.

"And the name-dropping — *top student, honor guest* — don't do that in my shop again. Seraphine sent you because I'm honest. I'd like to keep that reputation."

The faintest crack in the severe expression.

"We have a deal?"

"We have a deal."

Fifty-two gold counted and gone. The purse goes flat. Three items on the counter.

I walk out of the Gilt Lens with an empty purse, a satchel that holds fifty pounds, boots that want to run, and a knife that comes back.

The door closes. The bell chimes.

"And that's how you sing for your supper," I whisper.

---

The Silver Draft. Evening. I transfer everything into the Satchel of Holding. Rations. Bedroll. Map. Tinder root. Voss's ledgers. The Antler kitchen food. It all goes in — fifty pounds of gear swallowed without gaining an ounce.

The weight disappears. Shoulders straighten. The ironwood armor sits differently without a pack pulling at it. I feel *light*. Combat light. The way I was always meant to move.

The Windrunner Boots grip the floor with quiet authority. The silver thread pulses against my ankles. When I shift weight, they respond — not moving me, agreeing with where I want to go.

The returning knife on my belt. Blackened steel. The rune warm against my hip. Two doses of sleep on the blade.

I eat. Roast chicken, potatoes, bread, a second plate. Garrett watches. Doesn't comment anymore.

The Academy tower is visible through the window. Pale stone darkening as windows go out one by one.

Tomorrow. Dawn. Master Aldwin on the training grounds. Seraphine in the theory room. A week of learning what I am and what I can become.

Six days ago I woke up in the mud with nothing.

Tonight I fall asleep in a real bed with a satchel that holds fifty pounds, boots that want to run, a knife that comes back, armor a blacksmith made through the night, a mercenary guild generating income, a Council contract securing a highway, evidence that could imprison a corrupt captain, and the attention of a brilliant woman who's afraid of something she hasn't told me yet.

And somewhere between the mud and this moment — a dying god, a broken cycle, and a force called creation burning steady in my chest.

Not bad. Not bad at all.
