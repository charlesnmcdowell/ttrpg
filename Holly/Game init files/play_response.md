Mama Po reads the lean-in before Holly's halfway through the gesture. The big woman drops her elbows from the counter, leans in close enough that the mango-streak on her apron is six inches from Holly's nose, tilts her ear, and goes very still. Behind Holly, both longshoremen lose interest in the same heartbeat — when a vendor leans in to hear someone who's leaning in to whisper, that's *trade business,* not gossip, and trade business is none of theirs. The pretending-to-retie-the-boot one straightens up and walks. The other has already drifted upmarket. The pier returns to its usual texture: gulls, frying garlic, somebody hauling rope.

Holly tells her — copper shim, western pivot pin, four percent toward the merchant, six months of fish. Mama Po does not interrupt. She waits until Holly's done. Then she straightens, picks up the paring knife, looks at it, sets it back down. "Six months," she says. Quiet. Not surprised so much as *confirmed.* "I had a feeling. I had a *feeling,* and I didn't say it because I couldn't prove it. He always weighs heavy on a Forgeday and light when there's a Council inspector down the row, and I thought *that's just luck,* and it wasn't luck, was it." She closes her eyes for a second. Opens them. Smiles — but the smile is a different smile than the morning smile; this one has weight behind it. "I'll handle him. Quietly. He'll think the gods got tired of him. You don't need to know the rest. *Thank you,* snowflake. That was an honest trade."

She pushes the wax-paper bundle the rest of the way across the counter. Knife away. Hands clean on the apron. "Cobalt Fountains. East hall, third row. Madam Pesca. *Hands before mouth.* If she likes you, you'll have a roof by sundown. If she doesn't —" she shrugs, "— come back here and I'll put you up in my brother's loft, costs nothing if you fix his loom while you're up there. Now go. Day's getting hot and you're still in winter wool."

2. Linger one more question — ask Mama Po who *else* in this town is honest, who's rotten, who's worth meeting before the sun gets vertical.
3. Ask Mama Po her brother's name and where the loft is — bank the fallback in case Pesca turns you away.
