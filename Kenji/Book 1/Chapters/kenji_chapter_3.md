# KENJI

## *Day Three — What Walks Toward Us*

---

Morning light through Pip's window. Third sunrise in this world. The ember wakes before I do — a slow pulse at the base of my skull, warmer than yesterday, deeper. Like something that spent the night growing roots.

Pip is still asleep beside me. Her breathing is slow and even. Her hair fans across the pillow in auburn tangles and one hand rests on the space where my arm was. I lie still and let the thoughts come.

Six weeks. That's what Maren said. Whatever woke up in The Delve — whatever reached into Harlan Brennick's cellar and called him by name — it stirred six weeks ago. The Hexcrawlers started appearing. The farmhands started thinning. The pond went grey.

And then — roughly six weeks later — I woke up face down in the mud on a dirt road outside Duskfen with nothing in my pockets and an ember burning at the base of my skull.

Coincidence.

I turn the word over in the dark of Pip's room and it doesn't fit. It sits wrong in my mouth the way a lie sits wrong. Six weeks. The same stretch of time. The same forest. Whatever is out there pushed against the skin of the world — Edwyn's words, *divine bleed* — and I showed up on the other side of it with magic that isn't learned. Magic that rides weapons. That enhances steel. That responds to *will* rather than study.

The ember pulses. Not an answer. An acknowledgment.

Who am I?

I don't know my past. I know I can throw hard enough to crack bone. I know I can dig a pit trap by instinct. I know the weight of a blade before I've been taught to hold one. I know how to read a room and talk a blacksmith out of his best steel and talk a barmaid out of her better judgement. These things live in my body like muscle memory for a life I can't access.

And last night — the ember listened to me for the first time. Really listened. What I felt underneath the surface wasn't small. It was vast and patient and *mine*. A weapon-force with no ceiling I've found yet. I turned my feet into weapons through sheer stubbornness. I fought a bear with rocks and won. I picked a lock and delivered a foot massage.

Something brought me here. Something that knew what was waking up in The Delve and dropped a Blade Channeler on its doorstep.

I don't know what that means yet. But I'm going to find out.

The thought doesn't get to settle. My stomach clenches — hard, sudden, the kind of hunger that doesn't politely request attention but *demands* it. The magic that lets me heal like a furnace burns fuel like one too. Four hours since the stolen bread and cheese. My body is already running a deficit.

I need food. Now.

---

I ease out of bed without waking Pip. Armour on, sword on hip, down the stairs. The common room is pre-dawn grey. The fire is banked. Nobody up yet except the sound of Pip's father moving in the kitchen — pots clanking, the heavy rhythm of a man who starts his day before the sun does.

I push through the kitchen door.

The innkeeper — broad, weathered, flour to his elbows — turns and looks at me with the expression of a man who knows exactly which room I didn't sleep in and has decided that this conversation isn't happening before breakfast.

"Eggs," he says flatly. "Sit down."

He feeds me without small talk — eggs, bread, thick bacon, a cup of hot broth that tastes like someone's grandmother knew what she was doing. I eat fast and without dignity. The hunger eases. The ember settles.

I finish the plate. Push it back. Now business.

"I've got something for you. Two haunches of bear meat, fresh from last night. And I've got wolf meat if you want it. Enough to feed the inn for days. What's that worth to you?"

He stops wiping his hands. Bear meat. In a village where the northern forest has been too dangerous to hunt for weeks. His eyes do the math — supply, demand, the fact that his stores are running thinner than he'd admit to Pip.

"Bear," he says. "Fresh."

"Killed it myself. Six hours ago."

He looks at me. Re-evaluates something. Then nods once.

"I'll take both haunches and whatever wolf meat you've got. Three gold for the bear. One for the wolf. Fair price for fresh game in a village that hasn't had any in a month."

Four gold pieces hit the counter.

"And your breakfast is still free," he adds. "On account of my daughter being happier than I've seen her in a year. Which we are *not* discussing."

He goes back to his pots.

---

Aldric's forge is already hot. The hammer stopped when he saw me coming — he's standing in the doorway with his arms folded, watching me cross the square with the same measuring look he gave me two days ago. Except now I'm carrying wolf pelts and a bear hide over my shoulder instead of a stick and two rocks.

"Morning, Aldric."

His eyes go to the pelts. To the bear hide. Stay there.

"That's a brown bear," he says. Not a question. "Full winter pelt."

"Killed it last night. Also two wolves." I set the bundle on his workbench. "I need gold for the road and I need better armour. What can you do for me?"

Aldric unwraps the bear hide first. Runs his hand across it — checking thickness, checking cuts, checking whether the man who skinned it knew what he was doing. He grunts. Professional assessment.

"Fieldwork," he says. "Not a tanner's job. But the hide is intact where it counts. Thick. Winter grade." He folds it. Sets it aside. Examines the wolf pelts. The canines. The bear claws.

He leans against his workbench and looks at me.

"Two gold for the wolf pelts. Three for the bear hide. One gold for the canines and claws combined — they're component material, not finished goods." He holds up a hand before I speak. "That's generous. I know what these are worth and so do you."

He reaches for the bear hide again. Turns it in his hands. The craftsman replacing the trader.

"As for armour—" He taps the leather on my chest. His son's armour. "This is protection for a farmhand in a bar fight. Not for a man walking toward Hexcrawlers."

He sets the bear hide flat on the workbench.

"Bear leather, boiled and layered properly, is twice as hard as what you're wearing. I can reinforce your chest piece, add layered bear-hide plating on the shoulders and forearms. Won't slow you down like metal would — you're fast, I can tell that much — but it'll turn a claw that would've opened you up."

He looks at me.

"I can have it done before you leave if I start now. Cost you nothing — the bear hide *is* the payment." A pause. "But if you want the *good* version — hardened, oil-cured, riveted at the joints — I need something I don't have."

He reaches under his workbench and pulls out a cracked, empty glass vial with a faint amber residue inside.

"Ironwood sap. Comes from a tree that only grows in deep old forest — old growth, the kind near ley lines. The sap hardens leather to near-steel strength without the weight. There used to be ironwood stands north of Duskfen." He sets the vial down. "That was before whatever's out there poisoned the forest. If you find one on your way to the Brennick farm — or beyond — bring me back a vial of sap and I'll make you armour that'll last longer than you will."

I tell him the plan. Hold off on the basic version. I want the best he can make. I'll find the ironwood sap on the road and send a runner back with it. He holds the hide. The runner picks up the finished armour and delivers it to me.

"You've got someone who'll run through Hexcrawler territory to deliver armour," he says.

"I'm working on it."

"Hm." He folds his arms. "I'll hold the hide and keep your current leathers in working shape for now. You find the sap, get it to me, and I'll need half a day from the moment it's in my hands."

He picks up his hammer. Pauses.

"Ironwood trees have silver-white bark. Smooth. Distinctive — you won't mistake it for anything else. The sap runs amber and smells like hot copper. Cut a notch at chest height and it bleeds. Fill the vial." He pulls a second empty vial from under his bench. "Take this. Don't let the sap touch bare skin — it irritates before it cures."

---

The stable. Early morning light through the slats. Ryn is already up — sitting cross-legged on a hay bale with his lockpick set open, working a padlock with the focused silence of a man doing his morning prayers.

He sees me and the pick freezes mid-pin.

"Kenji." Cautious. The look of someone who remembers saying no and is wondering if the question is coming back around.

It is. But different this time.

I lay it out. Not a fighter. Not an adventurer. A porter. Carry the party's camp gear on the road. Stay at camp, away from combat, away from danger. And one specific job — when I find ironwood sap on the trade road, he runs it back to Aldric in Duskfen and picks up the finished armour. Delivers it to me wherever we've made camp. A straight supply run on a road he already knows.

His eyes narrow.

"So I'm... a delivery boy."

"You're the team's logistics. Gear, supplies, camp setup. And one round trip on a road you walked to get here in the first place. You stay behind the line. The party handles anything with claws. You handle everything that doesn't want to kill us."

He's quiet for a long moment. The padlock hangs forgotten in his hands. His eyes move to his pack — tightly rolled, ready to move at short notice.

"The road to the Brennick farm," he says slowly. "Half a day. And I'd be with a group. A fighter, a healer, and..." He looks at me. At the longsword. At the leather armour that still has wolf blood in the creases. "...whatever you are."

"I'm the man who brings you interesting curiosities. This is how I get close enough to find them."

Something shifts. The fear is still there — it lives in his shoulders. But underneath it, the same thing I saw yesterday. The part of him that wants to matter.

"I have conditions," he says.

"Name them."

"One. I don't fight. I don't hold a weapon. I don't stand anywhere near anything with more than four legs."

"Done."

"Two. I get first pick of curiosities. Not second. Not *after* the valuable stuff. First."

"Already agreed."

"Three." He hesitates. "If it gets bad — really bad — and you tell me to run... I run. No guilt. No *where were you when we needed you*. I run and nobody holds it against me."

He's looking at me with the raw honesty of someone who knows exactly who they are and isn't pretending otherwise.

I look him in the eye. Steady.

"If it gets bad and I tell you to run — you run. No guilt. No judgement. That's a promise. No harm will come to you."

Ryn holds my gaze for a moment longer than he's held anyone's since arriving in Duskfen. Then he nods. Once. Sets the padlock down.

"I'll pack light. When do we leave?"

"Dawn departure was the plan. We've still got an hour. Be at the village gate with your pack."

He's already rolling his bedroll before I reach the stable door.

---

I find them at the well. Together for once — Sera checking the straps on a heavy canvas pack stuffed with rations and camp gear, a light crossbow and a quiver of bolts leaned against the stone beside her. She did her part. Edwyn stands nearby with his satchel, freshly packed, the clay jars inside clinking softly when he shifts his weight.

Sera sees me first. Her eyes do what they always do — the sweep. Face, posture, hands, sword, boots. But this time the sweep snags on something. She looks again. Slower.

The bandaged forearm under my bracer. The slightly different way I'm favouring my left leg. The fact that my leather armour has new stains that weren't there yesterday evening.

She straightens up.

"You went out last night."

Not a question. Not an accusation. A reading.

Edwyn looks between us. Adjusts his satchel strap. Says nothing.

Sera's eyes haven't left mine.

"What did you kill."

"Two wolves, a tie against a cougar, and a bear."

She doesn't move. But something behind her eyes recalculates.

"A bear," she repeats.

"Brown bear. Full grown. Den near a granite outcrop north-northeast. The wolves were a warm-up. The cougar ran after I put a stone into its shoulder at range. The bear—"

"You fought a *bear*. Alone. At night. In a forest full of Hexcrawlers." She says each word like she's setting them on a table. "With a longsword you've never been trained to use."

"I didn't use the longsword on the bear. I used rocks."

Silence.

Edwyn is staring at me with the expression of a healer who's been told his patient performed surgery on himself in the dark.

Sera looks at my bandaged forearm. At my leg. At Aldric's forge across the square where a bear hide is spread on a workbench.

"That's the hide," she says.

"That's the hide."

"You're an idiot," she says.

A beat.

"But you're an idiot who killed a bear with rocks and walked back." She folds her arms — less wall, more assessment. "How."

I lay it out. Arcane Edge on sharp flint — the ember infuses anything with a cutting edge. Opening at distance. Forcing predators to close on my terms. The wolves went down in melee after I blinded one with dirt. The cougar ran when the math stopped working. The bear — Thunderous Strike on the opening charge, Arcane Stride to get in and out, then kiting at range until I stepped closer and put a stone through its ribs.

She listens without interrupting. Her jaw shifted three degrees to one side.

"Arcane Stride," she says. "You can move at double speed."

"Twelve hours on one spell slot. Learned it last night."

"And the rock-throwing. You infuse flint with force magic and throw it."

"If it has a sharp edge. Smooth stones don't hold."

Something in her expression reorganizes — the assessment from two days ago being rewritten in real time.

"Your instincts aren't the problem," she says slowly. "I told you that. I was right. But instinct without foundation gets you killed the moment something is faster than your reaction time. You fought a bear and won because bears are strong and stupid. Hexcrawlers are strong and *coordinated*. Three of them will flank you in the dark and the range game won't save you because one will circle behind while the other two hold your attention."

She picks up the crossbow. Holds it out to Edwyn without looking.

"Father. Take this. Go to the field behind the chapel. Set up against the hay bales and put thirty bolts into them. I don't care about accuracy right now — I care about loading speed. You need to be able to reload without looking down. Go."

Edwyn takes the crossbow with the careful reverence of a man receiving a holy relic he's not qualified to hold.

"*Go.*"

He goes.

Sera turns back to me. Draws her arming sword. Unhurried. Professional.

"One hour. That's what you asked for two days ago. I told you to survive the next fight. You survived three. So here's what happens now."

Her eyes are sharp and completely focused.

"I can't teach you instinct. You don't need it. What I can teach you is what to do when something fast gets inside your range and the rocks are gone and the magic is spent and all you have is three feet of steel and the space between your feet."

She shifts her weight. The guard tightens.

"Defensive swordwork. How to not die when everything goes wrong. Draw your sword, Kenji."

---

She comes at me without warning. Three fast cuts — diagonal, lateral, thrust. I catch the first on instinct, redirect the second, and the third stops with the point at my throat.

"Dead," she says. "But better dead than yesterday would have been. Your guard is raw but it holds under pressure. Stop dropping your elbow on the redirect — you're opening your ribs."

She resets. Comes again. This time I keep the elbow tucked. The redirect is tighter. Her thrust slides past my shoulder instead of finding my neck.

"Better."

She teaches me the thing that matters — what she calls *the cage*. A defensive framework built around three principles: keep the blade between you and the threat, move your feet instead of reaching with your arms, and when steel meets steel, feel which way their weight commits and give them the empty space to fall into.

The cage clicks. Not perfectly — my muscle memory keeps trying to turn defence into offence, to counter when she wants me to *absorb* — but the framework seats itself in my footwork. Blade between. Feet moving. Let them fall past.

She speeds up. Testing. Her blade comes from angles I didn't know existed and the cage holds for three exchanges, four, five — and then she feints high and sweeps low and I'm on my back looking at the sky.

"Dead again." She offers her hand. "But you lasted five. Yesterday would have been one."

The final spar is harder. She comes at full speed — the real Sera, not the instructor — and the cage breaks in four exchanges. My elbow drops. My feet plant. She puts me on the ground twice in thirty seconds and the second time the flat of her blade taps my chest with a finality that says *this lesson is over*.

She sheathes her sword.

"Your shoulder still telegraphs the rising cut. Your elbow still drops under pressure. And you plant your feet when you're scared instead of moving them." She picks up her waterskin. "But the cage is in there now. Under pressure, your body will find it."

She looks at me with something that wasn't there two days ago.

"You killed a bear with rocks, Kenji. That's not nothing. But out there—" She nods northeast. "—the things we're walking toward don't care about impressive. They care about weaknesses. And yours is that you don't know what you don't know."

She takes a drink.

"So stay behind me when it gets tight. Use the range game when you can. And when you can't—"

She taps her own chest where the flat of the blade landed on mine.

"Cage. Feet. Blade between."

From the field behind the chapel, the distant *thwack... thwack... thwack* of crossbow bolts hitting hay bales. Edwyn is practising. The intervals are getting shorter.

---

I stop at the village gate. Sera and Edwyn are ahead — Sera checking the crossbow bolts in Edwyn's quiver with the expression of someone who doesn't trust anyone else's packing. Edwyn endures it with priestly patience. Ryn lingers a few steps behind them, pack on his back, looking like a man who's made a decision and is trying not to unmake it.

The ember pulses. Different than before.

I go still. Something new — sitting in the space the ember grew into last night. A shape I can't see but can *feel*. Like reaching into a dark room and knowing there's a handle before your fingers close around it.

Blades. Small ones. Not steel — something darker. Shadow and force compressed into an edge. I can feel them waiting to be called, five of them, hovering at the edge of existence like words on the tip of a tongue. The ember shows me in sensation, not language. The throw. The impact. And then... something coming *back*. Warmth returning through the same channel the blade left by. Life drawn from the wound and fed back into me.

A weapon that heals me when it hurts something else.

My hand twitches. The ember is eager. But a spell slot is a spell slot, and the road ahead has teeth. I close my fist. Hold the feeling where it is.

*Not yet. But soon.*

The party forms up at the gate. Sera on point. Edwyn behind her. Ryn behind Edwyn, eyes already scanning the tree line with the nervous attention of a rabbit in open ground. Me at the rear.

Pip is in the doorway of the inn. She doesn't wave. She just watches — arms folded, one shoulder against the frame — and when I look back she holds my gaze for three seconds before she goes inside.

Maren is at her window. A hand raised. Brief. Then the curtain falls.

Aldric's hammer starts up behind us. The sound follows us to the tree line and then the forest swallows it.

---

The trade road is older than Duskfen. You can feel it under your boots — packed earth worn by decades of cart wheels and foot traffic, grooved and rutted and slowly being reclaimed by the forest on both sides. Once this was a proper road. Now it's a suggestion the trees are considering overruling.

Sera sets the pace. Not fast, not slow — the measured stride of someone who knows that speed means noise and noise means things with six legs know where you are.

Thirty minutes in, she holds up a fist. Everyone stops.

"Tracks," she says quietly. She crouches. Points.

Six-legged prints in the soft earth at the road's edge. Wide. Deep. The gait pattern spreading outward into the undergrowth to the east.

"Hexcrawler patrol. Two of them. Heading east." She touches the edge of the nearest print. "Eight hours old. Maybe ten. They crossed the road during the night and kept moving."

She stands. Pulls out her map. "We're here. This patrol crossed between zones one and two. If they're running on schedule, they won't loop back for another six hours."

She folds the map.

"We move. Eyes open."

---

The forest changes as we walk. Slowly at first — a tree with bark darker than it should be. A patch of undergrowth that's grey instead of green. A stream crossing where the water runs clear but the stones underneath have a faint iridescent sheen I recognise from the old mill pond.

I fall into step beside Edwyn and tell him what to watch for. Ironwood tree — silver-white bark, smooth, grows near ley lines. Aldric needs the sap for the armour. I've got the vial.

Edwyn nods without breaking stride. "I know ironwood. Rare but not unheard of. The Temple archives mention them as markers — they grow where the boundary between our world and the divine runs thin. Ley line anchors." He glances at the corrupted undergrowth. "If the corruption hasn't killed them. We'll be passing through deeper old growth near the Brennick farm — that area sits close to the same ley line the old mill tapped. If there's ironwood anywhere between here and The Delve, it'll be there."

He looks at me. "I'll watch for it."

Behind us, Sera's voice: "Edwyn. What kills a ley line marker?"

Edwyn doesn't miss a step. "Nothing kills an ironwood, strictly speaking. But something that drains ambient energy from the land could starve the ley line itself. In which case the tree would..." He trails off. Looks at the grey undergrowth. "Ah."

"So if we find one standing," Sera says, "the ley line's still active. And if we don't—"

"Then whatever is draining the land has already reached the deep infrastructure."

Silence. The party walks with slightly more purpose.

I scan the tree line as we walk. Dark-barked oak and ash and twisted elm. No silver-white bark. Not in this stretch. Maybe further northeast. I keep the vial in my pack and my eyes open.

Then I notice it. Not with my eyes. With the silence.

For the first hour of walking the forest was alive. Birds. Insects. Small things rustling in the undergrowth. Normal forest noise.

It stopped. I can't pinpoint when, but the air around us is silent now in a way that has texture. The trees are taller here. Older. The canopy thicker. And the sounds that should fill the space between them have packed up and left.

"The forest is quiet," I say.

Sera nods without turning. "Has been for the last ten minutes. You're the first to say it."

Edwyn has gone pale. His hand is on the flame symbol at his neck.

"It's the *thinning*," he says quietly. "The same thing my farmhands described. We're closer to the source. The ambient presence is stronger here — not dangerous yet, but strong enough to push wildlife out." He looks at me. "If it starts affecting us — fatigue, whispers, the feeling of being watched — we say it out loud. Immediately."

Ryn hasn't spoken since the village gate. His knuckles are white on his pack straps.

---

Another hour. The trade road narrows. The trees press in. The grey corruption is more visible now — patches of bark that look bleached, leaves curling inward, a wrongness in the colour of things that's hard to name but easy to feel.

My stomach tightens. Three hours since breakfast. The ember burns hot and the body that houses it doesn't care about Hexcrawler patrols when the fuel gauge hits empty.

"We need to eat. Soon."

Sera points. A rocky clearing just off the road — a fallen tree creating a natural windbreak, sightlines in three directions.

"Ten minutes. Eat fast."

The party pulls off the road. Sera takes a position facing northeast, chewing dried meat without looking at it, watching the tree line. Edwyn distributes rations — hard bread, cheese, dried fruit. Ryn sits on a rock with his pack between his knees and eats in small nervous bites.

I eat. The hunger settles. The ember calms.

Then Sera goes still mid-chew. Her hand moves to her sword.

"Nobody move," she says quietly. "Nobody speak."

Her eyes are fixed on a point sixty feet northeast through the trees. I follow her gaze.

Movement. Low. The size of a large dog but the shape entirely wrong. Too many joints. A head swinging in slow arcs — tasting the air.

Then a second shape. Flanking wide to the right. Same size. Same wrong geometry. Moving in coordinated silence.

Two Hexcrawlers. Moving toward the road. Toward us.

They haven't seen the party yet. The fallen tree and the rocky clearing are providing cover. But they're closing. Sixty feet and shrinking.

Sera's voice is barely a breath.

"Two. Coming from the northeast. Sixty feet. They're hunting — sweeping pattern. They'll hit our scent in about thirty seconds."

She draws her sword without a sound. Edwyn's hands are shaking but he's reaching for the crossbow. Ryn has gone absolutely white and frozen.

Sera is looking at me. Waiting. She follows the plan of whoever's calling the shots in the field. Right now that's me.

"Sera — go for the one fifty feet east-northeast. Edwyn — back her up with bolts but keep your distance. I'll take the one sixty feet out."

I grab a sharp flint. The ember catches the edge — instant, hungry, silver-white light blooming in my palm. I break from cover and sprint twenty-five feet toward my target.

Thirty-five feet. Long range. But the Hexcrawler doesn't know I'm here. I throw.

The flint crosses the gap in a silver streak and hits the Hexcrawler square in the flank. The force detonation blows chitin fragments in a ring — the creature shrieks, that sub-frequency vibration I remember from the mill, the one that lives in your back teeth. It staggers sideways. Six legs scrambling. Six eyes snapping to my position.

It sees me. All six eyes lock on.

From the east — the sound of Sera's blade finding its target. A lateral cut that takes the second Hexcrawler across two of its front legs. Chitin splits. Dark iridescent fluid sprays across the bracken. The creature screams in harmony with the first — the same awful song in different keys.

Then a crossbow bolt punches through the second creature's rear membrane. Edwyn. Ugly release, wobbly flight — but it hit. The Hexcrawler bucks.

The surprise round is spent. Both creatures are wounded. Alerted. And I can see them reorganizing — the coordinated intelligence Sera warned me about. My Hexcrawler isn't charging blindly. It's *circling*. Moving laterally, keeping distance, looking for a flank angle. The second one is backing up — drawing Sera forward. Toward the gap where the first one could loop around behind her.

They're not panicking. They're *coordinating*.

"They're pulling a split," Sera calls. "Don't follow the bait."

The ember forks down my legs. Silver-white light detonates under my first footfall and the world *lurches*.

Arcane Stride.

Fifty feet of forest disappears in four strides. Each step kicking force off the earth. The trees blur. Sera's back appears ahead of me. The wounded Hexcrawler beyond her — leaking fluid, bolt in its flank — doesn't see me coming because nothing in this forest moves this fast.

I blow past Sera on her left. The longsword clears the scabbard mid-stride. No Arcane Edge — the ember is busy being rocket boots — just naked steel and momentum.

The slash comes from the sprint. Every ounce of speed channeled into a diagonal cut aimed at the creature's wounded flank.

The blade bites through cracked chitin and sinks into the meat underneath. The Hexcrawler shrieks — and drops. Mid-shriek. The sound cuts off like someone closed a door on it. Six legs fold inward and the body hits the forest floor with a wet crunch.

One hit. One kill.

I'm already past it. The momentum carries me ten feet beyond the corpse. I dig my boots into the soft earth and stop clean — weight centered, sword up, no tree required.

I turn. The Hexcrawler is a heap of chitin and dark fluid. Sera is standing over it with her sword raised for a strike that's no longer needed.

"I had that," she says.

She doesn't waste time. Her head snaps toward the first Hexcrawler — the one that was circling to flank her. It's forty feet away now, six eyes locked on the spot where its partner just died.

Sera sprints. Closes the distance. Her blade catches the creature across the front two legs as it tries to pivot. Chitin cracks.

Edwyn's second bolt sails wide and thunks into a tree. He winces.

The creature is hurt. Badly. Its partner is dead. The flanking maneuver is broken. It's surrounded — Sera in front, me behind with silver light fading from my boots.

It runs. Six legs churning earth, the wounded side dragging, crashing through undergrowth heading northeast at full speed. In three seconds it's forty feet away. In five it's lost in the tree line.

Toward The Delve. Toward whatever sent it.

Sera watches it go.

"It's running home," she says quietly. "Which means whatever is at home now knows we're coming."

---

Silence. The forest holds its breath. Iridescent fluid pools under the dead Hexcrawler.

Ryn's head appears above the rocks. He stares at the corpse. Then at me. Then at the silver-fading footprints carved into the earth.

"What... *are* you?" he whispers.

Sera sheathes her sword. Walks to the dead Hexcrawler. Crouches. Examines it with clinical precision.

"You crossed fifty feet in about two seconds," she says without looking up. "And killed it in one swing."

She stands. Looks at me.

"You're not a swordsman, Kenji. You're a *striker*. You hit once, hard, and you're gone before anything touches you."

She looks northeast.

"That one will reach The Delve before we reach the Brennick farm. If there's anything intelligent directing these patrols, it knows our numbers, our direction, and that one of us moves faster than anything in this forest."

She picks up her pack.

"We move. Now. Fast pace. No more stops until the farm."

---

The trade road narrows further. The corruption deepens — trees leaning at wrong angles, patches of earth that look exhausted, a smell underneath everything like old pennies left in standing water.

Then through a gap in the canopy — the angular shape of a roofline. Timber-framed. Sagging slightly to the east. A stone chimney with nothing coming out of it.

The Brennick farm.

The clearing opens around us. Fifty feet of open ground between the tree line and the farmhouse. The grass is dead. Not autumn dead — *wrong* dead. Grey and brittle, crunching under our boots like old paper. A vegetable garden to the left has collapsed into itself, the plants blackened and curled inward.

The farmhouse is intact but abandoned. The front door is closed. One window shutter hangs loose, swaying in a wind I can't feel. A woodpile beside the house still has an axe wedged in the splitting log — untouched for six weeks.

I see them. Boot prints in the dead grass leading from the front door to the tree line north. One set. Deep. Evenly spaced. Walking, not running. Harlan's last walk.

Behind me, Ryn whispers: "The grass is *crunching*." He's staring at the ground. At his own boots. "Grass isn't supposed to crunch."

No return prints.

And then I feel it.

Not with my eyes. Not with my ears. With the ember.

A *pull*. Faint. Steady. Coming from below the farmhouse. Like standing at the edge of deep water and feeling the current tug at your ankles. The ember reacts — not with fear, not with eagerness. With *recognition*. It knows what's down there. Or it knows the *kind* of thing that's down there.

Edwyn has stopped walking. His hand is on his flame symbol. His face is grey.

"I feel it," he says quietly. "The bleed. It's stronger here than anywhere in Duskfen. Much stronger."

The cellar doors. Wooden double doors set into the ground at the side of the building. One slightly ajar. Dark underneath.

Sera looks at me.

"This is your call, Kenji. We talked about the plan — one hour maximum, seals ready, everyone watches for symptoms. How do you want to approach?"

"We may be able to do multiple tries," I tell her. "Let's do what we can in the first hour and make it back to camp with Ryn to discuss what we learned."

Sera nods. "Smart. Reconnaissance, not commitment."

Ryn is at the tree line. I tell him to set up camp in the trees, off the road, concealed. One hour. If we're not back in ninety minutes—

"I run. I know." He says it quietly. Without shame. "Ninety minutes. Then I'm gone."

He disappears into the tree line. Good. Safe. Out of it.

---

Edwyn prepares us. The silverleaf tea — pale green with a faint shimmer, brewed in an iron flask, poured into clay travel cups.

"Drink all of it. It dulls receptivity — you'll still feel the presence, but the *voice* won't get through as clearly. Lasts roughly two hours."

I drink. It tastes like wet leaves and copper. Something settles behind my eyes. A slight muffling, like someone draped a cloth over a part of my mind I didn't know was exposed.

The ember notices. Pulses once. Doesn't fight it.

The binding wraps come next. Consecrated cloth wound around our wrists. Cool and faintly damp. Blessed water.

"Ambient protection," Edwyn says. "Not a shield — a filter."

He pulls out the two Seal of the Wandering Flame discs. Iron. Etched with the cupped flame. Heavy for their size.

"If we find the door and the presence is still behind it — I place these on the frame and speak the rite. Thirty seconds." He looks between us. "Don't let anything kill me for thirty seconds."

Sera almost smiles.

"Sera takes point," I say. "Edwyn in the middle. I'll cover our rear. My speed won't work down there — our best bet is to see threats before they see us."

---

The cellar doors open to stone steps descending into darkness. Sera goes first. Sword in her right hand, left hand trailing the stone wall. Edwyn follows — and the light comes with him.

His flame symbol glows. Soft amber. The warm radius of a campfire, maybe fifteen feet. Enough to see walls and stairs and shapes. Not enough to see the far end of a large room.

"The Wandering Flame," he says. "Minor invocation. Lasts as long as my concentration holds. If I take a hit, it goes out."

So Edwyn is the light source. He takes a hit, we're all blind.

"Stay between us," Sera tells him. "If something comes from the front, I handle it. If something comes from behind—"

She looks at me.

"That's mine."

---

Eight steps. The air changes. Colder, damper, the smell of old earth thickening. The stone walls are rough-cut. Hand-dug. Twenty years of Harlan Brennick's stubbornness carved into clay and rock.

"He dug all this himself?" Sera says, running her hand along the wall. Respect in her voice despite everything.

Edwyn nods. "Harlan was the kind of man who saw bedrock as a personal insult."

The cellar opens. A root cellar — fifteen feet by twenty. Low ceiling. Shelves along the left wall holding clay jars of preserved food, most still sealed. A workbench against the right wall with tools — hand saw, chisels, measuring cord. Harlan was expanding the cellar when he heard the sounds.

And at the far end.

The stone door.

It doesn't belong. Everything in this cellar is clay and timber and human hands. The door is *other*. Smooth grey stone, no mortar, no frame. The surface covered in carved symbols worn almost flat by time that dwarfs the farm, the village, everything built by human hands in this region. The symbols catch Edwyn's amber light and seem to drink it.

The door is six inches ajar.

The pull hits me through the silverleaf like a wave hitting a seawall. Muffled but *present*. Coming through that six-inch gap with the steady patience of water finding a crack. The ember responds — not with fear, not with eagerness. With the taut attention of something that has met its own kind and isn't sure yet if it's friend or predator.

Sera hasn't moved. Her sword is up. Her knuckles are white.

"That's not human construction," she says. "That's not even close."

Edwyn's light flickers — not from wind, from his hands trembling. He steadies them. His eyes are locked on the symbols.

"Old," he whispers. "Older than the Wandering Flame. Older than any scripture I've read. These symbols — I don't know the language. But the *structure*... it's a threshold ward. This door was meant to stay closed."

Scratch marks on the floor. Deep gouges in the packed earth leading from the stairs to the door. Ten fingernails' worth of desperate clawing — but going the wrong direction. *Toward* the door, not away from it.

Harlan didn't claw his way out. He clawed his way *in*.

We need to secure the door open — a known route out. I grab both chisels from the workbench. Sera sheathes her sword and we put our hands on the stone. The surface is cold — not cellar cold. *Deep earth* cold. The symbols under my palms feel like they're vibrating.

We push. The door resists — not mechanically. It resists the way deep water resists a hand pushing through it. I plant my feet and push with everything my legs and shoulders have and Sera grunts beside me and the stone grinds against the floor — a sound that vibrates through my molars.

One foot. Two feet. Three.

The pull *surges*. Something pushes against the inside of my mind like a hand pressing on a window. The ember flares in response — a pulse of heat that feels like a door being bolted from the inside.

I jam both chisels under the door's base. They bite and hold. Sera tests it. The door doesn't move.

"It'll hold unless something stronger than us pushes from the other side," she says. "So we work fast."

---

I look at the three-foot gap. The darkness beyond. Even Edwyn's amber glow stops at the threshold — not fades, *stops*. The dark on the other side isn't shadow. It's *substance*.

"That's not natural darkness," Edwyn says. "That's the presence itself. It fills the space."

We need more than a candle and a prayer. We need something the darkness is afraid of.

I hold my sword up high. The ember has refused utility work. Night vision — flat no. But this isn't a request for utility. The darkness isn't empty air — it's the *enemy*. It occupies this space the way an army occupies a battlefield. And the sword in my hand is raised against it.

"We need a weapon of light!" I shout at the ceiling, grinning like a man who's either brilliant or insane.

The ember hears me. And for the first time since I've known it — it doesn't hesitate.

It *detonates*.

Upward. Through my arm, through my wrist, through my grip and into the steel. The longsword ignites. Not silver-white. Not the familiar force infusion.

Gold.

Blazing, furious, *radiant* gold. The light erupts from the blade like sunrise compressed into three feet of steel. It doesn't flicker. It doesn't waver. It *burns* — and the darkness *recoils*. Ten feet. Twenty. Thirty.

Thirty feet of clear space. The passage walls revealed in sharp gold detail — the organic curves of the stone, the moisture on the floor, Harlan's boot prints glowing wet like a road map into the deep.

Edwyn drops his concentration on the Wandering Flame. He doesn't need it anymore.

"That's not arcane light," he whispers. "That's *radiant*. That's the kind of light the old texts describe as *anathema* to entropy. The light that exists specifically to push back against the unraveling of things."

He looks at me with eyes that are rewriting everything they assumed.

"What *are* you, Kenji?"

Sera hasn't spoken. She's looking at the passage ahead and her expression says she'll process the theological implications later.

"Questions later. Move now. He just rang the doorbell."

She's right. Something below has changed. Something is *attentive*.

---

I tell them we've got about forty minutes. Double time. Eyes open for anything that looks important.

Sera increases pace. The golden light moves with us, the darkness retreating ahead in that measured thirty-foot boundary.

The passage descends in a slow spiral. The smooth grey stone develops veins of something darker — black threads running through the rock like capillaries. I don't touch them.

First find — an alcove in the wall. Waist-height. Inside, a clay oil lamp, ancient, cracked but intact. Symbols on its base matching the door. Beside it — a small iron ring set with a dull black stone that catches my golden light and *holds* it. The radiance enters the stone and doesn't come back out.

Edwyn reaches for it. Stops.

"Don't touch it. That ring is an *anchor*. See how the light enters the stone and doesn't reflect? It's designed to *absorb*. This is how the presence feeds — objects like this placed at intervals, drawing in ambient energy. Magical, spiritual, life force."

He wraps it in cloth without touching it and tucks it away. "Evidence."

Second find — the passage widens into a small chamber. Ten feet across. On the walls — *murals*. Faded almost to nothing, the pigment eaten by time and the presence both, but the golden light catches what remains.

Edwyn stops dead.

"Wait. *Wait.*"

He steps close to the wall. His fingers hover an inch from the surface.

"This is a record. A *history*. This figure — a circle with lines radiating outward — that's a divine symbol. A god. And these smaller figures around it..." He moves along the wall. "They're *offering*. Willingly. Hands raised. This wasn't always a place of corruption."

His voice drops to a whisper.

"This was a *temple*. Before it was a cellar, before it was a farm, before Duskfen existed — this was a place of worship. And the entity being worshipped..." He points to the central figure. "...is what's down there. It wasn't always a monster, Kenji. It was a *god*. A real one. The god of entropy — of endings, of things coming apart. And these people worshipped it because endings are *part of the cycle*."

He steps back.

"It was dying even then. The worshippers fade across the panels — fewer and fewer — and the god's symbol gets smaller. It was being forgotten. And a forgotten god is a desperate god."

Sera's voice from ahead. Tight.

"Kenji. Edwyn. Come look at this."

---

The passage ends. Opens into a chamber that the golden light fills completely and still feels too dark.

Circular. Forty feet across. The ceiling vaulted high enough that the light barely reaches it. The walls covered in symbols — thousands of them, layered, overlapping, carved and recarved over centuries. The black veins in the rock are thicker here. Pulsing. Slowly. Like a heartbeat you can see.

In the centre — a stone platform. Low. Altar-shaped.

And on the altar — a body.

Harlan Brennick.

Lying on his back. Arms at his sides. Eyes closed. Work shirt, heavy trousers, boots still on. His face is peaceful. Not decayed. Not injured. He looks like a man sleeping.

Except for the black veins.

They run from the altar surface up through his clothing and into his skin — his hands, his neck, his face. Dark threads spreading under the flesh like roots of a plant that's using him as soil.

Edwyn makes a sound. Small. Professional. Horrified.

"He's alive," the priest says. "I can see his chest moving. He's *alive*."

Sera is scanning the room. Sword up. Turning in slow circles.

"Where's the thing that walked beside him," she says. "The drag marks. Where did it go."

She's right. Harlan's boot prints end at the altar. The drag marks end at the altar. But the chamber is empty.

Then the vibration stops.

Not fades. *Stops*. The constant low hum that's been in the floor since we entered goes silent.

The black veins on the walls pulse once. Hard. My sword flickers — not dims, *flickers* — as if something pushed against the light from outside.

And a voice fills the chamber. Not sound. Not words. A *pressure* behind my eyes that the silverleaf tea catches and translates into something almost language.

**I KNOW YOU.**

The ember ignites. White-hot. Defensive. Like a dog with its hackles up.

**NOT YOU. THE THING INSIDE YOU. I KNOW WHAT IT IS.**

The pressure shifts. Focuses. On me specifically. Sera and Edwyn might as well not exist.

**YOU ARE NOT FROM HERE. AND NEITHER IS YOUR FIRE.**

---

I give the orders first. Whatever this is, whatever it says, we came for a man.

"Edwyn — Harlan. Stabilize him. Drop the crossbow. You're carrying him out. Stay in formation."

Edwyn doesn't hesitate. The crossbow hits stone and he's at the altar, satchel open. He checks Harlan's pulse. Strong. Breathing steady. The veins are connective, not parasitic — Harlan is plugged into the altar like a root system.

Edwyn presses a consecrated binding wrap against the point where the veins meet the stone. Murmurs something. The amber glow of his faith flares against the black threads and they *recoil* — pulling back into the stone like worms retreating into soil. Harlan's body shudders. A gasp. His eyes don't open but his breathing changes.

"He's disconnected. Alive. Unconscious. I can carry him."

"Sera. Thirty minutes. We burn ten more — I want to see what's feeding this."

She nods. "Clock's running."

I turn my attention inward. The presence is waiting — that pressure behind my eyes. Patient.

*Sure*, I think. *How about you stop this spread of darkness and we can talk this out like gentlemen. No one has to die here today.*

Silence. Then —

**STOP.**

The word lands like a stone dropped into still water.

**YOU THINK I CHOSE THIS.**

The black veins pulse. Slow. Heavy.

**I AM WHAT REMAINS OF WHAT I WAS. I WAS THE ENDING OF THINGS. THE GENTLE DARK AFTER THE LAST LIGHT. THE FINAL BREATH. THE LETTING GO. I HAD A PURPOSE AND A PLACE AND WORSHIPPERS WHO UNDERSTOOD THAT ENDINGS ARE NOT CRUEL — THEY ARE NECESSARY.**

The murals seem to shift in the golden light. The figures with raised hands. The circle with radiating lines.

**THEY FORGOT ME. ONE BY ONE. CENTURY BY CENTURY. A GOD WITHOUT WORSHIP IS A FIRE WITHOUT FUEL. I DID NOT CHOOSE TO BECOME THIS.**

Behind me, Edwyn's voice is barely audible: "It's telling the truth. The murals — the timeline matches. This wasn't corruption. This was *attrition*."

Sera, tighter: "Edwyn. Focus."

"I am focused. I'm focused on the fact that this thing is exactly what the old texts warned about. A divine entity in terminal decline."

The pressure shifts. Closer.

**THE DARKNESS YOU SEE IS NOT MALICE. IT IS HUNGER. I AM STARVING, LITTLE FIRE. I HAVE BEEN STARVING FOR LONGER THAN YOUR WORLD REMEMBERS I EXIST. AND STARVING THINGS DO DESPERATE THINGS.**

A pause. Choosing words with care.

**THE MAN ON THE ALTAR. HE HEARD ME. MOST CANNOT. HE CAME DOWN AND I... TOOK WHAT I COULD REACH. NOT TO HARM. TO SURVIVE. HIS LIFE FORCE SUSTAINS ME THE WAY A SINGLE COAL SUSTAINS A FIRE THAT ONCE BURNED IN A HEARTH THE SIZE OF A CATHEDRAL.**

The ember flares. The presence notices.

**YOUR FIRE. IT IS LIKE ME AND UNLIKE ME. IT BURNS WHERE I FADE. IT BUILDS WHERE I UNRAVEL. IT CAME FROM SOMEWHERE ELSE — THE SAME SOMEWHERE YOU CAME FROM.**

A beat. Precise. Intimate. Aimed directly at the question I asked myself this morning in Pip's room.

**YOU WANT TO KNOW WHO YOU ARE. I CANNOT TELL YOU THAT. BUT I CAN TELL YOU WHAT YOUR FIRE IS. IT IS THE OPPOSITE OF ME. I AM ENTROPY. YOUR FIRE IS...**

The presence hesitates. The first hesitation since it spoke.

**...CREATION. RAW AND UNFINISHED AND DROPPED INTO THIS WORLD SIX WEEKS AFTER I BEGAN TO BLEED THROUGH THE BOUNDARY. THAT IS NOT COINCIDENCE, LITTLE FIRE. SOMETHING SENT YOU. SOMETHING THAT FELT ME WAKING AND ANSWERED WITH YOU.**

The chamber is silent except for Harlan's breathing and the drip of moisture on ancient stone.

**YOU ASKED ME TO STOP. I WILL TELL YOU WHAT STOPPING LOOKS LIKE. IT LOOKS LIKE DYING. THE SPREAD IS NOT A CHOICE — IT IS THE INVOLUNTARY GASP OF A DROWNING THING. SEAL THIS DOOR AND I SUFFOCATE SLOWER. DESTROY THIS CHAMBER AND I END.**

A pause. Then quieter.

**UNLESS YOU CAN OFFER ME WHAT I ACTUALLY NEED. NOT STOLEN LIFE. NOT A MAN ON AN ALTAR. I NEED ESSENCE GIVEN FREELY. WILLINGLY. ENOUGH TO SUSTAIN WITHOUT CONSUMING. YOUR FIRE COULD DO THIS. CREATION CAN FEED ENTROPY WITHOUT BEING DESTROYED BY IT — THE WAY A RIVER FEEDS THE OCEAN WITHOUT THE RIVER DISAPPEARING.**

**BUT THAT IS YOUR CHOICE. NOT MINE. I HAVE TAKEN ENOUGH CHOICES FROM ENOUGH PEOPLE.**

The pressure withdraws. Waiting.

Sera's hand is on my arm. When it got there I don't know. She's staring at the altar with the expression of a woman running combat math on a problem that has no numbers.

"I understood about half of that," she says quietly. "The half I understood terrifies me."

---

I turn to face my party. The golden light catches the chamber walls and the slow pulse of the black veins that look less menacing now.

"I figured out what it is," I say. "It's a god. The god of entropy — endings, things coming apart. It used to be worshipped here, in this chamber, centuries before Duskfen existed. Its people forgot it. And a god without worshippers starves."

Edwyn's face goes white. Then something else — the look of a man whose worst theological suspicion just got confirmed.

"The divine bleed," he whispers. "It wasn't pushing through. It was *gasping*."

"The spread. The Hexcrawlers. The thinning. All of it — involuntary. The drowning reflex of something that's been dying for centuries." I look at Harlan's unconscious body over Edwyn's shoulder. "It kept him alive. Took what it could reach to survive."

"Three options. We destroy the altar — the god dies. Permanently. Or we seal the door — it suffocates slower, buys time. Or I can touch it. Make a sacrifice. Give it what it needs — essence offered freely. I think it would restore the god. And I think I'll be ok." I look between them. "I don't think it wants to hurt us."

Sera speaks first.

"You *think* you'll be ok. You *think* the god doesn't want to hurt us. A god that's been draining farmhands for six weeks and put a man in a coma on a stone altar." She points at Harlan. "That man has black veins under his skin, Kenji. Whatever *it* wants, what it *does* is consume people."

She steps closer. Her voice drops.

"What exactly happens when you touch it and you're *wrong*?"

Her eyes are hard. Not angry. *Scared*. Sera doesn't do scared.

"I need more than *I think I'll be ok*."

Edwyn speaks. Quieter.

"My condition was to understand the source. Not just destroy it. If this is a divine entity in its death cycle, destroying the altar isn't justice. It's execution." He meets my eyes. "But Sera isn't wrong. If you do this — if you touch it and offer freely — what guarantee do we have that it takes only what's offered?"

I close my eyes. Reach for the ember.

*Talk to me*, I think. *Hot if we can save it and I survive. Cold if I should destroy the altar. Warm if we shut it in and walk away.*

The ember listens. Weighs.

A flash of searing heat — there and gone in half a heartbeat. A pulse of cold so deep it locks my jaw. Then warmth — steady, sustained, filling my chest.

And with the warmth — a vision. Two forces. One gold, one black. Not fighting. *Orbiting*. Circling each other the way day circles night. The gold pours into the black and the black pours into the gold and where they meet there's a hum. The sound of a cycle working correctly.

The warmth stays. The others fade.

*Purpose*.

The ember wasn't sent here to destroy entropy. It wasn't sent here to observe. It was sent here to *restore a cycle*. Creation feeding entropy. Entropy making space for creation. The river and the ocean.

And underneath — a certainty that isn't mine. It belongs to the ember.

*You will survive this. This is what you are for.*

My eyes open. The golden light from my sword is brighter. The ember is *eager*. For the first time since I've known it, the thing inside me isn't just willing or ready.

It's *eager*.

I look over at Edwyn. Smile.

"Make sure Harlan gets home to his wife."

Then I turn to Sera. She's already moving — she can read the intention on my face.

I stare her in the eyes.

"So you *do* worry about me."

Her fingers close on air.

"Guess I'm more than just cute."

I stick my tongue out at her.

And dive.

"KENJI—"

---

My hand hits the altar.

The world *ends*.

Not dies. Not breaks. *Ends*. The way a sentence ends. The way a breath ends. I feel the god's nature pour into me through my palm and it is every final moment that ever existed — the last heartbeat, the last ember, the last star going dark in an empty sky.

My body tries to die. Every cell receives the message *it is time to stop* and for one horrifying instant I feel myself beginning to unravel — threads of who I am pulling loose at the edges like fabric caught in a machine—

The ember *detonates*.

Not outward. Not upward. *Against*. It meets the entropy at the point of contact and *refuses*. Gold light erupts from my hand — not the gentle radiance of the sword, something deeper, something primal — the light of the first thing that ever existed pushing back against the last thing that will ever exist.

The collision is *visible*. Gold and black meeting at my palm in a border that screams with energy. The chamber shakes. Dust falls from the vaulted ceiling. The symbols on the walls blaze — every single one, all at once, lit from within by something that remembers what they meant.

**IT HURTS.**

The presence. Not attacking. Not taking. *Receiving*.

**DON'T STOP.**

I wasn't planning to.

The ember opens. Not the surface — the *depth*. The vast, patient, limitless thing I felt in the clearing last night. It rises through me like a tide and pours through my palm into the altar and the altar drinks and drinks and drinks—

My vision whites out. My knees hit stone. I can't feel my hand anymore — it's just a point of light connected to a point of dark. Something essential is leaving me — not stolen, *given* — and the giving costs more than I expected.

Somewhere behind me Sera is screaming my name. Edwyn is praying — the real kind, the kind that sounds like a man bargaining with the universe.

The drain continues. I feel myself dimming. The edges of who I am growing soft. Thin.

And then — between one heartbeat and the next — the border *stabilizes*.

The screaming energy goes quiet. The gold and the black stop fighting and start *flowing*. I feel it happen — the moment the river finds the ocean and realizes they were always the same water. The creation pours into the entropy and the entropy *makes space* for the creation and where they meet there is a hum. The sound of a cycle working correctly for the first time in centuries.

The black veins in the walls stop pulsing. They *settle*. Like a heartbeat finding its rhythm. The thick darkness thins — not gone, but no longer hungry. It becomes what darkness is supposed to be. Just the absence of light. Nothing more.

**OH.**

Different now. Not pressure. Not weight. Wonder.

**I REMEMBER THIS. I REMEMBER WHAT THIS FELT LIKE.**

The symbols glow steadily. Gold and black intertwined. The murals are fully visible for the first time in centuries — the worshippers with raised hands, the circle with radiating lines. And beneath them, painted so faintly it was invisible before — a second symbol. A circle with lines radiating *inward*. Creation. The companion piece.

They were always meant to be together.

**THE CYCLE. YOU RESTORED THE CYCLE. NOT FULLY. NOT PERMANENTLY. BUT ENOUGH.**

The drain stops.

My hand comes free. I fall backward. Hit stone. The ceiling swims above me — vaulted stone and ancient symbols and golden light from a sword lying on the ground still blazing like a small sun.

Everything hurts. My hand is numb. My arms are shaking. My vision pulses dark at the edges. The ember is still there — not gone, not diminished, but *exhausted*. Like a muscle after lifting something at the very limit of what it can bear.

Sera is on her knees beside me. Her hands on my shoulders. Her face is — I've never seen that expression. Fury and relief and something else braided so tight she can't separate them.

"You *idiot*," she says. Her voice cracks on the word. "You absolute — you *stupid* —"

She can't finish. Her hands are shaking.

Edwyn is staring at the chamber. At the symbols glowing in steady paired light — gold and black, creation and entropy. Tears running down his face. Harlan still unconscious over his shoulder. The black veins on Harlan's skin *fading*. Like ink dissolving in clean water.

**LITTLE FIRE.**

Quieter now. Smaller. Not weaker — *contained*. A fire given a hearth after burning wild for centuries.

**WHAT YOU GAVE ME WILL SUSTAIN ME. NOT FOREVER. THE CYCLE NEEDS TENDING — A WILLING SOURCE, PERIODICALLY, TO KEEP THE BALANCE. BUT THE GASPING HAS STOPPED. THE BLEEDING WILL SLOW. YOUR FARMHANDS WILL RECOVER.**

A pause.

**THE CREATURES WERE BORN FROM MY DESPERATION. THEY WILL NOT STOP IMMEDIATELY — THEY ARE ALREADY ALIVE, ALREADY DIRECTED. BUT THE INTELLIGENCE DRIVING THEM WILL FADE. WITHOUT MY DESPERATE NEED PUSHING THROUGH THE BOUNDARY, THEY BECOME ANIMALS. DANGEROUS BUT NO LONGER COORDINATED. NO LONGER EXPANDING.**

Another pause. Longer.

**YOU ASKED WHO YOU ARE. I STILL CANNOT TELL YOU. BUT I CAN TELL YOU THIS: WHATEVER SENT YOU CHOSE WELL. YOU TOUCHED A DYING GOD AND GAVE FREELY AND DID NOT BREAK.**

**MOST WOULD HAVE BROKEN.**

The presence withdraws. Resting. Breathing — really breathing, for the first time in centuries, in a rhythm that doesn't sound like drowning.

---

The passage back up is a blur. Golden light bouncing off organic stone walls. Edwyn ahead with Harlan over his shoulder. Sera half-carrying me, her shoulder under my arm, her grip iron-steady. I refuse to drop the sword.

The stone door. The chisels holding. We squeeze through one at a time.

The cellar. Harlan's shelves. His tools. The stairs.

Daylight.

The grey afternoon sky hits my eyes like a fist. I'm on my knees in the dead grass beside the cellar doors, sword blazing gold, blinking against a world that seems too bright.

Sera says my name. I look where she's pointing.

The dead grass nearest the cellar doors. The brittle grey blades.

There's green at the roots. Faint. Barely visible. But *there*. The first green thing on the Brennick farm. Pushing up through six weeks of grey death like it just remembered how.

The cycle. Already working.

---

Ryn nearly falls out of a tree when we emerge. He sees Edwyn carrying a body. Sees Sera half-dragging me. Sees the blazing sword. Sees the blood on my hand.

"Is that — is he—"

"Alive," Edwyn says. Sets Harlan down on the bedroll Ryn laid out. "Unconscious. Stable. The veins are fading."

I let the Radiant Edge go. The golden light dies and the longsword is just a longsword and the clearing feels suddenly very dark.

My legs give out. I sit down hard on a fallen log.

"Food. Now. *Please.*"

Ryn moves faster than I've ever seen him move. Rations appear. I eat without tasting. Without pausing. Without dignity. The ember drinks every bite before it reaches my stomach — hardtack, dried fruit, a strip of salted meat — all of it consumed by the heat like fuel thrown into an open forge. Three rations gone in five minutes. I can feel the calories being pulled apart and fed into whatever the channeling tore open.

Edwyn works on me. Poultice on the hand that touched the altar — the skin is angry red, not burned but *stressed*. Binding wraps. Willowbark tincture. The healing spreads through me faster than he expects.

"Rest," Edwyn says. "Full rest. Your body just channeled something I don't have a name for."

Sera sets up a perimeter. She hasn't spoken since we came out. She's moving with the precise, aggressive efficiency of someone processing something enormous by doing the thing she knows how to do. Checking sightlines. Making sure nothing with claws gets close.

She drives her sword into the earth at the tree line and sits against a trunk facing the Brennick farm.

"I'll take first watch," she says to no one. "All night if I have to."

Then, quieter, without looking at me:

"Don't ever do that again."

A pause.

"Idiot."

---

Ryn has the camp together in twenty minutes. Bedrolls. A small fire — concealed behind the fallen tree, smoke dispersed through the canopy. Harlan is stable, breathing steady, the black veins almost invisible now. Edwyn checks him every fifteen minutes.

I lie on my back and look at the sky through the trees. The ember is there — quiet, exhausted, but *warm*. Not the desperate warmth of a fire fighting to stay lit. The settled warmth of something that did what it was meant to do.

*Good*, I think. *Stay lit.*

The ember answers. Faint. Barely a whisper.

But warm.

Tomorrow I'm carrying a man home to his wife. The Hexcrawlers are still out there — uncoordinated now, but alive and dangerous. The Delve is still a day and a half northeast. The cycle needs tending. And somewhere in this world, something sent me here on purpose — dropped a weapon of creation into the path of a dying god — and I still don't know who I am or why.

But I know what I'm for.

I close my eyes. Pip's face behind my eyelids for a moment. Then Sera's — the expression I've never seen before, the one she couldn't separate into its parts. Then the vision — gold and black orbiting each other, day and night, the cycle turning.

The ember burns steady in the dark.

The last thing I hear before sleep takes me is the sound of Sera sharpening her sword at the edge of the firelight, and the slow, peaceful breathing of a god that isn't drowning anymore.
