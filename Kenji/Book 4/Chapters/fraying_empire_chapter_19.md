### Suite dawn

---

**Ashmere 35**, the luxury suite — six forty-five by the forge bell.

She was already moving when he opened his eyes. Not awake the way civilians wake — slow, stretching, bargaining with the ceiling about whether the day has earned them yet. Mursha woke the way soldiers wake: one breath asleep, the next breath inventory. Hands checking the space for threat, body rolling to the exit side of the bed before the brain had finished agreeing to participate.

He asked how she felt.

The answer came without performance. Sore — the good kind, the kind that meant the body had been asked to do something it had agreed to. Slept deep. Hungry. The Vigor strength was still wrong-strong — the word she used for the way her own arms surprised her, the way a grip that should have been firm was instead architectural. She said she liked it, and the tone said she meant it, and the bronze-gold ring at the edge of her dark brown eyes caught the mirror light when she turned her head and neither of them mentioned it.

He watched her dress.

She let him.

The binding-wrap first — the wide linen band that a woman with her frame wore under plate because armor does not forgive what it does not account for. She wound it with the mechanical rhythm of a task performed ten thousand times: cross, pull, tuck, smooth, cross again. Then the plates. Breastplate lifted from the chair where she had set it the night before — not dropped, set, because fighters who drop their armor once learn the dent and do not drop it twice. Shoulder spaulder, right side, the heavy segmented piece that covered the sword arm's range of motion without restricting it. Thigh plates. Bracers. The dark leather wraps at the forearms that kept sweat from making the grip a question.

Every strap and buckle went to its station in order. She did not look at the pieces. Her hands knew the map.

He watched from the featherbed with the sheets still warm and the room still smelling like the night's decisions, and what he saw was not a woman getting dressed but a professional resuming a shape that the world expected. The body underneath the armor was the one he had held. The body inside it was the one that walked through doors with a shield up and a spear-hand ready.

She caught him watching. The look she gave him was not shy, not performative — it was the look of someone who had decided that this particular audience had earned the show, and the decision sat comfortably in a face that did not owe comfort to anyone who had not paid for it.

The children conversation from the night before — his question, her terms, the steam that she had named as the reason she could not sign a life in the grip of whatever his presence did to her judgment — stayed where they had left it. Parked. Not abandoned. She did not reopen it, and he did not push, and the silence between them had the specific weight of a thing that would be spoken when the air was sober enough to hold it.

### Breakfast

---

The suite came with a service bell and the service bell came with Calista, and Calista came with a tray that smelled like the Brass Hitch's best argument for staying another night. Eggs, bread with butter that had been whipped instead of sliced, cured meat in thin strips, a pot of tea that was mostly tea and partly someone's opinion about cardamom, and two cups because the staff had counted the bodies in the room and drawn conclusions that were, for once, correct.

He handed Mursha her cloak. She took it. The gesture rhymed with the night before — a repeat of the first approach, the come-hither, the follow-me — but the context had changed, and gestures that begin as seduction settle into something else when the seduction has already been accepted.

Then he put four hundred gold on the table.

Not a pouch. Not a discreet slide across the wood. Four hundred gold, counted and stacked, the kind of sum that would buy the best armor and the best weapon Bronzebarrow had to offer with enough left over to purchase a house and never work another day in a relay town again. He said so. He said last night was not just about what happened between them — that it meant more than the physical. He said the coin was freedom, not a leash. Staying was her choice. The door was still hers the same way it had been hers the night before, when he had named it and she had chosen to remain.

Mursha looked at the gold the way a factor looks at a ledger entry that does not match the column it is in. The ledger brain came first — the part of her that had spent years counting freight weights and margin percentages and knew exactly what four hundred gold meant in a town where a relay guard earned eight silver a week. Then the contract brain. Then, underneath both, something that was not a brain at all.

She did not refuse. She did not accept with gratitude that would have made the money a debt. She framed it the way her contract mind needed it framed — honest pay, gear money, a professional allocation — and pocketed the coin. Not pride-buy. Not charity-take. The space between those two things was where she lived, and she had been living there long enough to furnish it.

Armor first, she said. Then the rack. She would test blades herself and she would not be rushed.

### Rumors

---

The Brass Hitch common room at mid-morning held the residue of the night before and the scaffolding of the day ahead — tables being wiped, a drunk asleep in the wrong chair, the barmaid Calista doing the kind of double duty that relay-town staff performed when the morning shift was short and the tips had been large enough to buy loyalty until noon.

He asked after leads. Rumors. Anything the desk had heard that a traveler with coin and curiosity might find useful.

Calista gave him the desk version — the curated gossip that relay inns traded in, where information was a commodity priced somewhere between ale and lodging. Nothing that cracked open the world. Nothing that closed a door. The kind of intelligence that existed so that guests felt they had gotten value, and innkeepers felt they had given nothing away.

He filed it. Moved on.

### Forge row

---

Bronzebarrow's smithing quarter ran along the south side of the relay yard — a street that was more furnace than street, where the air tasted like quench water and ambition and every third doorway breathed heat that turned the winter morning into something the body could not agree on. Marthel's shop sat at the end of the row: a narrow frontage with a wide yard behind it, the kind of operation that spent its money on anvils instead of signage.

Marthel was a dwarf who worked metal the way a surgeon works tissue — with economy, with opinion, and with the settled conviction that everything he touched would be better for the touching. His yard held the usual relay-town inventory: repaired plate, road-grade swords, the utilitarian output of a smith who served a transit economy where everything was on its way to somewhere else.

And on a peg near the back wall, half-buried under a road shield and a cracked helm, a piece of armor that did not belong in any relay forge in any town south of the capital.

**Luck (CHA):** d20 = 14 + 5 = **19 vs DC 15 — PASS.** The ember house rule fired and the eye landed where the eye should not have landed — on the peg, on the armor, on the specific way the scales caught lamplight with a color that steel does not produce and bronze does not attempt.

**Steel Sight** confirmed it. Dragon-scale laminate. Medium armor. The real thing — not an imitation, not a trophy piece, not the kind of "dragon-scale" that market stalls sold to adventurers who had never seen an actual dragon and would not survive the education. This was tier-grade material sitting on a junk peg in a relay-town forge because the smith who owned it either did not know what he had or knew exactly what he had and had decided the knowing was not worth the trouble.

He asked about the piece.

Marthel gave him the junk-peg price. Ten gold. The number was so wrong it had its own gravitational pull — the kind of price that meant the smith had written the armor off as unsellable exotica, a curiosity that took up peg space and collected road dust and would eventually go to a scrapper or a wall display in some merchant's trophy room.

**Persuasion:** d20 = 11 + 11 = **22 vs DC 16 — PASS.** The buyout conversation was not a negotiation. It was a confirmation. Ten gold, said once, agreed to once, paid once. Kenji did not haggle. Haggling at ten gold for dragon-scale would have been an insult to the concept of theft, and some thefts were better conducted with a straight face and exact change.

He paid. Ten gold. The scales went into his hands and the look on Marthel's face said the dwarf knew something had happened but could not name the shape of it — the expression of a craftsman who has just been reminded that he does not know everything about everything, and the reminder cost him ten gold in the wrong direction.

The cleaning took time. Marthel worked the armor with the professional attention of a smith who may have underpriced the material but would not under-serve the craft. Road grime came off. Old lacquer stripped. The scales emerged from underneath their disguise the way buried things emerge — slowly, and then all at once, and then with a shine that made the junk peg look like a punchline.

The fitting was for Mursha. He had her stand in the forge light while Marthel took measurements — shoulder span, waist, the way her arms moved when she raised a guard. The plates cut to clear her biceps and deltoids because armor that restricted a fighter's primary tools was not armor but a cage with straps. She stood still for the measuring the way she had stood still for nothing else all morning — with the patience of someone who understood that good gear was worth the minutes it cost.

He called her a bard tale. The compliment landed somewhere between flattery and field report, and Mursha took it the way she took most things — with a look that evaluated the sincerity before deciding whether to bank it.

Then he dropped to one knee.

Marthel's hammer stopped.

It was not a proposal. It was the retrieval posture for something stored low — the Bag of Holding, and from the Bag, a sword. Not the relay junk he had been browsing. Not a road-grade replacement. A greatsword that filled the forge with a silence that had weight, because the metal knew what it was even if the room did not.

The Oathbreaker. Corwyn's blade. The death knight's weapon, pulled from a battlefield in the Pallid March and carried since in extradimensional storage where its provenance could not leak into the world.

He asked Marthel to reshape it. Not reforge — reshape. Fit it for Mursha's grip and reach. Make it hers.

Marthel looked at the sword the way a surgeon looks at a wound that is above his certification. The dwarf's hands did not touch the blade. His eyes did the touching, and what they found made them stop.

**Persuasion:** d20 = 2 + 11 = **13 vs DC 18 — FAIL.** The refusal was not rude and not negotiable. Marthel said no with the finality of a craftsman who has identified a piece of work that he will not put his name on, and the reason lived somewhere between professional judgment and the instinct that certain metals carry histories that transfer to the hands that work them. He did not explain. He did not need to. The hammer that had stopped when the sword appeared did not resume until the sword was gone.

The Oathbreaker went back into the Bag of Holding. The forge air settled. Marthel's hands found their rhythm again, and the rhythm said: *I sold you a bargain and you showed me something I wish I had not seen, and we are even in the way that two men are even when both of them know the other is carrying more than he has named.*

He deflected with a ring joke — the knee, the proposal posture, the absurdity of the gesture — and the deflection worked because deflections work best when everyone in the room wants them to.

Mursha did not laugh.

**"Where'd you get that?"**

The question was plain. Not accusatory, not frightened, not the dramatic confrontation of a woman who has discovered her companion is carrying a death knight's weapon. Just a question, asked in the voice of someone who had spent her career around blades and knew that the one she had just seen did not belong to the man who held it and did not belong in a relay-town forge and did not belong anywhere that smelled like quench water and ten-gold armor deals.

He answered — or did not answer, or answered in the space between answers where ronin live when the truth is a weapon they are not ready to draw. The question stayed open. Mursha filed it the way she filed the clone flicker from the night before: in the box marked *things I have noticed and not yet decided what to do about,* which was getting full.

### EDGE stall

---

The weapon stall operated under a name that was either an acronym or a boast — **EDGE** — and ran out of a canvas-roofed lean-to against the relay yard's east wall. The inventory was relay-grade martial: swords built for road work, hammers built for things that did not stop when you asked, crossbows for people who wanted distance between themselves and the reason they needed a crossbow. The owner had the look of a man who had been a soldier once and had traded the career for a margin.

Mursha tested blades.

She tested them the way she did everything — with method, with patience, and with a professional contempt for anything that wasted her time. She picked up a longsword, checked the balance, turned it in the forge light, set it down. Picked up another. Checked the edge geometry. Tested the grip wrap. Set it down. A third sword came and went. A dagger stayed in her hand for four seconds longer than the others and then returned to the rack with a shake of her head that said *close, but the tang was lying about its temper.*

She chose a longsword that *read* honest on the rack — balance, geometry, grip: no theatrics, no fake runes, no vendor lies she could feel in the steel. **Steel Sight** and her own hands agreed: this was the line worth carrying. *(**DM mechanical canon:** the table runs this blade as a **masterwork longsword** — **+3**, **3d12** slashing; **two-handed:** add **Str** mod again to damage. See **`dm_rules_tracking.md`** § **Mursha — masterwork weapons**.)*

Then the hammer. She went through three before settling on an oval-faced war hammer — heavy, blunt, the kind of weapon that existed because some problems could not be solved by edges. No vampiric draw in the stall. **Steel Sight** read it clean: the best heavy piece on offer. *(**DM:** **masterwork warhammer** — **4d8** bludgeoning, **versatile**, **armor break**; **two-handed:** add **Str** mod again. Same rules file.)*

The question of who paid arrived at the counter the way financial questions always arrive — with a silence that had a shape and two people standing in it.

He paid.

**125 GP** — the longsword at roughly seventy, the war hammer at roughly fifty-five. He paid from his own coin, not from the four hundred he had given her that morning, because the gift was the gift and the gear was the gear and the distinction mattered in the economy of a relationship that was still being denominated.

Mursha let him pay. The letting was not passive — it was a decision, made in the same contract-mind space where she had pocketed the four hundred gold that morning, and the decision said: *this is not charity and this is not purchase and the man who is paying knows the difference because he named it at breakfast and has not un-named it since.*

The weapons went to her belt and her back. The longsword sat on her left hip where the Iron Mule spear had lived for years — same draw angle, different weight, the body adjusting to the new geometry with the automatic fluency of a woman who had carried enough weapons to know that the first hour with a new blade was a conversation and the second hour was a marriage. The war hammer rode her right hip in a leather frog that the stall owner threw in because he recognized a customer who would come back and a companion who would make her come back, and free leather was cheaper than lost futures.

She stood in the relay-yard light with dragon-scale armor that had cost ten gold and a sword and hammer she had chosen like contracts — weight-tested, lie-tested — and the bronze-gold ring in her eyes caught the afternoon sun, and the half-orc who had been an Iron Mule freight guard until yesterday afternoon was not that woman anymore and had not yet decided who she was instead, and the ronin who had bought her freedom and stolen her employer's strongbox and dropped to one knee in a forge with a dead knight's sword was standing beside her with nine hundred and seventeen gold in his purse and a mask on his face and the knowledge that he had given her everything except the truth about the flicker and the sword and the things he carried in a bag that was bigger on the inside than it was on the outside.

Ashmere 35. Day 257. Mid-afternoon. Bronzebarrow.

The fitting was done.
