### I. The Dead Village

---

The portal opened three miles from the perimeter and Kenji walked the rest.

Not Wind Step. Not stealth. Walking — boots on corrupted soil, hat on, mask on, nodachi across his back, the morning light of Ashmere 40 already thin and gray this far south. Living Ground read the terrain change at the border: healthy root systems giving way to something that wasn't dead so much as *claimed*. Every tree, every stone, every grave with a name worn off it answered to the same signal. Thirty-two miles of organized silence.

She was waiting in the village square. Human form — the Lady, not the Lych. Tall, full-figured, deep brown skin catching what pallid light filtered through the overcast. Dark robe, simple, the kind of thing you wear at home when the only visitors are the dead. Hazel eyes, warm and steady and aimed directly at him from the moment he cleared the tree line.

Corwyn stood at the edge of the square. One good arm. No sword — Oathbreaker was lodged in a dwarven forge seam four hundred miles north. The dead knight watched the living man cross the cobblestones with the expression of a sentry who already knows the war is lost.

Kenji sat on the low stone wall at the square's edge. He took off the hat. The mask. Set them beside him. Looked at the most dangerous woman he'd ever visited on purpose and said something about hot air and wind.

She didn't laugh. She didn't need to. The conversation had started the moment he chose to walk instead of fly.

---

### II. The Hollowing and the Name

---

He asked about the rift in Kharn.

Nyx told him what she knew — not everything, but enough to confirm what the mountain hadn't said out loud. The Hollowing was an appetite, not a wound. The seam Kenji had pinned in the forge was a symptom of something eating its way through the ley substrate, and the dwarves were treating it like structural damage because their filing system didn't have a category for *hunger*. She'd felt it through the root network — a pull, distant but persistent, like a tide that only moved in one direction.

She couldn't close it. Neither could the dwarves, not with committees and ward-stone. The appetite had to be satisfied or starved, and starving it required understanding what it was eating, which required getting closer to the source than anyone with a heartbeat should want to be.

Kenji filed the information. Then he relayed it — not out loud, not through any channel Nyx could intercept. Through the bond. Through Solveth, through Frost Fang, through the thin connection that ran from his chest to a divine blade wedged in dwarven stone four hundred miles north. The understanding would arrive as impression, not transcript. Mursha would feel it. She would know what to do with it.

The conversation turned personal. She knew too much about him — the ronin, the disguise, the forge, the escape. She'd done research. He called her on it.

She smiled. Not the predator's smile. The one that said *of course I researched you; you walked into my territory and sat on my wall.*

He asked for her name. Her real name. Not the Lady, not the Lych — the name she was born with, the one that lived underneath six centuries of territorial claim and organized death.

The gate had conditions. The tracker said neutral-or-higher disposition at the moment of meeting. Kenji had arrived non-hostile. He'd sat on a wall. He'd made jokes about dwarves being too short to see the point.

"Nyx," she said.

One syllable. The only name she'd spoken aloud to a living person since before the Coalition existed. She gave it to him the way you hand someone a key — not because they earned it, but because the lock was already turning.

### III. Jeet Kune Do

---

He challenged her to a spar and then did something no one in six centuries had done in her presence: he put the weapon down.

The nodachi went on the cobblestones. The hakama top came off. The hat, the mask, the ronin silhouette — all of it stripped away until what remained was a shirtless man with a strength modifier of sixteen and the stance of a dead martial artist from a world that didn't exist in this one.

Hop. Hop. Weight on the balls of the feet. Hands floating at center mass. Nose rub with the back of the thumb.

*"Wataaah—"*

Corwyn sat on the bench as referee. The root network silenced. The six hundred and twelve frozen. Arena by consensus.

Kenji's passive reads fired the moment she moved. Captain's Read registered her threat tier past the useful part of the scale — not a creature, an *architecture*. Bond-Form Sight caught six hundred and twelve threads radiating from her sternum, each one a leash to a bound soul, and beneath them four heavier cables looping inward — three clean, one frayed, parasitized, leaking energy she either hadn't noticed or had chosen to tolerate. God Sight found the void beacon — a tower of nothing carved deliberately out of reality, the opposite side of the ember's coin. Living Ground, degraded at sixty feet in corrupted terrain, read her heartbeat: one every eleven seconds. Real. Alive.

She moved first. Ley-speed — not fast the way monks are fast, fast the way distance stops existing. An open palm placed three inches from his solar plexus as a statement of territorial claim.

He answered with his fists.

---

**Round 1** was a lesson no one in the Pallid March had received before.

Her rake landed — a grab at his neck, ley-fast, +19, contact confirmed. But Kenji's counter punches fired before the follow-up. The first caught her under the ribs: 4d8 plus sixteen strength, forty-three bludgeoning, the sound of a siege engine hitting a cathedral wall. She took it. Her eyes went wide — not with pain, with *surprise*.

The second counter hit her solar plexus. Forty-four bludgeoning. Her CON save came up a seven. The stun landed. Her arms dropped. Her turn ended before it began.

Then Kenji performed.

The showboat opened with a spin — a dancer's spin, one hand trailing — and when he came out of it the air around his fists vibrated with something between physical and divine. Spirit aura. Fifteen percent of her total vitality loaded into every strike. Performance check: thirty vs eighteen. The aura locked.

Three kicks. One punch. All hits. Spirit damage on every contact — sixty-three points per strike on top of the physical, bypassing armor, resonating through the binding architecture itself. Kick three took her legs: roundhouse to the standing thigh, DEX save twenty-two vs twenty-three. One point short. She went down on one knee.

The punch drove her flat. She passed the stun save but the damage total was catastrophic: four hundred and eighty-two points across six strikes, counters and attacks combined.

Nyx hit the cobblestones. KO save: eleven plus twelve, twenty-three vs twenty-three. Held by zero.

She pushed herself up on one elbow. Hair in her face. Hazel eyes finding him through the curtain of it.

*"...Again,"* she said.

---

**Round 2** was worse.

Kenji didn't attack. He performed — both checks, start and end — and told her to show him offense. Spirit aura active. Showboat buffs cycling. Seventy-five percent dodge chance.

Nyx came at him with everything she had left. Three attacks into disadvantage: a ley-rake that rolled a natural one, a palm strike that should have hit but Kenji spun off the line, and a root-thread lash through the cobblestones that finally caught his ankle. Twenty-two force-necrotic. The only damage she landed in two rounds.

His counter punches answered. The first stunned her — CON save three plus twelve, fifteen vs twenty-three, failed by eight. The second hit her below the KO threshold.

KO save: two plus twelve, fourteen vs twenty-three. Failed by nine.

Lady Nyx — Level 32, Living Lich, six hundred and twelve undead at her command — dropped unconscious on the cobblestones of her own village, knocked out by a man who wasn't using his main attacks, his weapon, or his shirt.

The six hundred and twelve screamed through the root network. Corwyn was on his feet with his hand closing on empty air where Oathbreaker should have been. The perimeter shivered.

Kenji hopped twice. Rubbed his nose.

### IV. What the Fists Read

---

He told her everything.

While she pushed herself to standing in the aftermath of Round 1, he spoke — casual, precise, the voice of a man reading a wine label at a dinner party.

Diagnostic Touch had fired on every contact. His fists had read her biology the way a physician reads a chart, and now he narrated the results to the empty square.

One-percent metabolism. Eleven-second heartbeat, clean rhythm, deliberately throttled. Bone density rewritten from the inside — reinforced, load-bearing walls that weren't in the original blueprint. Muscle tissue alive but frozen, cellular turnover near zero, preserved like a painting under glass. The ley-pool not *in* her but *of* her — blood and magic sharing the same circulatory system, every capillary a ley-line, the heart beating once every eleven seconds because the network did the rest.

And behind the heart, deeper than biology built, a structure with no biological name. The anchor. The thing that kept the whole system from tipping into undeath. The place where the four cables terminated.

He told her the cracked rib was real. He told her the bruises were real. He told her she was alive.

She stood very still for thirty-three seconds and then said: *"You are the most dangerous thing that has ever touched me. And I do not say that because of the rib."*

No one had ever read her architecture to her face and lived to narrate it. The last diviner who tried was now a paperweight.

### V. Five Kingdoms

---

After Round 2, after the KO, after Kenji squatted beside her and touched her face and counted ten seconds and healed her to full with a thread of creation energy that didn't announce itself to the ley-network, she answered his question.

Not the question she wanted him to ask. Not *why did you heal me*. He asked about kingdoms.

The Coalition — thirteen portal-cities, committee governance, sincere and undertrained. The dwarves — Kharn-Dural, committees about committees, slow but inevitable. The Iron Horde — orcs, sixty thousand, a warlord who conquered populations like a child collecting stones. The Red Court — vampires, political, institutional infection, a kingdom that had been running for a decade before anyone noticed the night council was making policy. The Wyrms — a mated pair beneath the southern range, geologically ancient, not yet awake. *Yet.*

Five powers. Six counting her. Kenji had walked through three in a single season.

She asked why he wanted to know. He didn't answer the question she asked.

He answered a different one.

### VI. The Proposal

---

"I want to take you on as one of my mates and partners."

The words came out the way Kenji's words always came out — not rehearsed, not diplomatic, not wrapped in the language of courts and contracts. Direct. The verbal equivalent of a straight right to the solar plexus.

He wanted her as an advisor. He wanted a title — Anku Nyx, coined on the spot, representing his connection to her and her realm. He wanted Corwyn to rebuild the village for the living: inns, a smithy, stores, a place where those who wanted the undead aesthetic could practice dark magic under whatever laws they set together.

He did not want the March to spread. He did not want a world full of death.

Nyx didn't take his hand. Not yet.

She gave him the math. Close to Death: one percent of his vitality per second, no save, no resistance. Every man she'd taken to bed had died in it. Corwyn lasted seventy-two hours. He was standing behind Kenji. Kenji could ask him how it went.

She tested the word *serve*. It didn't fit. She hadn't served anything since 1042.

She tested the word *stop*. Six hundred years of doctrine, of slow tide, of patient territorial claim — and he wanted her to hold the line.

She tested the word *Corwyn*. The man who loved her. The man Kenji had just told to stay behind and build inns while the living walked away with his Lady.

Corwyn made a sound. Not a word. The sound of metal on bone and something underneath that used to be a voice.

She turned back to Kenji.

*"I am not saying yes. I am not saying no."*

She took his hand and pulled herself up. Not because she needed it. Because accepting the hand meant something and she chose to mean it.

Three conditions.

One: Corwyn's chain was hers. Kenji could ask her, and she would ask him. Not the other direction.

Two: the March held for now. If the world gave her a reason — Coalition collapse, Horde incursion, the Wyrms — she would act. And Kenji would stand beside her, not in front of her.

Three: when they did this — and they would, because neither of them came here to leave empty-handed — he would look her in the eyes the entire time. She had watched every man she'd taken die with his eyes shut. She wanted to see what it looked like when one of them lived.

*"Those are my terms, Anku Nyx. Do we have a deal?"*

---

### VII. We Do Not Die

---

"From here on out, Nyx — call me Anku Nyx. For you will become my other half, and we do not die."

The IP save at the sixty-minute mark had been a natural one. The save at seventy-five was an eighteen — the ley-pool stasis clamping down hard, holding the line, freezing what Stack 2 was trying to build. She was still at Moderate Attraction. Still functional. Still calculating.

But the calculation was changing.

She placed her palm flat against his bare chest. Over the sternum. Over the place where the ember banked low and quiet. The void-signal that was her entire being recognized the creation furnace behind his ribs the way night recognizes dawn — opposite, equal, unable to look away.

She felt the regeneration through her palm. Three hundred and sixty-eight hit points per turn, every turn, every six seconds, a body that refused the concept of *broken*. She ran the math against Close to Death — one percent per second, three point six eight damage against sixty-one points of healing per second — and for the first time in six centuries the equation came out *wrong*.

He would survive.

The silence held for one eleven-second heartbeat.

Corwyn stood at the edge of the square. One good arm. No sword. Dead eyes watching the woman he died for place her hand on the chest of the man who was replacing him. Not replacing. That was the wrong word. There was no word for what was happening to Corwyn. There was only the sound a faith makes when it realizes the god it was pointed at has turned to face the sun.

Nyx did not step back.

The title hung in the air between them: *Anku Nyx*. His name for himself that was also her name. A claim and a bridge and a declaration of intent, coined in thirty seconds by a shirtless man who had just knocked out a Living Lich with his bare hands and then healed her and asked about real estate.

The dead village held its breath. The six hundred and twelve waited. The root network that spanned thirty-two miles of organized death held perfectly, absolutely still.

The woman who had not been surprised by another person in longer than most kingdoms had existed looked up at the man who had surprised her three times in one morning — with his fists, with his mercy, with his mouth — and the expression on her face was not the predator's calculation, not the seductress's mask, not the aristocrat's measured composure.

It was the face of a woman standing at the edge of something she hadn't known was possible.

"We do not die," she repeated. Testing it. Finding it held.

Her hand stayed on his chest.
