### Two things at once

---

The mountain asked one question at every gate: *who are you and what are you carrying?*

Mursha had two answers, and neither was the one the stamps wanted.

First was the Haldra-line liability — names on a manifest, blood inside skin, the factor contract that said Mursha of Iron Mule had vouched for bodies and their cargo, and if a body leaked or a crate went missing between checkpoints the ink on the liability page would turn into a chain around her wrist. Professional. Clean. The kind of problem a guard solved by counting heads and checking seals and walking the train geometry the way she had walked a hundred train geometries since the first contract that paid her enough to eat.

Second was the quarantine truth — the story official Kharn had wrapped in *malfunction* and stamp delays and the particular administrative silence that meant *we will tell you what we want you to know and the rest stays in the stone*. Her old Mule standing was leverage. Not magic, not social charm, not the kind of influence that came from a name or a title — the leverage of a woman who had moved cargo through dwarven checkpoints long enough that the clerks recognized her handwriting and the factors remembered her face. Close enough that when the lie broke, they would not be reading it secondhand from a tavern drunk.

She told Kenji both objectives the way she told him anything operational: flat, sequential, structured for a man who processed information through action rather than analysis. Contract and truth were the same path. He could take the romantic version or the true one, and if he could not tell which was which, that was his problem.

The queue ahead of them breathed the slow breath of bureaucracy eating time. Dwarf stone carried the sound of stamps falling three corridors away — a rhythm that measured the mountain's patience in thocks per minute. Mursha settled into her queue posture: weight balanced, warhammer accessible, eyes doing the work her voice would not.

### Coin, palm, pass

---

Noise carried in Kharn stone the way light carried in water — further than you expected, and to ears you did not choose.

A coin rolled. Silver on granite, a clear high note that cut through the stamp-and-shuffle ambience of the queue line and pulled dwarf attention sideways like a compass needle swinging toward metal. Not a mistake — a sound placed with the precision of a thrown knife, landing exactly where it needed to land, pulling exactly the eyes it needed to pull.

The young dwarf traveler was three bodies ahead in the queue — short-cloak, travel dust in her beard braids, the compact build of a person who walked roads rather than worked forges. Her perception was good for a civilian. It was not good enough for a man who had been stealing things since before most of the people in this corridor were born.

Sleight of Hand. DEX plus five, advantage from the distraction and the stone's acoustic generosity. Against the traveler's Perception — plus two, disadvantage because her ears were still hooked on the coin's trajectory, her attention committed to a direction that was not the direction that mattered. Sixteen versus four, margin twelve.

Weight moved between bodies — seamless if the hands are good enough, invisible if the hands are better than good. A belt settled. A strap adjusted. The geometry of the traveler's kit shifted by an amount that was not nothing but was less than the threshold of notice for a woman whose attention was elsewhere and whose hands had not been trained to feel what Kenji's hands had been trained to move.

She never looked at his hands. The coin's note died against the far wall. The queue shuffled forward.

Mursha's elbow touched his ribs once — contact brief enough to be accidental, positioned precisely enough to not be. *Finish or abort. Don't hover.* She had not seen the sleight. She had seen the stillness before it, and the stillness was a tell she had learned to read the way a pickpocket learns to read the moment before a crowd shifts: something was about to happen, or something had just happened, and the difference was academic.

The first stamp fell — *thock*. An old dwarf at a stone desk, his hand moving with the mechanical indifference of a man who had stamped ten thousand manifests and would stamp ten thousand more. He did not look up. He did not need to. The paperwork was clean. The faces matched the names. The seals had been verified at the outer checkpoint and the inner checkpoint and the checkpoint before the checkpoint, and by the time a body reached this desk the desk had already decided it was going to say yes.

They walked through clean.

Behind them, the queue reshuffled. Somewhere in the press, a short-cloak traveler carried weight that had not been hers when she joined the line — extradimensional kit repositioned from a man who could not afford inspection to a woman who had no reason to expect one. A moral problem disguised as a logistical solution. A time bomb ticking in someone else's luggage.

Mursha did not look back. *Vol-sha* — lucky. Don't make a habit.

### Manifest Revision II

---

The second wedge wore meaner geometry.

Manifest Revision II was a personal lane — single file, one body at a time, the corridor narrowing to a width that put you close enough to the clerks that they could smell what you had eaten for breakfast and read the tension in your jaw. Resonance spot-check language on the wall signs warned that forge-adjacent kit could be opened on ambient unease — not probable cause, not suspicion, unease. The dwarves had given their inspectors permission to act on feeling, and feeling in a mountain with a sick forge was a loaded weapon.

Two clerks worked the station. The older one ran tally — manifest pages cross-referenced against checkpoint stamps, cargo weights verified against the numbers that previous desks had signed off on, the kind of arithmetic that existed to catch the discrepancy between what people said they were carrying and what they were actually carrying. He worked with the unhurried competence of a man who had been doing this job long enough to find it boring and long enough to know that boredom was when mistakes happened.

The younger one was forge-sight.

Mursha recognized the type before she recognized the face — compact stillness, eyes that moved independently of the head, the particular attention of a person trained to see things that other people were trained to ignore. He remembered crowds the way pickpockets remembered wallets: not the mass but the individual, not the face but the anomaly, the thing that did not match the pattern that experience had taught him to expect. She had seen him at the outer checkpoint. He had seen her. More importantly, he had seen the ronin's satchel and his attention had stayed on it half a beat longer than professional interest required.

The slate came up — dwarf hand, coalition marginalia in the margins, amendment flags and delay codes and the administrative language of a mountain processing its own anxiety through paperwork. The slate said everything the queue had said but in writing, and writing was more dangerous than speech because writing could be read again.

Kenji read every line.

He did it the way a man reads a menu in a language he has spoken since childhood — without effort, without translation, without the particular pause that separates literacy from comprehension. His eyes moved across the angular dwarven letterforms and the coalition shorthand with the fluency of someone who had been reading this script his entire life, and the fluency was wrong. Six weeks ago this man could not read. Six weeks ago this man had hired a halfling scribe to read his own contract because the letters were shapes that did not resolve into meaning. Now he was parsing amendment flags in dwarf administrative shorthand at a speed that would have impressed a factor clerk.

Scribe's Eye did not care about cover. Scribe's Eye did what Tamsin's bond had wired it to do: it read.

He gave the room nothing to steal — bored sell-sword eyes behind the mask, the practiced indifference of a man who was looking at shapes he could not understand and waiting for someone else to explain them. The performance was competent. The performance was also a beat too late, because the reading had already happened, and anyone watching his eyes rather than his face would have seen them track the text with the lateral precision of a literate person.

Mursha laid Haldra's seals flat on the clerk's stone. The amendment Kenji could parse without speaking: delay flag cleared Tier I. Good news, delivered silent, requiring no response from the man who was not supposed to be able to read it.

The forge-sight clerk spoke without looking up from his own notes. Palms up. Slow.

Kenji obeyed. Hands open, fingers spread, the display of honesty that checkpoint cultures had been demanding since the first civilization decided that the space between a man's fingers was a place where weapons could hide. Straps, empty belt lines, the shape of honest travel — or honest enough for this beat, in this corridor, under the eyes of a clerk who was looking for something specific and had not found it yet.

The older clerk checked the stamps. The younger one checked the man.

A beat passed. Two. The forge-sight dwarf tilted his chin — note resolved. The tilt was not satisfaction and it was not dismissal. It was the bookmark of a man who had filed something for later and was letting you pass because the evidence was not yet sufficient for what he wanted to do with it. *Pass with your guard. Next lane: cargo reconciliation. Don't drift.*

Stamp — *thock*.

Mursha waited until they were three paces past the desk and the corridor had widened enough to walk abreast before she spoke. Her voice was pitched below the stone's carry — the register she used when the words were for one person and the walls were for everyone.

*Krath* — read like that again in public, make it look like you're counting boot laces.

The instruction was precise. The orcish was punctuation, not the sentence — a single guttural syllable that carried frustration the way a slap carries emphasis, followed by English that stood on its own and told him exactly what to do differently. She had seen the read. She had seen the speed. She understood, without needing to understand how, that something had changed in his literacy since the outer checkpoint, and the change was visible to anyone paying attention, and the forge-sight clerk was a man who paid attention the way Mursha paid attention: professionally, persistently, and with a memory that did not forgive.

### Lip

---

They cleared the second checkpoint into deeper air.

The harmonic wrongness was under the skin of the mountain now — not a sound Mursha could name, not a vibration she could locate, but a sensation that lived in the space between hearing and feeling — a cracked tang humming through the bones of a hand that had not yet looked at the blade. The stone was humming a note that was not the note it was supposed to hum, and Mursha's teeth knew it before her ears did.

The factor train ghosted their pace one corridor over — wax, wheel, the tube still someone else's problem until it wasn't. She could hear the axles through the stone, the familiar rhythm of cargo in transit, the sound that had been her working world for years. It was the same sound and it was not. The mountain was adding a harmonic to it that did not belong, a low undertone that turned the wheels' rhythm into something that sounded less like commerce and more like a pulse.

Ahead, the corridor opened toward cargo reconciliation — weights, seals, the kind of honesty that cost money. The last clean checkpoint before the geometry of Kharn's interior decided whether visitors went deeper or stopped. Mursha adjusted her pace. Kenji matched it. Behind them, the forge-sight clerk's attention was filed and would stay filed, and ahead of them the mountain was sick in a direction that stamps could not fix.

She did not slow down. Slowing down invited questions, and questions in stone became answers she could not take back.
