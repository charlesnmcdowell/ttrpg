# Book 4 — Fraying Empire
## Chapter 20: The Western Cut

---

### The lead

---

**Ashmere 35**, the Brass Hitch — late afternoon turning toward evening.

They came back to the common room because the common room was where relay-town intelligence lived, and intelligence improved with a second purchase. The first visit had given him desk gossip — curated, safe, the kind of information that existed so guests felt they had gotten value. The second visit, with five silver on the bar and Mursha in new dragon-scale armor drawing the kind of looks that new armor always drew in a town where most armor was old, gave him something better.

A teamster. Unnamed, road-worn, the creases in his cuffs holding the specific dirt of a man who loaded wagons for a living and worried about the wagons he had not seen come back. He had a story about a pack-train on the western cut — sealed manifests, coalition wax on the crates, days overdue. The kind of late that stopped being a scheduling problem and started being a question that the yard had been asking itself quietly and had not yet asked loudly because asking loudly meant admitting the answer.

The ronin listened. Filed the lead. Left five silver on the table because ale-bought intelligence was cheaper than coin-bought intelligence and the teamster did not seem to know the difference.

The Oathbreaker question from the forge that morning — *Where'd you get that?* — had not been reopened. The clone flicker from the night before had not been named. Mursha sat beside him with a longsword on her hip where a spear had lived for years and drank from a dark wood tankard with iron bands and quartered the room with the professional patience of a woman who was learning a new shape and had not yet decided whether the new shape was a choice or a costume.

### Western cut

---

They left Bronzebarrow through the western turnout at five o'clock by the forge bell.

The road changed character within a quarter mile. The main relay corridor was wide, graded, built for ox-trains and the commerce that justified their upkeep. The western cut was a narrower trace — wheel ruts pressed into clay that had been pressed into clay so many times the road had stopped pretending to be maintained and accepted its actual status as a suggestion. Turnout ruts branched from the main line into something the forest had been quietly reclaiming for years, and the forest was winning.

He read the trail. Ruts deep enough for loaded wagons. Recent enough that rain had not finished filling them. A pack-train had come through within the last week, loaded heavy, moving west, and had not come back.

Mursha walked beside him. Not behind — beside. The positioning said what it said about how she understood the arrangement: not employer and guard, not officer and enlisted, but two people walking the same road in the same direction because neither had told the other to stop. The war hammer rode her right hip in the leather frog the EDGE stall owner had thrown in for free, and the dragon-scale caught the last of the daylight with a color that said *I remember being expensive* to anyone who knew what they were looking at.

The trail narrowed. The light thinned toward the blue-grey that western cuts wore after sunset in late Ashmere, when the trees ate the horizon and the sky became a rumor. They walked for three hours without speaking because some hours do not need speech, and the silence between two people who have been intimate and are now walking into possible violence together is a different silence than any other kind.

---

### Recon

---

He felt it before he saw it.

**Living Ground** read the slope at sixty feet and found the earth wrong. Not dead — disturbed. The specific signature of ground that had been reshaped by deliberate hands: a slide, an excavation, a ridgeline that had been dug into by people who understood that the high ground above a switchback was not geography but real estate. Wrong earth. The kind of wrong that meant someone had been preparing a position, and positions prepared above narrow trails served one purpose.

**God Sight** confirmed the geometry. Eighty feet of darkvision cut through the dusk and found the rim — a ridge above a box switchback where the trail doubled back on itself in a hairpin that any loaded wagon would have to take at walking pace. Lanterns below. Small, hooded, the kind of light that people used when they wanted to see without announcing that they were seeing, except the discipline had softened over two days of waiting and the hoods had started to drift.

**Trade Warmth** read the cluster. Living signatures packed into the switchback below in the tight pattern of circled wagons — too many for a camp, too organized for refugees, and exactly right for a convoy that had pulled into defensive formation and stayed there because the alternative was worse. Above them, spaced along the rim in the deliberate spacing of an ambush line, warmer signatures that were not cold because they had been sitting in position long enough for the ground to learn their temperature.

A siege. Two days old, by the feel of the earth and the patience of the rim positions. A pack-train boxed into a switchback by bandits who held the high ground and the numbers and the luxury of time.

**Wind Step** twice. Two charges spent — silent, trackless, the wuxia ghost-run along the ridgeline that disturbed neither air nor attention. Two passes from two angles. The picture assembled: coalition-sealed crates visible in the wagon circle below, coalition markings that meant this freight had paper behind it and the paper had authority. The bandits on the rim had the look of men who knew what they had cornered and were negotiating among themselves about how much of it they could carry and how long the crew below could hold before the negotiation became unnecessary.

**Wind Step 3/5** remaining.

He came back to Mursha. The tactical conversation was short because tactical conversations between people who fight together are shorter and more honest than conversations between people who sleep together, and right now they were both.

### The ridge

---

They split the flanks.

Mursha took the left because the left was wider and the terrain favored a fighter who could hold ground and generate bodies. She went up the approach in dragon-scale that Marthel had cleaned and fitted that morning, with a war hammer on one hip and a longsword on the other, and the Vigor-enhanced stats that turned her baseline into something the bandits had not been briefed on.

Kenji took the right because the right was narrow and the narrow favored a single blade and the kind of speed that turned a ridgeline into a hallway with one exit.

The fight started the way ridge fights start — with the sound of certainty ending. Two days of siege had given the bandits ownership of the mathematics: they had the height, the numbers, the patience. The mathematics changed when a half-orc woman with wrong-strong arms came up the left approach carrying weapons she had owned for less than eight hours and an attitude that suggested the weapons had been waiting for her longer than that.

Mursha's hammer found its purpose. The oval face met shields and the shields discovered what **armor break** sounded like in practice — metal conceding to mass, guards collapsing inward under force that was not human-baseline because the arm behind it was running on Lover's Vigor and the Vigor did not care what the bandits had expected. Multiple went down on the left. She fought with method, not fury — each swing placed where a swing needed to go, each step controlled, the left flank folding under the weight of a professional who had carried a spear for years and had just discovered that a hammer said the same things in a language that left bigger marks. **Bloodlust** stacked on killing blows — the Soul Nexus bond ability firing its +25% per kill into a body already carrying +50% from Vigor, and the numbers stopped pretending to be reasonable and simply became what they were.

On the right, the nodachi came out of the draw.

**Iaido** — the guaranteed crit on the drawn cut, spent for the encounter. **0/1.** The red-black steel cleared the scabbard in one motion and the motion found a knife bandit on the rim and the knife bandit found something he had not been prepared to find, which was the specific mathematics of a 6d12 critical multiplied by a blade that carried a 25% chance of making the target stop existing. The percentage fired. The bandit was there and then the bandit was not — the absence that vaporization left behind, where the air closed over a space that a person had occupied and the ground where they had stood held no opinion about what had happened because what had happened was faster than opinion.

The rim broke. The bandits who had spent two days believing they were predators learned that belief is not a load-bearing material, and the crew in the switchback below heard a siege ending from the wrong direction and looked up at a ridgeline that no longer belonged to the people who had been starving them.

---

### The witness

---

The fight produced a prisoner. The ridge lieutenant — the one who had held the rim, organized the patience, maintained the two-day discipline that a siege required. Injured. Alive. The kind of alive that came with conditions attached.

Kenji was already invisible.

The **Smoke-Invis-Clone** charge had fired during the fight — Greater Invisibility on the real Kenji, the illusion double walking the perimeter in the masked ronin's easy stride, the smoke residue long since thinned into cold air. The survivors in the switchback below saw a swordsman checking the rim. They did not see the man who was actually checking the rim, because the man who was actually checking the rim did not want to be seen, and at Level 35 the difference between wanting and having was mechanical, not aspirational.

Mursha took the lieutenant aside. She took him into the dark between the ridge and the tree line where the wagon fires did not reach, and the taking had a quality that was not tactical in the way that Coalition officers understood tactical. It was older. Orcish. The part of her bloodline that her contract mind did not put on the ledger and her fighter habits did not include in the morning equipment check.

The **Power of P** was not an interrogation. It was the Barbarian Ways — the Soul Nexus bond perk that had replaced the ability she carried before with something that came from deeper in her ancestry than spear drills or freight manifests. Male humanoids could not lie to her during the act. The supernatural lock was absolute — auto-truth, no semantic dodge, the subject's mouth answering in the order she demanded because the compulsion did not negotiate.

The lieutenant's **CON save failed.** Not cleanly. The reroll fired — Vigor and Bloodlust stacking into a **DC of 29**, disadvantage from his injuries compounding with the mathematics of a wounded man's constitution against a half-orc woman running on supernatural strength and the killing-high of a battle that had ended minutes ago. The numbers did not favor him. They were not designed to.

The intel came out in the order she required: handoff points, factor names, Bronzebarrow leak threads. The operational anatomy of a ridge-bandit cell that had been fed intelligence by someone who knew which pack-trains carried coalition seals and which routes those trains took. The kind of intelligence that would mean something in Ch21 and would travel as information — clean, sourceless — because the source would require an explanation that Mursha was not prepared to give.

The **+2 STR permanent** settled into her baseline without ceremony. Soul Nexus gains did not announce themselves — a number changed in a body that was already carrying numbers it did not fully understand. **STR 22.** Drawn from the lieutenant's highest stat at scene start. Once per unique individual. Irreversible.

She finished. She adjusted her cloak. She came back toward the wagon circle with the professional composure of a woman who had just done something she would not discuss and had done it well.

Kenji watched from thirty feet away, invisible, silent, and carrying a new piece of knowledge that she did not know he carried. The Power of P joined the list — the clone flicker she had seen at the Brass Hitch, the Oathbreaker she had asked about at the forge, and now this: the Barbarian Ways she hid because hiding it was the only way she knew to keep the human side of her face pointed forward. She would make excuses if asked. Shame, not betrayal — the character tracker had it right. She was not deceiving him out of malice but out of the specific embarrassment of a half-orc woman whose orcish inheritance did things that her contract mind could not file under any heading that looked professional.

He had seen it. He did not tell her. The secret filed alongside everything else he had filed that day, and the filing was quiet, and the quiet had a weight he would carry until he decided what to do with it.

### Exit

---

The survivors stabilized. The wagon circle held its shape in the late-evening cold while the ridge wind thinned toward stars and the pack-train crew began the exhausted process of understanding that the siege was over. Two days of fear does not evaporate — it leaks, slowly, and the leaking looked like people sitting very still and staring at their hands because their hands were the only thing that had not tried to kill them recently.

The illusion double continued its work. The masked ronin — the visible version, the one the survivors believed in — walked the perimeter of the wagon circle in the easy stride of a man doing a security sweep. Checked the rim. Checked the approaches. Circled a cart, passed behind a wagon, moved out of the survivors' line of sight.

The gap was the seam. Kenji stepped into the space the double occupied. The double dissolved. Greater Invisibility dropped. The Smoke-Invis-Clone charge ended — **2/3 remaining.** A masked ronin walked out from behind the wagon and the transition was seamless because the transition was engineered to be seamless, and the only person who might have noticed was Mursha, and Mursha was managing her own return to the firelight and had her own composure to reassemble before she could spare attention for his.

She did not know about the overwatch. She did not know he had been invisible on the ridge while she broke the left flank. She did not know he had watched from thirty feet while the lieutenant's constitution failed against a DC that was built to break it. She knew the clone trick existed — the flicker from the Brass Hitch had been filed, not confronted — but the specifics of tonight's deployment were his.

They sat beside a fire that someone else had built. The pack-train was saved. The intel was secured. The western cut was open. The stars came through cold and clear above a relay corridor that smelled like wagon smoke and the end of something, and the day that had started in a luxury suite with breakfast and four hundred gold ended in a switchback with blood on her hammer and a new secret between them that only one of them knew was a secret.

Three secrets now. The flicker. The sword. The watch.

She carried two. He carried all three.

Day 257. Ashmere 35. Late evening. Western cut.
