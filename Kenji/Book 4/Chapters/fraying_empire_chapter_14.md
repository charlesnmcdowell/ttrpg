# Fraying Empire — Chapter 14: *Oathbreaker*

---

### Cold Signals

---

The junction stayed dark for three minutes after the courier disappeared south. Three minutes of root trunks pulsing nothing, mist curling through dead wood, and three people standing in a clearing that had just swallowed a man on a leash.

Jostin broke the silence first. Not with words — with his journal. Pen moving in tight, controlled strokes. Cataloguing. Filing. The scout's version of breathing exercises.

Brynn watched south. Shield up. Amber-gold eyes scanning the tree line where the courier had slid into the mist like something reeled in on a wire.

The ronin looked at his people, then looked at the road, and made the calculation that commanders make when the enemy knows they're coming and the only advantage left is geometry.

He split the party.

Not a retreat split — a *tactical* split. Jostin and Brynn would leave first, moving east off the crown onto the verge, then north along the birch line toward the waypoint he'd chalked at mile sixteen-point-nine in camp margin code. Their job was signal and shelter: Brynn's ring-fire when she had line of sight to the junction, Jostin's smaller burns answering on a stagger so the mist read living discipline, not panic. If a death knight came up the road — and the courier had said a death knight was coming up the road — he would follow the wheel ruts, the packed center, the path that read like an army's spine. He would not look east into birch scrub for a scout and a shield line hiding behind watch-fires.

Jostin closed the journal. One nod. Professional.

Brynn's eyes found the ronin's face — held there for a beat longer than discipline required — and then she turned east. Shield leading. Boots quiet on the frost.

They disappeared into the birch line in under a minute. Good movement. Clean lines.

The ronin stayed on the crown.

---

Smoke bomb. The cloud rolled low — grey-white, mist-colored, invisible against the ambient fog. Invisibility. Clone.

Kenji-two materialized in the smoke's wake. Same mask, same nodachi across the back, same unhurried walk. It started south down the crown — a second silhouette moving along the packed center where Corwyn's approach would register first. A decoy and a tripwire in one body.

The real Kenji followed at fifty yards, alternating between the crown and the east verge. Split-brief fires stacked as they moved: Brynn's waypoint to the northeast, Kenji's answer-flame at the junction behind, a second south-column burn when the clone pushed past mile seventeen-point-five. At the fence post there, Kenji pressed milepost chalk into the wood — a mark for Jostin's hand if the mist tried to steal bearings.

The root network under the road was quiet. Not dead — *quiet*. The circulation trunks still pulsed at depths Living Ground could barely read. Whatever the courier had reported, the audience had received it. The audience was thinking.

Mile seventeen. Seventeen-point-five. The clone walked point. The mist thickened. Living Ground contracted to a hundred feet, then eighty.

Then Brynn's fire didn't read clean from the south position.

Not gone — *wrong*. Shifted east by thirty yards, the ring pattern disrupted, the stagger broken. Either she had moved or something had moved her perception of where the signal should go.

Kenji burned Wind Step. Three charges — one to close north on her vector, one to correct the approach angle when the birch line shifted underfoot, one to light the Brynn-slot ring east of the crown where the watch-fire pattern said *I am here and I am not lost.*

He found them exactly where they should have been. Jostin crouched behind a fallen birch, arrow nocked, scanning south through the mist. Brynn shield-right, amber-gold eyes tracking movement that turned out to be wind through dead branches.

The fire shift had been wind. Just wind.

But the charges were spent. Wind Step 5/5 → 0/5. The road would have to be walked from here on legs that could not skip across treetops.

### Ridge Thread

---

Northwest of the crown, mist lifted once — a gap in the fog ceiling that lasted maybe ten heartbeats. Long enough to show a low ridgeline breaking the horizon: not a full estate, not a fortress, just the broken spine of hills that marked where the March's geography stopped pretending and started *being*.

Something wraith-cold threaded that gap. Not a figure — a *thread*. A sensation that Living Ground read as temperature and Bond-Form Sight read as intent. Cold the way hate is cold when it has been patient long enough to forget what warmth was for.

It skimmed the ridge for a handful of heartbeats. Tasting the air. Tasting the fires. Tasting the geometry of three living things spread across a road that belonged to dead things.

Kenji gave it no line and no duel. The clone's screen and the eastward fires were the message: *we are here, we are spread, and chasing one means losing the others.* The cold thread considered the math, found it unprofitable, and slid off-axis. It thinned against the mist like ink dissolving in grey water.

Not a kill. Not a spend worth the crown's attention. Just the March reminding them that the road had opinions about who walked it after dark.

### The Knight on the Crown

---

Mile eighteen. The mist was chest-high and the light had gone the color of pewter when Living Ground screamed.

Not a signal. A *wall*. A single signature so saturated with necrotic binding that it registered like a sinkhole in the sensory field — everything around it curved inward, drawn toward a gravity that had nothing to do with mass. Holy fire underneath, burning against the death like a coal buried in snow. The two signatures braided together in a pattern Living Ground had never read before: not corrupted, not clean, just *conflicted* at a depth that made the sensory field stutter.

God Sight confirmed at eighty feet: black plate armor, polished to a mirror finish despite the mist and the ash and the weeks of road. A greatsword carried across the back — and even at this distance, even through the fog, the blade's dual signature was visible. Holy light running down from the edge. Necrotic dark climbing up from the pommel. They met in the middle of the steel and they were *fighting each other*.

Sir Corwyn the Fallen stood in the center of the crown like a man waiting for a door to open.

The clone reached him first.

Corwyn's head turned. Blue fire in the eye sockets — not the guttering flames of the revenants from the shield wall, but *steady* light, controlled, banked like a forge that remembers what it was built to heat. The death knight looked at the clone the way a sentry looks at a traveler's papers.

**"Not you,"** he said. His voice was wrong in a way that took a moment to place — too *warm* for the body producing it. A living voice coming out of a dead throat. **"The real one. Behind me. Sixty yards east and twenty feet off the ground, if the roots are reading true."**

The clone stopped walking.

**"I have been patient,"** Corwyn said. The greatsword stayed on his back. His ruined gauntlets — one dented at the knuckle, one cracked at the wrist — stayed at his sides. **"Patient the way duty asks a man to be patient, which is not the same as choosing it. I let the thief walk away from the terminus with the chest. I let the thief walk away from the seal without looking inside. I have been reading intent since before your scout was born, and your intent does not read like a thief's."**

He turned fully. Facing south, where the real Kenji was perched in a dead elm with no Wind Step charges left and a blade that wanted to be drawn.

**"But you took something from me at the shield wall. You took it the way a surgeon takes a limb — not cruelty, not greed. Purpose."** A pause. The blue fire in his eyes dimmed, then brightened. **"I would like to know the purpose before the next part."**

Kenji dropped from the tree. Not Wind Step — just a thirty-foot fall that the boots ate without sound. He landed on the crown twenty yards south of the death knight, mask in place, nodachi still sheathed across his back.

The mist curled between them like a held breath.

**"You have her weapon,"** Corwyn said. **"You have her network in pieces behind you. You walked into her border, killed her sentries, broke her relay, and now you carry a blade that was sworn to protect her."** The gauntlets opened. Closed. **"Tell me you are here to end her, and I will step aside. Tell me you are here to bargain with her, and I will tell you what she costs. Tell me nothing—"**

The gauntlet went to the greatsword's hilt.

**"—and I will do what the oath requires."**

The road held its breath. Forty-two dead skeletons behind him in the mist. The courier gone south. The roots listening.

The ronin said nothing.

Corwyn's jaw set — not anger, not disappointment. *Confirmation.* The original oath — the one underneath, the one that said *confirm the target before you swing* — had done its work. It had asked the question. The question had been answered with silence. Silence was not *protect the weak.* Silence was not *I am no threat to her domain.*

Silence was a man with a stolen sword who would not explain himself.

**"So be it,"** Corwyn said, and drew Oathbreaker.

### The Duel

---

The greatsword came off the back in a single motion — practiced, fluid, the muscle memory of a man who had drawn that blade ten thousand times before dying and ten thousand times after. The dual signature flared: holy white down the edge, necrotic black up the spine, the two forces screaming against each other in a sound that was not sound.

Kenji's hand went to the nodachi's hilt at his shoulder. Left thumb against the guard. Right foot back. Weight low.

Iaido.

He won initiative because he was built to win initiative — the Windstrider boots, the stance, the six months of muscle memory plus seven days of back-room drill that had turned the draw into a reflex faster than conscious decision. Corwyn was still completing his overhead guard when the nodachi cleared the scabbard.

The draw came from the shoulder — a single rising arc, the same motion that had opened the shield wall — but targeted. Not center mass. Not the head. The *sword arm*.

The blade hit Corwyn's right shoulder at the gap between pauldron and vambrace. Six dice of slashing damage plus void energy plus the guaranteed critical that iaido always delivered on the first draw. The math was brutal: approximately one hundred points of structural damage concentrated into a joint that was already bearing the weight of a six-foot greatsword.

The pauldron *crumpled*. Not cut — crumpled, the black plate folding inward like tin under a hammer. The void edge carved through the gap, through the binding-reinforced bone beneath, through the ligaments that connected shoulder to arm. Necrotic sparks sprayed from the wound — dark confetti, the binding's emergency response to structural failure.

Corwyn's arm didn't fall off. Death knights don't lose limbs that cleanly — the binding holds them together even when the architecture fails. But the shoulder joint was *done*. The elbow locked at a wrong angle. The grip strength in the right hand went from a death knight's full press to something a child could break.

Oathbreaker stayed in his hand. Barely. The fingers held by stubbornness and oath-memory more than mechanical function.

Corwyn swung anyway.

The first strike came left-handed — the greatsword transferred in a motion that would have been impossible for a living swordsman but was merely *ugly* for a death knight whose body obeyed orders rather than physics. The blade caught Kenji across the ribs, necrotic edge biting through the hakama's ward with the weight of a man who had seventy-two hours of reasons to hit hard. Thirty-one points of damage. The necrotic rider burned cold — a flash of frost across the skin that tried to slow the blood underneath.

Kenji's regeneration ate it. 333 HP per round. The bruise formed, purpled, yellowed, and vanished before Corwyn's follow-through completed.

The second strike was overhead — desperate geometry, the ruined right arm trying to guide what the left arm powered. It caught the shoulder and carved a line from collarbone to sternum. Twenty-eight points. The wound opened, bled for approximately two seconds, and closed.

Fifty-nine total. Fifty-nine points of damage from a death knight's best remaining output against a target that healed faster than it bled.

Corwyn saw the wound close. The blue fire in his eyes did something complicated — not surprise, not despair. *Recognition.* He had watched a man's body reject damage the way a river rejects a stone, and somewhere under the binding, under the warped oath, the paladin who remembered what divine healing looked like understood that he was watching something adjacent to it.

The ronin's second attack was already moving. A horizontal sweep — not iaido, just clean swordsmanship — that caught the right arm at the elbow. The joint that had been holding by stubbornness and memory gave up the argument. The arm went limp from elbow to fingertip.

Oathbreaker hit the packed dirt of the crown with a sound like a church bell dropped from a roof.

Holy light flared. Necrotic dark surged. The two forces in the blade, freed from the grip that had been mediating their war, *screamed* at each other in a pulse that Living Ground felt as a migraine and God Sight read as a theological emergency.

Kenji picked it up.

The blade was heavy — not weight-heavy, *meaning-heavy*. The dual binding fought his hand the way a horse fights a strange rider. Holy fire tested his intent and found *no oath.* Necrotic dark tested his alignment and found *no anchor.* The sword settled into an uneasy truce: not accepted, not rejected. Carried.

He slid it into the Bag of Holding. The extradimensional space swallowed the dual signature whole — holy and necrotic arguing in the dark where no one could hear them.

Corwyn stood in the crown of the road with one ruined arm and no sword. The blue fire in his eyes had dimmed to embers. The mist curled around his boots.

He did not fall. Death knights don't fall unless the binding releases them, and the binding had no reason to release a servant who had merely *lost*. He stood the way a man stands when dignity is the only thing that still works — spine straight, shoulders set despite the ruin, head level.

The ronin sheathed the nodachi. Full sheathe — cord tied, weapon seated across the back. Iaido stance reset.

Then he turned and walked north. Back toward the birch line. Back toward the east verge. Back toward the people waiting for him in the dark.

Corwyn watched him go. One arm limp. No sword. The mist filling the space between them like a curtain drawn by someone who had seen enough.

He did not follow.

### Birch Copse

---

East verge camp. Mile seventeen, give or take what the mist had done to everyone's sense of distance.

Jostin had the camp built before Kenji arrived — watch-fires set in the stagger pattern, sight lines cleared through the birch scrub, the fallen trunk that served as a wind break positioned where it could also serve as cover. Professional. Efficient. The kind of camp a man builds when he's been building camps in hostile territory since before his beard came in.

He looked up when the ronin appeared out of the mist. Pen stopped. Eyes moved — face, hands, armor, posture. The scout's diagnostic checklist: alive, armed, bloodied? No blood. No visible damage. No limping.

One new item in the Bag of Holding that hadn't been there when he left.

Jostin opened his mouth, closed it, wrote something in the journal margin, and went back to the watch rotation without asking a single question.

Brynn was less contained.

She came around the wind break at a walk that wanted to be a run, shield still on her arm because discipline dies harder than worry. Her amber-gold eyes — Vigor-bright, luminous in the pewter light — found the ronin's face and stayed there. Reading. Checking. Cataloguing the same way Jostin had, but with a vocabulary that included *relief* and *fury* and the space between them where soldiers keep the things they can't afford to say on watch.

She stopped three feet away. Shield arm dropped. Jaw set.

Then she punched him in the chest — not hard enough to register against his HP, hard enough to register against his *attention* — and walked back to her post.

Jostin's pen scratched once. A margin note. He did not look up.

### The Tent

---

Night came the way it comes in the Pallid March — not gradually but *completely*, the mist swallowing the last grey light in a single breath until the watch-fires were the only things that proved the world still had edges.

Jostin took first watch. Automatic. Didn't need to be told. Didn't need to be thanked. He sat with his back against the fallen trunk, shortbow across his knees, journal open to the page where the margins were getting crowded.

The tent was standard field issue — Coalition surplus, canvas stretched over two poles, room for two if neither of them needed dignity. Brynn had set it while Kenji was south. Her kit was already inside. Her shield leaned against the outside frame the way a sentry leans against a wall — present, watchful, not quite off duty.

Kenji ducked through the flap. She was already there — boots off, armor off, the padded linen underneath still carrying the day's salt and exertion. Her amber-gold eyes tracked him in the dark. Vigor-sharp. The enhanced perception that came with the buff meant she could see details in the tent's darkness that a baseline human would miss: the micro-tension in his jaw, the way his hand flexed once at his side, the controlled exhale that was not quite a sigh.

She didn't ask about the duel. She didn't ask about the sword in the bag. She didn't ask about Corwyn standing alone in the mist with one working arm.

She pulled him down by the collar of his hakama and kissed him like a woman who had spent fourteen hours pretending she wasn't counting the minutes since he'd walked south into a death knight's road.

Permission was already standing from the copse before — Chapter 12's watch-fire night, the first time, when boundaries were set by her and respected by him. This was a continuation, not a renegotiation. The terms were the same: she led, he followed, the pace was hers to set and hers to break.

She took the lead the way she did everything — direct, certain, undecorated by hesitation. Hakama untied. Linen pushed aside. Her hands were calloused from shield work and warm from Vigor's metabolic gift, and she used both qualities without apology. When she straddled him in the dark, the amber-gold of her eyes was the only light in the tent — two points of Vigor-bright luminescence looking down at him with an expression that mixed want with something closer to *inventory*: checking that all the pieces were still there, that the road hadn't taken anything she needed.

Outside, Jostin's pen scratched steadily. The watch-fire crackled. The mist pressed against the canvas like a third presence trying to listen through fabric.

Inside, the world contracted to skin and breath and the sound she made — low, involuntary, the kind of sound that discipline cannot strangle because the body does not care about rank when it is being honest. She set the rhythm and he matched it, hands on her hips where the muscle met the bone, feeling the Vigor-enhanced strength in her thighs as she moved.

She was not gentle. The campaign had burned gentle out of her vocabulary somewhere around mile twelve. What she gave instead was thorough — the physical argument of a woman who knew what she wanted, knew the man underneath her could take it, and intended to leave no ambiguity about either fact.

When she finished — twice, because Vigor's stamina enhancement was not a metaphor — she collapsed forward with her forehead against his shoulder and breathed the way runners breathe after a sprint. Hard. Honest. Done.

He held her there until her heartbeat slowed. She let him. That was its own kind of trust — the shield-line soldier who never let anyone at her back, resting her weight against a man's chest in a tent in enemy territory and not pretending it was anything other than what it was.

The Vigor clock refreshed somewhere in the margins of the act — the biological reset that the mechanic tracked but the moment did not care about. Her eyes were still amber-gold when she fell asleep on his chest. They would stay amber-gold for five more days.

### Dawn

---

Ashmere 29. Rootday.

The light came back reluctantly — grey, thin, the mist admitting that morning existed without committing to the claim. The watch-fires had burned down to coals. Jostin was on his third rotation, journal closed, shortbow across his knees, eyes south.

The tent flap opened. Brynn came out first — boots on, armor on, shield in hand, professional from the waist up. She nodded to Jostin. He nodded back. Neither of them mentioned anything that the canvas had failed to contain.

Kenji came out second. Mask in place. Nodachi across the back. The Bag of Holding at his hip carrying a stolen holy weapon that was still arguing with itself in the dark.

Long rest complete. Charges fresh. Wind Step 5/5. Smoke-Clone 3/3. Iaido loaded. HP full. The body had done what bodies do when given seven hours of unconsciousness — reset every counter that exhaustion had been running.

Meals: two to one. The last March rations eaten cold before dawn, standing at the edge of the birch line, looking south where a death knight stood alone on the road with one arm and no sword and a conscience that neither his queen nor his killer had managed to cut out of him.

The road waited. The mist waited. Whatever was south of the mist waited with the patience of something that had been practicing patience since 1042.

Kenji looked north. Looked south.

Made a decision that Chapter 15 would carry.
