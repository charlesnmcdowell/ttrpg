### Dawn and the common room

---

Morning light found the suite honest — frost on the east window, forge-quarter smoke starting below, the bronze tub cold and quiet in its corner. The luxury that had been worth eighteen gold at two in the morning looked like what it was at seven: a good room in a relay town where good rooms existed for people passing through.

Mursha dressed in fighter economy. Binding-wrap first, then the dragon-scale laminate that Marthel had cleaned and fitted two days ago, the masterwork longsword riding her left hip in the leather frog, the warhammer on the right. Braids checked. Silver rings re-seated where sleep had shifted them. The whole process took less than four minutes and produced a woman who looked like she had been awake for hours and had been doing something more productive than sleeping. The ronin watched from the bed, then followed her downstairs for food.

The Brass Hitch common room did its job. Steam and bread and the low grind of relay people buying calories before they bought trouble. Calista worked the morning bar with the professional warmth of a woman who understood that gossip was a service and attention was payment, and the morning's gossip had four threads worth the price of breakfast.

The yard was edgy. Iron Mule counts — Cassia Vorn's people had been recounting crates and manifests since yesterday, and the kind of counting that took two days meant the kind of loss that required paper. The western cut story was mutating — a half-orc in new dragon-scale and a masked ronin had broken a ridge siege, and the story had grown legs overnight in the manner of relay-town stories, which meant the truth was already buried under three layers of embellishment and the embellishment was more interesting than the facts. The Kharn-Dural whisper was still in the margins — emissary correspondence stacking up at factor desks, drunk miners repeating the phrase "the note is changing" without understanding what it meant, the deep-forge rumor that had been ambient noise two days ago settling into something that sounded less like gossip and more like a warning no one was reading. The river tie-up was short-handed — dock hire, seasonal labor, the kind of work that filled a morning if a person needed a reason to stay in Bronzebarrow and did not have one.

Mursha chose the vector. Her eyes had found Haldra at the dwarf-leased corner before the tea was cool enough to drink.

### Deep-forge whisper

---

Mursha opened with Mule clearance diction — not romance, not charm, not the careful approach of someone testing a stranger. She spoke to Haldra the way a factor-house professional spoke to another factor-house professional: what paper was actually crossing the desk, who held the stamp, whether the phrase "ArchMagus absent" still filled the margins of coalition correspondence that no one in Bronzebarrow had the authority to answer.

Haldra answered in clerk steel. She was a dwarf woman at a desk she had owned for years, and the desk was her territory, and the answers she gave were the answers a territorial professional gives when another professional asks the right questions in the right language. Courtesy copies from Kharn-Dural that were not courtesy — they were cover for an inventory war that the surface gates had been fighting through paperwork instead of admissions. Coalition reminders addressed to titles that Bronzebarrow did not hold and could not act on. Receipts and seals that did not match north-row vault tallies in ways that suggested someone was keeping two books, and the someone was not being subtle about it. Surface gates not screaming yet — but the screaming was a matter of timing, not disposition, and Haldra's read on the timing was professional and specific.

She named the north-row vault line as the next check, if the masked ronin wanted more than smoke.

**Irresistible Presence** — passive **DC 23**. Haldra's WIS save failed at the desk. The failure looked like nothing dramatic — a pause too long between sentences, a re-straightening of papers that had already been straight, the particular strain of a woman whose attention had been pulled by something her professional mind did not authorize and her body did not override. Not collapse. Strain. The kind of strain that a clerk who had spent years negotiating with men who wanted something from her could manage without breaking posture, but could not manage without the ronin noticing that posture was being managed.

---

### Private hour

---

He paid for a closed parlor. **Eight gold pieces** — the sand timer rate, bolt on the door, terms stated at the desk before the key changed hands. Intel business was the stated purpose and the stated purpose was not a lie.

The transit from the common floor to the parlor strained Haldra again. Second exposure to the passive at closer range, the stairwell narrowing the distance between a dwarf clerk carrying ledger knowledge and a masked swordsman carrying a presence that her wisdom save had already failed once. The second fail was quieter than the first — less surprise, more resignation, the composure of a woman who understood that the thing pulling at her attention was not going to stop pulling and the professional response was to manage it rather than pretend it was not there.

Inside, negotiation collided with attraction. **Persuasion** with advantage — the aura providing what the aura always provided in enclosed spaces with failed saves — against **DC 19**. The roll landed at **20**. Margin of +1. The margin was enough to extend consent beyond the stated terms of the parlor hour into a longer private beat that was player-declared and table-owned, and the beat preceded the ledger voice returning because the ledger voice could not return until the body allowed it to.

The ronin stated a hard limit when the business resumed. He could not read. **INT 9** — the number that meant a man who could break a siege and vaporize a bandit on a ridgeline could not parse a column of figures on a clerk's page. The truth had to be verbal. Everything she knew about the Kharn-Dural paper trail, she had to speak aloud, because the man sitting across from her in a bolted parlor could not read the documents she had memorized.

Haldra complied. The remaining time after dressing became an oral briefing delivered in the steady voice of a clerk who had just done something she had not planned to do and was compensating by being extremely good at the thing she had planned to do. The Kharn-Dural courtesy copies were cover for an inventory war — receipts and seals that did not reconcile against north-row vault tallies, and the discrepancy was large enough that someone was either incompetent or cooking the books, and Haldra's professional opinion was that no one at Kharn-Dural was incompetent. Clerk names — who had signed, who had refused to sign, who had vanished on sick leave at convenient intervals. The Varenholm silence — the absent ArchMagus excuse replicating down every relay chain as margin blame, the coalition's empty chair becoming a bureaucratic weather pattern that every factor house in every relay town used to explain why nothing was getting done. Which Bronzebarrow factor house she suspected of laundering "lost" ingots under coalition wax seals. What yard habits a person would need to observe if they wanted to catch factors looking sideways at their own paperwork.

She spoke it all. The sand timer ran. The ronin listened and filed and did not write anything down because he could not write anything down, and the filing was in his head where it would stay until he found someone who could use it.

**Lover's Vigor** surfaced. The brass-amber ring at the pupil edge that was the dwarven tell — present in Haldra's clear blue eyes when she unlocked the bolt, sharper and more definite than the strain that had preceded it. The +50% stats settling into a body that was already carrying a clerk's discipline and a factor-house memory, and the settling was quiet, and the tell was visible only to someone who knew what vigor looked like in the eyes of a woman who had just earned it.

Exit protocol. Haldra left first — down the stairs, back to her corner, papers in hand, composure rebuilt between the parlor door and the common floor. The ronin waited two minutes after the bolt opened. The two minutes were operational, not romantic, and the distinction mattered because the distinction was the only thing separating a private business meeting from the thing the private business meeting had also been.

---

### Relay to Mursha

---

The ronin descended to the common floor and found Mursha where he had left her — at a table near the bar, tankard of dark tea, the ridge intel notes in her satchel where no one could read them and where they would stay until she decided otherwise.

He spoke the hour from memory. Syllable discipline — the precise recall of a man who could not write but could carry an hour of verbal intelligence in his head and reproduce it without error because his memory was the only page he had. The Kharn-Dural vault discrepancy. The clerk names. The factor house under suspicion. The Varenholm silence and its relay echo. The yard patterns.

Mursha threaded it to the ridge paper intel — not name by name but pattern by pattern, the contract mind finding the shape where the western cut bandit cell's coalition manifest leaks overlapped with the factor-house discrepancies that Haldra had described. Not the same crime. The same network. The handoff choice was urgent now — Cassia's factor wire was still churning, daylight movement in the yard increased correlation risk, and the merged intel package was worth more delivered to the right person than it was sitting in a satchel and a skull.

She did not demand bedroom detail in the common room. She named the next fork — food or move, before the shadows got honest and the yard started asking questions that the answers would not survive.
