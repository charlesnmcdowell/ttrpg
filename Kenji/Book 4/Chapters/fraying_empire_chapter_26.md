### Perimeter

---

Mursha did not think in minutes when the mountain was listening. She thought in rings: who stood where, which lantern threw which shadow, which dwarf boots meant business and which meant boredom.

The factor train had earned its halt three hours ago — wax applied to wheel hubs, tally sheets counted and countersigned, the diplomatic pouch line grinding through the tube-to-paperwork conversion that turned rumor into something a stamp could kill. Her contract named her liability for the wagons and the bodies that moved them. Not for the ronin's secrets, not for his silences, not for the particular way he had of being present without arriving. She treated both the cargo and the man the same way she treated steel: assume it could fail, plan for the break.

The gallery was dwarf-carved and dwarf-lit — lantern chains bolted into stone that had been cut square a thousand years before anyone alive remembered cutting it. The ceiling was high enough for ox teams but low enough that the air held heat and smoke and the particular staleness of a mountain that had been breathing through the same lungs for centuries. Traffic moved in grooves worn into the floor by generations of iron-shod wheels. Dwarves passed in pairs and threes, their conversations pitched at a register that carried through stone without effort, their eyes noting the factor train with the professional disinterest of people who had seen a hundred trains park in this gallery and would see a hundred more.

Mursha had positioned herself at the lead bench — the spot a guard occupied when the train was halted and the perimeter was hers. From there she could see the main arch, the side cut where the pouch clerks worked, the far wall where a second gallery opened into deeper traffic, and the bench where the sell-sword was supposed to be sitting.

When Kenji reappeared beside it, she did not startle.

That was the first wrong thing.

She should have startled. A man does not materialize on a bench in a lit gallery without crossing the floor first, and Mursha had been watching the floor. Her peripheral vision was not a gift — it was a habit she had paid for in scars, in missed meals, in the particular education that comes from learning what happens when a guard does not see the knife. She had been watching, and she had not seen him cross.

The second wrong thing was smaller: a lag in her gut, like a step that should have clicked and didn't. The feeling she'd learned to catalogue after Brass Hitch — the flicker in the tavern when one Kenji dissolved and another stood where the first had been, the Persuasion that had kept her following when every guard instinct she owned said *something was wrong with that*. She had filed it. She had not forgotten it. Filing was not forgetting, with Mursha. Filing was putting a blade in a sheath where you could find it again.

She didn't accuse him in dwarf hearing. That was discipline, not trust. Accusations in front of dwarves became paperwork, and paperwork in a mountain became stone — permanent, retrievable, and owned by people who were not her.

She adjusted her stance half a pace closer, warhammer hilt available under her left hand, dragon-scale catching the lantern light along the shoulder seam where Marthel's forge work had fitted the laminate to muscle. The armor was good steel worn by a body that deserved good steel, and the lantern knew it. She did not reach for the hammer. She did not need to. The geometry of her body said *I am here and I am armed and the next wrong thing gets answered*.

### Mule-clear

---

She waited until a loaded cart ground past — iron ore in open bins, the wheels protesting against the gallery floor with a sound that swallowed speech at three paces.

"You were gone."

Not a question. A fact she was handing him the way a factor clerk handed a discrepancy note — flat, professional, expecting a signature or an explanation, willing to accept neither as long as the silence was noted in her personal ledger.

Kenji could answer or not. Silence was its own answer, with her. It meant *I will not explain*, and Mursha's contract mind filed *I will not explain* in the same drawer as *I am hiding something*, and the drawer was getting full.

She exhaled once through her nose — annoyance, relief, both. The relief surprised her. She did not examine it. Examining it would mean admitting the annoyance was personal, and personal had no stamp in a contract.

"Fine." The word came out with mule-clear diction — the voice she used when speaking to ox drivers in rain, to dock bosses in dispute, to anyone whose attention she needed to hold through noise and ego. A voice built for yards and weather and the particular deafness of men who did not want to hear what a woman was saying. "Don't make me lie for you in front of a forge-sight clerk who already wants your bag."

The warning was specific. The younger dwarf — sharp eyes, compact build, the kind of stillness that meant training rather than patience — had examined the ronin's kit at the outer checkpoint with an interest that went beyond professional curiosity. Mursha had watched him do it. She had watched the way his attention snagged on the ronin's satchel and stayed there half a beat too long, the kind of attention that a guard recognized because guards were trained to produce it, not receive it.

Down the cut, the same dwarf passed through a side arch — not approaching, not done noticing. His pace said *I have other business* but his shoulders said *I remember you*.

Mursha tracked him the way she tracked ridge lines: geometry, not grudge. She noted the arch he used, the direction he turned, the corridor he disappeared into. She would know it again if she saw it. She filed that too.

### Almost

---

They stole ten minutes where ten minutes existed.

The supply niche was a cut in the gallery wall — shallow, functional, stacked with rope coils and capped oil drums and the dust that accumulated in places dwarves used but did not clean because cleaning implied leisure and leisure implied time and time in a mountain was measured differently than time on a road. The air smelled like hemp and tallow and the faint iron bite of stone that had been sweating since before either of them were born.

Mursha checked Kenji's straps. Not his shoulders, not his face, not the mask that hid everything she sometimes wanted to read — his straps. The buckles on the hakama where road wear loosened leather. The satchel harness where the bag sat against his hip. The boot laces where a loose knot could become a stumble in a corridor that did not forgive stumbles. She checked them with her hands, not her eyes, because hands were honest and eyes could be persuaded, and she was a guard checking a partner's kit the way she had checked every partner's kit since her first contract — not tender, inventory.

Her eyes, when they finally came up to his, held the bronze ring.

It was there at the pupil's edge — warm, metallic, the particular color of good alloy catching light from the right angle. Lover's Vigor doing what it always did to her: making the dark brown sharper, the stare hotter, the whole architecture of her face more honest than her mouth had been in a week. The ring meant she was still inside the window. The ring meant the power was still moving through her — the strength that had no name she would say aloud, the stamina that had no origin she would credit, the warmth that started behind her sternum and went nowhere useful during working hours.

She looked at him with that ring and said nothing, and the nothing was louder than the forge-glow humming through the gallery wall.

Marthel's face arrived uninvited.

Not the smith herself — the memory of the smith's expression when Kenji had pulled the greatsword from his bag at the forge row in Bronzebarrow. The fear in Marthel's hands. The way a woman who worked steel for a living had looked at a blade that should not exist and had known — not guessed, known — that something about the weapon was wrong in a direction her craft could name but her courage could not follow. *They looked at you different*, Mursha had told herself afterward, and the *they* included the dwarf and the sell-sword and the half-orc who had watched both of them and understood that the sword was a question she had not yet asked.

She had not asked it at Bronzebarrow.

She had not asked it on the western cut.

She had not asked it on the night road, or in the suite, or at the relay camp, or at the outer checkpoint where the forge-sight clerk had looked at the ronin's bag with the same expression Marthel had worn.

She opened her mouth.

The words were there. She could feel them — solid, angular, the shape of *where did you get that sword* and *why did the smith look afraid* and *what are you carrying through a dwarven mountain that makes their people stare at your bag*. The words were professional. The words were owed. The words were the correct output of a contract mind that had been filing unanswered questions since Ashmere 34 and the drawer was now so full that the mechanism was starting to bind.

She closed her mouth.

Duty won again. But thinner this time — like metal that had been stressed too many times in the same place. She could feel the fatigue line in her own discipline, the hairline fracture where *later* kept replacing *now* and the replacement was getting more expensive each time she made it. A smith would call it work-hardening past the ductile limit. Mursha called it nothing, because naming it would mean admitting the next bend might break.

The ten minutes ended because ten minutes always end.

### Yard tier

---

Morning came to Kharn the way morning came underground: shifts changed, bells rang, steam found new cracks to escape through.

The transition was not sunrise. It was sound — the particular shift in traffic density that meant the day crews had replaced the night crews, the voices pitching higher because dwarves who worked forge-adjacent schedules spoke louder to compensate for the ringing in their ears, the cart wheels moving faster because morning meant quotas and quotas meant speed. Mursha read the shift change the way she read any operational environment: by what moved, what stopped, and what changed its mind about moving.

The factor lead was a broad dwarf with hands like shovels and a beard braided into a pattern that meant something Mursha did not need to understand. He handed her the assignment sheet — two pages, dwarf script, countersigned by the gallery clerk and stamped with the factor house seal that Haldra's contract had authorized. Mursha could not read dwarf script, but she could read a signature line, and the signature line said *I will keep your blood inside your skin until the next stamp*.

She signed where guards signed. The pen was too small for her hand. It always was.

The train would move again — not deep yet, not final, but inward one tier toward yards where the forge-glow pulsed at a frequency that Mursha's teeth registered before her eyes did. Something about the light in the deeper corridors was wrong. Not broken, not dangerous in a way she could name with guard vocabulary, but *off* — the way a blade vibrates differently when the tang is cracked, a hum you feel in the bones of your hand before the steel shows the fault line. The dwarves around her had stopped using certain words. *Resonance* was one. *Harmony* was another. They had replaced them with words that meant the same thing but carried less weight, the way people replaced a dead friend's name with a title so they could keep talking about the dead friend without stopping every time.

Mursha noticed. Filing was what she did.

Kenji fell in where sell-swords fell in — behind the lead wagon, forward of the rear guard, in the gap where a hired blade's presence was expected and a hired blade's absence would be noticed but not mourned.

Mursha fell in where guards fell in. Beside him when the corridor allowed — two abreast, her warhammer on the outside, his nodachi between them where the long blade's reach covered her flank as naturally as her shield arm would have covered his if she had been carrying a shield. Ahead of him when the corridor narrowed — her back to his chest, the dragon-scale taking the forward position because the guard walked point and the sell-sword walked follow and the contract did not care about the bronze ring at her pupils or the warmth behind her sternum or the question she had not asked for the fourth time.

She did not ask permission to follow him through the mountain.

She was already doing it by contract. And the contract, for now, was enough — not because it answered anything, but because it gave her a name for what she was doing that was not the other name. The name she was not ready to sign.
