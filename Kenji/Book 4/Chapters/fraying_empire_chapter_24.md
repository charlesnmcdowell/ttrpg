### Mursha’s morning

---

She woke before the forge bell the dwarves did not ring but still obeyed in their spines.

Mursha checked the wagon circle first—tack, chains, wheel pins, the small dishonesties a road could hide until it chose not to. Her contract was not with Kenji’s bond; it was with Haldra’s stamp and the factor house’s name. She treated that the way she treated steel: if you insulted it, it would cut you later.

Kenji rose into the same cold that made every breath honest. The ronin’s mask was already etiquette when the teamsters started moving; Mursha did not tell him to wear it—she did not have to. Guards learned each other’s tells the way fighters learned stance.

When she passed close enough to hand him travel bread, her braids were still damp at the temples. The relay ring’s wash troughs had been busy before dawn; the soap there was harsh and anonymous, the kind of clean that belonged to everyone and therefore to no one. A trace of it clung to her skin—sharp, industrial, *shared*.

Kenji took the bread without comment.

Mursha did not explain baths.

---

### The climb

---

The road stopped pretending to be easy.

On the second day out of the cold ring, the crown relay narrowed into a climb that understood weight. Ox teams leaned into harnesses. Teamsters swore in three languages, none of them gentle. The air tasted like metal shavings and cold water.

Mursha walked sections on foot because a guard who only rode forgot how the earth lied. **Living Ground** was not her gift, but she could read a half-orc’s instincts in another person’s stillness: Kenji’s pauses at certain bends, the way his attention dropped to the gravel as if listening through soles. She filed it without naming it. Naming things was how civilians turned luck into stories.

The dwarf whisper followed them uphill—*the note changed*—until it stopped being gossip and became atmosphere.

---

### Milepost math

---

The friction found them at a painted stake driven into shale—a gnomish milepost maintained by coalition fee and dwarven tolerance.

The surveyor was small, furious, and correct: the factor train’s rear wagon had clipped a marker line last season, and the paperwork correcting it had not caught up to the stone. Words became geometry. Geometry became money. Money became pride.

Mursha stepped in before pride became blood.

She did not raise her voice. She did what she used to do in yards when two crews started measuring each other with chins: she named the stamp that mattered, named the contract clause that covered accidental verge wear, named the person who would pay if someone insisted on being stupid in front of witnesses. Her hand rested on her warhammer without threat—the way a scale rests on a counter.

The surveyor spat a word in Gnomish that was probably profane. Then he took the fee Mursha counted onto his palm—factor silver, not Kenji’s purse—and hammered a fresh witness nail with the enthusiasm of a man winning on points.

Kenji watched. The ronin did not need to speak for Mursha’s victory to be visible: the train moved again; the circle breathed.

---

### Second ring

---

The second night camp was higher, meaner, colder.

Mursha took the watch rotation the factor boss offered because that was what hired blades were for—and because watch gave her permission to be alone with her thoughts without performing intimacy for anyone, not even him.

On her return, she found Kenji still awake, still masked, still *present* in the way he had been since Bronzebarrow: not demanding, not bargaining. The Oathbreaker question hung in her like a splinter she could ignore until it infected. Marthel’s face flashed behind her eyes—the smith’s fear when steel that should not exist slid out of a bag.

She almost asked.

Duty closed her mouth first. Questions could wait until gates did not.

They shared heat the way travelers shared heat—economy, not confession. If her cloak carried road dust from where a teamster had brushed past her at the chain check, that was the road. Kenji did not accuse. Mursha did not volunteer.

### The gate line

---

On **Ashmere 38**, evening of **Day 260**, the mountain stopped being a rumor.

Kharn-Dural’s surface cut showed as a black line in cliff face—too straight, too stubborn for weather. Forge-glow should have been steady. It pulsed instead, slow as a sick heartbeat, wrong enough that even humans felt it in their teeth.

The outer checkpoint was coalition wax and dwarven stone braided together: a fortification pretending to be courtesy.

Queues formed the way queues always formed when fear wore official clothing.

Mursha took the front with the factor lead—papers, seals, inventory manifest, the diplomatic pouch line that made the tube *legitimate* instead of *smuggled*. Her voice stayed Mule-clear: names, dates, routes, the guard clause that put her body between wagons and trouble.

A dwarf officer with forge-ash in his beard studied Kenji the way smiths studied metal—looking for the flaw you could not see until it rang.

Mursha did not angle her body in front of the ronin like a lover. She angled it like a professional answering for a hireling.

**Irresistible Presence** pressed the checkpoint the way it pressed every room Kenji entered. The officer’s eyes tightened—not collapse, *strain*—then disciplined themselves back to work. The world’s unfairness was not new to anyone on this road.

The hard moment came when forge-sight was not metaphor.

A second clerk— younger, sharper, the kind of sharpness that came from talent without patience—asked for bags to be opened for “random verification,” and his gaze slid toward Kenji’s shoulders where a traveler carried weight.

Mursha produced language like a weapon on a lanyard: coalition random verification limits, factor immunity for sealed diplomatic pouches, the guard contract’s explicit clause that her sell-sword’s kit was *her* liability unless a writ named a crime.

Kenji remained silent. Silence, for him, was also a choice.

The clerk’s mouth thinned. He did not apologize. He stepped back one pace, which was dwarf for *later*.

They were not inside yet. They were *admitted to the line*—the first shell of the mountain’s patience.
