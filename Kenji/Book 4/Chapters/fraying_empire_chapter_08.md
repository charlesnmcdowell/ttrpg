# Book 4 — Fraying Empire
## Chapter 8: The Cell

**Date:** Ashmere 26, 1247 AR (Stillday)
**Location:** Gap zone camp → Thornkeep garrison

---

### The Clone Trick — Morning

---

I wait outside the tent for them to wake.

Grey light. Cold air. Frost on the branches. The hollow is quiet. The dead road sits empty fifty feet west. Living Ground reads nothing.

Elda stirs first. I hear her move inside the canvas — fabric shifting, a breath catching. She'll pull the blindfold.

Smoke bomb. Invisibility. Clone.

The smoke rolls out low and quiet. I'm gone before it clears. The clone materializes — me, but not me. Visual only. No biological signature. No aura. It posts up by the tent entrance, arms crossed, looking cool. The kind of pose you hold for a painting nobody commissioned.

Elda pushes the tent flap open. Blinks in the grey light. Sees the clone.

Her eyes hit his face. His neck. The strip of skin above the collar.

Nothing.

No save. No escalation. The stack holds at seven but doesn't push. The clone is wallpaper. A picture of a man. She looks at it the way you look at a well-made statue — appreciation without the heat.

The blindfold is folded on the ground beside her. She left it there. Didn't need it anymore — didn't know she still does.

I stand three feet away, invisible, and talk. Pitch it toward the clone so it sounds like it's coming from him.

"So want to go home? Think he's good enough to walk?"

---

Halden is awake. Color back. The ankle is wrapped and he's testing weight on it — gingerly, holding the tent pole, wincing. He can hobble. Not fast.

Elda watches the clone while she answers. Her voice is steady. The tears from last night are gone. She's a mother with a plan now — get the boy home.

"He can lean on me. We'll manage."

I pull a bag from my pack. A hundred gold. Heavy. I toss it — invisible hand, so it arcs out of nowhere and lands at her feet with a *clink* that doesn't match anything she's seeing.

She jumps. Looks down. Looks at the clone. The clone didn't move.

"Where I'm from, it's a custom to throw money at sexy women." A pause. "Um. Sorry. All I had was coins."

Her face does something complicated. Confusion. Embarrassment. The ghost of a smile she fights down. She picks up the bag. Weighs it. Her eyes go wide.

"This is — this is a *hundred* —"

She looks at the clone. The clone looks cool.

She puts the bag in her traveling sack. Tucks it deep. And then she smiles into the cup she's holding — the small clay travel cup, morning water — and the smile stays there where she thinks nobody can see it.

I see it.

---

"See you later, Elda. You know what to do from here. That patrol from earlier shouldn't be too far — they can get you back to town. If you need me, you know where to find me, beautiful."

She doesn't know where to find me.

The clone does a dramatic crouch. Then runs. Jumps to the nearest tree — full extension, one hand on a branch, swing up. Backflip to a higher branch. Lands in a ninja crouch. Holds it. Then works his way up and north, branch to branch, until the canopy swallows him.

Elda watches the whole thing with her mouth slightly open.

Halden, leaning on the tent pole: "Mom. Who *was* that?"

"I don't know his name."

She's smiling into the cup again.

---

I follow them. Invisible. Real. Twenty feet behind.

They're slow. Halden leans on her shoulder. She takes the weight without complaint. They find the road and follow it north toward Thornkeep. Elda keeps looking at the trees — looking for the man who isn't there. The man who's behind her, not above her.

Jostin's patrol finds them an hour in. Fourteen soldiers, road-worn, professional. Jostin does a double-take at the civilian pair on a closed road. Elda shows him the garrison waystation token from yesterday.

Handoff clean. Jostin assigns two soldiers to escort them the rest of the way. Halden gets a shoulder to lean on that isn't his mother's.

I watch until they're a quarter mile north. Safe.

Then I turn south and follow Jostin.

---

### Shadowing the Patrol

---

Jostin's patrol works the south road like professionals. Tight formation. Scouts forward. Jostin in the center with his journal out, marking terrain, noting tracks. They move with the quiet efficiency of people who do this every day and hate that it's necessary.

I follow from the trees. Invisible. Wind Step between branches. Eating jerky on a high limb, legs swinging, watching them work.

They find the phalanx two hours in.

---

### The Skeleton Phalanx

---

Eight skeletons. Not wanderers — *soldiers*. Shield wall. Interlocking formation. They're standing in the road like they own it, rusted shields overlapping, empty eye sockets facing north. Waiting.

Jostin holds up a fist. The patrol stops. He studies them through a spyglass for a full minute.

"Formation holders. Old stock — battlefield dead, dug up, re-dressed. The shields are Ashenmere garrison surplus from the border war. Twenty years in the ground minimum."

He gives the order. The patrol splits — six soldiers hit the shield wall from the front while four flankers swing wide. Jostin directs from twenty feet back, calling adjustments.

The front line hits. The phalanx holds. Shields lock tighter. The skeletons don't panic, don't scatter, don't break ranks. They absorb the charge and push back as a unit.

One of the flankers gets through. Destroys a skeleton on the left edge. The formation adjusts — the remaining seven close the gap. Seamless.

"That's remote command," Jostin mutters, writing in his journal. "Pre-programmed patrol routes. The formation holds until physically broken."

They break it in four minutes. Methodical. Professional. Not a single soldier down, though two take hits — one shield arm, one thigh. Walking wounds.

I watch the whole thing from a branch. Chewing jerky. Taking notes that don't exist on paper.

When the last skeleton falls, the bones just... stop. No residual animation. No death rattle. The command signal cut and the puppets went slack. Whatever is running these things isn't here. Isn't within a mile.

Jostin collects bone fragments for his report. Marks the location in his journal. The patrol reforms and continues south.

I follow them back to Thornkeep.

---

### Bracken's Office — Invisible

---

Jostin goes straight to Bracken.

Her office. Second floor of the garrison hall. Stone walls, narrow window, a desk covered in dispatches and maps. Commander Renna Bracken sits behind it in practical steel, dented at the shoulder. Forty-four years old. Scar from her right ear to her jaw. Grey-green eyes that could cut glass.

I'm already inside. Sitting in the corner. Invisible. Eating.

Jostin reports. The phalanx. The formation. The remote command structure. Old stock from battlefields. Three escalations in two weeks — lone walkers, then pairs, now drilled formations.

Bracken listens. Doesn't interrupt. When he finishes, she pulls out paper and starts writing.

Three letters.

The first to Katya — requesting sixty soldiers and one combat mage. The second to Vess — requesting a Coalition-level response timeline. The third to the Ashenmere garrison — asking what they're seeing on their side, courier only, she doesn't trust whisper stone relays.

"That's three dispatches in four months that say the same thing," she says. "Requesting reinforcement. Confirmed escalation. Forty-two soldiers against whatever is organizing these things. If I don't get a response to this round, I'm pulling the garrison back to the waystation perimeter and letting the south road go."

Jostin nods. He looks like he hasn't slept in weeks because he hasn't slept in weeks.

"Dismissed."

He leaves. Door closes.

Bracken stares at the dispatches. Alone in her office. Except she isn't alone.

I drop visibility. Sitting in her chair — the guest chair across from the desk. Visible. Munching on food. Staring at her.

---

Her sword is out before the jerky clears my teeth.

The blade kisses my throat. Fast. Professional. Twenty-six years of muscle memory in the draw.

"Who are you. How did you get in here. Start talking or start bleeding."

Her grey-green eyes are locked on my mask. She's not shaking. She's not scared. She's a woman who held a bridge with twelve soldiers against a cavalry charge and the scar on her jaw proves it.

I stare at her. Take in everything. Not the soldier — the woman. Five-eight, five-nine. Lean. Narrow waist under the leather armor. Modest chest. Strong legs. The grey-threaded hair in the soldier's knot shows the line of her neck. High cheekbones. The scar from ear to chin should be ugly. It isn't. It draws the eye to her face instead of away.

She's not beautiful the way Elda is. She's the kind of woman who looks better the longer you stare.

She can tell I'm staring.

"I'm here to help you, old lady. Elda's been reunited with her son, so now I'm here with you. And from what I'm seeing — you need some help." I glance at the sword. "Also, can you please get your stick out of my face? It's touching my throat and I think it's going to make me gag at this rate."

---

"Elda. The herbalist. And Halden, broken ankle. They were on the missing roster from the south road. Three days overdue." Her grip shifts on the sword. "Where are they."

"And I'm *forty-four*, you little shit. Not elderly."

The sword drops two inches. Still pointed at me. But she's listening.

"Start with your name. Then tell me how you found two missing civilians and walked through my garrison like an open market — and then I'll decide whether you get a cell or a conversation."

---

### The Gamble

---

I'm here for you specifically, actually. But before I ask a favor — do you like to gamble?

I lay out the terms. Duel. If I win, I get her for three days — she puts someone else in charge and comes with me. If she wins, I give her my name, the intel on the skeleton in the mist, and I enlist under her command.

"No."

She doesn't bend. Forty-two people. Closed road. Organized undead. Four months of unanswered letters. She's not gambling her post.

Fine.

"Well then I guess you're going to have to arrest me. From this point on I'm not talking until you accept my gamble. But I'll follow any physical instructions you give."

I shut up. The bit starts.

---

### The Cell

---

Converted storeroom. Iron-banded door, solid wood, no viewing slot. Barred window near the ceiling. Bench bolted to the wall. Bucket in the corner. Smells like old grain and damp stone.

They never ask for my weapons.

I sit. Chew jerky. The bit is the bit.

---

Forty-five minutes. The door opens.

Bracken. Outer armor off. Fitted linen shirt, belted at the waist, sleeves pushed to elbows. Sword on hip. She tells herself it's because the office was warm.

She drags a stool in. Sits across from me. The cell door stays open behind her.

"No swords, no guards, no rank. Just two people in a room."

She leans forward. Elbows on knees. Forearms bare — old training scars, campaign marks.

"I'm not gambling my post. But a cell isn't going to stop you and we both know it. So tell me what you actually want."

I stare her in the eye. "I think I already told you. I'm here to help you, old lady. You wanted to get me alone in a cell first for some reason, so... here we are. We really should do something about that undead, though. Let me know when you're ready to duel. Have the blacksmith make me a reverse katana so I don't hurt you."

"You're the most arrogant man I've ever met. And I served under General Cedwyn during the Thornwall Offensive, so that is a *high* bar."

She crosses her arms. "If I duel you — *if* — and you win. Three days. What exactly do you think happens in three days?"

We solve the undead problem. If you win, I'll join your army as a resource... to use... anyway you like, love.

Her ears go red.

She asks what happens if she just asks me to help. Because people are dying and I clearly can.

"Who *are* you?"

Softer than anything she's said since I met her.

---

### The Duel

---

I sit on the stool. Lean back. Arms crossed.

"Hmm, you're right. People are dying. Belay that last order, no reverse katana."

I walk my nodachi to the corner. Set it against the wall. Turn back. Crack my knuckles.

"I'll use my hands. Fight like your life depends on it — you can use your real sword. If I die, I have a letter on me with enemy intelligence that'll still help you win."

I don't have a letter.

"Come at me, toots."

She draws.

---

My body drops into something I've never been taught. Left foot forward, right heel raised, hands up — lead hand open and low, rear hand guarding the chin. Weight centered but mobile. It's not from this world. A muscle memory from before.

"*Hwaaaaaaaa.*"

She blinks.

The jab catches her on the nose. Palm strike. Straight line, no wind-up. Her head snaps back. Eyes water.

The kick folds her. Side kick to the solar plexus. She hits the far wall with her shoulder blades and slides down to one knee. Sword still in hand. Twenty-six years of *never let go*.

I hop on one leg. Jab the air. Swipe my thumb across my nose. Spit on the stone.

"You call this a fight, Captain? You must become like *waaaaaater*... and not be rigid like stone."

She adapts. Stops lunging. Goes defensive. Sword level, eyes on my hips. Waiting for the counter.

"I'm a *commander*, not a captain. And I've fought things faster than you."

She hasn't.

---

I take the top of the hakama off. Drop the hat. Drop the mask. Purple hair falls to my back and shoulders.

She sees me. All of me. Not the ronin. Not the mask. The face that has broken eighteen women across four books.

Her sword drops three inches.

I drop into a full split on the stone floor. Punching air. Casual. Hand on chin. Looking up at her midsection.

"I see, Captain... you are afraid. It is good to fear death. Or is it something else you fear?" I study her like a painting. "I wonder what you look like under that armor."

"You are *in the middle of a duel*." Her voice cracks. "Stand up."

---

Leg sweep from the split. She goes down. I take her boots before she can process what happened. Then the cuirass. Belt. Everything that isn't the linen shirt goes in the corner with the swords.

"People are dying. You really should take this seriously."

I wave an old piece of mail from my pack like it's battle plans. It's a receipt from Pip's inn.

---

She's on her back. Barefoot. Hair down. Looking up at me.

"Are you not going to fight, grandma? The strange man said he had enemy intel and he has your boots."

Something snaps.

She comes up swinging. The best swing of the fight — angry, technical, real. The longsword catches my left forearm. Thin line of blood. First blood. Mine.

I don't flinch.

She stands over me, breathing hard, sword extended, barefoot, hair in her face. She sees the cut. She sees it barely bleeding. She sees the muscle underneath.

"Who *are* you?"

---

I drop the boots. Roll backward.

"Hmm, I said fight like your life depends on it."

I sit on the stool. Arms crossed.

"But if you really want to yield, I'll give you an advantage. A different battle. Whoever makes the other finish five times first wins. Take off your clothes to let me know you consent. You don't consent — I accept your yield, duel's over, and I'm going to enjoy the help around my garden for three days."

---

The silence is absolute.

She walks to the door. Barefoot on cold stone. Each step deliberate.

The door closes. The latch clicks.

She turns around.

"If a single word leaves this cell — I don't care what you are, how fast you are, or how pretty your hair is. I will make the Thornwall Offensive look like a *garden party*."

The shirt comes off.

---

### Fade to Black

---

I walk up slow. Hand on her chin. Kiss her. And I channel — Creation energy, raw, unfiltered, pouring through the contact. The split lip seals. The bruise fades. The scar from her ear to her jaw — twenty years old, healed white — smooths and vanishes. The slash across her ribs. The puncture on her shoulder. Every mark the border put on her, erased in a kiss.

Her fingers find her jaw. Feel smooth skin. She trembles.

Then I pick her up.

---

The wall cracks. Floor to ceiling.

---

She pins me. Fifteen minutes. The stool breaks. The bench bolts groan. Lover's Vigor transmits on the first contact — her eyes shift, grey-green brightening with a golden ring around the pupils. +50% to all stats. She feels it hit like a second wind that doesn't stop.

She rides like she fights. Relentless. Tactical. She finds what works and stays there.

---

The cell bars bend. Two of them bow outward three inches. Stone dust showers from the ceiling anchor points. Creation energy heals every mark on her shoulders the moment it forms.

---

She's winning. 4-2.

I fall back. Drained. On the stone floor. For the first time since Book 1, I've been in a fight I'm actually losing.

She looks at me. Something clicks behind those Vigor-gold eyes. The same thing that clicked when she held the bridge with twelve soldiers.

She punches me in the jaw.

Vigor-buffed. Hits like a mace. Before I can recover she's on me — determined, furious, a woman who hasn't won anything in four months and is going to win *this*.

Then — before losing it herself — she forces herself up. The discipline is written across her whole body. She drops to her knees. One hand on my throat. The other where it needs to be. Her mouth finishes the job.

5-3. Bracken.

---

She sits back on the cold stone. Wipes her mouth with the back of her hand. The same gesture she used for the blood from her split lip an hour ago.

The cell is destroyed. Crack in the wall floor to ceiling. Two bars bent outward. The stool is kindling. The bench bolts half-pulled from stone. The door hanging crooked on its hinges.

She looks at the damage. Looks at me. Flat on my back. Purple hair on the floor.

"I win."

A pause.

"Now tell me your name. And tell me about the undead."
