### The circle

---

The siege was over and the surviving crew did not know what to do with the information.

Two days of ridge fire and rationed water had compressed them into the wagon circle the way siege compresses everything — tight, quiet, operating on a mathematics of fear that did not update when the mathematics changed. The bandits were gone. The ridgeline was clear. A masked swordsman and a half-orc woman in dragon-scale had broken the rim in under fifteen minutes and the survivors were sitting in the same defensive formation they had held for forty-eight hours because standing up meant admitting the standing-up was safe, and admitting safety after prolonged fear is slower work than anyone who has not been besieged understands.

Mursha had gone up the ridge. She had taken the lieutenant into the dark between the tree line and the fire, and she had not come back yet, and the ronin did not explain where she had gone because the ronin did not explain things. He sat with the crew. He shared what the Bag of Holding carried — dried bread, cold meat, a flask of apple cider that had been purchased in a market town two days and a lifetime ago — and the sharing was not generosity but proximity, the specific kindness of a man who understood that the people around him needed someone who was not leaving.

The driver's mate ate bread from his hand. She was young, sun-dark, shaking in the way people shake when the shaking has nowhere left to go, and the bread was the first thing she had been offered in two days that was not fear or patience. She did not say thank you. She said nothing. She ate and her hands stopped shaking by the third bite, and the ronin sat beside her and did not move because not moving was the only medicine he had for a wound he could not touch.

The coalition-sealed crates in the wagon circle caught the last of the fire. Manifests intact. Cargo accounted for. The pack-train had survived with its paper, its people, and the specific gratitude of a crew that would remember a masked swordsman who shared bread and a woman in new armor who broke a siege — and would remember nothing else about them, because the nothing else was engineered.

### Return from the ridge

---

Mursha came back.

She came down the approach in dragon-scale that caught the fire differently now — blood on the left pauldron where a bandit had found her before she had found him, and something in her stride that was not injury but recalibration. The kind of walk a fighter has after doing something that used muscles the fighter does not discuss.

The intel was in her head. Handoff points. Factor names. Bronzebarrow leak threads — the operational anatomy of a bandit cell that had been fed coalition shipping schedules by someone who knew which trains carried sealed manifests and which routes those trains took. She had gotten the information the old way, the orcish way, the way that the contract mind in her did not put on any ledger and the fighter habits did not include in any morning equipment check. The lieutenant's constitution had failed against a DC that was built to break it, and the information had come out in the order she required, and the +2 STR that settled into her baseline without ceremony was a number changing in a body that was already carrying numbers it did not fully understand.

She did not tell the ronin what she had done. She sat beside him at the fire and said the intelligence was secured and her voice was professional and her composure was the composure of a woman who had just done something she would not discuss and had done it well.

He knew. He had watched from thirty feet, invisible, silent, carrying a new piece of knowledge that she did not know he carried. But the fire was warm and the crew was stabilizing and the night had already held enough weight without adding the weight of naming what he had seen, so he filed it alongside everything else he had filed that day and the filing was quiet.

Three secrets. The flicker. The sword. The watch. She carried two. He carried all three.

### Inventory

---

Mursha took the manifest.

She did it without being asked — the contract mind finding its lane the way water finds the lowest ground. The pack-train clerk was a thin man with a ledger he had been clutching for two days like a talisman against siege mathematics, and Mursha's factor-house training read his columns faster than he could explain them. Coalition cargo accounted for. Crates matched seals. The strongbox contents reconciled against the manifest's ink, and the reconciliation was clean because the bandits had not breached the circle — they had starved it, not looted it.

The ronin worked the ridge.

The bandit hoard was not a single cache but a distributed operation — relay float in sealed pouches, cut purses from earlier jobs, a ridge cache that the lieutenant had maintained with the discipline of a man who understood that siege required logistics. The mathematics of a two-day operation against a coalition-sealed train suggested scale, and the scale suggested coin, and the coin confirmed the suggestion. **Five hundred gold pieces** and **eighteen silver** came out of the ridge in stages — pouches, lock-boxes, a leather satchel buried under a cairn that the bandits had built to mark their patience. The DM retcon was honest: an operation this size did not carry forty-two gold. It carried what an operation this size carried.

The purse updated. **+500 GP, +18 SP.** The train's manifest cargo stayed with the clerk and with Mursha's count — the ronin did not touch coalition property. The distinction mattered because distinctions always matter to a man who stole a floor coffer two days ago and has been operating on the correct side of the line ever since.

### Night road

---

They left the switchback at something past eleven by the stars and walked the western cut back toward Bronzebarrow in a darkness that was not hostile but was not friendly either. The trail that had narrowed under their feet three hours ago widened in reverse — the suggestion becoming a road becoming a trace that remembered it had once been maintained, the forest giving back what it had been reclaiming, the wheel ruts filling with the specific quality of night that relay corridors wore when the forge fires at their end had been banked for hours.

Three hours of walking. No speech. The silence between two people who had fought together and one of whom had done something private on a ridge and the other of whom had watched it — that silence had a texture, and the texture was not uncomfortable but was not simple. She walked beside him. Not behind. The positioning had not changed since the afternoon and the positioning still said what it said: two people on the same road moving in the same direction because neither had told the other to stop.

The crown road appeared as light before it appeared as road — Bronzebarrow's forge-quarter glow reaching through the trees in the amber-orange frequency of banked iron and late-shift lanterns. Relay towns did not sleep. They dimmed. The gates were open because the gates of a toll-and-weigh town stayed open for the traffic that justified the toll, and the traffic at one-thirty in the morning was a masked swordsman and a half-orc woman in dragon-scale who smelled like cold air and someone else's siege.

### The room

---

The Brass Hitch admitted them without commentary. Common desk. Ledger. The night clerk — not Calista, someone older, someone whose job was the hours between midnight and dawn when the guests who arrived were the guests who had reasons for arriving late — took coin and produced a key and the transaction was as clean as transactions are when both parties understand that questions cost more than rooms.

Standard room. **Six gold, four silver.** Walls, a latch, a bed that was horizontal and was not the ground. Mursha dropped her pack. The ronin dropped his. The room was adequate the way adequate means *better than a switchback* and worse than what the night deserved.

He upgraded.

The luxury suite was available because luxury suites in relay towns are always available at two in the morning — the guests who can afford them arrive at civilized hours, and the guests who arrive at uncivilized hours cannot usually afford them, and the gap between those two facts was a gap that a man carrying fifteen hundred gold pieces could close with a sentence and eighteen more gold and six more silver.

**Suite 2.** Bronze ley-steam tub — the dwarf-engineered plumbing that Bronzebarrow's factor-houses had installed because relay commerce attracted the kind of money that wanted hot water after a long road. Featherbed. Privacy latch. The kind of room that existed so that the people who paid for it could stop pretending they were fine with adequate.

### Steam and steel

---

The late tray arrived because late trays arrived when you paid for a suite at two in the morning and the kitchen understood the request. Apple pie — the relay-town kind, dense pastry and spiced filling, the food of people who baked for workers who ate with their hands. Tea in a glazed pot with iron bands. Calista or the night kitchen — the tray did not announce its source and the source did not matter. What mattered was that the food was hot and the tea was strong and the pie was the first thing the ronin had eaten since sharing bread with a shaking driver's mate at a switchback fire.

He took the bath.

The ley-steam tub filled from a bronze pipe that hummed at the frequency of dwarven alloy under pressure, and the steam that rose from the surface carried the mineral signature of water that had been heated by something older than fire. The ronin stripped — mask off in the steam because the steam was privacy and the latch was closed and the only person in the room was a woman who had already seen his face in a different suite two nights ago. He sank into water that was too hot in the way that too-hot water is medicine after a fight, and the heat found muscles that had been carrying a nodachi and a ridgeline and three secrets, and the muscles surrendered to the temperature because muscles are honest about what they need even when the person carrying them is not.

The pie was within reach. The tea was within reach. The man who had vaporized a bandit on a ridge three hours ago ate pastry in a bubble bath and watched the woman across the room through steam that softened everything into suggestion.

Mursha did not take the bath. She took the table.

The masterwork longsword came out first — the +3 blade she had owned for less than twelve hours, the 3d12 slashing edge that Marthel had fitted to her grip that morning. Oil. Cloth. The specific discipline of a fighter who maintained steel the way other people maintained faith — methodically, without shortcuts, because the blade that failed you was the blade you had not cleaned. The warhammer followed. The armor-break head inspected for ridge damage, the haft checked for stress where the oval face had met shields that had not been designed for the force behind it. Dragon-scale last — the laminate that Marthel had cleaned and fitted, checked now for blood that was not hers and for seams that a ridge fight might have tested.

She worked and she wrote. The intel from the ridge lieutenant — handoff points, factor names, leak threads — scratched to paper in a hand that was neat because contract hands were always neat. Notes for morning. Notes for whoever would receive the intelligence that a bandit cell on the western cut had been fed coalition manifests by someone inside the Bronzebarrow relay network. The kind of intelligence that would mean something when delivered, and would travel as information — clean, sourceless — because the source required an explanation she was not prepared to give.

Her pace. Her voice low when she spoke, which was rarely. The sound of oil on steel and a quill on paper while the steam thinned and the pie cooled and the hours between two and four in the morning became the hours they were: quiet, domestic, the specific shape of two people sharing a room after violence who had not yet decided what the sharing meant but had decided that it was not nothing.

### Predawn

---

The tub cooled. The lamps dimmed. The ronin dried and dressed in the linen underlayer that was not armor but was not nothing, and Mursha finished her kit discipline with the economy of someone who expected dawn to ask something of her. Steel oiled. Intel written. Dragon-scale hung on the stand where it could breathe overnight. She claimed the featherbed with the professional composure of a woman who was tired and was not going to pretend otherwise.

The private beat followed because private beats follow when two people who are intimate share a featherbed after a fight, and the beat was theirs — player-declared, table-owned, the specific fiction of a bond that renewed what bonds renew when the body is willing and the terms have not been renegotiated since the last time.

**Lover's Vigor** held. The five-day window that had opened in a different suite two nights ago reinforced through the bond per table mechanics — the bronze-gold eye ring that was Mursha's tell still present in the predawn half-light, the +50% stats still running on a body that was already carrying a baseline STR of 22 and a set of masterwork weapons she had broken a siege with. The immunity to his aura held with it. She was not under the influence of the DC 23 passive. She was under the influence of her own choices, and the choices had led her to a featherbed in a luxury suite with a man who carried secrets she did not know about and pie crumbs in his hair from a bath he had taken while she cleaned steel.

The ridge notes waited for morning. The Oathbreaker question from the forge waited for morning. The Cassia investigation in the yard waited for morning. Three secrets waited for a conversation that had not happened yet and might not happen tomorrow and might not happen the day after that, because some conversations wait until the person carrying them decides that the weight of carrying is heavier than the weight of speaking, and the ronin had not reached that decision yet.

Dawn came in through the suite's east-facing window as a suggestion — Ashmere 36, Forgeday, the kind of light that relay towns wore when the forges started their first heat and the yard bells began their morning count. The masked swordsman and the half-orc woman slept through the first of it because they had earned the sleep and the sleep was the most honest thing either of them had done all day.

Day 258. Ashmere 36. Predawn. The Brass Hitch, Bronzebarrow.
