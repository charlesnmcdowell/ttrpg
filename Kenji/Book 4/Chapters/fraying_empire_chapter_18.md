### Ghost egress

**Ashmere 34**, dawn — forty minutes before first bell.

He dressed in silence because silence was what the room demanded, and left the captain's quarters the way a man leaves a place he was not supposed to be: with the latch trick she had shown him and the corridor sense he had earned from three days of pretending four hundred paces was a safe distance.

The garrison was already waking underneath him — runners, chalk slates, the clatter of a kettle someone was angry at. A runner knocked on the captain's door as he cleared the officer wing threshold, and the Stealth check that mattered was not against the runner but against the architecture: the floorboard that knew his weight, the hinge that had an opinion about guests. Both held their silence. Bracken's attention went to the handoff at her door, not to the corridor behind it, and the beat closed without a question asked in the wrong direction.

He caught Brynn in the secure-corridor traffic — or rather, he kept pace with her from thirty feet back and two shadows to the left, because watching someone you have already complicated is a different verb than following them. She moved through admin-heavy routing, the part of garrison mornings where shield-bearers become errand geometry. He peeled off when her route turned paper-ward and his turned elsewhere.

No contact. No words. The gap between them had a shape he was not ready to name.

He left Thornkeep through the north gate as a masked traveler with a nodachi peace-bonded to his back, and no one at the sergeant's ledger asked where he had slept, because the ledger does not have a column for that.

### Crown road

The road north out of Thornkeep ran like a promise someone had started keeping and then forgotten — first mile paved, second mile graveled, third mile clay ruts and honest apology. The border was behind him. The quiet he had told Bracken to check was still quiet, which proved nothing except that silence was not the same thing as safety and he had known that when he said it.

Mile three. A coalition-marked supply train — ox hung in a wet rut, near-axle creak, the sound wood makes when it is thinking about becoming two pieces. A driver and a merchant's boy waved down traffic with the particular desperation of people whose problem is too heavy for their arms and too small for their pride.

He did not announce himself. He walked to the stuck wheel, found the tongue-bite where leverage lived, set his shoulder against it, and waited for the driver to find the count.

Athletics: the ox lurched forward onto firm crown, the rut broke, the near-axle unloaded its complaint. The merchant pressed five silver pieces into his hand the way people press coin into the hands of strangers who solve problems without asking for a name — grateful, quick, and already looking past him toward the road that worked again.

He pocketed the silver. The train rolled north. The mask was noted politely.

### Industry smoke

By midday the road had changed languages. Southern relay — Thornkeep, Millhaven, the garrison cadence of boots and chalk — had given way to something older and wider: feeder ruts joining from an eastern clay road, converging wheel marks that spoke of tonnage, not troops. The air carried industry smoke — not battle oil, not the sweet-ash of the March — and underneath it, a bell note. Bronze-green. Dwarven alloy, the harmonic that meant a town had been weighing things before anyone put a crown on the road.

Working name in his head: somewhere with forges and ledgers and people who counted before they fought.

He read the approach the way he read all approaches — what the smoke said about fuel, what the ruts said about frequency, what the bell said about who lived here and whether they were the kind of people who asked a masked man his business or the kind who asked his coin.

The last two miles he covered in Wind Step — cloud-stepping over the worst of the clay, touching down on packed stone where the gate road firmed. Two charges spent. The gate sergeant gestured at his nodachi with the tired patience of a man who had gestured at a hundred weapons that week, and Kenji peace-bonded it to his back in the manner of a traveler who understood the gesture was not a request.

**Bronzebarrow.** The name arrived the same way the town did — not announced, just present, like a fact that had been waiting for someone to walk into it.

### The Brass Hitch

The common room smelled like stew and commerce and the particular warmth of a place where three species ate at the same tables without anyone writing a treaty about it. Dwarf voices and halfling laughter and human argument braided into a noise that was not quite music and not quite war.

He ordered mutton stew, black bread, and small beer — four silver pieces paid because the economy at this latitude did not care about his name — and ate in the common room with his mask down far enough for the spoon and not an inch farther.

She was across the room before the stew was gone.

Half-orc. Caravan guard build — heavy delts and biceps that came from years of spear-and-shield work, not gym vanity. Dark metal breastplate in a minimal cut, segmented spaulder on the right shoulder, olive-grey cloak draped across the neck. Jet-black cornrows pulled tight, silver ring cuffs on the braids catching lamplight. One scar through the right eyebrow. A second on the cheek. Ears long and pointed enough to announce the lineage before the tusks did — small, steel-capped, visible only when her mouth moved past neutral.

Dark brown eyes. They quartered the room exits and his hands before they found his face.

Mursha.

### Hundred gold

He set a hundred gold pieces on the table the way a man sets a stone on a map — not an offer, a marker. The kind of money that meant something different depending on who was looking at it and what they were willing to name.

She did not grab the coin. She named the purchase.

Escort or buyout — those were the shapes the gold could take, and she was not the kind of woman who let money sit on a surface without filing it into a category. Contract mind. Iron Mule diction. The words came out organized because her life was organized around the fact that people paid for her presence and she kept the receipts.

He gave her the shape: off the Iron Mule roster. Private to him. Do as she pleases. A year framed as travel, as seeing the world, as the kind of freedom that costs someone else's gold.

The coin stayed on the table. The Mule contract was still unpriced.

Her eyes stayed on his face too long.

The aura did what the aura always does — not charm, not domination, just the pressure that lives inside proximity when the DC does not negotiate with willpower. Her WIS save hit the floor: natural one, plus one, total two against twenty-three. The kind of failure that does not feel like failure from the inside — it feels like noticing, like the room getting smaller, like the man at the table suddenly being the only thing in it with edges sharp enough to cut.

Stack eight. Fixation heat. Not mind control — she could still refuse on duty, on policy, on the personal code that kept her upright in rooms full of drunk teamsters. But the refusal would cost more than it had cost five minutes ago.

**"Let's go talk to them now,"** he said, and Persuasion rolled with advantage because the stack was high enough to earn it: seventeen and thirteen, plus eleven. Twenty-eight against seventeen. She had wanted to say *tomorrow morning.* She said *now* instead, and stood like a woman who had made a decision and wanted to believe it was hers.

He scooped the hundred gold off the table before following her. The common room watched the scoop the way common rooms watch money move — with interest, without comment.

### Double

The Iron Mule factor yard was four blocks south and one species of architecture removed — dwarven lease stone, iron-banded loading gates, the smell of ox and ink and contractual obligation. On the walk there, between the Hitch and the yard, Kenji asked for the introduction and then stopped talking, because the next part required a different version of him.

The walk cost her another save — eight against the same twenty-three, and the result was the same. Stack nine. She did not notice it as a number. She noticed it as four blocks of walking beside a man and finding his jawline instead of the road twice before the factor yard came into view.

Smoke-Invis-Clone. One charge spent. The smoke popped in the alley between two factor-houses, and the real Kenji went invisible while the double walked out beside Mursha — same height, same mask, same silence.

Not the same man. The Deception check to sell the illusion as genuine rolled a seven plus five: twelve against fifteen. Failure. Mursha caught something she could not name yet — a hairline wrongness in the weight, the breath, the timing. The kind of thing a fighter notices because fighters live inside the gap between what the body promises and what it delivers, and this body was delivering the wrong promise.

She did not say it out loud. The wrongness filed itself somewhere behind her eyes where contract minds keep things they cannot price yet.

The Persuasion to keep her walking rolled nineteen and one, advantage, plus eleven. Thirty against fourteen. She walked.

Inside the yard, invisible, Kenji listened while Mursha did the talking.

### Factor's desk

Cassia Vorn ran the Bronzebarrow office the way a receipt runs a transaction — clean, complete, and aware of every line item. Human, or close enough that the distinction did not matter at desk height. Numbers-first voice. Stamp-and-seal posture. Ink smudge on the dominant hand because the pen was a weapon she never put down.

She quoted Mursha's early release without looking at anyone in particular, because the numbers spoke to the room and the room was expected to listen:

Fifty-two gold, contract buyout. Twelve gold, spring-roster break fee. Six gold, factor processing. Ten silver for the optional Coalition severance stamp — the notary line that made the separation legal on paper instead of merely legal in practice.

Total: seventy gold, ten silver. Ninety-day non-compete on the same crown relay band. No advance clawback — Mursha's ledger was clean this season.

The double stood mute at the desk because doubles did not have opinions. Mursha covered the conversation the way she covered everything — with the competence of a woman who had been managing her own exit since before the gold appeared on a tavern table.

Kenji — the real one, invisible, three feet from Cassia's desk — did two things at once.

First: Sleight of Hand. Seventy gold and ten silver appeared at the double's feet as if the man had dropped them from a pocket that did not care about dignity. Advantage roll: seventeen and four, plus five. Twenty-two against seventeen. Cassia did not catch the palm trick. The double shrugged, pointed down. Mursha read the gesture, slid the coin across the desk. Cassia counted. The stamp came down.

Second: Perception. Fourteen against fourteen — exact, the kind of success that felt like eavesdropping on a secret the room was whispering. Under the desk, rear-right, a lift-board concealed an iron floor coffer. Ring key on Cassia's belt. She dropped the seventy gold into it, locked it, pocketed the key with the muscle memory of a woman who locked things every day and had never yet been given a reason to wonder if that was enough.

Mursha was free. The paperwork said so. The stamp said so. The ninety-day non-compete said so in smaller print.

The double left the office wordless, walking toward the Brass Hitch with a come-hither gesture that was either an invitation or a command depending on who was reading it. Mursha followed. The real Kenji, still invisible, stayed behind.

### Coffer

What happened next was theft.

Not the kind of theft that apologists dress up in necessity or the kind that moralists strip down to greed — just theft, clean and professional, committed by a man who had already decided to take the money before the stamp hit the paper and was now executing the decision with the same calm he brought to everything else that other people would have called wrong.

He doubled back. Still Greater Invisibility. Still silent in the Windstrider boots that left no prints and made no sound on dwarven stone.

Stealth: nineteen and eighteen, advantage, plus five. Twenty-four against nineteen. He was not in the room. He had never been in the room. The room did not remember him because the room had never met him.

The lift-board came up with the finesse of a man who had been reading locks and levers since before locks and levers were the most dangerous thing about him. The floor coffer was heavy — iron and coin and the weight of Cassia Vorn's professional trust.

Sleight of Hand: fifteen and ten, advantage, plus five. Twenty against twenty. Exact. The kind of margin that would have been failure if the die had rolled one number lower, and the kind of success that would give a philosopher material for weeks about the nature of fortune and whether a man who slides into a room by the skin of his numbers deserves what he finds there.

The coffer went into the Bag of Holding. Inside extradimensional silence, he opened it and emptied the contents into his purse: four hundred and eleven gold, one hundred and fifty silver. His buyout money returned, plus the relay float — the working cash Cassia kept for road expenses, factor advances, and the daily commerce of keeping a freight operation liquid.

He kept the empty iron box. Not as a trophy — as evidence management. An empty coffer in the Bag of Holding was a coffer that was not on Cassia's floor looking robbed.

Cassia would discover the loss at morning reconciliation, Ashmere 35. The masked ronin and the same-day guard release would sit in the same timeline, and timelines are the thing that investigators correlate when they have nothing else to hold.

But that was tomorrow's problem, and tomorrow was a long way from the suite he had already paid for.

### Flicker

The swap back to himself happened on the approach to the Brass Hitch, in the stretch of street where the double was walking Mursha toward the door and the real Kenji needed to be the one who walked through it.

Sleight of Hand, advantage: eight and five, plus five. Thirteen against eighteen.

Failure. The seam showed. Not the full truth — not *this man is a magical construct and the real one is standing behind you wearing a different kind of silence* — but the flicker. The half-second where the double's edges went wrong and the real Kenji's edges went right, and anyone watching with a fighter's eye for the gap between real and performed would have seen the splice.

Mursha saw it.

She did not run. She did not swing. Her eyes tracked the wrongness the way they tracked exits and weapons — filing, categorizing, deciding how much of her next minute to spend on a question she could not yet frame in words that would not sound insane.

*Magic. Twin. Trick. Something.* The file stayed open behind her eyes.

Persuasion, advantage: eighteen and five, plus eleven. Twenty-nine against fifteen.

**"Follow me."**

She followed. Not because the words were magic — because the alternative was standing on a Bronzebarrow street with a question she did not want to ask out loud and a stack of aura pressure that made asking feel less important than moving.

Thirty gold bought Suite Two at the Brass Hitch: bronze-rune steam tub, featherbed, privacy latch, and the kind of walls that a relay-town inn builds when it wants guests to believe their business is their own. Lobster and steak, sent to the room by way of Calista — the barmaid, human, warm enough to smile about a big order without asking what the occasion was.

### Unmask

The suite door latched behind them, and the room became small in the way rooms become small when two people are in them and one of them has been running the math on the other since the common room.

He took off the mask. Then the hat. Then the shirt, because the tub was already heating — bronze runes in the stone basin doing what dwarven engineering does when you pour water over it and wait — and a man who is about to get into a bath does not need a shirt to negotiate.

Her eyes did the thing eyes do when the DC arrives in person.

Irresistible Presence. Mursha's WIS save: fifteen plus one, sixteen against twenty-three. Failure. Not the floor-drop of the natural one in the common room — a slower failure, the kind that feels like choosing to look and then discovering that looking is the same as falling.

Stack ten. Four hours and fifteen minutes since the first failure at the tavern table. Four minutes of hard line-of-sight on the full face, unmasked, in lamplight and steam.

He ran the bubble bath. Bronze-rune heat did the rest.

### Armor

He asked the question the way he asked all questions that mattered — plainly, without decoration, as if the answer would not change the architecture of the next hour.

The door was hers. He needed skills, not submission. If she stayed, could he see her without the armor.

Mursha stayed.

She stripped the armor the way fighters strip armor — methodically, by the numbers, because ten thousand repetitions had turned every strap and catch into muscle memory that did not require the mind's permission. Breastplate first, the minimal-cut dark metal that cleared her shoulders for spear work. Segmented spaulder from the right shoulder. Thigh plates, heavy on the unstrap. Dark leather forearm wraps, unwound. Olive-grey cloak already off from before.

What remained was road binding-wrap and linen — the underlayer that lived between a fighter's skin and the world's opinion of it. The braids stayed. The silver ring cuffs stayed. The scars stayed, because scars are not equipment.

She stood in the suite lamplight in the space between armored and not, and the body underneath was the body the tavern portrait had promised: heavy delts, powerful biceps, the narrow toned waist and thick muscular thighs of a woman who had been carrying a spear and shield for long enough that her frame had organized itself around the job.

The IP stack was ten. Consent was verbal and bodily, co-present, simultaneous — not Vigor, not charm, not the DC pretending to be a decision. She had chosen. The choice was recorded.

### Tub

He stripped — hakama, boots, gloves, the ronin kit that made him a traveler instead of whatever he actually was — and joined her in the bronze-rune tub.

The water was too hot the way all good baths are too hot: a punishment the body wants. Steam turned the suite into a smaller version of itself, and inside that smaller version the only clear things were hands and faces and the space between them.

He took her foot and started mapping the calluses.

Foot massage. Fighter hands reading fighter feet — the pressure points that said *spear stance, left lead*, the wear pattern that said *shield wall, not skirmish line*, the old breaks that said *I did not stop walking when the healer told me to stop walking*. She let him.

The fourth save of the afternoon died the same quiet death as the others — seven plus one against a number that had not changed and was not going to. But the fiction around it had changed. Contact. Steam. Full unmasked presence at range zero. The stack climbed past ten into the band where the aura stopped being something she could file and became something she was living inside.

Her body was honest. Her mind was still hers. The distinction mattered, because IP is not mind control and a woman whose mind is still her own can still look a man in the face and say *no* — it is just that saying *no* costs more when the body and the aura and the steam are all saying something different, and cost is not the same as coercion.

She did not say no.

---

### Children

He said it in the tub because the tub was where the words lived and he was not the kind of man who waited for better timing when worse timing would do.

He wanted her to bear his children.

Mursha did not say yes. She did not say no. She said *not here* — not in the steam, not with the aura, not with the pressure of proximity turning every answer into something she could not trust to be sober.

She named the IP by its shape if not its name: the thing in the room that made her want to agree with whatever he said, the thing that meant she could not sign a life into existence in water hot enough to confuse wanting with deciding.

She needed sober air. She needed terms — his name, a roof, knowledge of his other oaths, what happens when he vanishes. She needed time.

The children ask logged in the fiction as intent, not agreement. The thread stayed open the way a door stays open when someone has put their hand on the frame but has not yet stepped through.

### Qualifying

What came next was hers.

She rode because that was how she wanted it — her terms, her pace, her satisfaction framed as the thing the act was for, not his. He watched. He received. The distinction was not academic; it was the difference between a woman who had been persuaded into a room and a woman who had decided what to do with the room once she was in it.

**Lover's Vigor** triggered at the qualifying moment. The clock started: five days from this hour, tracked in the engine, the bronze-gold ring appearing at the pupil edge of her dark brown eyes like forge-light on green-tan skin.

The strength hit her body the way Vigor always hits — not a new woman, a sharpened one. Fifty percent to everything. The arms that had been heavy with spear-work were heavier now. The thighs that had been thick with years of stance-work were denser. She was still Mursha. She was Mursha with the volume turned up.

IP-immune for the window. She could look at him now without the save spiral, and looking at him without the save spiral meant looking at him for the first time as a decision made by a mind that owed nothing to the pressure in the room.

She looked.

---

### Night

He asked if it was his turn. She invited him to the bed.

The suite night went the way suite nights go when a Level 35 man and a Vigor-buffed half-orc fighter run out of reasons to pretend that what they are doing is anything other than what they are doing: lifted, held, sequences that tested the architecture of a featherbed and found it adequate in ways its builder had probably not anticipated.

Multiple rounds. Multiple finishes. Hours. The bronze-rune tub cooled and no one reheated it because the bed was a better argument.

The children topic did not resolve in the sheets. She had said *not here, not in steam*, and the bed was not steam but it was not sober air either, and she was the kind of woman who kept her word about the conditions under which she would say yes even when the conditions for saying yes were trying very hard to be met.

### Dawn ledger

**Ashmere 35**, luxury suite — long rest complete.

He woke in a featherbed that smelled like two kinds of sweat and one kind of decision, and the half-orc beside him was still breathing the deep slow breath of a woman who had earned unconsciousness.

Dawn came through the Brass Hitch window the way dawn comes through relay-town glass — grey-green, tinted by the forge smoke that Bronzebarrow breathed before breakfast. The dwarven bell that had called him into town the day before was already sounding the first work quarter, and somewhere below the suite a kitchen was making noises that promised bread.

Slots and charges refreshed at table. HP full — three hundred and sixty-eight of three hundred and sixty-eight. Wind Step five of five. Smoke-Invis-Clone three of three. The long rest had done its mechanical work while the personal work lay beside him with a bronze-gold ring at the edge of her eyes that she had not yet seen in a mirror.

Mursha stirred. Not awake — the precursor to awake, the moment where the body remembers it is not alone and decides whether that memory is a threat or a comfort. Her hand found his arm in the way fighters' hands find things in the dark: fast, accurate, and then deliberately gentle when the target turns out to be warm.

Bonded Lovers: twenty. The number meant something to the engine and nothing to the woman, and that was the correct distribution of meaning.

Downstairs, Cassia Vorn was about to open her factor ledger and discover that the floor coffer under her desk was not where floors leave coffers. The morning reconciliation would fail. The investigation would begin. The timeline would be the thing she looked at — the masked ronin who paid for a guard release at three in the afternoon and the floor coffer that vanished between three-thirty and close of business — and timelines do not lie even when the people inside them do.

But that was the morning's problem, and the morning was still young, and the woman beside him had a bronze-gold ring in her eyes and a question about children that he had asked and she had not yet answered, and the world outside the suite window was a town he had never been to before yesterday and might never come back to, and for a moment — one moment, between the bell and the bread — the ronin mask and the archmagus underneath it were both quiet, and the man who wore them was just a man in a bed with a woman who had chosen to stay.
