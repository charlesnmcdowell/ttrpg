# Book 4 — Fraying Empire
## Chapter 15: Root and Steam

**Date:** Ashmere 29–30, 1247 AR (Rootday dawn → Forgeday dawn)
**Location:** East verge mile ~17 → south road crown (solo march) → Pallid March forward cordon (root-crown amphitheater, circulation heart, guest chamber)

---

### The Split

---

Rootday dawn. Frost on the tent canvas. Frost on the birch bark. The mist sat low and thin on the east verge, ghosting between tree trunks, and the sky above it was the color of steel before it heats.

Jostin had broth going. His journal was open. He had not looked directly at Brynn since she emerged from the tent.

Kenji told them what he'd already decided.

Jostin and Brynn north. Thornkeep. Bracken. The Coalition paper spine — dispatches, maps, reinforcement timelines, the things that keep armies standing when the ronin walks away from them. Safety that could be explained to captains and councils. Jostin would file his field report. Brynn would rejoin her squad rotation. The geometry would hold.

Kenji south. Alone.

Not because courage is a monologue and not because the road asked politely. Because the March's command vector — the voice from the deep marsh, the thing that built nineteen months of infrastructure and sent a death knight to guard a seal — would not come to him if he stayed polite beside other people's fires. It came to its own table. He would go to the table.

Jostin closed his journal. The pen stopped for three seconds — the longest Jostin's pen ever stopped — and then resumed. He wrote something. Crossed it out. Wrote it again differently.

"That's either brilliant or suicidal."

He'd said the same thing at mile eight. The answer was the same both times.

Brynn said nothing. She was standing with her shield on her arm and her amber-gold eyes on the ronin and her jaw set in the way it set when she was holding a line she hadn't been ordered to hold. She wanted to argue. Infantry discipline said she couldn't — the commanding officer had given an order, and the commanding officer was the man she'd spent last night underneath.

She held the line.

"Watch-fire geometry stays as language," Jostin said. He was already rebuilding the signal pattern in his head — east ring, crown sightline from the copse, proof the corridor remembered discipline even when the ronin walked away from it. "We keep the fires on stagger until we're inside Bracken's perimeter. If you need extraction—"

He stopped. Looked at the ronin. Looked at the nodachi across his back. Looked at the bag that was still humming.

"You won't need extraction."

---

They split at the copse's north edge. Jostin first — bow across his chest, journal in his belt, moving through frost-grass toward the birch line with the efficiency of a man who had walked this road ten times and stopped being afraid of it three relays ago. Brynn followed. She looked back once. The amber-gold eyes caught the morning light and held it for a heartbeat too long.

Then the mist took them.

Kenji turned south.

---

### Crown March

---

Plain miles. The road south of mile seventeen was territory the party had already bled — former node sites, former sentry lines, the ghost infrastructure of a relay network that had taken nineteen months to build and thirty-six hours to die. The dead zone was dying. Grass pushed through frost at the road's edges. A bird — a real bird, alive, brown and small — startled from a hedge at mile fifteen and fled east. First living wildlife on this road in months.

Kenji walked the crown. No Wind Step unless the road argued. No clone — the last person who needed deceiving was the one he was walking toward. The nodachi rode his shoulder. The Bag of Holding hung at his hip, and the hum from inside it had settled into something lower and steadier overnight, as if Oathbreaker had stopped screaming and started breathing.

The mist thickened past mile thirteen. The root network woke beneath his feet — the circulation infrastructure, the thing that was not relay but *terrain*. Living Ground contracted to forty feet. The sensory web registered his passage the way a spider's web registers a fly — not with violence, but with attention. The roots knew he was here. They had known since mile fourteen. They were not sending skeletons.

They were routing.

At mile eighteen — where Corwyn had stood yesterday, where the wheel ruts still held the furrows of a death knight's boots — the road was empty. Corwyn was gone. The arm-shaped indent in the frost where he had stood was three hours old, already filling with new ice. He had walked, or been pulled, or simply decided that standing on a road without a sword was not what paladins do.

South of mile eighteen the road changed.

Not the surface — the packed dirt was the same dirt that had been the same dirt since the road was laid. But the root network surfaced. Thick trunks, each wider than a man's leg, broke through the road's eastern ditch and arched across the packed center before diving underground on the western side. Green nodules pulsed along them — slow, rhythmic, always south. The mist sat heavy between the root arches, chest-high, and the air tasted like turned soil and copper and something chemical underneath that the back of the throat wanted to name but couldn't.

Living Ground contracted to thirty feet. Then twenty.

At mile twenty the road stopped pretending to be a road.

### The Forward Cordon

---

The root-crown amphitheater was not a camp. It was not a fortress. It was not a throne room. It was all three and it was none of them, because the thing it most resembled was a *heart* — a circulatory junction where six trunk lines converged from the south and spread into the sensory web that covered forty miles of Ashenveil border.

The roots formed walls. Not constructed — grown. They arched overhead in a lattice that filtered the mist into columns of grey light, each column landing on a different patch of bare earth in a pattern that was either random or deliberate in a language older than the road. The floor was packed root-matter, smooth, warm underfoot through boot leather. The air was ten degrees warmer than the road.

Green light pulsed through the walls. Through the floor. Through the ceiling lattice. The circulation rhythm was visible here — the heart beating, the network breathing, the Pallid March's infrastructure doing the thing infrastructure does when it's alive and the thing controlling it is nearby.

Two thralls flanked the entrance. Not skeletons — living thralls. Human. Male. Young. Root-collars at their throats, tendrils descending into the floor. Their eyes were open. Their faces were calm in the wrong way — not peaceful, *managed*. The expression of people whose expressions were being selected for them by something that understood what faces should do in the presence of visitors.

They did not speak. They did not move. They watched him walk past with eyes that tracked without blinking.

---

The room opened.

She sat in the center of it.

Not on a throne — on a root-structure that had shaped itself into something between a seat and a dais, organic, alive, the root-matter curving around her hips and supporting her back the way a chair built specifically for one body would. The seat grew from the floor. It was the floor. The floor was the heart. She sat in the heart of her own circulatory system.

Human form.

Living Ground read her before conscious observation caught up: alive. Not undead. Not lich-form. Real heartbeat — slow, measured, the resting rate of something that breathed every eleven seconds and had been doing so for centuries. Real warmth — body heat radiating from skin that was genuine skin, not illusion, not glamour. Metabolism at one percent, ley-suspended, but the body was *present*. The body was *real*.

She was tall. Full-figured. The kind of frame that fills a room before she speaks — broad hips, strong thighs, defined waist, shoulders that carried authority the way Faelindra's carried armor. Deep brown skin, warm and dark, catching the green pulse-light from the root walls in a way that made the darkness of her skin glow with undertones of amber and gold. Full-figured in the way that meant *everything* was present and accounted for — heavy breasts beneath a loose robe that draped without hiding, hips that the root-seat accommodated because the root-seat had been grown to accommodate exactly these hips.

Her hair was brown — warm chestnut shading to dark where the green light didn't reach. Long. Wavy, bordering curly. Loose across her shoulders and down her back, catching the pulse-light the way water catches firelight. She had not braided it. She had not pinned it. She had left it the way a woman leaves her hair when the person she's meeting is worth looking at.

Her face. Full lips, high cheekbones, a jaw that was strong without being hard. Expressive brows that were currently expressing *amused surveillance* — the look of someone watching a thing she expected to be afraid and finding it walking toward her table instead. She smiled. The smile was real. The smile was warm. The smile was the most dangerous thing in the room and the room contained a woman who had killed thousands by sleeping with them.

Her eyes were hazel. Warm. Alive. Disarming. The eyes of a woman who looked younger than her centuries and did it on purpose.

**IP Check — DC 23. First viewing. Female humanoid. Direct eye contact in an enclosed space with green light and the warmth of a living heart-chamber.**

The living attendant who had been in the room before Kenji arrived — a woman, young, root-collared but with more autonomy in her expression than the entrance thralls — was already climbing the stack ladder. Fifteen minutes since first viewing. Cumulative -1. She was at Stack 2 and rising, her breath coming faster, her eyes returning to Kenji's face with the kind of regularity that said the aura was doing its work regardless of the collar.

The Lady on the root-seat watched this happen with the expression of someone who had watched it happen a thousand times and still found it interesting.

### The Lady's Table

---

A thrall stepped out of the mist behind the root-seat. Face too calm. Hands steady in the wrong way — the steadiness of muscles being operated, not used. He carried a tray: dried meat, dense bread, a clay cup of something that smelled like broth and root-sap and warmth. He set the tray on a root-shelf that extruded from the wall at exactly table height — a shelf that had not been there ten seconds ago and was now as solid as stone.

Kenji ate.

Not fast. Not polite. Not with the gratitude of a guest or the suspicion of a prisoner. He ate the way a man eats when pride stops negotiating with hunger and the body makes the decision for both of them. The bread was dense and real. The meat was dried and salted and tasted like it had been prepared by hands that remembered how to cure protein even if the mind behind them had forgotten why. The broth was warm.

He was at one meal. He had been at one meal since the birch copse. The body didn't care whose table it was sitting at.

She watched him eat the way a cat watches a bird that refuses to fly. Not hungry — *fascinated*. The hazel eyes tracked his jaw, his hands, the way he tore bread without looking at it, the way he drank broth without checking it for poison first. Either he trusted the food or he didn't care. Either answer interested her.

"Most men who walk into this room do one of three things," she said. Her voice was low. Measured. The voice of someone who had been speaking for centuries and had learned that volume is a concession to weakness. "They fight. They kneel. Or they try to charm a person they think they can win."

She tilted her head. The hair shifted across her shoulder. The green light pulsed once through the root-wall behind her, and for a heartbeat a thread of wrong light traced under her cheekbone — teal ink trying to remember it was blood once. The Living Lich's infrastructure showing through the Lady's skin like a draft showing through a curtain.

"You sat down."

She leaned forward. Not much. An inch. The robe shifted with the motion and the neckline accommodated physics in a way that was not accidental.

"Keep looking," she said. An invitation. A test. A dare.

Because most men fight, kneel, or try to charm. Kenji had done none of these things. He had walked into the heart of her power, sat down at her table, eaten her food, and looked at her the way a man looks at a woman he finds interesting and dangerous and worth the investment of honest attention.

She didn't know what to do with that.

She was going to find out.

### Rations

---

He asked for bulk rations.

The audacity landed exactly where he put it — on the border between insulting and hilarious. He had walked into the Pallid March's command presence, eaten at her table, and now he was asking for *groceries*.

She laughed. Not the measured laugh of a queen performing amusement. A real laugh — short, warm, surprised out of her by a man who had just asked the most dangerous woman on the continent for trail mix.

She permitted it. Thralls staged lean march weight — three weeks of preserved stores, dried meat and dense bread and sealed waterskins, scaled to mock his appetite and respect it at once. The provisions went into the Bag of Holding with the sword that was still humming. Holy-necrotic duality and dried venison, sharing extradimensional space.

The living attendant brought the last bundle. Her hands were shaking. Not from the weight — from the aura. Stack 4. Forty-five minutes of sustained viewing. The cumulative -1 per fifteen-minute window had pushed the DC below her save floor, and every check since had been a fail. Her breathing was audible. Her pupils were wide. She set the bundle down and her fingers touched the back of Kenji's hand — not on purpose, not by accident, but in the space between where intention and biology stop being distinguishable.

She pulled her hand back. Clasped both behind her. Professional. Shaking.

The Lady watched this too. The hazel eyes tracked the attendant's physiological state with clinical precision — the breathing, the pupil dilation, the hand tremor, the flush climbing her throat. She had seen this before. She had *caused* this before, in her own way, with her own tools. But this was different. This was not her doing. This was the ronin's presence — the passive, involuntary, DC-23 biological pressure that simply existed because he existed, and the attendant was a living woman in a room with it.

The Lady's expression shifted. Not alarm. Not jealousy. *Appraisal*. She filed it the way Vess would file a trade report — with interest, with precision, with the understanding that this data point would matter later.

### Baths — As Equals

---

He asked for a bath.

She named the price of courtesy. Courtesy in this room meant something — it meant acknowledging that the host has rules and the guest has limits and the space between them is negotiable. The bath was a negotiation.

Then he narrowed the room.

No thralls. Only her. He would wash her, then she him. As equals.

She laughed again. The second real laugh — deeper than the first, older, the laugh of someone who has lived centuries and has been called many things and "equal" was never one of them. It was a pretty lie and she knew it was a pretty lie and she accepted the theater because the theater was more interesting than the truth.

---

Steam and stone. The bath chamber was behind the root-lattice wall — a natural basin fed by a hot spring that the root network had redirected, the water clear and mineral-sharp, the steam rising in columns that the green pulse-light turned to jade. The floor was smooth root-matter, warm, the walls alive with the same slow breathing as the amphitheater.

He took her hand like a gentleman. Full bow — the waist-bend, not the head-nod. The kind of bow Faelindra would have recognized as court-trained if Faelindra had been in a position to see it, which she was not, because she was walking toward Thornkeep and did not know that the ronin she'd kissed in front of ten thousand troops was currently bowing to a woman who had killed more people by sleeping with them than most armies killed by fighting.

She followed him into the steam.

He stripped. Weapon first — the nodachi set against the basin's stone lip, within honest reach, without performing threat. Then the hakama. Then the rest. No hesitation. No performance. The body underneath was the body that had put a sword through a death knight's shoulder yesterday — lean, athletic, sword-calloused hands, dark skin, the build of someone who moved precisely rather than powerfully. STR 39 didn't show as bulk. It showed as certainty.

He went to his knees. Head bowed. Not submission — *invitation*. The posture said: I am here first. I am lower. Enter the water and I will be where you placed me.

She entered.

The robe came off at the basin's edge. Beneath it the Lady's body was exactly what the form promised — full, warm, dark skin catching the jade steam-light, curves that were not aesthetic choices but biological facts of a woman who had been this shape for centuries and had never apologized for any of it. The water received her. The steam pressed against her skin and the teal-green runic lines beneath the surface glowed once — faint, quick, the Living Lich's infrastructure acknowledging that its host was warm and wet and in proximity to something that made the ley-suspension pulse faster.

He looked up when commanded.

"I'm Kenji."

A plain name. In the wrong house. Given freely, without leverage, without conditions. The name was a weapon and he handed it to her the way he had handed Corwyn's iron chest back — openly, because the giving was the point.

She filed it. The hazel eyes processed "Kenji" the way a jeweler processes a stone — weight, cut, provenance, value. She did not give her name in return. She was not yet sure what his was worth.

---

He framed the bargain while oil met skin.

His hands on her shoulders. Washing. Not seduction — *service*. The distinction was deliberate and she felt it immediately. He was not trying to seduce her. He was doing what he said he would do: washing her, as an equal, with the thoroughness of someone who understood that the act of cleaning another person's body is an act of intimacy that does not require sex to mean something.

Two demands from each. Equals language. She corrected the framing without letting go of the game — not equals, she said, because equals was a fiction and they both knew it. But the fiction was worth maintaining for the duration of the bath, because the fiction made the conversation possible.

Her first demand: no lies he believes. Omission allowed. Performance allowed. But if he says a thing and means it — it must be true. Not true-for-strategy. Not true-for-leverage. True. She was asking for the only currency she couldn't counterfeit.

Her second demand: when she asks who his enemies are — names, or silence. Not poetry. She wanted intelligence or she wanted nothing. She would not accept the middle ground where clever men name enemies in metaphors designed to protect the names.

His demands were begun. Simple, he said. Never finished clean — because the bath turned before the words did.

---

### Water and Withholding

---

What followed was touch that walked the edge of a blade neither of them was willing to draw.

Not intercourse. Not close. The line her biology treated as execution — Close to Death, 1% max-HP per second, the contagion that had killed Corwyn in seventy-two hours and killed everyone else in under two minutes — never triggered. The trigger was specific. The trigger was *the act*. Everything else was the approach.

And the approach was where the power lived.

His hands moved from her shoulders to her back. Oil and water and the slow deliberate pressure of fingers that knew where tension lived in a body because Diagnostic Touch read it without asking. Her muscles were tight in places that centuries of ley-suspension should have made loose — the trapezius, the lumbar, the band across the hip flexors where a body holds stress it won't admit to. He found them and he worked them and she let him.

Her legs shifted. Over his. Not straddling — adjacent. The water was chest-high and the proximity was close enough that Living Ground read her heartbeat accelerating without Diagnostic Touch being needed. The heart that beat every eleven seconds was beating every nine. Then every seven. The ley-suspension was losing its argument with the woman's body underneath it.

He washed from shoulders to waist. The small of her back. The curve where the waist met the hip. His hands were precise and unhurried and they stopped exactly where they stopped.

The IP clock was running. On *her*. DC 23. Female humanoid. Direct viewing in an enclosed steam-filled chamber with skin contact and proximity and the aura doing what the aura does without permission or intent.

She was not immune. She had not taken Lover's Vigor. She had no five-day grace period. She was a woman — alive, real heartbeat, real body — in a room with Kenji, and the aura was biological, not magical, and her Wisdom was high but her Wisdom was not infinite.

The cumulative -1 per fifteen minutes of viewing was stacking.

Her composure held where pride mattered. Her breathing was controlled. Her face was the face of a queen who had sat across from enemies and allies and lovers and thralls and had never once shown the room what she was actually feeling.

But her body was honest where her face was not.

The flush. The heartbeat. The involuntary lean toward contact when his hands pulled away between strokes. The hazel eyes tracking his face with a frequency that was not strategic. The teal-green runic lines beneath her skin pulsing brighter with each fifteen-minute window — her own infrastructure betraying her, the Living Lich's power responding to arousal the way it responded to all biological input, by making it *visible*.

She wanted. The want was real. The want was not an act. She was Chaotic Evil and she had zero inhibitions and if the DC had been lower or the viewing had been longer or if he had pushed instead of held — she would have acted. She was the easiest woman in the story to bed and the most dangerous, and the distance between those two facts was narrowing with every minute he stayed in the water.

He stopped.

Stood. Looked down at her. The water ran off his body and the steam framed him and the green light pulsed once through the basin walls, and for a moment the ronin was a silhouette in jade fog — dark skin, hazel eyes, the build of a man who killed things for a living and washed queens for sport.

Inspection without charity. Not cruel — *honest*. The look of a man who has seen what he came to see and is choosing, deliberately, to walk away from what he could have.

She stayed in the water. Composed where pride mattered. Honest where biology betrayed her. The hazel eyes were darker. The lips were parted. The heartbeat was at five seconds and the ley-suspension had given up pretending it was in charge.

He grinned.

The grin. The annoying grin. The one that had started bar fights and ended wars and made Faelindra cancel drill. The one that said *I know exactly what I just did and I'm going to walk away from it now and you're going to let me because neither of us is ready for what happens next.*

Long rest. "Mam." A room.

She let him leave the bath. She stayed in the water. The steam closed around her.

The attendant, who had been standing outside the bath chamber with Stack 6 written across every line of her body, handed the ronin a towel with hands that were no longer shaking — they had gone past shaking into the stillness that comes after shaking, when the body decides that trembling is insufficient and simply *holds*.

---

### Guest Chamber

---

She granted it. Warm. Dry. A root-chamber grown from the amphitheater's wall — a bed of layered moss and clean linen, a basin of fresh water, a latch that locked from inside.

"The latch locks inside," she said. The voice was the queen's voice again — measured, low, the composure rebuilt from the materials the bath had tried to dissolve. "But every room in my house listens."

A truth. The walls were roots. The roots were her infrastructure. The infrastructure was her nervous system. Privacy in this place was a courtesy she granted, not a condition that existed.

He went in. He latched the door. He set the nodachi against the wall within reach and the Bag of Holding beside it, Oathbreaker still humming inside.

He slept in the house of the thing that commanded the Pallid March, under a roof made of roots that reported to her, in a room whose latch was a formality, with a stolen sword humming in a bag and a woman's heartbeat still at five seconds on the other side of the wall.

Long rest.
