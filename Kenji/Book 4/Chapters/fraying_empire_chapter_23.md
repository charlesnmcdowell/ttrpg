### Fork before noon

---

Mursha did not let the morning settle into comfort.

She finished the last of the bread on her plate, wiped crumbs from the dragon-scale with the same care she gave steel, and leaned in close enough that her voice stayed under the common room’s grind. The half-orc’s eyes held the brass ring that meant **Lover’s Vigor** still had days left—sharp, warm, not soft—and her meaning was practical: *if we’re moving, we move before the yard remembers our shapes in daylight.*

Kenji listened the way he always listened when she switched from bed-voice to contract-voice: no performance, no demand that he agree out loud. The ronin’s mask stayed etiquette; his hands stayed empty of paper he could not read. That fact had already shaped the morning upstairs with Haldra. It would shape what came next, too.

They left the Brass Hitch by the side stair, not the front—Mursha’s choice, and a guard’s habit. Frost had burned off the lower forge quarter; smoke climbed in disciplined columns. Somewhere behind them, Calista’s laugh chased a tray through steam. Somewhere ahead, the Iron Mule’s yard still owned a fraction of Bronzebarrow’s attention the way a missing tooth owns a mouth: you could avoid looking at it, but your tongue kept finding the gap.

### Paper, stamp, and a second door

---

Haldra received them at the dwarf-leased corner with clerk calm rebuilt layer by layer—the kind of calm that meant *I have decided what risk I am willing to carry.*

Mursha laid the ridge notes flat. Names from the western cut. Handoff points. The leak threads Kenji had carried down from the parlor hour, spoken into Mursha’s ear and then translated into the blunt shorthand of someone who had spent years translating violence into paperwork. Haldra’s pen moved. Her mouth stayed polite. Her eyes tracked every line as if the paper might bite.

**Irresistible Presence** pressed the room the way winter pressed a window—DC 23, always on—and Haldra’s jaw tightened once, a small failure of perfect composure, then steadied. She was not collapsing under it. She was *accounting* for it, the way she accounted for everything else.

The bargain that mattered came after the ink dried.

A courier run north, crown relay toward the Kharn-Dural approaches—not the treaty theater, not the pretty caravans for officers’ eyes, but the workhorse line factors used when wax still had to arrive on time and excuses did not. Four wagons. Dwarf teamsters who would not waste breath on gossip unless gossip paid. A slot for a sell-sword who could pass as coalition-neutral muscle.

Mursha’s smile was not romantic. It was relief dressed as professionalism.

She signed where Haldra told her to sign. Her hand did not shake. A guard’s contract was a kind of honesty Mursha understood: *you feed me, you stamp me, I keep your blood inside your skin until the miles run out.* The numbers Haldra named were not a fortune; they were *permission*—to ride without pretending she was only following a lover’s road.

Kenji witnessed without needing to read. The table could fill his silence however it chose: approval, calculation, or the simple truth that he had learned to trust Mursha’s ink more than his own.

### The tube that should not have been on a breakfast tray

---

The mis-delivery arrived disguised as courtesy.

A brass tag. A wax runner’s apology. A cylinder of dark metal scored with dwarven runes that made Haldra’s breath catch hard enough that even Mursha’s ears sharpened.

Haldra did not pretend it was nothing. She bolted the parlor door again—same sand timer discipline, different stakes—and opened the tube with tools that spoke of practice. Inside: not gold, not orders, not anything that belonged on Bronzebarrow’s breakfast routes. A copy, unofficial, desperate in its phrasing—the kind of letter a sub-clan sent when formal emissaries drowned in silence and the mountain still screamed.

Kenji could not read the pages. He did not have to.

Haldra read aloud what mattered, voice flat to keep it bearable: courtesy copies masking inventory war; receipts that no longer matched vault tallies; clerks who signed and clerks who vanished; the deep-forge whisper made concrete—*the note is changing beneath us*—and a plea for coalition aid that an absent ArchMagus had not answered because absence was its own kind of policy.

Mursha’s hand rested on her warhammer pommel without threat, the way a fighter rests a hand on something solid when the world tilts.

The tube’s existence changed the road choice from *preference* to *pressure*. Not the Iron Horde’s sixteen-day drum. Not a sentimental turn south. North—where the mountain’s glow was wrong even in rumor, where dwarf silence was louder than human gossip.

Haldra sealed the copy back into the tube after Kenji indicated—by gesture, by the simple fact of his presence—that he would carry it as witness, not sell it as curio. The dwarf clerk’s nod was a favor marker and a warning braided together: *you are choosing a corridor where forge-sight sees deeper than masks.*

### Yard noise without a face

---

They did not cut through the Iron Mule’s front gate.

Mursha led a skirting path along the weigh-yard’s blind side, where halfling dray ponies argued with harnesses and humans swore at mud in voices too tired to notice one more half-orc in masterwork kit. The correlation clock Cassia had started was still ticking—Kenji could feel it like humidity. A factor clerk looked up from a tally board, eyes tracking Mursha’s armor for half a heartbeat too long, then returned to chalk as if discipline could erase curiosity.

No confrontation. Not yet.

Just the world doing what investigation always did before it became accusation: collecting days until a pattern turned into a name.

### Predawn muster, Day 259

---

**Forge** **bell** **—** **Ashmere** **37**, **cold** **clear**.

The factor train assembled without ceremony: wagons greased, ox teams sworn at lovingly, waxed crates strapped down by people who treated straps like theology. Mursha took her place as *hired*—not behind Kenji, not beneath him, but beside the lead wagon’s bench where a guard belonged.

Kenji boarded as the sell-sword Haldra’s paperwork could describe without lying: masked, armed, quiet. The tube rode where tubes rode best—out of sight, out of idle hands, still a weight.

Wind Step charges stayed untouched. Smoke-Invis-Clone stayed sheathed. Iaido stayed unfired. The road asked for patience first.

### First leg — the note that changed

---

The relay road north began as a continuation of everything Bronzebarrow had been built to serve—wide, graded, honest gravel. By midday the grade tightened. The air thinned in a way humans felt first and complained about second. The dwarf teamsters spoke in short bursts about weather and axle grease, then fell into a rhythm that was almost superstition: *don’t name the mountain’s temper; don’t whistle in a tunnel mouth; don’t joke about the deep forge unless you want the deep forge to listen.*

No one said **Hollowing**.

They said the note changed.

Mursha rode with her braids bound tight and her longsword loose in its scabbard, eyes on the treeline and the road’s shoulders—the old habit of someone who had survived ambush once and intended to survive it again. When the train stopped to adjust a chain, she walked the circle the way she had walked the western cut’s rim: not performance, *geometry.*

Kenji watched the way he always watched when the world narrowed to miles: Living Ground’s wrong-earth sense alert for anything that pretended to be ordinary soil and was not.

No fight came. The threat was not a blade in the brush—not today. The threat was the story repeating in low voices until it stopped being rumor and became weather.

---

### Cold ring camp

---

They made camp in a relay ring where stone fire pits had been used so many times the soot lived in the rock. The dwarves banked their cookfires with a miser’s care. The sky over the crown road turned the color of cooled iron.

Mursha shared a blanket edge because cold was democratic and pride was expensive. Her shoulder against Kenji’s was warmth without theater. When she shifted to check the wagon line, the night air carried a faint trace of cheap pipe smoke—someone else’s camp, someone else’s shift—not hers, not his, just the relay world being crowded in the way relay worlds always were.

Kenji did not ask.

Mursha did not offer explanation.

The mountain ahead was a darker tooth on the horizon. The forge-glow that should have been steady flickered wrong in distant cloud, too slow to be lightning, too stubborn to be lantern.
