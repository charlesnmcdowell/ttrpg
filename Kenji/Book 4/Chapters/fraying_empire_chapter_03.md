# Fraying Empire — Chapter Three: *Stillday*

*Book 4 · The Ronin arc · Kenji*

## Chapter Three: Stillday

### Ashmere 11-18, 1247 AR — Thornfield

---

The Greenveil is done. The Nexus is dead. The Broodmother is composting in a tunnel full of ferns. The ley lines run clean and the soil is waking up and Amaris is Level 16 and standing in a ruined chamber looking for a man who's already gone.

I don't reappear. The smoke bomb illusion fades in the tunnel. I take a side passage. Wind Step through the dying forest that isn't dying anymore. Across the dead farmland that's already starting to feel different underfoot.

The Stubborn Mule. Delia's tavern. The green door.

"Room. Privacy. One week."

---

### The Week

---

Seven days in a back room. Draw, strike, sheathe. The nodachi whispering through the air a thousand times. The ember quiet. The Whisper Stone off.

Living Ground reads the village through the floorboards. The soil healing. The clicking gone from the treeline. Day by day Thornfield starts to breathe again.

Through the wall — fragments. Delia's voice. Villagers returning.

"—the druid killed it. In the cave. Alone."

"—walked in there and—"

"—what about that swordsman? The one in the mask?"

"—gone. Just gone."

---

Amaris doesn't come to the inn. She went back to Briarstone. She checks the logging road. The creek crossing. The barn roof. The couch.

Nothing. No prints. No tracks. Windstrider boots.

She sits at the kitchen table. The couch still smells like him. Root Network searches for a heartbeat through a mile of healing soil.

She doesn't find it.

---

### Delia

---

Delia comes to the back room on the third day. Not with the food tray. With fresh bread she baked that morning. Two cups. A bottle of honey mead — a trader came through, the first in two months.

"My people don't need me this morning." She sits on the edge of the bed. "The stew has beef. Tom is fixing his own fence. Twelve people last night."

She looks at me.

"Last time I chose the stew pot. I'd choose it again. But this morning I'm choosing breakfast."

---

She's different from the first night. She's not the woman holding a dying village together with turnip stew and stubbornness. The weight is gone. What's left is a forty-three-year-old woman with broad shoulders who kneads dough and pours ale and decided to bring two cups upstairs.

She takes her time. Forty-three years of knowing herself. She laughs once — at the situation, at herself, at the fact that she's in the back room of her own inn.

"I run a *tavern*. I kneaded *bread* this morning."

---

She comes back every morning after that. Real eggs. Warm bread. Tea. She sits for an hour. Sometimes she stays longer. She doesn't ask about the mask or the sword or where I'm going.

On the last morning she stands at the door.

"Breakfast in an hour. The eggs are for you. Not the tray-outside-the-door eggs. The real ones."

A pause. Softer.

"Thank you for letting me choose this time."

---

### Wynn

---

Ashmere 18. The last day. South end of the village. The herb garden that's the only thing in Thornfield that's always looked alive. Now the rest of the village is catching up.

She's in the garden. Kneeling between rows. She feels me at the gate.

"You're leaving." Not a question.

I tell her I'm glad her door is open. Ask if I can spend the night. I want to watch her garden and hear about Stillday.

"You want to watch my garden."

"Yeah."

"And hear about Stillday."

"I don't know what it is."

That stops her. She files it somewhere different from the aura notes. The place where she keeps things that don't fit.

"Everyone knows what Stillday is. Children know what Stillday is."

She opens the door wider.

---

She cooks while she talks. Moves between the stove and the cutting board with the efficiency of a woman who's lived alone long enough to make the kitchen a single-person choreography.

"Stillday is the sixth day. The rest day. Five days of work, one of still. Temples open. Shops close. Families eat together."

She tells me about the calendar. The seasons. The months I didn't know had names. Seedmere. Bright Turn. Rainmarch. The words for the time I've been living in without knowing how to count it.

She tells me about Harvest Week — the biggest event of the year, which happened while I was alone in a valley. About the Long Day — tournament season, which happened while Sera sat in a house she bought with champion's gold. About the Still Night — the winter solstice. The one about family and warmth and saying the things you've been carrying.

"How do you not know this? Where are you *from?*"

---

Night. The garden by greenhouse light. She walks me through every row. What grows where and why. The thornmint. The pale sage. The eastern beds with new growth where corruption used to live.

"I spent six months watching that treeline and being afraid. Now I watch it and I hear crickets. Normal crickets. The real kind."

She's quiet for a moment.

"That's because of you."

---

Inside. Late. She stands at her bedroom door. Hand on the frame.

"I'm not going to ask you to come to my room. Not because I don't want to. But I'm choosing to stand here and say goodnight because that's the version of me I want to be."

She turns. The hazel eyes steady.

"But if you knock, I'll answer."

The door closes.

I strip down. Walk to the washroom.

"Where can I wash these? And where'd you want me to sleep?"

The door opens. Wynn in the sleep shift. She stares.

"The — there's a basin. In the — the washroom is —"

She points at a door she's walked through ten thousand times and cannot name.

"You're standing in my kitchen. Naked. Asking about laundry."

---

She comes to wash me. The soap on the right. Calloused hands and soil under the nails. Clinical for thirty seconds. Then not.

"I've been alone for three years. The work was more interesting than anyone available."

Her forehead against the back of my shoulder. Her hands still on my chest.

"I can't manage this."

The filing system closes. Deliberately. The way she'd close a notebook when the experiment changes direction.

She steps into the tub.

"You knocked."

---

### Morning — Ashmere 19

---

She's been awake for an hour. Notebook open. Pencil in hand. Page eight.

"My grip strength has increased by approximately forty percent. I appear to have gained functional darkvision. My resting heart rate has dropped twelve beats per minute."

She holds up her hand. Opens and closes the fist.

"Something you do — during intimacy — changes the person you're with. Physically. Measurably."

I tell her all I remember is coming inside of her and her enjoying it.

She stares at me. Picks up the pencil.

"Chapter five. 'The Subject Has An INT of 9 And It Shows.'"

---

She suggests the mechanism is biological. Delivered through intimate contact. I suggest she collect a sample for study. She agrees. For science.

She has two labeled vials by mid-morning. *Sample A — in situ, activated.* *Sample B — oral extraction, unactivated.* In her small precise handwriting. On her shelf between the thornmint and the beetle specimens.

"Sample A has a faint luminescence. Gold-green. Sample B does not. The activation occurs during sustained internal contact."

She's written ten pages. She has charts.

"This is the most important discovery of my career and I collected it on my knees in my own kitchen."

---

I leave a clone nodding at her explanations. Smoke bomb under the table. Greater Invisibility. The phantom double sits in my chair, tilting its head attentively while Wynn describes luminescence decay rates.

She'll figure it out in ten minutes. The clone doesn't breathe.

She's going to be furious. Then she's going to write about it.

---

### The Crossroads

---

South road out of Thornfield. Ashmere 19. Windday. First frost on the ground. The road crunches underfoot.

Diagnostic Touch is live — every plant I brush feeds information. Living Ground reads healthy soil ahead. Road Sense traces the trade routes in every direction. Faint warmth south. Cold north. Distant warmth east. Nothing west.

Four roads. Frost on the ground. The treeline behind me stands straight for the first time in a year.

Thornfield is going to live. Amaris is Level 16 on a farm with legendary soil. Delia is planning Stillday seating. Wynn has two vials and ten pages of notes and a clone that doesn't breathe sitting at her kitchen table.

The Still Night is twenty-six days away. The holiday about family and warmth and saying the things you've been carrying. Wynn told me about it yesterday over stew.

I've been in this world for eight months and I've never celebrated a holiday.

The ronin walks.
