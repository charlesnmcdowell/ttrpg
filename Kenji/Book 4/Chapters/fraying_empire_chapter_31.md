# Book 4 — Fraying Empire
## Chapter 31: Two Weeks and a Green Door

**Session span:** Ashmere 39 late night → Ashmere 40 predawn (Day 261 → 262). Kharn-Dural seam-witness / wedge approach → bench bundle → equipment receipt → decoy / invisible egress → portal jump off cordon.

**Kenji dialogue note:** Spoken lines player-declared (hot air wind, Mursha thanks, Solveth / elf dating bit, two weeks / call, Kahryn / Korrim threat band, etc.).

---

### The hour

Korrim did not begin with words. He turned a brass dial on the bench instrument until the seam light shifted from amber to a tighter white — calibration frequency, not theatrical lighting — and nodded once at the forge-wright standing ready with chalk and caliper at the observation rail.

The wedge work began. Mursha walked it.

Not because the bench asked her to. Kenji addressed her directly — not the court, not the elder, not the clerks copying everything onto slate in handwriting that would outlive every person in the chamber. He spoke to the woman behind the barrier as if the barrier were an accident of geography rather than a ruling, and the bench let it happen because the alternative was admitting they could not stop it.

She described the seam pin in mechanical terms: load distribution across both blades, the thermal argument between creation and entropy running through the steel like a current through wet copper, the crack geometry narrowing at the wedge points and widening below where the instruments could not yet reach. She gave the mountain its own problem back in language the wrights could use — and Korrim, who had heard a hundred forge-priests describe similar readings in similar chambers, heard something he had not heard before: competence without institutional backing. The half-orc woman at the witness bench was not reciting. She was reading the seam the way a surveyor reads a fault line, from memory, because she had already done the math.

The bench scribed took notes. The brass instrument ticked. Kenji stood inside the ward circle with his arms crossed and his weight on one heel, the posture of a man watching someone else do excellent work and knowing enough to stay quiet.

An hour passed. Then another. The seam light held steady while the findings compiled into a summary that tasted like honest medicine: the pin was holding, but the pin was not a cure. The crack below the wedge points was still eating. The instruments agreed on direction if not on speed.

---

### The voice in the steel

Solveth spoke.

Not through dream, not through the muffled resonance that Kenji felt when the blade shifted inside extradimensional storage. Frost Fang was wedged into stone at blade proximity — close enough for the entity bound in the steel to reach air — and the voice that came out was not the sword's. It was divine. Old. Clear in a way that made the bench scribe's quill stop moving and the nearest forge-priest take one step backward without deciding to.

The god in the crack had opinions.

Kenji tried to make it casual. He poked at the voice with a nickname, the tone of a man ribbing a difficult roommate rather than addressing a divine entity bound in entropy steel. Something about elf dating. Something about the war being old enough to laugh at. The bench ate every syllable — the clerks were writing so fast their knuckles were white, because what they were logging was a conversation between a declared Archmagus and a deity speaking from inside a sword wedged into a fracture in the heart of their mountain, and neither participant seemed to think this was unusual.

Solveth accepted Mursha as handler. Not cleanly — there were limits. The entity would work through her hands for the removal sequence, but there would be no clean telegraph, no neat instruction set relayed through mortal speech without distortion. What Mursha would get was proximity and attention, not translation. The rest was engineering judgment and whatever the half-orc's nerve could hold when the entropy couplets shifted under her tools.

Korrim's face did not change during any of this. The elder wrote one line on his personal folio, closed it, and resumed watching. Whatever he wrote was not shared with the bench scribe.

---

### Two weeks

The timeline was honest because the mountain demanded honesty and Kenji had already burned his capacity for theater.

Best case: two to six days. The seam team would complete their readings, the wrights would confirm extraction geometry, and Mursha would supervise a controlled pull — Frost Fang first, then Oathbreaker with a brace vent path to manage the thermal recoil. The crack would stabilize or it would not, and the instruments would tell them which before the blades moved.

Worst case: two weeks. If the entropy couplets resisted clean extraction. If the creation residue from Kenji's conduit vent had fused deeper than the surface readings showed. If the crack below the wedge points accelerated under intervention and the wrights had to pause, reassess, rebuild the brace geometry, and try again.

Kenji took the two weeks and turned it into a weapon.

He told the room he would give it that long. Two weeks. If the blades were not out and the seam was not stable by then, he would come back and do it himself — the way he should have done it the first time, without asking permission, without filling out forms, without standing inside a circle drawn by people who needed his help more than he needed their process. The yank language landed on the bench like a rock through a window, but Kenji softened it in the same breath: he would not coerce the witness. Mursha worked on Mursha's terms. That line was real.

Then the ultimatum. Equipment parity. Mursha treated as engineer, not prisoner's associate. He named Kahryn — and the name carried weight in the chamber, enough to shift the air. Ten versus six. The numbers meant something to Korrim that they did not mean to the clerks, and the elder's jaw set in a way that suggested the math had landed.

The green light appeared.

Kenji tried to open a portal inside the ward circle. Gold-green creation light bloomed at his fingertips — the same signature that had incinerated his cover two chapters ago — and the wards pushed back. Not violently. The dwarf-made containment did what dwarf-made containment does: it absorbed the pressure, distributed it across the circle geometry, and held. The portal construct flickered, half-formed, a door to somewhere the room was not allowed to see.

He canceled it. Before the forge-priests could call breach. Before the watch commander could order action. Before Korrim had to make a ruling that would turn this chamber into a different kind of room. Kenji let the light die in his hands and stepped back into the center of the circle, and the silence that followed was the loudest thing the bench had heard all night.

---

### The split

Mursha did not forgive him. She accepted beats — the apology language, the peace phrasing, the acknowledgment that he had used her name as leverage without asking whether she wanted to be leveraged — but she held a line that Kenji could see and could not cross.

She would finish the closure work. On Kharn time. Not his poker table. Not his two-week ultimatum. Not his green-light theatrics that made every dwarf in the room remember why their ancestors built wards in the first place. The engineering was hers. The schedule was Korrim's. The relationship was hers. And she would not let him make her both shield and excuse — the woman he protected and the justification for protecting only himself.

Korrim read the ruling bundle. Witness-engineer status: Mursha Haldra-line, recognized by bench authority as technical consultant to the seam restoration effort, with duties and protections thereof. She would rotate between witness rail and seam approach as the work required. Equipment returns would be authorized where seam risk allowed. Vault holds would apply where it did not. The ruling was bureaucracy — meticulous, impersonal, and exactly the kind of paper that keeps people alive in rooms where the alternative is trusting someone's word.

The bench entered the ruling. The scribe copied it. Korrim closed the folio for the second time that night and looked at the man standing inside his ward circle with the expression of someone who had been doing this job for decades and had never once been surprised by how much trouble a single person could carry into a room.

---

### The door

The inventory came first. Nodachi — verified, returned per receipt. Bag of Holding — link check only, no dump. The wrights confirmed the extradimensional space was intact and stable; whatever was inside was still inside. Kenji took both back with the careful hands of a man who understands that the people giving him his weapons are making a calculated decision, not a generous one.

Then the clone walked out of smoke.

Not the combat version — this was Performance. The decoy stepped into the chamber doing something between a shuffle and a tap dance, arms loose, head bobbing, the body language of a man who had decided to make his exit a comedy bit. The clone grinned at the nearest clerk. The clerk did not grin back. The forge-priest at the observation rail watched the duplicate with the professional attention of someone cataloging a new entry under *things the prisoner can do that we did not previously know about*, and the watch commander's hand went to his belt before training overrode instinct.

Performance check: the clone held. Twenty-three against a distraction threshold the room never had time to set properly. Every eye tracked the dancing duplicate — because the body was doing something impossible and the brain follows impossible before it follows invisible.

Kenji went invisible.

Greater Invisibility layered over the distraction like a second coat of paint on a door that was already closing. Stealth with advantage — the kind of advantage you get when every person in the room is watching a clone do a soft-shoe routine in a seam-witness chamber at three in the morning. Eighteen against a gate check that should have been harder but was not, because the distraction had already eaten two points off the difficulty and the remaining sixteen could not find what it could not see.

Wind Step carried him through corridors that were not designed for someone who could run on surfaces that should not hold weight. Two charges spent — distance, not combat. The mountain passed around him in stone and amber light and the deep hum of a place that was still sick beneath its procedures.

Three miles out, where the relay cordons thinned and the mountain's jurisdiction became a suggestion rather than a law, Kenji opened the door for real.

Green light. Gold-green edges. The portal bloomed in open air without wards to push back, and the path on the other side was not a path the relay owned. He stepped through — and behind him, inside the mountain, the bench would be logging the breach. Decoy class. Invisible egress. A man who had declared himself Champion of the Elves and Archmagus of Varenholm Academy, who had broken his companion's restraints with his bare hands, who had hovered in creation light and spoken to a god in front of clerks, had just tap-danced a clone through their security and walked out of their mountain like a man leaving a restaurant.

The relay alarm would reach Korrim within minutes. The coalition relay was already in transit from the night before. And somewhere behind a glass barrier she had not asked for, Mursha was still doing the math — because the math was hers, and the man who had promised two weeks was already three miles gone with a green door closing behind him.
