## Fraying Empire — Chapter One: *Greymere*

### Day 229 — Morning — The Hearthstead, Greymere Valley

---

The fog hasn't burned off yet. The valley smells like wet grass and woodsmoke. I step off the porch barefoot, two training swords from the barn rack — oak, weighted to match Emberfrost's balance. The linen shirt sticks to my shoulders in the damp.

Haste clicks into place. The world slows. The fog droplets hang. The creek at the bottom of the hill sounds like it's moving through honey.

Enhanced Arcane Edge hums through the wood. Force energy sheaths both blades — faint gold shimmer along the grain. Training swords that hit like enchanted steel.

The bear is where it always is. Forty yards south, at the blackberry thicket on the property line. Brown. Old. Fat from six months of nobody bothering it. It's been eating my blackberries since I moved in.

I walk toward it. Stance loose. Not the cross-guard. Not the dual-wield. The new form — thumb on the guard, left foot forward, weight low. The iaido stance I've been carving into muscle memory for six months. Draw, strike, return. One motion. One breath.

The bear looks up. Berry juice on its muzzle. It huffs. Stands. Nine feet tall on its hind legs. Paws the size of dinner plates.

Just like the forest outside Duskfen. Day Two. Rocks and borrowed armor and no idea what I was doing.

Two hundred and twenty-seven days later, I know exactly what I'm doing.

The bear charges.

---

Do. The nodachi draws low across the belly in a single arc — red-black steel trailing gold force energy. The abyssal edge passes through fur and muscle and rib like they're fog. The bear doesn't make a sound. It doesn't have time.

Men. The blade rises without pause. Same breath. Same draw. The return arc catches the falling skull from crown to jaw. The abyssal edge hums — red-black light flaring once along the cutting plane.

The bear's head doesn't hit the ground. It comes apart in a wash of dark red particulate — ash and light and nothing. The body follows a half-second later. Dissolving from the neck down. Red-black motes rising into the morning fog like embers from a dead fire.

Nothing left. No blood. No carcass. Just blackberry bushes and trampled grass and the faint smell of ozone.

Five steps back. Measured. The hakama whispers against the wet grass.

Chiburi. One sharp flick — the blade snaps right, parallel to the ground. Nothing to clean. The abyssal edge doesn't hold blood. It holds nothing. What it cuts, it unmakes.

Noto. The blade returns to the scabbard. One motion. The click of the guard seating.

The fog is burning off. The blackberry thicket is empty. Somewhere in the valley, a bird that stopped singing starts again.

---

The porch. The chair I built wrong twice before it held weight. Coffee — or whatever the local equivalent is, dark and bitter, bought from the village three weeks ago.

The fog lifts. The valley opens up. Green hills, a creek at the bottom, the treeline where the blackberry bear used to live. Quiet. The kind of quiet that took three months to stop feeling wrong.

The ember banks low. The nodachi leans against the door frame. The book from the peddler sits open on the arm of the chair, page down, spine cracked. I haven't turned a page in an hour.

No Whisper Stones humming. No construct network pulsing. No Garrett knocking. No NPC asking something, needing something, wanting something.

Just the creek. The wind. The valley.

Day 229. Nothing happens. That's the point.

---

The bag of holding swallows the old life. Emberfrost. The leather. The ring. The bracelet. All of it goes into the dark and weighs nothing.

I lock the Hearthstead door. Not that anyone's coming. The garden needs water. It'll keep.

---

Four miles of dirt road through the valley floor. Late morning sun on the fields. A cart track worn into the grass by farmers who've been using it longer than Varenholm has had an Academy.

The hakama moves quiet. The boots leave no prints. The nodachi rides my hip. Hat low. Mask up. Hazel eyes on the road.

Nobody passes me. A hawk circles the eastern ridge. The creek runs parallel to the road for the first two miles, then cuts west toward someone's irrigation. Sheep on a hillside. A dog barks once from a farmhouse and stops.

---

Greymere village. Maybe two hundred people. A crossroads — north toward the highland passes, south toward the trade roads that eventually connect to Crestfall if you walk long enough. One inn. A general store. A smithy that doubles as a farrier. A temple to something local. A baker. A well in the square.

Mid-morning. A handful of people in the square. A woman hauling water. Two old men on a bench. A kid chasing a chicken. A merchant's cart outside the general store — just arrived, canvas still tied down.

The inn sign reads *The Stubborn Mule*. The door's open.

---

The Stubborn Mule smells like stew and lamp oil. Low ceiling. Five tables. A bar with stools that don't match. The innkeeper — a thick man with a red nose and flour on his apron — looks up when the door opens. Decides I'm not his problem. Goes back to wiping the bar.

Two farmers at a corner table. A merchant woman eating bread alone. An old man nursing something dark by the window.

I take a stool. Order cider.

The merchant woman is talking to the farmers. Not loud — but the room is small.

"—won't take the south road anymore. Not worth the contract." She tears bread. Chews. "Thornfield's done. Three caravans turned back this season. The last driver who pushed through said the road itself was wrong — webs across the path, things moving in the treeline in broad daylight. He didn't stop. Didn't deliver. Came back white as milk."

The older farmer leans in. "My cousin had a plot south of there. Barley farm. Packed up two months ago. Said the soil went dead overnight. Not dry — *dead*. Cold to the touch. Nothing would take."

The other farmer shakes his head. "Millhaven sent men. Six soldiers. Three weeks ago."

A pause.

"And?"

"And nothing. They didn't come back. Millhaven isn't sending more."

The merchant pushes her plate away. "There's a woman down there — some mage — bought a farm right before it all went bad. They say her land is the only green thing left on that side of the village. Walls of vine. Nothing gets through. But she doesn't leave her property. Doesn't help anyone else. Just sits behind her plants and watches the village rot."

---

I pick up the cider and move to the merchant's table. The two farmers go quiet. Three sets of eyes on the hat, the mask, the nodachi.

I set the cider down. Sit without asking.

Her name is Breca. Runs dry goods between Greymere, Millhaven, and — until recently — Thornfield. She talks like someone billing you by the minute.

"Last delivery I made was eleven weeks ago. Feed grain. Took the south road, normal run, two days." She taps the table. "Webs. Across the road. Thick. Ropy. Chest-height. And the smell — like turned meat and something chemical underneath."

The older farmer — Oren, her uncle — talks about his cousin Hal. Barley farm south of Thornfield. Beetles through the soil. Entire field dead in two days. Packed up. Gone.

The younger farmer's sister is in Thornfield still. Temple is safe — the priest's ground doesn't get the corruption. But she said something came out of the forest in daylight last month. Bigger than a horse. Stood at a fence line. Looking.

Breca tells me about the Broodmother. The herbalist named Wynn who maps the corruption. The mage who grows her tomatoes behind a vine wall while the village rots.

The Millhaven soldiers — six armed men, garrison-trained. Three weeks. Nothing.

She hands me a map. Trade route markings. Distance notes.

"The road forks at the Stonecross marker. Left goes to Millhaven. Right goes to Thornfield. You'll know you're close when the trees start leaning."

She pauses.

"Don't cut the webs."

---

I buy a room and a round. Breca stays for an hour. She tells me more about the route, the village, the slow death of a place she delivered to for nine years.

Halfway through the second ale she undoes the top two buttons of her collar. Not looking at me. Looking at the fire.

*"Warm in here."*

It is not warm in here.

A few minutes later her boot nudges mine under the table and stays there. She starts telling me she hasn't liked a man's face in six years and my face, she is warning me now, is on her list. She is saying it flat, with the same tone she used to describe the webs, which is the funniest part.

She leaves fast when the ale is gone. Pushes the chair in hard. Gives me the map at the door.

*"I'm in the yard. Cart with the red axle. I'll be there a while if you've got anything else to ask me."*

She does not mean the map.

---

Later. The copper tub. She came back — because she wanted to, and she said so at the door. Widow, six years on her own, a ronin she liked the face of passing through the only village she still delivered to. She told me at the threshold she was done being coy about it.

In the tub she laughed once at something I said and it surprised her — she put a hand over her mouth like she'd forgotten the sound. She had opinions about where my hands went. She gave them.

Afterwards, she dressed with her back to me. Steady hands. Every buckle a piece of armor going back on.

At the door she stopped. Half a smile.

*"I'm going to drive north. I'm going to tell the next village I stopped for the bottle. I'm not sorry, ronin. Come through Fallowmere again and I'll stop for you first."*

Cart wheels on gravel. Gone.

### Day 230-231 — South Road to Thornfield

---

Dawn. The south road.

Road Sense kicks in before I reach the fork. The north road toward Millhaven pulses warm — traffic, life. The south road is cold. A thread with nothing on it. Dead trade route.

The Long Haul settles into my legs. Two days of walking feels like a morning stroll. I don't stop. Don't eat. Don't need to.

---

Day one. Normal road. Packed earth, wheel ruts, wildflowers. Nobody in either direction.

Night falls. I walk through it. The boots leave no prints. The stars turn overhead and the road unspools south and I don't feel a mile of it.

Day two. Afternoon. The Stonecross marker — weathered granite at the fork. Someone scratched into the stone with a knife, recent:

*DONT GO SOUTH*

I go south.

---

The last mile. Breca was right.

The trees lean in. Not wind — the trunks curve toward the road like they're reaching. The canopy closes overhead. The light changes. Gets grey. The air smells damp and chemical and sweet in a way that's wrong.

Webs. Across the road. Chest-height. I don't cut them. I step around. Six webs in a quarter mile.

Then chimney smoke. Faint. The trees open up.

---

### Thornfield

---

Half of it is empty. The eastern side — boarded windows, sagging fences, silence. Webs on everything east of the main road. A farmhouse with its door open. A barn with the roof caved.

The western side is alive. Barely. Smoke from chimneys. A man repairing a fence with his back to the treeline. Children near the well — close to it. Not wandering.

The temple at the village center has people outside it. Not worshipping. Sitting. Blankets. Cook fires. A refugee camp in the yard of a harvest god.

The tavern. Green door. Faded sign. *Delia's.* Still open.

---

Inside. Four people. Three sharing a bowl of stew and a heel of bread. An old man asleep in the corner. No coin on the tables. The stew pot over the hearth is thin — more water than meat. Delia is feeding people, not serving them.

Delia. Broad. Ruddy-cheeked. Arms like she kneads dough and carries kegs. But hollowed out. Dark circles. Ten years older than her age.

I put five gold on the bar. In Thornfield right now, that's a fortune.

"Get us a room with privacy and honey mead. I heard the rumors. Breca sent me."

She stares at the gold like she's checking if the words are real. Two coins go in her apron. Three go to the stew fund.

---

The back room. She brings a bottle she's been saving. Dark amber glass, wax seal intact. Pours two measures. Sits across from me. I take off my shirt, sit back on the couch, and close my eyes.

"Talk," I say. "Tell me everything."

She tells me everything.

Six months of a village dying by inches. The webs. The livestock kills. The coordination. The mage who came and stayed and stopped. The Broodmother. The daytime swarms. Tom Buckley's farm gone in an hour. Delia at the mage's gate saying *people are going to die* and the gate closing. The Millhaven soldiers — six men, twenty-one days, nothing.

Forty people left in the first months. Then twenty more. Then ten. Father Crewe's temple yard as refugee camp. Wynn the herbalist mapping a corruption she can't fight. Harwick filling his logbook with a record of failure.

And Delia. Stretching turnip stew into its third day. Keeping the green door open because closing it means admitting it's over.

"Nobody came," she says. The voice of a woman who held a village together for six months with stew and stubbornness. "Until you."

I tell her I'm going to save her inn. I tell her to see Wynn in the morning.

She leaves. Goes back to her stew pot. Feeds her people. That's what Delia does.

---

### Day 231 — Dawn — Wynn's Cottage

---

South end of the village. The herb garden is the only thing in Thornfield that looks like it belongs to a different season. Raised beds. Trellises. A drying rack. The greenhouse. And at the far eastern edge — dead beds, quarantine borders, the corruption pressing close.

Wynn. Late thirties. Lean. Sun-darkened. Short dark hair. Calloused hands. She feels me before she sees me.

"Delia sent you." Not a question.

Three hours. Mask off. She walks me through six months of data. The corruption map. The ley lines — three, converging deep in the Greenveil. The creature patrol patterns charted at dawn and dusk for weeks. Soil samples in labeled jars — healthy on the left, corrupted on the right. Dead beetles pinned to a board with purple-black veining under a magnifying lens. Charts. Timelines. The small precise script getting less steady on the recent pages.

She marks my map. The treeline. The creek — webrot moss source. The patrol rings. The Broodmother's range. The old logging road. The red circle at the center of the Greenveil with a question mark inside.

"The source is in there. Three miles in, maybe more. Whatever is generating the corruption sits at the convergence."

She tells me about Amaris. The druid who could have purified the ley lines. Who went into the forest once, killed two sentries, and never went back.

"Delia is upset because she stopped believing anyone was coming," Wynn says. "And now you're here and she's terrified you'll do the same thing."

---

### Briarstone Homestead

---

Twenty minutes east. Every step the land gets worse. Overgrown to grey to dead. Cold through the boot soles. Abandoned farms on both sides. Webs on everything.

Then — green. Violent, defiant, impossible green in the middle of a wasteland. Twelve acres of life surrounded by death.

The bittervine wall is six months mature. Eight feet tall. Amber sap catching the morning light. Behind it — crops. Rows of green. The farmhouse patched. The barn standing straight. Smoke from the chimney.

A voice from the barn roof.

"That's close enough."

---

Amaris. Silver-white hair. Green eyes. Five-three and sitting on the roof peak with a double-bladed scimitar across her knees, looking down at me the way a cat looks at something that wandered into its yard.

"The village sent you. Delia? Harwick? Wynn with her charts? Whichever one, the answer's the same. I protect my land. The forest is not my problem."

She drops off the roof. We go inside. I bring out food from the bag of holding — Silver Draft roast pork, herbed potatoes, honeyed carrots, sourdough. She stares at it. Then eats like someone remembering what real food tastes like.

"If you're trying to bribe me it's working."

Three hours. She tells me everything. The Academy. Fifteen years growing things for people who didn't care. The stipend savings. The listing. *Exceptional soil.* Maren's shaking hands. The spiders. The thrown scimitar. The miss.

The bittervine wall. The ley line purification under her own farm — one-third of the corruption's supply severed. The two visits from Wynn she couldn't act on. The daytime swarm she watched from her roof. Delia at her gate. The Millhaven soldiers marching past with a nod. The combat sounds from the forest. Then clicking. Then nothing.

"I'm one person. I grow things. And everything out there wants to kill what I grow."

She identified the aura at the first hour. Siren-Elf charm awareness. She handled it the way she handles everything — out loud.

"You're unreasonably attractive and I'm furious about it."

---

I tell her I need her help. Her druid magic. I'll handle the monsters. She walks behind me and fixes the ground.

"That's the plan?"

"Tonight we hunt."

---

The afternoon. The narrow couch in her farmhouse. Her back against my chest. The silver hair against my collarbone. She reaches back, finds me, guides me in, and stays. Connected. Not moving. Just breathing.

"Don't move yet. Just — stay."

Eight months of empty. Six of them alone on a farm surrounded by nightmares. She held position for a long time. Then her hips started. Slow. Small. Building it at her own pace.

She slept connected. Still pressed against me. The first real sleep in months. The afternoon light moving across the floor.

She woke with the light going gold. Separated slowly. Stood. Didn't look at me for a full minute.

Then the armor. Buckle by buckle.

"I don't know your name."

"Kenji. The ronin. Wandering warrior."

"Kenji." She tested it. "I'm going to think about that name at inappropriate times for the rest of my life."

---

### Day 231 — Dusk — The Barn Roof

---

Both of us flat on our stomachs. The Greenveil treeline fifty yards away. Dusk settling.

Living Ground — new, inherited from the bond — reads through the warden's root network. Every inch of Briarstone alive with information. The bittervine wall humming. The healthy soil. And beyond the property line — cold, dead ground crawling with movement.

The clicking starts.

Six shapes at the treeline. Waist-high spiders. Purple-black veining. Moving in pairs. Patrol formation. They approach the bittervine wall. Test it. Retreat. Spread along the perimeter.

Then the ground vibration. Heavy. Rhythmic. The treeline parts.

The Broodmother.

Wagon-sized. Legs like tree trunks. Abdomen pulsing with corruption so thick it looks like it's wearing her. Multiple eye clusters catching the last light. Mandibles the length of my arm.

On her back — a dozen smaller spiders. Riders. Juveniles. A mobile deployment platform.

She stands at the treeline. Watches the farmhouse with eyes that reflect the bittervine's amber glow. Two full minutes. Then turns. Walks back into the Greenveil. The ground shakes.

Amaris is rigid beside me. Her hand on my forearm. White-knuckled. Her breathing fast and shallow.

"That's her. That's what I've been watching from this roof for six months."

The clicking fades. The sentries follow the Broodmother into the dark.

Amaris rolls onto her back. Stares at the sky.

"Still want to hunt?"

## Fraying Empire — Chapter Two: *The Greenveil*

### Day 232 — Dawn — Briarstone Homestead

---

Dawn. Grey light through the shutters. The clicking has stopped. The sentries have pulled back.

Amaris is already up. Armored. Pack loaded. Bittervine cuttings, webrot moss vials, pale sage bundles, warden's root seeds, component pouch, scimitar on her back. She's standing at the kitchen table eating cold soup with one hand and checking her supplies with the other.

The south gate. The gap in the bittervine wall. Beyond it — grey dead farmland and the dark line of the Greenveil a hundred yards east.

Morning light hits the treeline. The leaning trunks cast long shadows. No movement. No clicking. The window is open.

Amaris stands at the gate. Looks at the forest. Her hands are shaking. She grips the gate post until they stop.

"Okay." One breath. "Okay."

She steps through.

---

### The Logging Road

---

The old cart ruts are barely visible under dead undergrowth. Wide enough for two people. Packed earth under the rot. Heading northeast into the canopy.

The forest closes around us. The canopy cuts the light to grey-green murk within thirty yards. The temperature drops. The chemical sweet smell is immediate — stronger than the road, stronger than the treeline.

Living Ground activates through the root systems under my boots. The trees are alive but wrong — root networks twisted, sap running dark. The soil is cold. Not dead yet — the logging road is on the southern edge — but sick.

Amaris feels it too. "The soil here is corrupted but salvageable." She speaks low. Marks a mental note. Keeps walking.

---

Fifteen minutes. Half a mile. The logging road curves east. Dead ferns, grey moss, webs in the upper canopy. Thin ones. Old. Perimeter markers.

A funnel web between two trees. Silk tube disappearing into a hollow trunk. Empty. Daytime.

Amaris: "Silk Lurker nest. Ambush predator. Nocturnal. We're fine."

A pause.

"We're fine *right now*."

---

### The Creek Crossing

---

Thirty minutes. The logging road intersects a shallow stream. Clear water over smooth stones. Patches of pale green-grey moss on the rocks.

Webrot moss. Same creek Amaris harvested on Day 1. Growth is thicker now — six months undisturbed.

She kneels at the bank. Cutting. Hands steady. This part she knows.

On the trunk of an oak ten feet above — claw marks. Eight parallel grooves. Fresh. Something large climbed this tree recently and sat looking down at the water.

Amaris sees it. "Those marks weren't here six months ago. The sentries were dog-sized. Whatever made those is —"

She measures the spacing with her eyes.

"Bigger."

---

### The Silk Lurker Colony

---

I hand her the map. Tell her to keep going. Check every point Wynn marked. I'll be nearby in stealth.

"Did you just call me —"

Wind Step. Silent. Gone. The canopy above the logging road, twenty feet up.

Amaris stands alone at the creek crossing holding a map and a scimitar, staring at the empty space where I was.

"I hate him," she says to nobody.

She's smiling.

---

I shadow her from the canopy. She navigates well. The Academy taught her maps and the forest taught her soil.

The Silk Lurker colony — sixty feet of funnel webs strung between dead trees. Wynn counted twelve nests in Month 3. Amaris counts twenty-three. I count twenty-five from above. Two she missed — high canopy, bigger than the ones below.

"Nearly doubled," she says. Marks the map.

She's through the corridor in five minutes. Doesn't touch a thread.

---

### The Dead Zone

---

One mile in. The soil transitions. Behind her — sick but alive. Ahead — finished. Root networks ending like they were cut. Trees standing but hollow.

Amaris kneels. Palm flat on the soil.

"Nothing. The ley line under this stretch isn't corrupted. It's *drained*. The energy is being pulled toward the center. The soil didn't die from poison. It died from starvation."

She marks the map. Stands. Her face is pale.

"My feet hurt."

She keeps going.

---

### The Millhaven Soldiers

---

One and a half miles. The creek intersects the logging road. The water is grey now.

She smells it before she sees it.

Webbed to the trees. Both sides of the creek. Six cocoons — man-sized, wrapped in grey silk so thick the shapes are barely human. Armor fragments on the ground. A shield with Millhaven's crest. A sword still in its scabbard. A boot standing upright in the creek bed like someone set it there neatly.

One cocoon is open. Split at the chest. Drag marks leading deeper into the forest.

Amaris sits on a rock. Hard. The map in her lap. She breathes through her mouth.

"They came in a straight line. Standard military. Fastest path." Her voice is thin. "Something pulled one out. Fed on it. Or took it somewhere."

She marks the map. Her charcoal hand is unsteady.

She doesn't stand up for a full minute.

---

### The Lurker Colony — Clearing

---

I drop from the canopy. Tell her to start destroying the colony. I'll kill whatever wakes up.

She does the math. Pale sage smoke drives them out disoriented. Earthen Command collapses the funnels. They come out blind.

She lights the sage. Holds it high. 

"Earthen Command."

The ground erupts. Three nests collapse simultaneously. Silk tears. Funnels implode. Sage smoke pours into the gaps.

Three lurkers spill out. Legs scrambling. Eyes clouded. Stumbling blind.

The nodachi leaves the scabbard.

First lurker. Draw. Red-black steel through the crown to the abdomen. Dead before it hits the ground.

Second lurker. The return stroke. Abyssal edge hums — red-black light flaring once. The lurker dissolves. Vaporized. Nothing left but dark motes rising in the sage smoke.

Third lurker. Running. Not fast enough. The blade finds it mid-stride.

The nodachi rises in a slow arc over the right shoulder. Tip skyward. Rotates down behind the back. Slides home. *Shhhhiiiing.* Click.

Three lurkers in four seconds.

Amaris doesn't stop. South to north. Five nests at a time. The sage smoke everywhere. Lurkers spilling out blind and panicked.

Four more hit open ground. Wind Step — silent landing. The nodachi drawn.

First: draw-strike. Dead. Second: follow-through. Vaporized — red-black motes drifting like dark snow. Third: pivot. Dead. Fourth: return stroke. Wounded. A second cut finishes it.

The blade flips in my hand. Full rotation. Red-black steel catching the dim light for one second. The edge finds the scabbard mouth blind. Click.

Seven lurkers. Eighteen nests collapsed.

The upper canopy nests. The big ones. Wind Step to the branches. The first lurker senses me a half-second before the draw. Not fast enough.

The nest dissolves with the occupant. Silk, chitin, branch — all of it becoming particulate. Dark snow through the canopy.

The second lurker bolts. Punches through its own silk and launches down the trunk. I'm faster. Clean kill. It hits the ground in two pieces.

One sharp flick — the blade snaps horizontal. Nothing to clean. The abyssal edge doesn't hold anything. Slide. Click. Silence.

Twenty-five nests. Destroyed. Fourteen lurkers killed. Eleven absent — deeper in the forest.

Amaris stands in the wrecked corridor. Sage smoke drifting. Looking at the vaporization marks.

"What the *hell* kind of wandering warrior are you?"

---

### Inner Ring — Amaris Solo

---

We fall into step. Shoulder to shoulder.

"The kind that satisfies you on your couch."

Her mouth opens. Closes. The flush hits her ears.

"I hate you."

She doesn't hate me.

---

The inner patrol ring. Four silk hammocks in the canopy. Each spider bigger than a large dog. Darker chitin. Deeper veining. The garrison.

I tell her to take them. I'll step in if needed.

She stares. She calculates. She does it.

Sage smoke first. Webrot on the low hammocks. Entangle — the thorned roots from Day 1, meaner now, climbing the trunks and finding the shapes inside the dissolving silk.

Two spiders pinned and pierced in the canopy. A third drops — she catches it with Earthen Command, flips it on its back, drives the scimitar into the exposed underbelly. Twice. Dead.

The fourth drops on her. Full weight. Mandibles snapping. She holds them open with the scimitar crossguard. Legs wrapping her body. The bite gets through — shoulder, through the leather. The venom hits. Paralysis. She goes down.

She grows roots through her own back. Through the spider above her. Thorned runners punching through the abdomen from below. It staggers off. She rolls free. Drives the blade through the eye cluster.

The remaining two in the canopy — she brings the branches down with Earthen Command. Walks through the wreckage. The scimitar rising and falling. Methodical. Every strike placed. Joint between thorax and abdomen. Eye cluster. Underbelly.

Four dead spiders. Purple ichor on everything. Blood on her arm from the bite.

"I just killed four giant spiders."

---

### The Stilthorn Recipe

---

She asks how big the spiders are going to get. I tell her I brought something for her — a paralytic recipe from a master herbalist. Stilthorn. Three-second onset. Full motor paralysis. One minute.

She reads it at INT 16 speed and almost drops it.

"Whoever wrote this was doing field refinement without lab equipment. This is grandmaster-level work." She stares at me. "You have INT 9 and someone handed you this and you just *kept it in your bag?*"

She tucks the recipe into her component pouch like it's worth more than gold. It is.

"If I'd had this twenty minutes ago I wouldn't have almost died."

---

### The Short Rest

---

The creek crossing. Running water. Healthier soil. She heals herself — Cure Wounds on the mandible wound. Grinds a poultice. Drinks. Eats.

I tell her we're not heading back. Short rest. She won't need to fight. I'll take the big one.

"Good job taking out those four. I had complete faith in you."

She looks at me. Nobody's said good job to her in six months.

"Were you actually scared?" she asks. "When that last one was three feet from me?"

I tell her I wanted her to have the victory. She earned it. But I'd never let anything hurt her.

She leans into me. The ichor-covered armor against my chest. The silver hair against my neck.

"Nobody's said that to me either. The not letting anything hurt me part."

---

Thirty minutes into the rest. I pick her up. She lifts the mage robe. Her back against the bark. Brief. Intense. Her face against my neck.

When I finish, something slips. A thread of gold warmth through my hands and into her body. The ember — just a whisper. The remaining wounds close. The venom purges. The exhaustion lifts.

I don't have family. I don't have a home. I have an iron key pulling south and a road I've never walked and a bag with a stolen chest inside it.

The key pulls. I walk.

End of Fraying Empire — Chapter Five: *The Eastbound Run*

## Fraying Empire — Chapter Six: *The Grove That Held Its Breath*

### Ashmere 22–25, 1247 AR — Millhaven, the frontier road, Seravane's grove, back again

---

The iron key pulled south-southwest. Something sealed. Something waiting. The road I'd never walked.

I didn't walk it.

The east pivot happened before my boots knew they were turning. Bandit letter, the circle of wagons that stopped existing, the border Seravane drew fifteen miles north of where Bracken's maps ended — east first. The key would keep. The key had kept for a very long time already.

---

### The Roadside Chapel

---

Clone 120 meters ahead. Ronin hat low. Dreads tied under the brim. Nodachi across my back. No Frost Fang on my hip — there never was one on my hip. The ember stayed banked. I walked invisible.

The chapel was stone, half-collapsed, frontier shrine to one of the small saints nobody remembered the name of anymore. A man was sitting on the step. Black polished plate. Greatsword across his knees. Bronze ring at his throat glowing soft green even in daylight. He watched the clone walk up the road with the patience of someone who'd been watching the road for two centuries.

Then he looked past the clone. Straight at me. Eighty feet back.

*"I see you, ronin. Come up."*

Wind Step disengaged. I walked the rest.

I knelt on the chapel step in seiza, nodachi laid flat across my thighs. Unwrapped a cloth. Roasted duck. Fruit tea still warm in a sealed clay flask. Assorted vegetables. I set it out in a line between us, took off the ronin hat — let the dreads fall — pulled the ninja mask down.

*"I am Kenji. This —"* I gestured at the food *"— is for the dead homies."*

I started eating.

The paladin watched me for a long time. Then he laid the greatsword against the wall. Not sheathed. Just set aside.

*"Sir Corban. Ashenmere Order. Second Banner, Siege of — doesn't matter. I've been dead two hundred and four years."*

*"Yea. I heard you call her name out last time I was here when I stole that chest you were guarding. Seravane. Can you take me to her?"*

He chewed duck for a while. Then:

*"The rule is the road fork at the weeping elm. You give me your nodachi in its sheath, the chest you took from me, and the key you used on it. I give you my word as a dead man you walk out again."*

I took the chest out of the storage ring. The iron key on top of it. I stood up. Stripped. Folded the ronin hakama, the threadwalker gloves, the windstrider boots. Nodachi in its sheath on top of the clothing. Chest on top of the nodachi. Key on top of the chest. Kept the enchanted undies. Handed the whole stack to him.

He didn't laugh. He looked at the stack. Looked at me standing in my underwear on a frontier chapel step with dreads blowing in the wind.

*"...dress. Follow me."*

I dressed. I followed him.

---

### The Grove

---

He led me four miles north through woodland that got less normal the deeper it went. Trees I didn't know the names of. A silence that wasn't quiet — it was *listening.* Amaris's Living Ground whispered *a grove remembers who it lets in,* and I felt the grove notice me, and then I felt it let me pass.

The weeping elm was the size of a small temple. Roots in a lattice. A pool of water underneath. Ley-light in the water, soft blue, rising every eleven seconds in a slow ring.

A woman was suspended above the pool. Not floating. *Held.* Robes of deep green. Hair that had been a dark brown two centuries ago and was now something less accurate than a color. A bronze ring on her right hand matching the bronze ring at Corban's throat.

Her eyes were open. They moved when I walked in. She was watching me.

I'd come expecting undead. I wasn't looking at undead.

Her chest rose. Held. Fell. Eleven seconds. Rose. Held. Fell.

*Diagnostic Touch* fired before I asked it to — Wynn's bond reading her from across the chamber without a hand on her. No necrosis. No soul displacement. No death signature. A metabolism running at one percent. A woman holding her breath on the ley.

*Field Notes* wrote her stats on the inside of my eyes. High WIS. Item-boosted, massively. INT modest. CHA not low. STR and DEX both asleep — two centuries of no movement will do that.

She was alive. She was a person. She had a thesis.

*"Warden. You, are not dead. You are aging very slowly. You have an item giving you massive wisdom. I am Kenji."*

Her lips moved. No sound came out. Then:

*"...Kenji."*

Trying the shape of the word.

---

### The Proposal

---

Corban's sword hand went to the hilt when I stepped closer. Then eased. Professional courtesy. We'd eaten together.

I put the question down straight. I have never been good at the long way around things.

*"My question is — when I conquer your territory... will you..."*

I glanced at Corban. Grinned. Looked back at the Warden.

*"Will you bear my children?"*

Corban's face didn't move. Two centuries of training. The bronze ring at his throat brightened a quarter-shade.

She considered the question. Took eleven seconds to consider it — one full breath. Then:

*"I was a Warden-elect of the Ashenmere canon. In 1042 I chose this suspension over what was coming for me. I have not had the option of that question for two hundred and four years. I do not know what I would answer if I were unbound. I will not promise from inside the cage."*

*"Int 9,"* I said. *"So you saying there's a chance. I'll take it — thanks for answering. As for your binding, don't worry, I'll take care of that soon. Whether you agree or not — the choice is totally yours, I respect that, Warden. I was just struck by your beauty and had to ask. My last question is — may I show you a god?"*

Eleven seconds. A ripple in the pool.

*"...show me."*

---

### The Summon

---

I turned to Corban. Gestured, slow and open, the two-handed form of a warrior offering a blade to another warrior.

*"Please, Sir Knight. I'm about to hand you a sword."*

I raised my left palm. Empty.

Then I stopped hiding.

The ember opened in the center of my palm — gold-green, small as a pomegranate seed. The grove air cooled around my wrist. Cold vapor curled off the light. The ember unfolded — creation-energy spooling out in a thin helix, lengthening, thickening, darkening. Blue-black steel poured into existence blade-first, edge-last, a green entropy line waking along the cutting edge like frost crawling across a window in reverse.

Frost Fang settled into my grip. Hilt-first. Perfectly balanced. Cold enough that the grass under my palm stiffened.

I turned the blade flat across both hands. Extended it to Corban.

*"Sup sup, god of entropy."*

The blade answered before Corban's hand closed on the hilt.

*Solveth.* Not loud. Sourceless. Dry.

*"You say 'sup' to me, little arsonist. Two centuries I've tolerated that."*

Corban's jaw did something Corban's jaw probably hadn't done in two hundred years — it opened slightly, unguarded, hearing a voice he didn't screen for. The green glow at his sternum flickered.

*"Paladin. You die at the edges and heal in the middle. Holy in your chest, rot in your hands. That's not a curse, that's a contract — you signed both sides. Who did you sign the second one with?"*

Corban took the blade. Respectfully. Looked once at the Warden.

*"...I signed it with her."*

*"Ah. Love. Cheapest metal in the forge, strongest weld. Hand me back, knight. I have a lady to greet."*

Corban turned. Offered the blade to the Warden, flat-palmed. She did not take it. Wardens-elect don't take offered weapons from strangers. But she looked.

Solveth's attention swung, and now the dry tone had grain in it. *Interest.*

*"Warden-elect. You're holding your breath. That's a long breath. Somebody hung you on the ley and walked away before finishing the sentence. Whose thesis was the ring? Yours or his?"*

The pool pulsed. One slow ring. Then still.

Her lips parted.

*"...mine."*

*"Thought so. You built your own cage. That's the good kind. Most gods only meet people who built the bad kind."* A beat. *"Your metabolism is at one percent. Your reproductive system is intact but dormant. Your cells age at one percent. You are, by every measure I use — and I use all of them — a living woman in a very long pause. Your paladin knows this and has been very polite about it."*

Corban's face did not move. The bronze ring at his throat went from green to something closer to white for half a heartbeat, then back.

---

### The Arrangement

---

*"Solveth,"* I said. *"I was hoping you'd keep my future baby mama company while I finish this epic quest to free her."*

The entropy edge dimmed. Considering.

*"I accept."* Then: *"Terms. One — she is in my domain. Stasis is an ending that refuses to land. I want to study it. Not probe her. Observe. Two — you will come back. Before the second full moon. You die, I ride back to whoever picks up the ember, and that is usually worse for everyone than you can imagine. Three — when you return, we discuss the cover you're burning through. Five creation-tells in two months. I've been counting."*

I thought about it. Then told him the truth.

*"I'll be back. Solveth is part of me — I can't leave him even if I wanted to. I'd just summon him. But I'll be back. If for nothing else, to see Seravane. May I head out?"*

A pause.

*"...yes. Correct. The ember is you, arsonist. I'd drift back to you inside a day. I was curious what you'd offer. You passed. Barely."*

He turned his attention back to the Warden.

*"Warden-elect. The terms adjust. I cannot stay. But I will visit, every time he summons me, and bring what I've learned between visits. You have two hundred and four years of unheard thoughts. Start sorting them. I'll want the interesting ones."*

Her chin lifted one degree. In a woman who moved on an eleven-second clock, it was a great deal of agreement.

*"Arsonist,"* Solveth said, without turning — Solveth didn't turn. *"Dismiss me when you're done showing off. And next time — lead with the tea."*

Corban offered the blade back to me, hilt-first. I took it. Closed my hand. The blade dissolved — entropy edge first, steel unspooling into cold fog, the ember drawing back into my palm and winking out under the skin.

Empty hand. No tell.

She spoke once more. Quiet enough that the pool didn't ripple.

*"...Kenji. Don't be long."*

---

### The Road Back

---

The root-lattice at the edge of the grove lifted as I passed — not for me to walk through, but the way roots move when they've been asked to remember a face. Amaris's voice, far in the back of my head: *a grove remembers who it lets in.*

Corban walked me to the treeline. Stopped where the Deepwood-stuff ended and the Pallid March air came back. Cool. Iron-tasting. Evening falling.

*"Ronin. The grove opens for you. When you come back — you come through the chapel at the road-fork. I'll know. I'll meet you."*

Not a question. A paladin confirming the shape of a contract.

He did not say goodbye. Paladins of his era considered farewells presumptuous. He watched me until I was past the first ridgeline.

---

I walked the ridge thinking about it.

I am not a smart man. INT 9 is a running joke among people who love me. But I have a habit of putting the pieces I do pick up down next to each other until they look like something.

The cage is hers. She built it. A Warden-elect in 1042 sat down and designed a two-hundred-year hold on her own body, on purpose. People don't do that unless the alternative was worse. There is a reason she went in — either a thing pursuing her or a thing she was waiting to outlast — and I don't know which. First question.

The cage is bronze. Her ring and Corban's ring are talking to each other. His binding is running off her mechanism. If her ring comes off clean, his ring resolves with it. The whole thing unwinds together or it doesn't unwind at all.

The cage is ley-mounted. Cut the ley and she drops to zero. That's not a release. That's a murder. I don't want that door.

What Solveth told me without telling me: stasis is an ending that refuses to land. That's his domain. If anyone can see the seam in her ring, it's him. He volunteered. My job is to summon him more, feed him what he asks for, let him map the thing. He doesn't work free. He'll want a price. I file it.

What I have in my pocket that I didn't use in the grove: Wynn's Diagnostic Touch — hand on the ring. Taryn's Steel Sight — read the ring as an item. Amaris's Living Ground — read the root-lattice and the ley-node. Three lenses I didn't put on the cage because I was playing a different game in the grove. Next visit, I look.

Thessaly. 1042 AR is her archive wheelhouse. One whisper-stone. Low risk — she's sealed. I ask about Warden-elect records and whatever was hunting them.

The chest. I gave Corban the bronze ring I took from the chest he was guarding. Why was there a chest? What was in it? I didn't ask. Next visit, I ask.

Not doing: cutting the ley, forcing the ring off, fighting Corban, bringing an army. The answer here is small and careful, which is not my speed. I'll learn.

---

I camped in a shallow draw on the lee side of the ridge. No fire. Four hours sitting against a rock, nodachi across my lap. I didn't dream. I rarely do anymore.

False dawn Ashmere 25. I moved. One Wind Step charge — ten minutes of 300-foot stride with Haste — ate twelve miles of open March road before the sun was up. Walked normal after that. Cool morning. Light drizzle at noon. Sky clearing by the time Millhaven's south gate showed on the horizon.

Hood up. Mask half-down. Same kid on gate duty, same wave-through.

---

### The Garrison Office

---

The clerk at the front desk had me rack the nodachi. Led me down the hall. Last door on the left.

Bracken stood when I came in. Late thirties. Kingdom-brown hair cropped short. Half-plate over linen. Gorget off, hanging on a peg. Eyes that had read too many patrol reports and slept too few full nights.

*"Ronin. You're earlier than I expected. Seal hold?"*

The ruin seal was resolved. The Ashenveil scouting I'd handed off to Taryn, and her letter had hit Bracken's desk already. There was no pending work between us. There was no pending money between us. I'd come back for a reason that wasn't either.

She looked at me. Held the look a beat longer than the report warranted — watch captain noticing she was enjoying the view of a ronin in her doorway and choosing not to pretend otherwise.

*"I have a lot of report. I need more time than we have right now. When does your shift end? I'll come to your place and stay over. Give me eight hours of your time. I'm trying to crack a mystery."*

She did a small thing with her eyebrows. The corner of her mouth twitched.

*"Stay over."*

She said it flat, like she was testing the words. Liked the shape. Sat down.

*"Sixth bell. Cooper's Row — third door from the end, green shutters. Two-story, no sign. I don't have a guest room. I have a couch, which is more honest than most guest rooms."*

Terms: off-books until we decided otherwise. If it was actionable and urgent, we'd stop and act. I'd eat, because she wasn't running on field rations for a full night.

*"You came back in five days,"* she said. *"The ruin seal is done and paid. The Ashenveil job you handed off to your halberdier — she's a cyclone, her letter hit my desk, I've already escalated it. So when the treasurer asks me what you're doing back in my office, the answer is... what, exactly?"*

She waited. I didn't answer yet. Some things keep for sixth bell.

*"Anything else for the next four hours,"* she said, *"or should I let you go find a bath? You smell like a road and a grove and I can't tell which is worse."*

Slight lift at the corner of her mouth. Brief. Gone.

I took the hat, the nodachi at the rack, and Millhaven's afternoon.

Bathhouse on Carver's Lane. Clean linens. A barber who did not talk. Two hours of being a man instead of a road.

---

### Cooper's Row

---

Sixth bell. Third door from the end. Green shutters. Two-story, no sign.

She opened the door in plain linen, hair out of the garrison tie, gorget off for the first time I'd seen. Stew already on. A second bowl already set on the table. She'd planned for me to eat the moment I came through.

*"Sit. Eat. Then we talk. I don't take reports on an empty man."*

I sat. I ate. Mutton and barley and black bread. Watch-captain food — hot, heavy, plain, functional. She ate across from me and did not ask a question for twenty minutes. Just let me come back into a room.

When the bowls were down I told her.

I told her about the bandit letter. The wagons that stopped existing. The column of bodies still walking a patrol route that was decommissioned two summers ago. Four garrison dead on their feet — Karel, Pasten, Meg, Yon. Corban on the chapel step, two hundred and four years dead, bond-sworn Ashenmere, civil exchange at the threshold. The grove that lets people in and closes permanently on columns. The weeping elm. The pool. A Warden-elect hung on a ley-node, breath every eleven seconds, metabolism at one percent, alive, awake, choosing the cage because 1042 AR was worse. Bronze ring on her hand. Matching bronze ring on his throat. One mechanism, two bindings, unwinds together or doesn't unwind.

I told her I summoned a god in the grove. I told her what the god said. I told her the god volunteered to keep Seravane company while I finished the job.

I did not tell her about the ember. I did not tell her my cover. Those were not tonight's to give.

She listened. She took notes once — quick, shorthand, a watch captain's margin scratch. She asked three tactical questions and they were all sharp:

— *How long since the column was last seen moving.*
— *Whose ley-node is the grove sitting on and who else has pull on it.*
— *What does the bronze ring look like — can you sketch it.*

I answered what I could. I sketched the ring. She studied the sketch a long time.

*"I've seen this shape. Not this piece. The shape. Ashenmere Warden-elect bond rings had a specific geometry in the 1040s — I trained on the forms in my Coalition rotation. There's a woman in Ashenveil named Vellin who keeps a private archive from that era, she takes contracts for outside readers, she's not cheap and she's not polite but she doesn't miss. I can write you a letter of introduction. She'll want a week of your time and she'll make you sit in a room she doesn't heat. If it's actually a 1042 Warden-elect binding, she's your clearest read."*

I took the name. Vellin. Ashenveil. I filed it in the plan.

We walked the brute-force door and she closed it. Not mine to bring soldiers through Corban's chapel. Not hers to commit troops. Ley-mount kills her if pulled. Living ward closes if forced. Her four dead men die twice. We agreed.

We walked the careful door and it stayed open. Me, alone or with her, through the chapel at civil terms, lens-work on the ring and the lattice and the node, Solveth mapping the seam between visits, Vellin reading the archive in parallel.

Somewhere around the fifth hour she stopped taking notes. Set the charcoal down. Looked at me across the table like a watch captain and like a woman, and did not pretend the two were separate.

*"Kenji. I'm going to be straight with you, because I don't run a house where people pretend. I've enjoyed sitting across a table from you for five hours. I've enjoyed it more than I enjoyed the last three men who sat across this table for five hours. I'm telling you that out loud so you know where I am. You do what you do with that. I'm not chasing and I'm not asking. I'm telling."*

I told her I liked her too. I told her the grove was in the morning and what I wanted tonight.

She told me straight. I told her straight. We worked out the shape of it at her kitchen table the way you work out a patrol route — honest, unhedged, both of us nodding when the map agreed.

Sex after the grove was my first frame. She held it a second. Asked if I was sure that wasn't me stalling. I thought about it. Said no, said I wanted the night now, grove first light. She said good, because she was going to make that counter-offer anyway.

The couch stayed empty.

### The Morning

---

Sixth bell. Temple bell answering from two blocks south.

She was up on the first tone and dressed by the second. Nineteen years of watch-captain waking does not care what the night held.

She stopped at the small bronze mirror. Pressed two fingers to her sternum. Frowned once — the kind of frown where you check for a thing you remembered feeling and find the feeling missing. She did not announce it. She just rolled her neck, tied her hair back at the nape, and went down the stair to lay kindling.

By the time I came down she had tea on, oats in the pot, and a traveling pack open on the bench by the door — light, two days at most, a watch captain's pack with a watch captain's restraint. Her half-plate sat on a chair. She was in field linens.

A folded square of parchment sat sealed on the table beside the teapot. Black wax. Her captain's stamp. Vellin's name on the front in watch-captain block script. She tapped it once with two fingers as she walked past.

*"Letter. For Vellin. You hand it to her directly. Don't mention me by rank in the room — she hates the Coalition, she'll read it faster if she thinks I'm a retired lieutenant. Which, if she asks, I am."*

I took the letter. Slid it into the storage ring with the iron key and the sketch of the bronze ring.

On the bench by the door, alongside her pack, a second folded parchment — this one open. Orders to her second, a sergeant named Teilen. *Captain on detached business, four days expected, seven outside. Sergeant has the desk. Patrols on standard rotation. No escalations signed without my seal.* She folded it, sealed it, set it on the bench to be walked over to the garrison on our way out.

She poured two cups of tea without asking how I took it. Handed me mine.

*"We're going back through Corban's chapel at civil terms. You vouch me. I carry the seax and nothing else armed — garrison blade stays in the pack. I read the ground and the root-lattice. You read the ring. Solveth reads the seam. We do not touch her. We map. We come back. Vellin gets the archive pull in parallel. Three angles, one slow answer. Yes?"*

I nodded.

*"Yes. Good. Oats first."*

She set a bowl in front of me. Cinnamon. Honey from a jar she kept on a high shelf. Butter. She had not had butter on oats since last winter, probably. She did today.

She sat down across from me. Ate. Caught me looking. Did not pretend not to notice.

Half a smile, brief, real.

*"Eat your oats, ronin. Grove's waiting."*

A beat. The smile faded. Not gone — just set aside. She looked at her bowl, not at me.

*"Four men. Karel. Pasten. Meg. Yon. I sign their names on the roster every morning as present-on-long-patrol because I do not have a body to write dead against. I'm going to see them today. Walking. I need to tell you now, before the door — I'm going to see them and I am going to keep working. I am not going to stop, I am not going to try to speak to them, I am not going to freeze. I'm telling you so you don't have to manage me in the grove. I'll manage me. I just need you to know I know what's coming."*

She looked up. Steady.

*"After. We'll talk about what to tell their families. After."*

I nodded. Ate. She finished her tea.

A watch captain at my table in her own kitchen. A pack by the door. A blade she trusted. A mystery she'd chosen to walk into with me.

## Fraying Empire — Chapter Seven: *The Fourth Seam*

### Ashmere 26, 1247 AR — Millhaven east gate to Seravane's pool

---

Eight miles to the road-fork. Corban was not on the step. Corban was standing in the middle of the road thirty paces out, and the watch captain at my left shoulder did not break stride.

Six hundred and twelve.

That was the number that came back out of the grove with us. The rest of the chapter is how we got to it.

---

### The East Road

---

Same gate kid, same wave-through, now with a second mythril-sheen shadow walking beside me. He did a double-take. Straightened. Saluted as Bracken passed. She nodded without breaking stride.

Two miles out of town the bonds came online.

*Captain's Read* settled first. Quiet. Not a spell, not a hum, just a sudden extra channel in the peripheral. A farmer at a fence line forty yards off became *worried about his cow, not armed, not lying.* A courier on a tired horse passing the other direction became *carrying a sealed letter not his, knows what's in it, scared of the contents, legitimate commission.* It read everyone I saw, baseline, and quieted them back out of my head when I stopped looking.

Then *Bond-Form Sight.* This one I felt in the storage ring before I saw it — the iron key flared in my awareness, a lattice of geometry I didn't have a name for before but now *read.* Two-century binding. Feeder: a ley-node south-southwest. Terminator: something sealed, waiting. It was the same shape, at a smaller scale, as the bronze ring I'd sketched for her last night.

I filed it. The key was still the key. East first.

She walked at a pace I did not expect — faster than watch-captain standard. Nineteen years on patrol routes had given her a stride that ate ground without looking like it. Every quarter-mile she did a slow sweep — treeline north, treeline south, ridgeline ahead, back to road. Captain's Read told me what she was reading: *corner of the field three hundred yards out — crow behavior wrong, something dead in the furrow, not our problem, file.* She did not say any of this out loud.

Four miles in she broke the silence once.

*"Weeping elm. Before we get there. I want to know what you want me doing with my hands when I walk in."*

A watch captain's question. She was asking for her orders in my grove.

*"Empty. No blade drawn. You carry the seax but you don't touch it unless I say. You stand at my left shoulder, one pace back. You let me speak first to Seravane. After I've opened, you can speak. Don't approach the pool."*

She nodded. Filed.

*"Corban?"*

*"Same. He'll read you. Let him read you. Don't fill the silence — he's two centuries dead, he's got more patience than both of us."*

*"Noted."*

She dropped back to her own pace, and for a mile she was thinking. I felt Captain's Read reach across the Nexus and give me the shape of what she was thinking — she was rehearsing how she held her face when she saw the four. Karel, Pasten, Meg, Yon. Not in the grove. They were out in the column somewhere. She was rehearsing anyway, just in case.

---

### The Chapel

---

Seven miles in. Corban was standing in the road.

Greatsword across his back. Bronze ring at his throat a soft, steady green in the overcast light. Facing the way we were coming.

Bracken saw him. Her pace did not change. She did not put her hand on the seax. I felt Captain's Read light up around him — the channel came back *nothing, dead, no living intent to catalogue* — and then, slower, a second layer she was not used to: Bond-Form Sight echoed through her bond and painted the oath ring at his throat for her the same way it painted for me. She inhaled sharply — small, contained — and did not break stride.

She had never seen a bond-form before outside training drawings.

Corban waited until we were fifteen paces out.

*"Ronin. You brought company."*

Dry. Professional.

His attention turned. Not rude.

*"Watch Captain. Millhaven garrison. Four of yours are in the column — I've been meaning to get word to you, but the road to your office does not open for a man who died two centuries ago. I'll speak their names with you after, if you want."*

Bracken stopped walking. A pace off my left shoulder.

She did not speak. She took him in — the plate, the green ring, the road dust that didn't settle on his shoulders the way it settled on hers. She met his eyes.

Corban held the look a long beat. Then, to me, quieter:

*"She carries a civil form. Coalition rotation. Ashenmere-trained. I can read it on her shoulders. She comes through with you."*

To her:

*"You were not on my list for today. You are on it now. Welcome, Captain. The grove will let you pass. Walk behind the ronin — one pace, left shoulder. You already know that."*

She nodded once. Small.

*"Thank you, Sir Corban."*

The first words she'd spoken since seeing him. Formal. Measured. Watch-captain to knight.

---

### The Grove

---

Corban turned. Walked ahead of us both, twenty paces out, leading north off the road into the treeline.

The grove started five paces past the chapel marker. I felt it through Bond-Form Sight before I saw it — the living ward was a geometry too, a slow breathing lattice, and it opened for us like a door that had been left unlocked for a friend.

It opened wider for Bracken. I didn't know why. Filed.

Three thresholds. Each opened wider than the last.

At the second threshold a living root lifted six inches out of the loam as we passed. Not blocking. Just acknowledging. Bracken's eyes tracked it. She didn't say anything.

At the third threshold, a small brown bird sat on a branch we walked under, watched us pass without flying, then followed — hopping branch to branch alongside Corban for about a hundred feet before vanishing back into the canopy.

*"The wren,"* Corban said, not turning. *"It does that for the first three. It means the grove marked you. You'll get a bird when you come back. Different bird each time — the grove picks. Don't feed it."*

Bracken filed this. I felt her file it.

---

### The Weeping Elm

---

Same as last time. Lattice of roots. Pool of water underneath. Ley-light in the water, soft blue, rising every eleven seconds in a slow ring.

Seravane suspended above the pool. Robes of deep green. Hair the color-less-than-brown. Bronze ring on her right hand. Her chest rose, held, fell.

Her eyes were closed when we walked in. They opened on the second breath. They moved to me. Then, slowly — no hurry in her, two centuries worth of no hurry — they moved to Bracken.

Bracken stopped one pace back at my left shoulder. As ordered. She did not approach the pool. She did not speak.

Seravane watched her a long time. Six breath-cycles. Sixty-six seconds.

Then her lips moved. No sound at first — then, thin:

*"...watch captain. Ashenmere rotation. Coalition. You have stood in the form-room at Dawnsreach, where the bond-ring drawings are kept on the west wall under glass."*

Bracken's mouth opened, closed.

Then, quiet:

*"Four months. Nineteen years ago."*

Seravane's mouth moved the tiny fraction that was her version of a smile.

*"...then you know what I am. Without needing to be told. That is...efficient. Kenji-who-brings-a-captain. You pick well."*

Her eyes moved back to me. Waiting.

---

### The Lead

---

I stepped a half-pace back. Opened my hand palm-up, the small form the Deepwood teachers used for *yours, if you want it.* Bracken saw the gesture. Didn't question it.

She took the step forward to my place at the pool edge.

She did not kneel. She was a captain, not a supplicant. But she pressed the back of her right fist to her left shoulder — the Ashenmere civil greeting for a lesser rank addressing a Warden in a field sanctum. She held the posture a beat longer than strictly formal. Respect.

*"Warden-elect. Captain Bracken, Millhaven garrison, seconded Coalition liaison, Ashenmere rotation spring of 1228. I was a clerk in the form-room the summer they were replacing the west-wall glass. I have seen ten of the twelve bond-ring schematics. Yours is the fourth one on the wall, third from the left, though I did not know it was yours until today."*

She paused. Let Seravane absorb.

*"I am here because the ronin asked me to walk this with him, and because four of my own men are in the column you are holding, and because if there is a thing I can do for you that does not cost you breath, I would like to do it."*

Careful. Not asking for anything yet.

*"Before I ask the captain's question — I would like to ask the clerk's question, if you will permit. The schematic on the west wall shows a three-seam closure. The ring I see on your hand has four. Is the fourth seam a later addition, or was the schematic we trained on redacted?"*

She stopped. Waited.

---

### The Fourth Seam

---

Seravane took three breath-cycles to answer. Thirty-three seconds.

*"...the fourth seam is mine. Added 1041, three months before I went into the pool. The schematic on the west wall is not redacted — it is the Warden-elect standard, and I was not Warden-elect standard. I was running from something standard could not hold. The fourth seam is a grief-seam — it ties the ring's closure to a second ring, worn by a paladin who has sworn to walk until I wake. Cut the fourth and his binding fails. He dies the second death. I chose that seam because I did not want a rescuer who would not pay to rescue me. The grief-seam filters for the right hand."*

A beat. Her eyes flicked to Corban behind us. Then back.

*"You are well-trained, Captain. Better than the form-room taught. Ask your captain's question."*

---

### The Captain's Question

---

Bracken took the half-breath she took before a report.

*"My four men walked a patrol route that was decommissioned in 1245. They are on their feet in a column you are holding. I need to know — when the ring unwinds properly, what happens to them. Do they fall. Do they age the two centuries they borrowed all at once. Do they keep walking until their borrowed bodies give out. Do they have any awareness. I am not asking you to release them now. I am asking what the release looks like so I can write the right letter to four widows."*

She stopped.

She did not mention that she was also asking because she might need to step on one of them at some point in this grove, and she wanted to know what she was stepping on.

---

### Six Hundred and Twelve

---

Seravane took five breath-cycles. Fifty-five seconds. Longer than any answer she had given yet.

*"...the column is a secondary structure. Not mine. A parasite contract that attached to the fourth seam when I was seven years into the pool. A wandering death-binder — I do not know his name, never saw him — found the grief-seam's resonance and hung his own work on it. His work is the column. He uses my anchor to feed his raised. He has been feeding them for perhaps sixty years now. The ones you knew died within the last three — Karel went down by arrow in the Thornfield lowlands, summer of 1244, the others within eighteen months of him. I can feel the four fresh ones. I can feel all the fresh ones. I can feel every one of the six hundred and twelve."*

*"...six hundred and twelve."*

Bracken's voice, flat. Repeating the number to be sure she heard it.

*"...six hundred and twelve. Over sixty years of harvest. Patrol dead, farmer dead, bandit dead, caravan dead. All on my fourth seam."*

The pool rang.

*"When my ring is properly unwound, the grief-seam closes. My anchor stops feeding. The parasite contract loses its feeder. The column drops where it stands — not aging forward, not awareness-granted, just...released. Six hundred and twelve bodies. Falling. Most of them in places their families would know, if their families are still alive. Some in places no one will find them. Your four will fall within the hour — three in the lowland patrol route you decommissioned, one further south. I can tell you which grid squares, if you bring me a map on the next visit. It will be my apology, Captain. Sixty years of apology I will have to write."*

Another pause.

*"The death-binder — when his feeder stops — will know. He will come. He has been waiting sixty years for someone to find my seam. He assumes it will be a paladin. He is not ready for a ronin. He will be very not-ready for a watch captain."*

A thinner, dryer breath.

*"That is the release. Not clean. Not painless. The least cruel door I can hold. Your letter to the widows is not long. 'Your husband's body has come to rest. The man who has been walking it for sixty years is no longer walking.' The truth is enough."*

---

Bracken had not moved. Her hand was resting closed at her side. She was not reaching for the seax. She was not shaking. She was watch-captain still.

*"Six hundred and twelve. The death-binder. Name unknown. Sixty years. When the seam releases — he comes. He expects a paladin."*

Quiet. Locking it in.

*"Warden, I am going to ask one more before I step back. The death-binder — can you give us anything else on him. Territory. Signature. Preferred form. A scent, a tell, a tendency. Anything the ronin or I could brief from before we walk into the release door."*

She waited.

---

The pool rang. Corban had not moved behind us. The bronze ring at his throat had brightened a half-shade during this exchange and stayed bright. Solveth, in my palm, was awake and paying attention — I could feel him leaned in, not intruding, counting the shape of what he was hearing. He had not asked to speak.

Yet.

---

*[End of Chapter Seven.]*

---

## Fraying Empire — Chapter Eight: *The Fifth Voice*

### Ashmere 26, 1247 AR — Seravane's pool to Millhaven east gate

---

Four of us standing at the weeping elm, and a fifth one in my palm who had not yet asked to speak.

He asked now.

---

### The Warden's Profile

---

While Seravane drew breath to answer, I let *Bond-Form Sight* widen on the pool.

The lattice that had been a bright interlocked shape for the whole exchange held steady — 1040s Warden-elect ring, four seams, three clean and the fourth with the crooked tie to Corban at the chapel. What Bond-Form Sight added was the *parasite layer* beneath it. A second geometry, narrower gauge, hooked into the fourth seam from underneath. Not tangled with her work. *Hung* off it, the way a cloak hangs off a peg. The peg was Seravane. The cloak was the death-binder's column. I could see where his tie entered her seam — a thumbprint-sized knot of foreign work, clean-edged, someone else's hand entirely. It was the first time I had laid an ability directly on the thing we were here about, and the geometry matched Seravane's account to the inch.

Also — iron key at my hip, quiet reminder: still tugging south-southwest. The grove had not shifted its bearing a degree. Whatever the key was pointing at was not here. Good to confirm. Noted for later.

The pool went from drawing-breath to speaking.

*"Territory. He moves. He does not hold ground. In sixty years I have felt him set work in four regions — the Thornfield lowlands, the Drycreek marches two kingdoms south, the Irongrave ridge east of here, and a patch of coast I could not name. He rotates. He does not return to a worked region for a generation. The column on my seam is his one standing anchor. He will come *to* that anchor. You will not find him by hunting him. He will arrive on his own line when his column drops."*

*"Signature. His binding is clean. Cleaner than mine. He does not tie grief into his work — he ties *patience.* The raised under his hand do not rage. They wait. They walk patterns. They do not break formation unless told. The ones on my seam have been walking a thirty-two-mile perimeter for sixty years and have not varied it by more than a yard in any season. That is his hand. Patience-binding. It is rarer than grief-binding. Older school. He trained somewhere that does not train anymore."*

*"Preferred form. When he arrives, he will not arrive as a corpse. He will arrive as a man. Middle years. Worn clothes. Walking. He will carry nothing obvious and everything quiet. There will be a small bag at his hip — binder's kit. A knife. A piece of bone. A cord. He does not cast from distance. He works within a pace of what he is binding. Close work."*

*"Tell. He does not blink at the rate a living man blinks. Closer to once a minute than once a second. His jaw is a little too still when he listens. If you are looking for him in a crowd, watch for the man who is too patient to be a man."*

*"Scent — ozone and old lanolin. The ozone is the binding. The lanolin is the cord he carries. It has been worked in sheep-fat so long it will not come out. If you are within ten feet of him in an enclosed space, you will know."*

*"Tendency. He opens with a question. Always. He will ask you what you are doing at his column. He will wait for an answer. He will decide whether to fight or walk from your answer. If he walks, you will not see him again in this country. If he fights, he fights from inside a raised body — not his own. He will not let you cut him. He will let the raised catch the blade. You do not win by killing the raised. You win by closing the column. When the column closes he has no anchor in this province. He becomes a middle-aged man with a bag on a road. Then you have him."*

A quieter note, at the end.

*"That is what I know. It is sixty years thin and one Warden's angle. A paladin would read him differently. The captain beside you will read him differently once she sees him. The ronin will read him once he moves. I have given you the shape I have held. Use it."*

The pool went still again. The parasite knot under *Bond-Form Sight* stayed exactly where it had been. Unmoving. Patient. Fitting.

---

Bracken did not speak for a ten-count. She was locking the profile in — I could feel the Captain's Read channel on her side go quiet and concentrated, the way a watch officer goes quiet when she is memorizing a face from a sketch. Then a small nod. One.

*"Patience-binding. No rage. Thirty-two-mile perimeter, sixty years unvaried. Middle-years man, ozone and lanolin, blink rate wrong, opens with a question, fights from a raised body. Column closes — he loses the province — then he's a man on a road."*

She set it in her own words. Standard watch practice. Seravane had no eyes, but the pool seemed to acknowledge the re-frame anyway — a single ripple that was not wind.

*"Understood, Warden. Thank you."*

Then Bracken did something I watched carefully, because it was her, not her training. She stepped back a half-pace from the pool's edge, put her left hand flat against her chest over the watch brooch, and said: *"The four men you carried — you do not owe us sixty years of apology. The one who owes that is the man with the bag. We will collect from him. Your ring is the door. That is enough."*

The pool answered very faintly. *"...thank you, Captain."*

---

### Solveth Speaks

---

I felt him lean up through my palm before I heard anything in the air. Not a voice yet. A request.

*"Ronin. May I."*

I let the channel open.

The ring at my hip warmed by half a shade. The bronze at Corban's throat brightened in response — recognition, not alarm. Seravane's pool went from still to attentive. Three paladins acknowledging a fourth voice entering a formal space.

Solveth's voice, when it came out through the ring, was not loud. Older than I had heard him yet — the voice of a man who had been a soldier before he had been a paladin, and who pulled the soldier back up when he needed to brief.

*"Warden. Solveth of Ridgeward. Ashenmere Second Chapter, bonded to the ronin six days. Permission to add."*

Seravane's pool: *"...granted, Brother."*

*"I did not train with you but I trained on the same forms. I know patience-binders. I fought one in the Spoke Wars in 1196. He was a generation older than yours and worked the same school. I want to give your captain three more things and then step back."*

*"...please."*

*"One. Patience-binders travel with a second. Not an apprentice — a handler. The handler is the one who carries the bag. The binder does not. If your captain sees a middle-years man with a bag at his hip and no other companion, it is *not* him. It is the handler. The binder will be within a hundred paces, watching. The handler opens the question. The binder decides from the answer. Watch for two men, not one."*

Bracken's head moved a quarter-inch. The Read-channel on her side re-weighted. She had already been planning for one man. She was now planning for two.

*"Two. When the column closes and he comes to the anchor, he will not come the same day. He will come on the seventh day after the drop. Always the seventh. It is his school's discipline — give the land a week to settle before arriving at the wound. If you close your ring on Lathmere 4, he arrives Lathmere 11. You have a week of warning inside his own rules."*

The pool: *"...I did not know that. Thank you."*

*"You would not have known it, Warden. It was handler-craft, not binder-craft. Third thing. When you meet him, do not give him a true answer to his question. Do not lie either — he hears lying. Answer with another question. His school is trained to wait on answers, not on counter-questions. It buys you between two and four seconds of still-jaw. That is the window."*

A small Solveth-pause. Then:

*"That is what I have. I step back now."*

The ring cooled a half-shade. Briefed, cited his line, handed the floor back, withdrew inside his permission. Chapter manners.

I felt Bracken's Read-channel *register* him — moved him from *context item* to *person.*

She said, quietly, to the pool and also to my palm: *"Brother. Thank you."*

Solveth did not answer. Being named Brother at a Warden's pool by a watch captain who was recognizing him was a larger sentence than he had expected to hear in an afternoon, and he took it in silence.

---

### The Captain's Close

---

Bracken squared up to the pool one last time. Same flat hand on the brooch.

*"Warden, we leave you here with what you have given us. We will return on the second full moon — Lathmere 4 — with the ring gear, the map of the perimeter, and a column-drop plan. The ronin and I will hold the door. The handler and the binder will be briefed for. We will come for the seventh-day arrival ready. The chapel post stays intact. Corban stays on the step. If anything changes on your side before the moon, the chapel post will carry. Confirm."*

*"...confirmed, Captain."*

*"One more. If a paladin other than Brother Solveth attempts to approach your seam in the next nine days — stranger, pilgrim, chaptered or not — the chapel post carries to me within the hour. I will handle it."*

*"...understood."*

*"Rest, Warden. Sixty years is long enough."*

*"...I will try."*

The pool rang once and went quiet.

---

### The Three Thresholds, Out

---

We left the way we had come. Weeping elm. Alders. Bracken stone with the wren on it. The wren was still there. It watched us pass without moving. Corban had not said a word through the whole interior — he was paladin-still against the edge of the clearing, bronze ring at his throat holding its half-brighter shade, not approaching the pool, not stepping back.

At the first threshold Bracken paused. Looked at the wren. Did not reach into her pouch. Said quietly: *"Not feeding it."*

The wren tilted its head. Bracken moved past.

At the second threshold the sound of the grove came back — birds returning, the small noise of water somewhere to the left. At the third, the air changed temperature a full degree as we stepped across, and the ordinary wood was ordinary again.

Corban walked us to the chapel road-fork and stopped there.

*"I will carry, Captain. Hour by hour if anything moves."*

*"Thank you, Brother Corban. If any pale-binder type arrives on your road — any man at all with a bag at his hip who opens with a question — you do not engage. You carry. Do you understand."*

*"I understand."*

He did not bow. He inclined his head the exact shallow amount a chapter-man inclines toward a captain he has just recognized as his operational senior on a seam-matter. Then he turned back toward his chapel. The bronze at his throat stayed bright a while before dimming.

---

### The East Road, Back

---

We walked the eight miles back in mostly quiet. The bonds held on their own — *Captain's Read* gently open on one shoulder, *Bond-Form Sight* on the other, the ring at my hip the steady third. The body was still running the morning's lift — day one of five on the Vigor stack, strength-reserve idling high in the background, nothing to spend it on yet. Iron key at the hip: still south-southwest, unbudged by the detour. Whatever it pointed at was a problem for next week.

About three miles from the gate Bracken said the first thing she had said since the chapel.

*"Patience-binders. Handler first, binder second. Seventh day. Counter-question in the doorway."*

Drilling it. The way a watch captain drills a briefing into the part of her head that will still have it at three in the morning when she has to walk into it.

Then, half a mile later, flatter:

*"Ronin. Your ring. He is good."*

*"He is."*

*"He fought in the Spokes in '96."*

*"He did."*

*"He is older than he sounds."*

*"Yeah."*

A small smile at the edge of her mouth. Not big. The kind of smile a watch captain allows herself when she has added a trained second to her order of battle and it has been a good afternoon at the grove despite everything.

*"Glad he's on the wall."*

*"Me too."*

---

### Millhaven East Gate

---

Same gate. Same kid. Sun three hands down from high — mid-afternoon on Ashmere 26. The kid saluted Bracken. Bracken returned it. The kid looked at me and did not salute, because I was not his captain. He did a proper civilian acknowledgment — head-nod, eyes-to-the-ground-and-back — and stepped aside.

Inside the gate Bracken turned to me.

*"Watch-hall first. I need to log the exchange with the Warden before the shift changes, and I want Teilen on the column-drop prep inside the hour. Then we feed you. Then we sit down in my kitchen with the map Seravane asked for and draw the thirty-two-mile perimeter with the grid squares her four are in. Then — " she paused on *then* " — then we write to Vellin of Ashenveil tonight so the letter catches the next southbound rider at first light."*

*"Southbound rider?"*

*"Captain's privilege. There's one every morning. I usually don't spend the privilege on letters I want back. Tonight I am."*

She started up the street. Four paces, stopped. Turned.

*"Ronin. Nine days to the moon. We use them."*

*"We use them."*

*"Good."*

She kept walking.

---

### Solveth, Quietly

---

About a block behind her, as I followed, the ring at my palm warmed the same half-shade it had warmed in the grove. Not asking to speak. Noting.

*"Ronin."*

*"Captain."*

*"She is the best I have worked with in a very long time."*

*"I know."*

*"Keep her alive."*

*"I intend to."*

*"Good."*

He cooled. I walked on.

---

*[End of Chapter Eight.]*
