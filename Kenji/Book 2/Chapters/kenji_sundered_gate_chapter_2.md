# KENJI — THE SUNDERED GATE

## Chapter Two — *The Spear and the Net*

## Day Fourteen — Midday

---

The Gilt Lens. Maren behind the counter — the same sharp-boned woman who watched me spend fifty-two gold two weeks ago, though the man standing in front of her now would be unrecognizable to the one who walked in then. Her copper-brown eyes track the Academy blue, the silver mask, the Threadwalker Gloves, the iron ring, the bracelet on my wrist where a god's prison used to be.

"You look different from the man who spent fifty-two gold in my shop two weeks ago." She tilts her chin — angular jaw, no jewelry, the practical face of a woman who values precision over beauty. Her hands rest flat on the glass counter, unadorned, nails cut short to the beds. "Better dressed. More dangerous. Same empty pockets?"

"Whisperstone Ring pair. Matched set."

She reaches beneath the counter without looking — every drawer memorized by decades of muscle memory. Slim fingers pull a velvet-lined box from the second shelf and set it on the glass. Inside, two pale stone bands. Each threaded with a single copper filament that pulses faintly when the box opens, like a heartbeat finding its twin.

"Two gold, eight silver. Range is half a mile in open terrain, quarter mile through walls. Speak the attuned word and the paired ring warms. Speak normally and the other wearer hears you as if you're standing beside them."

She pauses. Looks at the box. Looks at me.

"Who's the second ring for?"

"Combat operations."

"Mm-hm." She doesn't believe a word of it. The copper-brown eyes track from the rings to my face with the precision of a woman who sells intimacy in gemstone form for a living. "Combat operations. Of course."

Two gold, eight silver. I count it from the pouch and leave it on the glass. The box goes into my belt.

---

The Silver Draft. The smell of bread and bacon grease hits me before the door finishes opening and the ember reminds me — six hours since dawn, one meal down, the timer ticking. Fast Metabolism doesn't negotiate.

Pip is behind the bar. Sleeves pushed to her elbows, a cloth over one shoulder, hands working a drying rack of tankards with the practiced rhythm of someone who's been doing this since before she could see over the counter. She's clean today — no flour, no kitchen dust. Her dark hair loose past her shoulders, warm brown skin catching the light from the front windows, the kind of face that makes the Silver Draft feel smaller and warmer than it is. The Whisperstone ring — her ring, the matched pair to a set that doesn't exist yet — glints on her finger as she works.

She sees me and stops. The hands go still on the tankard. The brown eyes — large, direct, the eyes of a woman who reads people the way innkeepers do, which is to say honestly and without apology — sweep the Academy blue, the mask, the weapons, the expression I haven't had time to hide.

"You're leaving."

Not a question. She reads departures the way I read threat posture.

"Eastern highway. A week. Maybe less."

She puts the tankard down. Comes around the bar. The Silver Draft is quiet — midday lull, two old men in the corner nursing ales, nobody watching. She stands in front of me and doesn't touch me and that's worse than if she had.

"I need food," I say. "Enough for seven days. The satchel keeps it fresh."

"I know what your satchel does." She's already turning toward the kitchen. "Sit. Eat. I'll pack the rest while you do."

I sit. She brings a plate — double portion, roast meat and root vegetables and bread still warm from the oven. The ember drinks it like water hitting dry earth. I eat fast. Not because I want to but because the timer doesn't care about moments.

Pip comes back with a canvas bundle. Dense. Heavy for its size. She sets it on the bar beside my satchel.

"Seven days of you. Bread, dried meat, hard cheese, three jars of preserves, salt. The honey cakes are separate — wrapped in the cloth on top." She pauses. "There's fourteen meals in there. You'll need twenty-eight at your pace. Buy the rest on the road or hunt."

"Pip."

"Don't." She holds up one hand. The ring catching light. "Don't do the speech. You always do a speech."

"I wasn't going to —"

"You were going to say something charming and sincere and then leave, and I was going to stand behind this bar and think about it for a week." The brown eyes hold mine. Steady. The knowing warmth of a woman who chose this and keeps choosing it with her eyes open. "Just come back. That's the whole thing."

She leans forward. Kisses me — brief, deliberate, the kind of kiss that says *I know exactly who you are and I'm not asking you to be different.* Then she picks up the tankard and goes back to drying.

"Eat your vegetables."

I eat my vegetables. I pack the canvas bundle into the satchel. Fourteen meals. Halfway there. I'll hunt the rest.

---

Garrett's room upstairs. The door open. The table different from last time — supply manifests replaced with documents and a regional map pinned to the wall. Red marks on the eastern border. He's standing over it when I knock on the frame.

He looks up. Sweeps the Academy blue, the mask, the gloves. The broad shoulders shifting under the chainmail as he reaches for a sealed envelope on the table.

"Merchant interviews. Three attacks mapped. Dates, locations, approximate times." He holds the envelope out — thick, heavy, the wax seal stamped with the Broken Antler mark. "The witnesses aren't reliable on detail — scared people remember feelings, not facts. But the locations are consistent. Forty miles east of the Ashward crossroads. All three in a twelve-mile stretch."

I take the envelope. It goes into the satchel beside fourteen meals and a god's bracelet.

"The first was nine days ago. Latest was two days ago. Frequency is increasing." He taps the map — three red marks clustered on the highway like a wound grouping. "The Vanguard Hall posted the contract out of Keldara but the kills are payable at any guild post. Including Thornwall."

He straightens. The mace on his belt shifting with his weight.

"The mine — the assessor came back. Asking about ownership documentation that doesn't exist because you claimed it by force and we both know it. I can handle him while you're gone. The assessor isn't the problem. The Council behind him is. But that's a fight for when you're back."

"Handle it."

"I will." He folds his arms. The pose of a man who was hired as a mercenary and somehow became a chief of operations. "Don't die on a highway contract. It would be embarrassing for the guild."

---

The Academy courtyard. Late morning light cutting through the colonnade at a low angle. The rain has stopped but the stones are still dark with it — everything glistening, the crest on my chest catching the sun.

Aldwin on the steps. Grey hair loose, the cracked staff across his knees, wire-and-resin binding the fracture from yesterday. The grey eyes don't move from the staff as I cross the yard, but they're watching. They're always watching. Forty years of reading rooms written into the weathered face, the deep creases around the eyes that could be age or could be squinting through combat wards at things that wanted him dead. The hands wrapped around the broken wood are swollen at the knuckles — decades of ward-casting and weapon drills compacted into joints that ache when it rains.

Sera is at the gate. Checking saddlebags on the bay mare. Efficient. Methodical. Her dark hair pulled back from the sharp line of her jaw — olive skin holding shadow in the hollows beneath her cheekbones, the lean build of three years training alone in the dark carved into every movement. Her hands work the leather straps with the same precision she uses to draw a blade — calloused fingers, sword-grip wear at the base of each palm, the knuckles paler where she's been gripping too hard for too long.

She's maybe two inches shorter than me. I don't know when I noticed that.

"Sera."

She turns. The dark eyes sweep — the Veil's moonstone threading, the gloves, the Academy blue, the expression on my face that she's learned to read like weather.

"Whatever you're about to do —"

I drop to one knee.

The courtyard goes quiet. A student crossing behind the fountain freezes mid-stride — young man, ink-stained cuffs, mouth open. Aldwin's grey eyes lift from the staff with the slow precision of a siege weapon acquiring a target.

I hold up the ring box. Open. The Whisperstone catching the light — pale band, copper filament pulsing.

"Sera." My voice carries. I make sure it carries. "Give me your hand."

Her face cycles through four expressions in two seconds. The dark eyes — nearly black in this light, the kind of dark that swallows detail and gives back only sharpness — go wide, then narrow, then very still.

The faintest twitch at the corner of her mouth.

"Kenji."

"Your hand."

"I know what you're doing."

"Then give me your hand and let me do it."

Three seconds. The courtyard holding its breath. The student by the fountain has not moved. Aldwin has not blinked.

Sera extends her left hand. The olive skin paler at the knuckles. The fingers calloused in patterns I've memorized — sword grip, force magic discharge, three years of daily combat drills pressed into the topography of her palm.

I take her hand. Slide the Whisperstone onto her ring finger. The copper filament pulses once — warm — and settles. A matched heartbeat finding its pair.

I look up at her from one knee. Hazel eyes under the silver mask. The grin. Not the careful kind.

"It's a communication ring."

"I know what it is."

"For the mission."

"Get up."

"Is that a yes?"

"It is a *get up before I hit you in front of the entire courtyard.*"

I stand. Still holding her hand. She hasn't pulled it back yet — and we both know it, and the moment hangs there like a held note.

Aldwin's voice from the steps. Dry as chalk dust.

"In forty years of teaching at this institution, I have never once seen a tactical communication device deployed with that much theater." The grey eyes move from me to Sera to the ring to the student who is desperately pretending to be a statue. "Well done. She's blushing."

"I am *not* —"

She is. Just barely. At the tops of her cheekbones where the olive skin runs warmest. The combat mage who testified before fifty people with wet eyes and a steady chin is flushing pink because a man put a ring on her finger in a courtyard and didn't let go.

She pulls her hand back. Folds her arms. The twitch at her mouth fires twice.

"The *mission.*" Firmly. "Explain the plan."

---

I lay it out. Two forces on the same road, covering different ground.

"I'm running Stride. Burning past the safe stretches, scanning the attack zone with God Sight. If I find gates, creatures, wreckage — I call you through the ring. Landmarks, terrain, threat assessment. You get a full picture of what's ahead before you reach it."

She nods. Not the nod of someone receiving orders. The nod of a squad leader integrating field intelligence into her own plan. The arms unfold. The posture shifts — shoulders squaring, weight settling forward onto the balls of her feet. Officer stance.

"I'll ride the bay with whoever I bring. Thirty miles a day. Slow sweep — tracks, debris, survivors, anything you blew past at a hundred and fifty feet a round." She's already mapping it behind the dark eyes. "If we find something in my stretch, my squad handles it. If it's beyond our tier, I call and we hold position."

"How long to build your roster?"

The question lands different than anything I've said today. The dark eyes sharpen. The olive skin pulling tight across her cheekbones as the jaw sets — the face of a woman who has been waiting for someone to ask that question since the day she got her magic back.

"Give me an hour." No hesitation. "I want Ryn."

She pauses. Calculates. The tactical machinery behind her eyes running at full speed.

"Pip's ley-dependent. She needs proximity to the Crown or a ley source. On an open highway that's you, not me. I won't build a squad around someone who loses power when the power source walks away at five times my speed."

She's right. The tactical read is clean.

"Ryn watches. Learns. Transcribes. He saw both Delve fights from behind cover and came out with diagrams." She ticks off the assessment on calloused fingers — one, two, three. "He doesn't need a ley line. He needs exposure. And he doesn't flinch. I want people who don't flinch."

"He's yours if he's willing."

"He'll be willing." The faintest twitch. "I'll pull one more from the combat track. Aldwin will know who's ready for field work."

She straightens. The arms at her sides — relaxed, capable, the posture of a woman who just received a command and is already three steps into executing it.

"I'll have a three-person squad on the road by midday. We stay linked through the rings. You run point, I run sweep. Anything between us is covered."

She turns toward the Academy. Then stops. Looks back over her shoulder — the dark hair catching the morning light, the Whisperstone glinting pale on her ring finger.

"Four minutes."

"What?"

"If I call. You said you cover a mile in under four. That's the standard you set."

The grin. "You timing me?"

"I time everything."

---

The ley alcove. Last stop. I press both palms to the stone and the ember drinks.

God Sight first — the world sharpening, hazel brightening to something closer to gold, the cracks in the alcove walls resolving into individual stress lines from fifteen feet away. Darkened corners surrendering their secrets. The magical resonance of Varenholm itself humming beneath my feet like a song heard through a wall.

Stride next — the current flooding my legs, the ground already moving, every surface calculating distance and angle and time. A hundred and fifty feet per heartbeat. The road east like a runway.

I refill between each cast. The ley line an open reservoir. Frost Fang humming at my hip, Solveth's satisfied warmth pulsing through the blade. Emberfang on the other side — the creation blade, the five-hundred-year-old weapon that started all of this, burning its faint amber glow through the Academy blue leather.

Full slots. Full charges. The Circuit Bracelet warm on my wrist.

I stand in the alcove and I feel the weight of everything — the swords, the mask, the gloves, the boots, the bracelet where a god's cage used to be. Two weeks ago I woke up in the mud with nothing. Today I'm walking out of an Academy in armor that belonged to the most dangerous man in the building, wearing a dead chancellor's mask, carrying weapons older than the institution, and giving communication rings to women I care about while their mentors watch from the steps.

Solveth, from the blade: *You are thinking loudly.*

"Just taking inventory."

*Of what?*

"How far the mud is."

---

Sera finds me at the gate an hour later. Not alone.

Ryn is beside her — the scroll case at his hip like a quiver, satchel on his shoulder. He's dressed for travel now. Sturdy boots. A borrowed leather jerkin a half size too big that makes his wiry frame look younger than his years. Tawny skin — lighter than mine, darker than Sera's olive — catching the midday sun across sharp cheekbones and a narrow jaw that's just starting to lose the softness of adolescence. Straight black hair cropped unevenly at his ears, the kind of cut you give yourself with a knife and a still pond. Dark eyes — darker than Sera's, steadier, set deep under a brow that furrows when he's thinking, which is always. A thin pale scar along the left side of his neck, old enough to be smooth, the kind you get from something you don't talk about. His hands are still — always still. The hands of someone who watches the world like a diagram and is constantly transcribing it in his head.

He sees me and nods. One nod. The nod of a man reporting for duty to someone he chose.

Behind him, a woman I haven't met. Young — early twenties, Ryn's age. Taller than Sera by an inch, broader in the shoulders. Light brown skin and dark curly hair cropped close to her skull. A wooden training staff across her back and a belt knife at her hip. Her hands are interesting — large for her frame, scarred across the knuckles, the right one wrapped in a thin leather brace that speaks of an old fracture healed slightly wrong. She stands the way fighters stand — weight distributed, shoulders loose, eyes already cataloguing the exits. She looks at me and doesn't look away. No deference. No fear. Just an assessment, professional and unhurried.

"Tarin Voss," Sera says. "No relation to Aldren. Combat track, third year. Force projection and shield work. Aldwin recommended her. I tested her this morning."

Tarin doesn't smile. Doesn't extend a hand. She just meets my eyes — dark brown, sharp, a vertical scar through her left eyebrow that catches the light — and waits.

"ArchMagus," she says. Even and clean. No inflection.

"Kenji."

"I know who you are." Still no inflection. But her eyes flick to Emberfang — just once, just for a heartbeat — and come back. She read the weapon before she read the rank. That's a fighter.

Sera watches the exchange with her arms folded. The twitch at her mouth — the one she can't control — fires once.

"She'll do," I say.

"She'll do because I already decided she'll do." Sera's voice is level. Warm underneath, if you know where to listen. "This is my squad. I pick my people."

---

The four of us at the gate. Sera's bay mare saddled and loaded. Ryn on a grey gelding that Aldwin pulled from the Academy stable — the horse already decided it doesn't like him, ears flat, side-eyeing the satchel. Ryn's thin scar catches the light as he turns his head to murmur something at the animal. It doesn't help. Tarin on foot. The staff across her back, the scarred hands at her sides.

"Tarin runs," Sera says, catching my glance. "Thirty miles a day on foot in training. She'll keep pace with the horses."

I look at Tarin. She shrugs. One shoulder. The shrug of someone who doesn't explain things they consider self-evident.

I like her already.

The road east stretches from the Academy gate through the merchant district and into open country. Two days to the Ashward crossroads at horse pace. Hours at mine. Garrett's envelope in my satchel beside fourteen meals, rations for a week of burning through calories at a rate that would kill a normal man.

"I run point. You sweep behind. Anything between us is covered. Ring goes warm, you talk. Ring goes warm and silence, you hold position and prepare."

Sera nods. Professional. The flush from the courtyard gone entirely — replaced by the sharp face of a squad leader deploying into the field with her team.

I look at the three of them. Sera in the center — dark hair, olive skin, the Whisperstone catching light on her left hand. Ryn on her right — quiet, watchful, the straight black hair and deep-set dark eyes and the scroll case at his hip. Tarin on her left — tall, broad-shouldered, the training staff and the scar and the eyes that read weapons before rank.

The Darkblades. Before they had the name.

"One more thing."

Sera waits.

"What's the name?"

"What?"

"Your squad. Vael named his. The Ironveil." I gesture at the three of them — the combat mage, the spell thief, the force projector. Her unit. Her people. Her command. "What's yours?"

Sera looks at Ryn. At Tarin. At the road east. The dark eyes come back to mine and the expression on her face isn't the twitch — it's something older, something that comes from three years of practicing alone in a dark room because someone stole her magic and no one believed her.

"The Darkblades," she says. Quiet. Like she's been carrying it.

The name settles over the courtyard like a drawn weapon.

Solveth, from the blade, barely audible: *She chose well.*

I grin. Not the careful kind. The kind that makes people either trust me or reach for a weapon.

"Darkblades. I like it."

I turn east. The Stride hums in my legs. The road unrolls.

"Four minutes," I say, without looking back.

"Go."

I go.
