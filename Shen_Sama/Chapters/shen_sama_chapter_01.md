# Chapter 1 — *Stand Down*

**Day 1 dawn → Day 3 mid-afternoon (~3 days, ~28 miles south of Dragonspine peaks)**
**Level 1. First Ankuspawn True Dragon ever recorded. First combat: FATALITY.**

---

## Day 1 — The Ledge

The wind on Vorathiel's ledge has a particular texture before sunrise — a slow exhale off the peak, two degrees colder than the air below it, smelling of granite and old ash and the iron-rich runoff from the spring three switchbacks down. Shen has slept on this ledge for twenty-four years and knows that smell the way most people know their mothers' voices. It is, in a way, his mother's voice.

The nest is empty.

Vorathiel left at the hour she always leaves — the moment the high cirrus turns from blue-grey to grey-blue, ten minutes before the sun will actually crest. Her hunting circuit is six hours minimum and she is six minutes into it. The downdraft from her takeoff is still hanging in the cold air at chest-height; he can feel it on his bare arms if he holds still.

Shen does not hold still.

He moves to the nest, kneels at the curved stone basin where his egg cracked twenty-four years ago, and runs one hand across the granite. The basin is warm where Vorathiel slept against it. Closes his fist. Opens it.

The black scale is in his palm. Palm-sized, smooth, edges still warm. He took it from the inner curve of the nest — the place she keeps the scales that fall when she sheds, her quiet vanity, the place she would notice missing if she paid attention to it, which she will not, because she does not believe anyone alive is brave enough to take from her. He has learned, over twenty-four years, the precise shape of his mother's blind spots. The scale is one of them.

He stands. Folds the scale into his pant-pocket. Steps off the ledge.

---

## Days 1–3 — The Descent

The descent isn't dramatic. Twelve hours of granite and goat-paths and one half-frozen creek, and the dragon-tattoo stays cool against his chest the entire way. He keeps the claws retracted. He keeps the breath quiet behind his teeth. He moves like something that has watched humans for twenty-four years from a thousand feet up and is, this afternoon, learning the muscle-memory of looking like one. The boots-print of the dwarven trade road shows up around three in the afternoon to his left; he stays right of it, in the broken country, a deliberate quarter-mile off so that he is reading dwarves rather than meeting them.

By dusk Day 1 he is in the lower passes. By dusk Day 2 he is below the cloud line entirely and the air is twenty degrees warmer. By the morning of Day 3 the goat-paths feed into a single rutted track, and the rutted track feeds into something that has actually been graded — a road, maybe a hundred years old, mossy edges and granite culverts.

A road means people.

---

## Day 3 morning — The Waystation

It is mid-morning when the road bends around a granite shoulder and Shen sees the first one.

A waystation. Single-storey, log-and-stone construction, smoke from a chimney, a stable lean-to with two ponies, a covered porch with a kettle on it. Four hundred feet ahead. Wind in the right direction. He has not been smelled yet.

There is one human visible — older, broad-shouldered, wool-shirt-and-leather-apron, working a whetstone against an axe-head on the porch.

The man on the porch sees something in his peripheral. Stops the whetstone. Lifts his head. Squints. Picks up the axe.

"Step into the gate slow. Hands where I can see them. We don't get walkers up the pass without a wagon. *Slow.*"

The two voices inside have stopped talking.

Shen does not stop walking.

The man on the porch keeps the axe at half-raise. Tracks him from the shoulder. The woman in the doorway shifts the crossbow up to bracket-position — not aimed, but ready — and follows him with it. Inside, the third voice (the younger man) has come to the doorway behind her and is whispering something Shen's hearing isn't quite far enough to catch. The waystation does not fire.

He walks the gate-line at his pace. Past the porch. Past the lean-to with the two ponies (one of which whickers and presses against the back wall of its stall, ears flat — animals know what he is even when humans haven't decided yet). Around the granite shoulder where the road bends south.

A hundred yards later he is alone on the road. Behind him, the *clack* of the man setting the axe head down on the porch boards. A pause. Then the rasp of a rope being pulled — the lantern hook by the front door, the kind a waystation keeper uses to signal couriers down the line.

The story has been written. It is now traveling.

---

## Day 3 afternoon — The Patrol

Four hours later Shen is twelve miles further down. The road has dropped into proper foothills — pine forest, real wagon-ruts, the air green and warm and smelling of resin instead of stone. He has been moving at a pace that would tire a man and is not tiring him.

The road bends. Behind him — not close, maybe two miles back where the pass narrows — there is a sound that does not belong to wind or pine. Hooves. Multiple. *Organized.*

Eight minutes pass at a walking pace. Shen does not slow.

Four riders round the bend behind him at the same trot they were holding before — two-and-two formation, the lead pair in mail hauberks under green-and-grey livery cloaks. Polearms in saddle-loops. The lead rider has a sergeant's chevron on his sleeve. The fourth rider, the smaller one at the back, is in courier leathers, a lantern-signal slate strapped to his thigh.

Fifty feet out, the sergeant lifts a gauntleted hand. The four pull up.

"Hold position." Voice carries clean through the pine-air, no shouting. "I'm Sergeant of the Highford road-marshals, on patrol authority of Lord Highford himself. Watchpost three flagged a lantern-courier signal four hours ago — *unusual stranger on the south road, no wagon, no pack, dragon-blood markings.* You match that description in every particular."

He doesn't dismount. None of them dismount.

"You will give me your name. You will give me your origin. You will give me your destination. You will do this from where you stand. If your answers are clean, we will escort you to Highford's east gatehouse where the steward will document you and let you pass through to the heartland roads. If they are not clean, or if you decline to give them, we will either subdue you on this spot or, failing that, send word to the Lord's tower that something I cannot subdue is walking south through his county. *Your call which.*"

The sergeant adds, more quietly, almost — almost — sympathetic: "I have done this for twenty-two years. I have stopped men of every height and description. I have *not* stopped anyone with markings like yours. Whoever or whatever you are, I am asking you for the easy version of this conversation, not the hard one. Speak."

Shen keeps walking.

---

## The Subdual Attempt

The sergeant's hand drops once. The order is single-syllabled and does not need volume — *take.*

Polearms come down out of halt-position. The two armored men-at-arms kick their horses forward in a coordinated ride-and-thrust — left flank, right flank, polearm-tips angled for the muscle-shoulder. The courier behind them has the crossbow up.

Shen does not stop walking.

```
Marshal A polearm charge — d20+5 = 13+5 = 18 vs AC 135 → MISS
Marshal B polearm charge — d20+5 =  5+5 = 10 vs AC 135 → MISS
Crossbow Courier        — d20+4 =  9+4 = 13 vs AC 135 → MISS
```

Marshal A's polearm-tip *connects* with Shen's right shoulder — and stops. The steel skids across dragon-hide skin like a knife across river-stone. No penetration. The haft jolts back against the marshal's grip with the kind of recoil that travels up the arm into the shoulder socket. The marshal grunts. Looks at his weapon. Looks at the unbroken skin under the dragon-tattoo. Looks at his weapon again.

Marshal B's thrust passes through the air where Shen's shoulder *was* a half-second ago. The crossbow bolt buries itself in a pine ten feet past Shen's right shoulder.

The sergeant is, for one breath, completely still.

Then he speaks. Not to Shen. To his men.

"*Hold.* Stand down. Stand the *fuck* down. *Now.*"

Marshal B reins back. The crossbow drops to courier-rest. Marshal A is still staring at the unbroken skin where his polearm landed.

"Marshal A. You hit him at twelve feet of charge with a weapon that has gone through chain hauberks in this county and his skin did not part. Did you hear me?"

"Yes Sergeant."

"He is not subduable by us today. Possibly not subduable by any patrol Lord Highford has on the southern road. Courier — write *that* down. Verbatim."

The courier is already writing.

The sergeant looks at Shen — who is now twenty feet past them, still walking, the dragon-tattoo on his chest faintly green in the afternoon sunlight.

"Walker. I will report what I have seen to Lord Highford. The report will say: *encountered a Dragonspine traveler matching the watchpost's description; attempted lawful subdual; failed without harm to either side; recommend Lord's audience by request, not by force.* That is the courteous version. If you would like a different version reported — if you have a name you'd prefer documented — *now* is the moment to give it. We will not pursue you further today."

He waits.

---

## The Truce Broken

Shen turns.

Twenty feet of road becomes nothing. The sergeant has not finished the breath after his "we will not pursue you further today." The courier's slate is still moving. Marshal A, still on horseback, is in the act of unstringing his thumb from the broken arc of his polearm-grip when the man closes.

The right hand opens. Black dragon-scale claws extend.

```
Shen claw attack on Marshal A — d20+126 = 16+126 = 144 vs AC 16 → HIT
Damage: 1d8 (1) + STR(+124) = 125 slashing
Marshal A: 27 HP → -98 → FATALITY (HP ≤ -10, body destroyed beyond resurrection)
```

The strike does not so much cut Marshal A as *pass through him.* Twenty feet of road and the man-at-arms is no longer in his saddle and no longer in any single coherent piece. The horse rears, blood-flecked, and bolts forty feet south. The sergeant's mouth opens before his hand reaches the sword-hilt.

"*Mother of —*"

Marshal B has wheeled his horse one-eighty degrees. He is not engaging. He is *running.* He kicks the horse hard and is off the road into the pines before his cloak has finished settling.

The courier has dropped the slate. The crossbow is raised but his hands are shaking too hard to aim. The sergeant — twenty-two years on horseback, has buried four men he commanded, has never seen this — pulls his sword and does NOT charge. He puts the blade between him and Shen and walks his horse three paces backward. His eyes have not left Shen's hands.

"You were *walking past us.* I was *letting you walk past us.* What in the *whole* of the kingdom was that for?"

Shen does not answer. The dragon-tattoo on his chest is bright green, pulsing with each heartbeat.

---

## Coda — The Road South

The road holds its silence the way the marsh did three days ago.

The sergeant does not pursue. The courier holds the slate against his chest like the last warm thing on the road. Marshal A is no longer on the road; Marshal A is, in pieces and aerosol, *across* the road for about twenty feet of arc. The horse — riderless, terrified — has stopped against a pine, breathing hard.

Marshal B is gone. The pines closed behind him three minutes ago. He will reach the lord by sundown. The lord will read what Marshal B says about a single claw strike at flag-of-truce distance and will sit very still in his audience chair for some time. The petition for a Mage-Hunter writ will be written before he sleeps tonight.

Shen walks. The dragon-tattoo dims back to its inactive black, the green-fire pulled inward, only the faintest residual glow at the collarbone. The road south stretches in front of him. The blood is already cooling on the granite culverts.

The Cult of Anku does not know yet, but a courier-pigeon out of Highford's tower at dusk Day 3 will carry a one-line description that intersects the Reader's pipeline within four days. By Day 8 a pale thin man Shen has never seen will be walking south from Stormhaven with an Ember-Shade ward in a leather pouch.

The pines smell of resin and iron. Shen walks. The road south of Highford waits.

None of his options for what comes next are good ones.
