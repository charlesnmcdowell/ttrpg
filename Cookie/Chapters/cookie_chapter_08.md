# Cookie's Campaign — Chapter Eight: *Six Gold and a Stew*

*Starter Campaign · The Bard arc · Cookie*

---

## Chapter Eight: Six Gold and a Stew

### Stillday, 7 Bright Turn 1247 AR — Adventurer's Guild, Varenholm

---

### Afternoon — Loaded Silence (16:25)

Senna leaned one elbow on the bar — slow, casual, the body language of a woman who had decided not to be standing for this — and let the silence stretch a second longer than was polite.

"Once," she said. "A long time ago. Before he knew what he was."

Cookie's sternum did the thing where the heart double-checks the heart. The Heartstring resonance was loud in the room. Senna felt it. Senna *knew* she felt it. That was the exact problem — when a fighter with twenty-five years of encounter-scaling and an intelligence score in the high-genius range felt herself being moved by another person's emotional aura, she did not stop noticing. She let it sit and went back to assessing.

"Finch is a grown man," Senna added. "Bang who you like, halfling."

A beat.

"But I want to hear you say it again. Slow. Who did you just call your father."

Behind Senna, Varn the half-orc had not yet returned to parade rest. Beside Cookie, Finch the halfling rogue had not put his beer down in approximately thirty seconds, which was the longest he'd held a beer without drinking from it since the day he'd become a halfling. Across the bar, Thessaly had her hand wrapped around her crystal focus, and the crystal was glowing through her fist like a small caged sun, and Thessaly had stopped pretending Cookie wasn't worth looking at.

The whole hall was waiting on the next halfling-sized syllable.

Cookie sighed.

It was a small sigh. The kind a person makes when they realize the conversation in front of them is a knife and they just put down their armor.

"I'm an Ankuspawn," she said. Out loud. In a guild hall. With about thirty other adventurers, six clerks, and one robed gentleman in the contracts alcove who had been writing in the same ledger for about four hours. "I'm also a support. So if anybody needs to hire one, I'll be over here sipping mead."

She turned. The corner table had been free five minutes ago. It would not stay free.

She walked.

Senna watched her go.

In the contracts alcove, the man in the scholar's grey robe stopped his quill mid-stroke. Hovered the nib above the page for a half-second. Lowered it slowly to the margin. Wrote four small letters in a space that previously had nothing in it. Blew on the ink. Closed the ledger. Went back to writing the contract he had been writing.

He did not look up.

That, more than anything, was the tell.

---

### Honey-Strong (16:35)

The mead came in a pewter mug the size of Cookie's head, and the keep slid it across the wood without asking for coin. Pretty Privilege never advertised itself. It just bought drinks.

Cookie sat. Took a sip. Took another. Then took a real one, the kind a 21-year-old halfling takes when she's just dropped a personal cosmological revelation into a public room and would prefer her face to feel some other way for a minute.

The honey hit the back of her throat, then the bridge of her nose, then her sternum.

What nobody had explained to Cookie about Heartstring was that when *she* was warm and loose and a quarter-step closer to laughing, the resonance in her chest was warm and loose and a quarter-step closer to laughing too, and the thirty-foot bubble of it pushed *outward* with that same temperature. Two bronze-tier rookies near the hearth straightened up and didn't know why. Gruff the clerk accidentally re-alphabetized into a worse order. A retired half-elf bowyer noticed a halfling for the first time in nine years.

Cookie's cheeks pinked. Her smile slid half a click looser. The mead was beautiful. The mead was a slow-rolling problem. Both of those things were true at the same time.

Two figures peeled off an iron-tier bench and made for her corner table.

---

### Selwyn and Beldra

The man led with his hat off, which was how a halfling could tell he meant business at an Adventurer's Guild. Patched leathers. A longbow. Eyes the color of old slate, currently going a shade pinker around the edges as the Heartstring caught him at thirty feet of approach.

"Ma'am — Cookie, sorry, ma'am — Selwyn of Greyrush. We're an iron crew. Took a breach contract at the Sunwell granary last week and got dinged worse than the contract priced for. Got a follow-up tomorrow morning, escort and pacification at a flooded mill east of town. Two days. Room and board. One wight rumor we're discounting. Posting says you charge five gold a quest. We can do five gold and Beldra throws in an axe-edge regrind for whatever you want regrind on."

Beldra was the dwarf. Mid-forties. Bandaged left forearm. Carrying the contract sleeve in her good hand and an opinion about the schedule on her face.

"Don't oversell, Sel," she said.

"Right. Five gold. Tomorrow dawn. Yes or no, ma'am."

He looked at the mug in Cookie's hand and then very deliberately at the floor.

Cookie set the mug down without drinking. Half a head shake. The kind of small, polite no that didn't need a sentence to carry it. Beldra got it before Selwyn finished blinking — the dwarf had run twenty years of contracts, she had a vocabulary for a no that was *circumstantial* versus a no that was *not interested*. Beldra's good hand was already going to Selwyn's elbow.

"Sel."

A beat.

He closed his mouth. Adjusted his hat against his thigh. Got businesslike again.

"Right. Apologies, ma'am. If your schedule opens up — we run out of the Hen & Hammer, two streets over. Beldra books appointments there most evenings."

"Six minutes flat, Sel," Beldra said. Drier than dwarven bread. "New record."

They peeled away. Senna at the bar saw the head shake, saw the elbow, saw Cookie set the mead down without drinking, saw the iron crew leave without bad blood. The corner of her mouth did the not-quite-smile thing for the second time that hour. Twenty-five years of student-watching, and the kid had the social footwork. It was the *other* Senna's-kid traits she was still cataloging.

---

### The Board

Cookie left the mead half-full at the corner table and crossed the room to the contract board, and the room rearranged itself around her trajectory the way rooms did. A bronze-tier rookie's chair went back two inches when he straightened up. A vendor pressed an apple into a stranger's hand for the sheer disorientation of it. Gruff the clerk, who had been pretending to alphabetize, accidentally re-alphabetized the entire stack.

The board was mostly dawn caravans. Multi-day work. Hunting contracts that needed pre-noon mobilization. None of it fit the morning class she had at Varenholm Academy at the eighth bell.

Three same-day jobs were posted. A 5-silver courier run to the Pale Lantern for the Alderman's office. A scriptorium witnessing, two gold, deeds-read-aloud at a sealing ceremony. An undertaker's bounty for a second evening service at Halbert's parish — the same trade Cookie had run at noon, three gold, an emotional re-tread.

A throat cleared at her elbow before her hand even left the board.

"Forgive me, miss. Saw you reading."

The man in the charcoal cutaway coat was guild-stamped and salon-circuit. Mid-thirties. Hair gone silver at the temples in the polished way that men paid for. He felt the Heartstring at the bow-and-arrow distance of a polite introduction and his voice went a quarter-step higher than he meant. He didn't seem to notice. The card was already in his hand.

"Jareth. Something more your range came in twenty minutes ago. Lord Calverton's house, three streets past the Starling, where the old playhouse used to be. His daughter's engagement party tonight. Their booked entertainer took ill. Eighteen-thirty for the door. Performance from nineteen to twenty-thirty hundred. Twelve gold flat plus whatever the guests put in your hand, and Calverton's set is *generous*. The house knows your name from the Starling — Lady Calverton was in the third row."

He produced the card. Address. Guild-stamp. Letter of introduction.

"Performance ends ninety minutes before the city watch second bell. Home in your bed by twenty-two-thirty if you walk slow. School in the morning, I assume. You've got the V.E.A. stamp on your wrist."

Twelve gold was a month of rent. Twelve gold *plus* tips, with Pretty Privilege riding shotgun in a room full of male nobles, was three months. Lady Calverton in the third row at the Starling meant the door was already half-open when Cookie walked through it.

Cookie folded the card into her sleeve and reached past it to pull a different posting off the board.

Wererats. The Gilded Thread cellar. Daylight only, half-day, six gold. Hazel the proprietress had been waiting since the fourteenth bell.

Jareth's eyebrows did a small thing that he immediately put away. He nodded, tipped his hat, said "as you like, miss," and didn't fight her on it. Great User: protective ran the protocol — when a Cookie chose, a Cookie chose, and the man's job was to leave the door open for next time, not push.

Senna at the bar saw all of it. She didn't move.

The mead was still warm at the corner table. Cookie didn't go back for it.

---

### The Brass Whistle

Three figures peeled off the silver-tier bench. They had clearly been waiting for someone to take that exact posting.

Falconer was the human. Mid-thirties. Ranger's leathers worn smooth at the shoulders where the longbow rode. Stocky. Urban tracker. Hat off the moment he reached the table. The Heartstring landed in three seconds, his ears went pink, and he kept his hands carefully visible.

Tova was the dwarf. Mid-sixties. Iron-grey braid. Axe-and-shield. She read the situation in one full-body glance and decided Cookie was the contract-holder, the situation was professional, and Tova's ale was getting cold. She had a vocabulary for halfling-pretty and it lived at "noted, professionalism preserved."

Mograth was the orc. Brawler-build. Silver-shod cudgel. Spoke in single words.

"Begging your pardon, ma'am. Falconer of the Brass Whistle. We were two tankards from claiming that Gilded Thread posting ourselves. Wererats are our trade. Wanted to offer a partnership before you pinned a different crew."

"Standard four-way split," Tova said immediately. "Equal shares. No support discount. Six gold, that's one and a half per head. Shop-owner's discount you can keep — you're the customer there, not us."

"Pups," Mograth said.

"Mog reckons it's a breeding nest by now. We'll know in the cellar."

Falconer caught himself before he said something Heartstring-induced and stupid. "...I'll throw in tracking gratis. If any get loose into the alleys, I'll run them down without re-billing."

Tova glanced at him. Falconer pinked. Tova returned to neutral.

Cookie nodded once. Tova nodded back.

Done.

---

### The Gilded Thread

Crown Quarter was uphill from the guild — paved streets, lamplighters starting their rounds early because the overcast was darkening sooner than it should have. A noble-house footman tipped his hat unprompted. A baker's apprentice in a doorway nearly dropped a tray. Falconer's hand found his bow-grip every time a male civilian's eyes tracked Cookie. Tova ignored it. Mograth scanned rooflines.

The Gilded Thread was *fancy*. Bay windows. Gold-script signage. A velvet display rope. The CLOSED — Stock Inventory placard in the window was a polite fiction.

Hazel the proprietress was a halfling herself — mid-fifties, silvered curls, more pearls than three nobles combined. She met them at the door, locked it behind them, and exhaled like she'd been holding it eight days.

"Bless you for coming. Wait — Cookie? *The* Cookie? Lady Calverton was at the Starling, she — never mind, never mind. Cellar. Down the back stairs through the workroom. I haven't been past the workroom door in eight days. Whatever's down there's been *singing* at night. Not animal noises — singing. Like a child copying a lullaby."

A brass key on a velvet ribbon.

"Three of my seamstresses live above the shop. They're in their rooms now, doors locked. One of them is pregnant. If anything comes up the back stairs — please."

That last sentence hadn't been part of the contract.

---

### The Cellar

Falconer paced the stair landing while Hazel talked. He came back with a tracker's read.

"Three sets of stairs into the cellar — main, scullery, and the dressing-room trapdoor. Layout below: workroom, then a partition wall with one door, then the sub-cellar where they'll be nesting. I count claw-marks on the workroom door. They're using it. Mograth's right, it's a breeding nest. Pups, mothers, two or three adult breeders."

Tova handed Cookie a small clay jar. Silver-paste, the iron-tier remedy for a non-silvered weapon.

"Smear it on the soles. One fight. Don't lick your toes."

Cookie applied the paste. Soles silver-tinged.

Mograth at the doorframe. Falconer nocked, broadhead, silver-tipped. Tova at the lock.

"On three. One — two —"

The brass key turned. The workroom door swung open.

Four wererats in human form, spread across a room of mannequins in half-finished gowns. They had not been lounging. They had been *waiting*. They had heard the key turn and braced.

Two females in the front. One male behind them. One male sprinting for the partition door before Cookie's eyes finished focusing on him.

The females moved first.

Hybrid form was a terrible thing — too much teeth, too little face, a wrongness of joints. The first one hit Cookie low, claws skidding off the Anklet's enchantment, fangs missing the artery and finding shoulder muscle instead. The second's bite caught higher and stuck for half a second before the Ember rejected the lycanthropy curse with a small gold flicker that scorched the imprint clean off the bite mark. *Cookie wouldn't be a wererat tonight.* The Ember had thoughts about other people's biology.

The male grappled her from behind. Capture, not kill. The wererats had instincts and the Heartstring was singing in all of them, and the males' instincts went *take her, don't hurt her,* which would have been comforting if it weren't, in its own way, about three times worse.

The runner reached the partition, threw it open, shouted *INTRUDERS, HIVE.*

Cookie's spell-form had not been designed for graceful response. It had been designed for getting out of exactly this.

She vanished.

Stunning Kicks was a teleport before it was a kick. Cookie became eight kicks at eight wererats inside a second and a half — quick punctuating impacts, each one delivered in the half-instant before the next jump took her. The grapple released the moment her body wasn't there anymore. Eight chitin-cracking, jaw-snapping, rib-crushing little applause-moments distributed through the workroom and the back room, and then Cookie was standing exactly where she'd started, breathing hard, and every wererat in the building was on the floor stunned like statues someone had pushed over.

The Brass Whistle did not waste it.

Falconer's first arrow took a back-room nest-rat through the cephalothorax and pinned it to the wall — silver-tipped, broadhead, drawn to the ear. The second went through another's throat and exited the dorsal carapace. Two of the back room dropped before the front room's stunned could blink.

Tova closed on the female nearest the door, axe arcing low and professional. Mograth crossed the floor at a deliberate orc-pace and brought the silver cudgel down on the male grappler's shoulder hard enough to feel personal, which it was.

Cookie pivoted. Planted. The Anklet's golden filigree flared once across her sole at the apex of a *spinning crescent*, and the female wererat in front of her went through a half-finished ballgown, snapped a mannequin in half, and landed somewhere under a bolt of indigo silk that was not now and never would be salable.

That was the first one.

The second was the male grappler.

He was at one hit point, stunned, kneeling on the workroom flagstones with Mograth's first cudgel-impact still resonating through his clavicle. Cookie's silver-paste sole caught him in the diaphragm at the moment his frozen-stunned body had committed to staying upright. The kick took him through three things in sequence: the air, the workroom partition wall, and the brick *behind* the workroom partition wall.

THUNK.

He did not get back up because there was not enough left of him *to* get back up. The Anklet's filigree flared again — the kick was not done — and Cookie's foot was already snapping back, planted, and snapping forward again before the body could finish hitting the brick. Three follow-up kicks at full power. By the third, the dance had moved past technique and into something the back of her brain kept calling *closure*.

What the Brass Whistle saw afterward was a roughly halfling-shaped impact crater in Hazel's brickwork, a smear of silvered fur and red, and the distant impression that the wererat who had grappled the dancer was now distributed across approximately twelve square feet of formerly-pristine seamstress workspace.

Tova, who had seen a lot of things in fifty years of contracts, said one word.

"...subtle."

The remainder of the fight was housekeeping. One nest-rat saved out from his stun late, scrambled for the dressing-room trapdoor, climbed three rungs before Falconer's arrow took him in the spine and the fall took the rest. Mograth confirmed him with a professional, not-dramatic-this-time tap to the temple. The other two stunned in the back room lapsed past their critical window without healing — eight days of nest, and their crew didn't have a healer either.

Eight wererats. Eight quiet endings. About thirty seconds, total.

Hazel's seamstresses upstairs never had cause to scream.

---

### Hazel Pays

The Brass Whistle came back up the workroom stairs covered in silver-paste residue, wererat fur, and a thin coat of brick powder. Hazel met them at the back-stairs landing already counting coins onto the cutting table.

Six gold.

"Contract clear," she said. "And the back room — the brick — don't worry about it. Insurance covers acts of professional adventurers, my husband's brother-in-law writes the policy, you saved my building."

A small breath.

"Lifetime ten percent off everything in the front of the shop. Goes on your account. The seamstresses are crying, the pregnant one twice, you have a standing order to come in any time and the first fitting is on the house."

Six divided four ways was a gold and a half. Tova swept her share into a leather pouch and Falconer's into another and tossed Mograth his with the flat of her palm without looking. Cookie's coin landed in Cookie's hand. Hazel held onto it for a second longer than strictly necessary, looked Cookie in the eye, and added the part that was not the contract.

"Lady Calverton was in the third row. I will tell *every* upper-tier woman in this city what you did down there. By tomorrow lunch the names will be on Torren's books."

Cookie pocketed the coin.

---

### Goodbyes

Outside the shop, lamplighter doing his rounds, sun two fingers above the Crown Quarter rooftops.

Tova clasped Cookie's forearm with the grip of someone who had decided this halfling was competent. "Good contract, Cookie. If you ever need iron-tier urban work — Brass Whistle posts at the guild board most mornings. We don't take charity hires and you're not charity, so don't be a stranger."

Mograth did the half-bow that orcs did at people they grudgingly respected. One word.

"Halfling."

Walked off.

Falconer lingered. The Heartstring was still humming in his chest. He said, with effort, "Walk you home? Crown Quarter to wherever you're staying — silver-paste residue plus a 21-year-old halfling alone after dark is —" He caught himself. "— not my call. But the offer's there."

Cookie shook her head, smiled — the small one, the closing-up-shop one — and tipped a little curtsy that was somehow not mocking.

The Calverton card stayed folded in her sleeve.

She walked east.

---

### Home

Crown Quarter to Millward Chandlery was fifteen minutes if a person walked it slowly, which Cookie did. The buzz from the mead was thinning, was not gone yet, and she liked the way the cobblestones felt under the silver-paste a little more than she should have. Pretty Privilege fired four times on the walk — a watch sergeant tipped his helmet, a vegetable vendor pressed an apple into her hand without payment, a noble-house footman stepped off the curb to give her the inside lane, a baker's apprentice nearly walked into a lamppost.

Standard Tuesday for a halfling with golden eyes and a hum.

Fern was in the room when Cookie opened the door — sitting on the floor by the bed with the makeup tray Torren's hired her to organize, half-organizing, more tongue-out-in-concentration than productive. She looked up when the door creaked. Saw Cookie. Saw the silver-paste residue. Saw the brick dust. Saw the small smile.

"...you went to a CONTRACT? On a SCHOOL NIGHT?"

She held the look of betrayal for exactly three seconds, then folded.

"I made stew. There's enough for two if you tell me everything. EVERYTHING. Did you actually kill anyone? Wait, no, eat first, then everything. You smell like a forge."

Stew was mutton and barley and the kind of root vegetables a person ate in spring, when better vegetables hadn't quite started yet. Bread on the side. Water for Cookie, because Cookie had had enough fermented anything for one day. Fern's running play-by-play questions while Cookie ate, half of them rhetorical, all of them affectionate. Mead-buzz off by the bottom of the bowl.

By the time Cookie was in bed it was twenty-one hundred bell. Fern blew out the lamp.

In the dark, something quiet sat in Cookie's chest — quieter than the Heartstring usually sat. The Ember had been *wanting* something all evening. Cookie didn't know what. The way you don't know the lyrics to a song you can hum. There had been an opening, somewhere — Senna's bar, maybe, or the dance-spell that had teleported Cookie eight times in a second and a half, or the mead, or the kick that had made the brick crater. Something had been close.

Whatever it had been, the night ended without it. The Ember sat down inside her. It had hit the top of the room it was in, and it was looking for a door.

Cookie closed her eyes.

Outside, Crown Quarter went to sleep on schedule. The lamplighters finished their rounds. A robed gentleman in the contracts alcove three streets away rolled a fresh sheet of vellum into a leather tube, sealed it with grey wax, and addressed it in a hand that did not match his ledger.

He did not look up.

### Day 8 — Rootday, 8 Bright Turn 1247 AR

Cookie woke a little before five-thirty. Fern was still face-down in her pillow making a sound like a small tea-kettle. Outside the air smelled like baking bread three doors down, and the lamplighters were putting lamps *out*, not in, which was Cookie's favorite small pleasure of dawn.

She lay in the dark for a minute longer than she needed to.

Class was at eight.

There was a long day ahead.

She got up.

*End of Chapter Eight.*
