# Cookie's Campaign — Chapter One: *First Night in Varenholm*

*Starter Campaign · The Bard arc · Cookie*

---

## Chapter One: First Night in Varenholm

### Day 1 — Kettlebrook & Varenholm

---

### The Kitchen

---

Morning. Kettlebrook. The Wren's Rest kitchen smells like flour and sugar and the particular tension that fills a room when a mother knows she's losing an argument she lost months ago.

There's a cake on the counter. Daisy made it before dawn — she does that when she doesn't know what else to do with her hands. Cookie's twenty-first birthday. Her little bean is leaving today and there's a cake with too much frosting because love has to go somewhere.

Cookie comes downstairs with her pack over one shoulder. Five gold in a pouch — every copper saved from years of clearing tables and mopping landings at the inn. Everything she owns in one bag.

Daisy's standing at the counter with her arms crossed.

"Little bean." Her voice is careful. Controlled. "You're really doing this."

Cookie's pack hits the kitchen table.

"No daughter of mine is going to Varenholm to dance for men. You have a good life here. You have the inn. You have—"

"Mom."

"You have your father's eyes, you know that? Same look. Same *impossible* look. Like the world owes you something it hasn't delivered yet."

Cookie doesn't flinch. She's heard this before. The father she's never met — a ghost made of half-sentences and deflections.

"Also, Mom — do you have any birth control herbs?"

Daisy puts down the frosting knife. Slowly. The way a person puts down a weapon when they're deciding whether to pick up a bigger one.

Silence. The cake sits there with its stupid frosting.

"You — I —"

She's not angry about the question. She's angry because the question means it's real. Her little bean isn't going to Varenholm to *try* being a dancer. She's going to *be* one. The birth control isn't reckless — it's practical. And that's worse, because it means Cookie's already thinking past the part where she comes home.

The back door opens. Tomas Wren walks in smelling like cut grass and morning air, sees the two women in the kitchen — one vibrating, one standing her ground barefoot — and reads the room in about half a second.

"Well now." He looks at the cake. "That for the birthday girl? Twenty-one. Gods, I remember when you couldn't reach the bar top."

He doesn't wait for an answer. Cuts himself a slice. Eats it standing up, leaning against the counter, crumbs in his beard. Eyes crinkling.

"Good cake, Daisy. Too much frosting. Perfect."

The temperature drops ten degrees. Daisy's still holding the silence where the argument was. Tomas keeps eating cake like the room isn't on fire.

"So, birthday girl." He licks frosting off his thumb. "All packed up. Where's the road taking you?"

"Varenholm. The Academy. I'm going to be a bard." Cookie says it the way she says everything — like it's already true and the world just hasn't caught up. "Like Ignis."

Tomas nods. Doesn't laugh. Doesn't tell her it's a dream. That's always been the difference between him and everyone else in Kettlebrook.

"And how are you getting there?"

"I saved five gold. I'm going to hire a guard for the road."

"Five gold." Tomas sets down the plate. "Listen to me, little one. Don't hire a guard off the road. Half of them will overcharge you and the other half are worse than what they're guarding you from. It's half a day's travel — I know a man with a cart heading that way. He'll take you. Save your gold for the city. You'll need every copper."

Cookie looks at her mother. Daisy hasn't moved. She's standing at the counter with her hands flat on the wood, staring at the cake like it personally betrayed her.

Then she deflates. Not all at once — slowly, like something leaving her that she'd been holding for twenty-one years. She looks smaller than usual. Smaller than Cookie's ever seen her.

"Silverleaf tea." Daisy's voice is quiet. Flat. "Steep it ten minutes. Drink it every morning. *Every* morning, Cookie. Don't skip days."

She looks up. Eyes wet. Jaw set.

"You look just like him."

Cookie doesn't ask. She's never gotten an answer before and she won't get one now.

"I'll write, Mom."

"You'd better."

---

### The Cart

---

The cart is waiting at the south end of Kettlebrook. An old man with a mule and a board seat. Cookie throws her pack in the back and climbs up.

A blur from behind.

Fern — out of nowhere, pack already on her back, grinning like she's been waiting behind a hedge for twenty minutes which she absolutely has.

"I'm coming."

"Fern—"

"My mom doesn't care. Move over."

The cart driver looks at the two of them. Does the math.

"Two people. Eight copper."

Cookie pays. The mule starts walking. Kettlebrook shrinks behind them. The road to Varenholm is flat and easy and half a day long. Fern talks the entire way. Cookie watches the city grow on the horizon and doesn't look back.

---

### Varenholm

---

The south gate. Bigger than Kettlebrook's entire market square. Guards who don't look twice at two young women with packs and plans. The city swallows them whole.

Varenholm. Stone and noise and more people on one street than Cookie's seen in a year. Street performers on the corners — bad ones, mostly, the kind who play lute with three working strings and a hat that never fills. Cookie watches them the way a swordsman watches someone swing wrong. *I can do better than that.*

First things first. Food — something hot, something that isn't road dust. They find a place near the market. Fern orders too much. Cookie watches the room and notices that every man in it notices her.

Then a room. The Millward Chandlery — not a tavern, a boardinghouse. Clean enough. Cheap enough. One room, two beds, shared with Fern. Monthly rate.

---

### The Academy

---

Stone building on the second terrace. Old. Important-looking. A registrar behind a desk who's processed a thousand hopefuls through that door and remembers none of them.

Cookie signs her name. Enrollment. The registrar doesn't look up.

Next — the Varenholm Entertainment Association. A stamp on a card. Official. Legal permission to perform for money within city limits. Cookie holds the V.E.A. card like it's made of gold. Fern tells her she's being dramatic. Cookie tells Fern she's not being dramatic enough.

---

### The Outfits

---

Four outfits. White, black, red, blue. Firebird-style — the look Ignis made famous. Flowing wraps, open shoulders, the kind of clothes that say *I am performing right now even when I'm standing still.* Cookie tries each one in front of Fern, who sits on a stool and provides commentary that ranges from "yes" to "YES" to "Cookie that's not even clothing, that's just string."

They cost more than they should. Cookie doesn't care. You can't be a bard in road clothes. You can't be Ignis in a kitchen apron.

---

### The Gilt Lily

---

Evening. The Gilt Lily is the kind of tavern where the candles are real wax and the drinks cost silver. Cookie sits at the bar in the red outfit — Firebird-style, the kind of thing Ignis made fashion — and orders a honey mead.

Then she waits.

Heartstring doesn't announce itself. It just *is*. Thirty feet of quiet emotional gravity, pulling at anyone close enough to feel it. Cookie doesn't even know she's doing it. She doesn't know what she has. She just knows that when she sits in a room, the room changes.

A merchant at a corner table. Married. Mid-forties. His spoon stops halfway to his mouth and he stares for three full seconds before his wife reaches across the table and redirects his attention back to his soup. He doesn't look up again.

A soldier at a long table with four others. Military posture, dress uniform — off-duty but not off-brand. He considers. She can see him running the math. But the Gilt Lily is too nice for catcalling and when Cookie's gold-flecked eyes meet his across the room, whatever courage he was building collapses. He turns back to his friends. Laughs too loud at something that wasn't funny.

Then Torren.

Tall. Well-dressed. The hands of a man who handles fabric for a living — soft, careful. He's nervous and he's doing a terrible job hiding it. He walks up to the bar like each step costs him something.

"I — forgive me. I don't usually — you are *extraordinarily* beautiful."

He offers to buy her a drink. His voice cracks on "drink."

WIS save versus Heartstring DC 12. Torren fails by 9. He was lost before he opened his mouth.

"You're cute." Cookie smiles. Takes the mead. "How are you doing?"

He tells her he's a textile merchant. Imports, exports, the kind of work that sounds boring until you realize it means money and connections. He asks if she's new to the area.

"Yeah. You should show me around tomorrow."

Not a question. Not a request. A statement that Torren receives like a gift he doesn't deserve.

The barkeep — stocky woman, cropped hair, seen everything twice — leans over while Torren's flagging down the server.

*"He's staying upstairs. Tips nice."*

Cookie files it. Doesn't use it. Not tonight.

"Walk me home?"

He does. Of course he does. He'd walk her to the edge of the world if she pointed that direction. They reach the Millward Chandlery. Cookie stops on the steps. Turns. Smiles at him — the real one, the one that isn't performance.

Then she walks inside. Door closes.

Torren stands on the street for probably ten seconds before he remembers how to leave.

---

### The Room

---

Fern is already in bed.

"How'd it go."

"I have a tour guide tomorrow."

"You've been here SIX HOURS."

Cookie pulls the blanket up. Varenholm hums outside the window. Street noise, distant music, someone arguing about cart parking. The city that's going to make her famous.

Five gold minus eight copper minus food minus outfits minus rent. The math is tight. The math is always going to be tight until it isn't. That's fine. Cookie has never needed money to get what she wants.

She has something better. She just doesn't know its name yet.

Day one. Done.
