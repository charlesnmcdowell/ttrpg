# Cookie's Campaign — Chapter Nine: *Daughters*

*Starter Campaign · The Bard arc · Cookie*

---

## Chapter Nine: Daughters

### Rootday, 8 Bright Turn 1247 AR — Varenholm Academy

---

### Class — Professor Cadence Ashworth, 08:00

The bardic-wing lecture hall held twelve students, a raked-bench horseshoe, a small performance well at the front, and a lacquered wood floor that had been kept that way for ninety years because the right wood under the right footwork was its own kind of magic. Professor Ashworth was already there when Cookie came in — folio open, dove-grey teaching robe, the kind of standing-still that told experienced students she had been rehearsing the day's lesson for forty-five minutes before any of them arrived.

Team Two was in their usual triangle. Silka on the right, conservative high-collared dress, the silver chain at her throat tucked under the fabric where nobody could see it. Ivor with his lute on his lap. Fenella sharpening her shortbow's nock with a knife she had brought from home like a professional.

Silka's grey eyes found Cookie the moment Cookie crossed the doorway. The warmth neither halfling could name flickered between them — the chain dampened Silka's side, but Cookie's full Heartstring rolled over Silka like a tide for the second time that morning. Silka's eyes went wide, then hooded, then dropped to her own folio. She had been processing things since dawn that she didn't have a vocabulary for.

Ashworth ran the cohort through emotional-projection exercises. Three short pieces, each requiring a different emotion to land cleanly on the audience. When Cookie's turn came, the professor looked at her over the rim of the folio and picked the longing one. Of course she did.

The longing piece was nine lines in plain meter. Cookie sang it without dancing — feet still, hands relaxed at her sides, body forward. The longing she projected wasn't generic. It had specifics. It had a halfling girl who had left a kitchen and a mother and an inn called the Wren's Rest and had done it by climbing out a window with five gold pieces sewn into a cloak-lining. It had a Day-One walk into Varenholm with the V.E.A. stamp not yet on her wrist. It had a body that had been chasing an idol for as long as the body had been a body.

Halfway through the second verse, Silka made a small sound at the back of her throat that wasn't crying but had the geometry of a woman keeping herself from crying. Fenella stared at the lacquered floor. Ivor's lute string slipped a half-note flat without him touching it. The Heartstring landed on all twelve students. Ashworth — who had trained against bardic charm her entire teaching career — did not lose her composure, but she did close the folio, set the quill down, and watch Cookie for the rest of the piece without taking notes.

When Cookie finished, the silence held for two breaths.

"Thank you, Cookie," Ashworth said. "Sit, please."

The exercises continued for the other students, but the air in the hall had changed. Silka did not look at Cookie again. Cookie could feel her trying not to.

Class ran to the half-eleven bell. Ashworth dismissed the cohort, paused, and added: "Cookie — a moment after, please."

The other eleven filed out. Silka was the last to leave. She lingered at the doorway for half a beat, half-turned as if to say something, didn't, and went.

---

### After Class — The Sunken Playhouse Fieldwork

Cookie crossed to the music stand. Ashworth had a fresh page open and her quill was poised. The Halbert procession seal went down on the stand. The professor studied Cookie's face for a beat and nodded once.

"I was at the procession," Ashworth said. "The vocal adaptation was correct. The river-song reframing of the eulogy was a choice I'm not sure I'd have made, but it was the choice the family needed. Maret has already written to me. The fieldwork is satisfactorily completed. Term 2 closed."

She made a small mark in the folio. Closed it. Sat on the edge of the music stand with the relaxed weight of a senior teacher about to ask a question she already had the answer to.

"Now. You're here ahead of class to ask me about the final fieldwork and the recital."

She didn't make it a question.

She lifted a sealed parchment from beneath the folio and held it without giving it. "I have two requirements left for accelerated graduation. The fieldwork I'm prepared to assign would test what I have suspected about you since the first class. I would not assign this to any other student in the cohort."

A breath. The professor's eyes were the kind of green that has been measuring rooms for thirty-eight years.

"The Sunken Playhouse."

She let the name sit.

"It is a ruin south of the city, in the wetlands where the old river bent before the founders rerouted it. Pre-Golden-Age. It was a performance venue of some renown for about forty years before it sank — sank literally, the marsh took it, the building is still down there in the silt with most of its proscenium intact. Local shepherds report music from the ruin at night. Lyssa Vane, the regional booking gatekeeper, has been very discouraging of any researcher seeking access to it. My professional read of why is uncharitable to her."

Her eyes were on Cookie's face when she said the name. Lyssa Vane. The name had gravity in this room. Cookie's expression did the thing Cookie's expression had been doing for two months around any name that mattered, and Ashworth filed it.

"The fieldwork," Ashworth said. "Two-day expedition, one buffer day. Recover any pre-Golden-Age performance material that has survived. Assess the reported nightly music phenomenon and report on its source. Stipend voucher up to fifteen gold for hire. Bring a party — guild crew, healer if you can afford one."

She unfolded the second item. A grand-auditorium booking voucher, gold-pressed.

"The recital. The Civic Auditorium of Varenholm seats four hundred and twenty. Eighth bell evening, the date to be set within thirty days of fieldwork return. Forty-minute solo program drawn from material you compose, adapt, or recover from your fieldwork. The Council attends. Vess attends. The Master of the Bardic College from Ashenmere has been invited. Pass it and you graduate with First Honors and life-of-license performance privileges across the Coalition. Fail it in front of four hundred and twenty people, and there is no soft retake."

She held out the parchment.

"You have my permission to refuse the Sunken Playhouse fieldwork and request a substitute. I will not pretend the substitute would be of equivalent caliber."

Cookie reached for the parchment.

Ashworth held it for one more second — long enough for a teacher to know the student understood — and released it. Cookie signed. The professor counter-signed, dripped wax, pressed the Academy seal. The stipend voucher went into Cookie's satchel.

Ashworth stepped back. Folio open again. Quill ready for the next student. The conversation was over but she added one thing to the room before Cookie left it.

"Cookie. I have been watching you produce visible magic through dance since your first class, and I have been writing to two colleagues in Ashenmere asking what they know about 'the children of fire.' I am not your enemy. I am a witness. If you find anything down there that frightens you — bring it to me first."

### The Corridor

Silka had not gone far. Stone benches lined the bardic-wing corridor outside the lecture hall, and Silka was sitting on the third one with her folio in her lap, not reading it. She looked up when Cookie crossed.

The sister-warmth flickered in both their chests. Silka's grey eyes did the wide-then-hooded thing again. Whatever calculation she had been running since dawn was at a different stage now.

Cookie asked her to come.

Two days. Sunken Playhouse fieldwork. Ashworth's stipend. Want in?

The math behind Silka's face ran in real time. Saying yes fulfilled Lyssa's standing instruction perfectly. Saying no would attract attention and require an explanation Silka didn't have.

What Silka said, after an inhale that took longer than it should have:

"Yes. Yes — I'd, yes. Two days. Let me get my pack. And tell Mistress Adine I'll be on fieldwork." A small breath. "Thank you for asking me, Cookie."

She stood, folio against her chest, didn't quite meet Cookie's eyes, and walked the other way down the corridor toward the boarding wing. Quickly.

### Adventurer's Guild — Senna's Offer

The walk back through Crown Quarter took Pretty Privilege through its standard rotation of effects. The lawyer's clerk, the awning, the temple acolyte. By the time Cookie pushed through the guild door at the eleventh bell, she had a stipend voucher, an idea, and a list.

The Wardbreakers were at the corner table. Senna with a cup of something hot. Finch already on his third game of dice with a bronze-tier crew across the room. Varn at parade rest behind Senna's shoulder. Thessaly's crystal focus out on the table this morning, openly reading.

Senna saw Cookie come in. Watched her cross. Didn't move.

"Halfling," she said. "Tell me what brings the gold-eyed problem to my table at the eleventh bell with a stipend voucher in her satchel."

She could see the parchment-fold through the satchel weave. Twenty-five years of contract reading.

Cookie laid out the offer. Two-day fieldwork, Sunken Playhouse, fifteen gold stipend, three-day window with buffer.

Finch's dice game ended.

"The Sunken Playhouse?"

Varn didn't move but his eyes narrowed a fraction.

"...Lyssa Vane's locale," Thessaly said.

Senna let it sit for two breaths and folded her napkin. "Fifteen gold won't move our team for two days at standard rates. You know that. So you didn't come here for a market negotiation."

She leaned an elbow on the table. The quarter-inch mouth-twitch came back.

"Wardbreakers Bronze rate. One gold per head per day. Twelve gold for the three-day window. That's professional courtesy and it's not charity, because what I get out of it is — I get to come. I want to see you in the silt. I want to see what your Ember does when something old underneath you is trying to wake up. And —"

She let the next part land.

"— I am owed an answer to the question you didn't answer at this bar yesterday. You can answer it on the road, in the ruin, at the campfire — but you will answer it before we come back to Varenholm. That's my term."

Hand extended.

Finch was already in. Varn said one word: "In." Thessaly was packing the crystal.

Cookie reached across the table and clasped Senna's forearm — the soldier-grip, not the merchant grip. Senna's grip closed back. The contract locked.

Senna lifted her cup. Small, formal. "South gate, thirteenth bell, noon-light. Don't be late, halfling."

### Prep — Crown Quarter, Ninety Minutes

Bursar at the Academy. Fifteen gold pieces stacked into Cookie's hand, two lines in a ledger, out the door.

Gilded Thread. Hazel saw Cookie come in and gasped like she had been told a secret. The shop was half-staff today — two of the seamstresses still recovering from the wererat scare, the third (the pregnant one) out front pretending to dust. Hazel pulled an ensemble off a form: spring-green lace bralette, flowing skirt with white snowflake embroidery, gloves with cuff frills, thigh-strap garters with brass buckles, lace-up sandal-boots in green, brass-and-gold belt-chain at the waist, black choker with a gold scrollwork pendant.

When Cookie stepped out of the dressing alcove the pregnant seamstress dropped the duster.

The price on the tag was 7 silver. With the lifetime discount Hazel charged 6 silver and 3 copper, made Cookie promise to come back wearing it after the recital, and added — without payment, without prompting — that Lady Calverton was in the third row at the Starling and that by tomorrow lunch every upper-tier woman in Varenholm would know what Cookie had done in the cellar.

Pale Lantern. Isolde alone at the counter. Three vials of silver paste, a healer's kit including silvered surgical thread and tincture of moonsilk, three and a half gold all in. Then she dropped one extra item into the parcel without a word — a small twist of dried Ember-reactive herb wrapped in parchment.

"For the ruin," Isolde said. "Burn it if anything down there tries to read your mind. Don't ask me where I got it. Don't tell anyone you have it."

General store. Rations, lantern oil, hempen rope, sealskin overcloak, marsh-grease for the boots. Two gold and two silver, paid in coin.

Millward Chandlery. Fern was awake. Fern was dramatic. Hugged Cookie, made her promise three things (write, don't die, don't sleep with anyone Diamond-tier without telling her first), helped her pack. The yellow Gilded Thread piece, the indigo Maeven dress, one daily wrap, plus the green ensemble currently on Cookie's body. Anklet attuned. Earrings on. V.E.A. stamp inked. Heartstring on, always.

Fern at the door: "GO. Be amazing. Don't get eaten by a marsh-ghost."

Cookie ran the last two streets to the south gate.

---

### South Gate — Noon-Light

The Wardbreakers were already there. Senna at parade rest, weight on one hip, watching the clock. Finch with two short-bladed throwing knives in his belt and a satchel of unknown things. Varn with a pack roughly the size of a small horse and a bundle of silver-shod cudgels strapped to it. Thessaly's marsh maps folded in three different ways across her satchel.

Silka arrived at one minute past — flushed, breath short, traveling cloak over her grey class-dress, one hand pressed to her sternum where the silver chain sat. She didn't look at Senna directly. She caught Cookie's eye and gave a tiny smile that meant *we're really doing this*.

Senna's INT 18 read Silka in approximately one full-body glance. She didn't say anything. She filed it.

Marching order spoken aloud: Varn front, Thessaly and Cookie center, Finch ranging, Silka and Senna rear. Six hours on the south-marsh road, arrive Marshmouth Inn by sunset, camp the inn-yard, Sunken Playhouse approach at dawn.

The gate guards checked the V.E.A. stamps, logged six souls and one halfling-sized wagon-permit, and waved them through.

The road south left the city through old vineyard country. Spring sun on the cobbles. Pretty Privilege fired on every farmer they passed — a cart driver, a girl with a goat, a young vintner's apprentice. Cookie walking center, Heartstring rolling outward in a thirty-foot bubble, the green skirt swinging at mid-thigh, the snowflake embroidery catching daylight. Twice on the road a passing merchant's male driver stopped his cart for no reason and watched them go by until Senna walked back to make sure he wasn't going to follow them. He didn't follow them.

A mile from Marshmouth, Senna walked beside Cookie for the first hour without speaking, then said — quietly, no mockery — "Ignis would approve. Kenji would have *opinions* about marsh terrain. Don't let the second one stop you doing the first one."

That was as endorsing as Senna got.

---

### Marshmouth Inn — Camp

The half-timbered roadhouse, marsh-rushes thatching the roof, three lit lanterns swinging in the dusk wind. The marsh sprawled west of the inn like a long sleeping animal.

The innkeeper had six beds available if they wanted to share two-to-a-bed. Senna booked the inn-yard for camp instead. Wardbreakers don't sleep indoors when they can avoid it. Thessaly negotiated the use of the cook-fire for breakfast.

Camp came up in fifteen minutes. Bedrolls in three pairs (Senna and Varn, Finch alone, Cookie with Silka — Silka volunteered, Finch did not protest, Senna noted), a small fire ringed with stones, watch shifts assigned (Thessaly first, then Varn, then Senna, then Finch).

Dinner was rabbit stew, dark bread, a wedge of strong cheese.

After dinner Senna pulled Cookie aside, sat her on a log, and said: "I'm not asking the question tonight. Tomorrow we're walking into something that may try to kill all of us, and I want your head on the work, not on me. But I do want you to know I'm thinking about it. Think about it too. So that when you answer me — and you will — it's the version that's true."

She walked back to her bedroll. The fire popped. The marsh started its night-noise — frogs, something larger moving in the reeds, a distant bird that sounded like it was making fun of the others.

Silka was already in her bedroll when Cookie returned. Awake. Breathing too even to be sleeping. Her face turned away from Cookie's. The chain at her throat sat very still.

By twenty-one-hundred bell Cookie was in her bedroll. Lights out.

The Ember under her ribs — the un-asking thing — went a little louder, the way it had the night before. Whatever it had been wanting all yesterday, it was wanting it again tonight, and it was closer to the wanting now than it had been then.

Cookie's WIS 7 brain heard the wanting and made a series of decisions over the next four hours that the rest of the camp would have very little patience with in the morning.

### The Stunt

Cookie slid out of her bedroll quiet as a small thought, peeled the green ensemble down to the lace bralette and brass garters, and crossed the camp on light feet at a quarter past midnight. Senna woke up at the second the ropes shifted but did not move. Thessaly was at the fire reading marsh maps, oblivious. Cookie crossed unseen and slid into the bedroll behind Finch's curled rogue-form.

She pressed butt-to-boxer-briefs and went still.

Finch had been asleep until that exact second. He was awake by the next. The rogue-trained part of him said *do not move, do not breathe loud, this is not yours to escalate*. Great User: protective layered into the silence — Cookie was Heartstring-drunk on her own privilege and the only correct play was *be a wall, not a man*. He held position. Three hours of holding position.

At twelve-fifteen Cookie's body shifted on its own — small, ambiguous, a sleep-shape — and a noise left her open mouth into the dark of the inn-yard. Then another, less ambiguous. Across the camp Thessaly's crystal pulsed bright enough to throw color on the underside of her chin and Thessaly cupped her palm over it. Senna's decision-tree advanced one tick toward intervene. Silka, in her own bedroll, pressed her hands over her ears through her hair and the blanket-edge.

Cookie was awake the whole time. She had brought a flask.

At twelve-twenty-five — Senna's watch about to start, Thessaly winding down — Cookie pulled the small clay flask from beneath the bralette-bundle she had brought across the camp and poured about half of it down her belly and across the inside of her thighs. The white ran in cold irregular splotches. She set the flask in the dirt under the bedroll edge where it didn't show.

Then she rolled onto her back. Splayed her legs at angles bodies don't pick when they're unconscious. Tongue out, eyes rolled back so the whites showed, expression of theatrical halfling-grade idiocy. Added one more half-conscious sleep-moan into the dark for staging.

Then she went still.

Behind her, Finch had been doing crisis math for three hours and had just received another input. Footsteps. Senna walking over, not rushing, the way Senna walked when she was already at one-of-three-options-decided. His survival instinct said *out of the bedroll, fast, look like a man with nothing to hide.* He extracted in two motions, grabbed his cloak, fumbled for his shirt, got halfway upright with one boot in the wrong hand at the moment Senna arrived at the bedroll edge.

Senna stopped. Looked down.

Twenty-five years of student-stunt cataloguing. The geometry of the camp's last three hours fully indexed. White splotches in halfling-aimed patterns. Legs spread to deliberately dramatic geometry. Tongue out and eyes rolled in a face nobody actually makes during anything. Small clay flask just visible under the bedroll edge. Finch with one boot, mortified, half-dressed by panic, clearly not the source of any of it. Math finished in under a second.

Senna did not raise her voice.

"Wardbreaker."

Finch froze mid-boot.

"Letting a contract kid pull a milk-bottle stunt in your bedroll at twenty-five past midnight on the night before a Lyssa-Vane ruin. The first iron-tier rookie who ever drilled with me would've been awake and three feet from his bedroll five minutes ago. I expected significantly better, halfling."

Finch's color did something humans don't have a word for.

Senna turned her head a quarter-inch.

"Cookie. Get up. Get cleaned. Get dressed. Get back to your own bedroll. We move at dawn and you are not carrying that tongue around the marsh tomorrow. If you have one more spare moan in you tonight, save it for the moment you're inside the proscenium and a wraith reaches for your throat. I'd rather hear it then."

She turned and walked back to the watch fire.

Finch returned to his bedroll having aged. He did not look at Cookie for the rest of the night. Thessaly had heard everything and filed it as *halfling chaos behavior, non-arcane*. Varn, already awake, decided permanently never to mention it. Silka in her bedroll heard the entire reprimand and the sister-resonance hurt did not get better; the chain at her throat stayed cold.

Cookie cleaned with the inside of Finch's spare cloak (he would find this in the morning and say nothing). Bralette back on the bundle. She crossed the camp in lingerie back to her own bedroll and slid in next to Silka. Silka did not turn around.

The space between the two bedrolls had gone a degree colder. Cookie did not understand why.

Lights out for real. Three hours to dawn. The inn yard went quiet. The fire popped. Whatever the Ember had been wanting, it had not gotten it tonight either.

### Day 9 — Rootday plus one, Sweet Turn 1247 AR

Dawn pale over the marsh. The Marshmouth innkeeper's daughter at the kettle. Senna at the fire stirring barley with bacon scraps. Varn sharpening something. Thessaly annotating maps. Finch at the pump.

Cookie woke. The green ensemble went back on. The flask had not survived the night intact. The bedroll-blanket stain had set. Cookie rolled the blanket up tight so nobody had to discuss it.

Breakfast in the kind of silence that had purpose. Senna handed Cookie a tin cup of barley and bacon. Eye contact for exactly the time it took to hand the cup over. Finch ate facing the road. Varn ate professionally. Thessaly read with one hand. Silka picked at hers and gave the rest to the inn-cat that had been circling.

Camp broke down in fifteen minutes. The innkeeper waved them off from the porch.

The road south became a track, the track became a path, the path became the suggestion of a path through standing reeds. Mist on the marsh thinner than yesterday — sun climbing. Frogs absurd. A long bird-shape lifted off a reed-island with a fish in its claws.

Two hours in, Thessaly stopped. Held up the crystal. It was glowing pale gold, continuous, not a pulse.

"Resonance source within a kilometer. Bearing south-southwest. Strong enough to register continuously. We're close."

The reed-line ahead broke. Through the gap, two hundred yards on, the upper edge of a stone proscenium peak rose out of the marsh like a half-buried molar. Algae and reed-shadow climbed the visible part. The top of one stone column caught the morning sun.

The Wardbreakers came to a halt without anyone saying so. Senna scanned the marsh-horizon. Varn unhooked the greatshield. Finch went still in the way rogues do. Cookie's Heartstring pushed outward into the reeds and something pushed back — not hostile, not friendly, just present. The Ember under her ribs went from quiet to *very awake*.

Senna's ground briefing. Three approach lanes — north reed-flats, west causeway, south marsh-wade. Cookie picked the causeway.

The Anklet got a fresh coat of silver-paste at the reed-line. Tova — wait, no; Tova wasn't on this trip. Senna helped Cookie with the back of the right heel where Cookie couldn't see. Two minutes.

Marching order on the causeway, three feet wide: Varn front with greatshield, Senna second, Cookie third, Silka fourth, Thessaly fifth with the crystal cupped in one hand to dim it, Finch rearguard with the bow at half-pull.

A hundred feet in, the causeway curved around a stone pillar.

That was when they heard the music.

A flute. Then a soft drum. Then a woman's voice humming a melody that almost made sense and then bent to one that didn't.

Silka stopped walking. Her face went the color of wax. Her hand went to the silver chain at her throat.

She knew the song. She didn't say so.

Thessaly dimmed the crystal under her palm — gold to red-shot, pulsing with the music. *"Charm-resonant. Active. Bardic — College of Glamour signature. There's a performer ahead and the song has a target lock or this thing wouldn't be reading like a beacon."*

Senna held up a fist. The party stopped. She listened for a count of four. Her face was unreadable.

Then she turned a quarter-step, looked at Cookie, and waited for the answer to the question that nobody had asked yet — *what kind of leader walks toward a song like that.*

The marsh behind them went quiet. The frogs stopped. Some lower-frequency working had taken note of the second halfling on the causeway.

Cookie's Heartstring was on. The Ember was awake. The proscenium peak was a hundred feet off. Silka was standing still with the chain in her fist and the song was forming syllables that almost were words.

The party waited.
