# Chapter 10 — *The Reader*

**Day 9 mid-morning → Day 11 mid-morning**
**Level 10. Sunken Playhouse fieldwork resolved. Wardbreaker dependent status begins. Capstone signed.**

---

The frogs went quiet thirty paces back, and Senna noticed it before anyone else.

"Stop."

Varn planted the greatshield without being told — angled across the causeway so that anything coming from the proscenium had to choose between marsh on one side and a half-orc with two hundred pounds of spiked steel on the other. Finch was already low, three steps off the path, eyes fixed on the south reed-line where the mist hadn't burned off yet.

"Frogs." Senna didn't turn her head. "Eight breaths back. Whatever's in those reeds isn't us."

"Reeds carry weight wrong on the south side. Could be Eira. Could be something heavier. Either way, somebody decided to wait for us instead of running." Finch's voice was back to its working pitch — quick, dry, professional, the Day-8 night entirely scrubbed from it. He never quite stopped watching the water.

"Or somebody told them to wait." Senna finally looked at Cookie. The look wasn't a question. It was a check-in. *You hired us, performer. You're also the only one of us who can read Ember. What does the marsh feel like to you right now.*

The Anklet was silver-paste fresh, golden filigree dim against the leather. The Heartstring was on. The Ember under Cookie's ribs was awake and listening to something the marsh was listening to back.

She gave Senna her answer in a single word. *Push.*

---

"On me." Senna took the lead at a sprint without looking back to check anyone was following — the kind of trust that only happens between people who've fought together before. Varn broke left of center with the greatshield held at chest-high so it could swing into a block in either direction. Finch ghosted right, low and silent, an arrow nocked but not drawn. The causeway was firmer than the marsh deserved.

Twenty seconds. Maybe less. The frogs stayed silent the entire way. Whatever wanted them to be silent was letting them be silent.

The proscenium pillar resolved into shape — a single stone column, weathered, half-swallowed by marsh water, the upper third still carved with theatrical glyphs from whatever amphitheatre this had been three centuries ago. Around its base, scattered across the dry stone like broken glass, were silver chain-fragments. *Silver.* Senna saw them at the same moment Cookie did and her jaw worked once.

The chain Cookie had shattered off Silka's collarbones at Halbert's funeral. Someone had brought the pieces *here.*

A voice from the dry side of the pillar, pitched carefully, no edge in it, hands raised before Cookie's eyes had even adjusted to the shadow.

"Cookie. Before anyone does anything — *please.* Ninety seconds. That's all I'm asking for."

Eira stepped into the light. Pale, sweating, dressed in a charcoal travel cloak that had seen two days of swamp. Her hands stayed open and visible. Behind her, two people in marsh-village rags stood stock-still — eyes glassy, mouths slightly slack, not registering the Wardbreakers at all. Charmed. Old charm, hours-set, deep enough that they weren't afraid because they didn't know they should be.

"I'm not running. I have nowhere left to run. Lyssa got a letter from her patron last night. The patron sent back a name. It wasn't mine. It wasn't Silka's either." Eira's eyes flicked — quick, deliberate — to Cookie's. "If you let me give you what I know, I'll go to the Wardbreakers cuffed. If you decide I'm worth more dead, do it fast. But I am *telling* you the patron isn't who Lyssa thinks they are."

The Heartstring was wide open. Eira's tells were panic, exhaustion, and underneath, relief — the body language of someone who has finally stopped lying to themselves. The two charmed villagers behind her weren't reading at all. They weren't there in any sense Heartstring could find.

---

The moment split four ways at once.

Senna nodded Varn forward without speaking, and Varn was moving before the nod finished. He stepped around Eira at a wide arc — not threatening, not gentle, just *space* — and reached the two charmed villagers from the side rather than the front. He knelt to halfling-eye-height with the older of the two, placed a callused palm flat against her sternum, and waited. The villager blinked once. Twice. Didn't speak yet, but her pupils were tracking him now. Combat Magnetism running on a half-orc's professional restraint, and what came through was a man who knew how to be *there* for someone whose mind was being given back to her in pieces.

Finch produced a Guild-issue deposition slip from his belt pouch — folded, rain-wrapped, the kind of thing rogues carried because rogues filed paperwork — and a graphite stick. He handed them across to Eira at arm's length. His bowstring was fully relaxed now. His voice was the working voice.

"Sit. Write everything you know about the patron. Names you heard, dispatches you saw, drop locations, dates. Sign it at the bottom and date it to today. While you're writing, I'm going to read it over your shoulder and any time I think you're hiding something I'm going to tap the slip with this graphite stick and you're going to add what you left out. We are not going to fight about it. Yes?"

Eira sat. Eira wrote. Her hand shook for the first three lines, then steadied.

> *Lyssa called him the Reader. I never saw his face. The dispatches arrived monthly via a tea merchant out of Stormhaven who didn't know he was a courier. The drops went to a dead-letter box behind the Pale Lantern's loading dock — not Isolde's hand, the building's exterior. The Reader paid in trade weight: five silver per gold-eyed name, ten if I confirmed Ember activity. He wanted gender-flecked detail — eye color, hair, performing names, addresses if I had them. He never asked about Silka. He never named me. Two days ago Lyssa got his last letter and I watched her read it and turn the wrong color. The name in the letter was a halfling's. From Kettlebrook.*

She underlined that twice. Signed at the bottom. Dated it. Handed the slip to Finch without looking up.

---

Senna walked Cookie five paces away from the pillar — toward the marsh-edge, away from witnesses. She didn't unhand her sword and she didn't lower her voice and she didn't soften the moment. The Heartstring read her: a Diamond-tier veteran who had held this question between her teeth for two days and was finally ready to swallow it.

"I'm asking you under contract terms, performer. Plain language, plain answer. The man you named at Halbert's funeral. Are you telling me it's the man the Wardbreakers worked under for two years in the Vyranth campaign. The one we called Kenji. The one who held the Sundered Gate. The one who's *alive.* Yes or no."

Cookie answered.

Senna did not move for a count of three. Then she nodded once — small, precise, the nod of a contract being filed and closed in the same motion. The hand finally came off the sword hilt. "Then we have a different problem than we had ten minutes ago, and not a worse one." She took one step back, restoring the formal distance. "Bronze rate stands for this trip. After we turn this in, you're not a client anymore. You're a Wardbreaker dependent. Different paperwork. We'll do it in Varenholm."

She turned back toward the pillar. The contract had been refiled in real time.

---

The return trip compressed the next thirty hours into the kind of montage that felt long while it was happening and short the moment it ended.

Varn walked the two villagers back along the causeway and the marsh road, talking to them in two-syllable increments, and by the time they hit the eastern crossroads both villagers were eating dried apple and answering questions about their own names. Finch kept Eira a half-step ahead of him for the entire walk and never quite stopped watching the back of her head. She did not run, because there was genuinely nowhere left to run. The chain-fragments rode in a sealed Guild evidence-pouch on Senna's hip. They didn't camp Day 9 night because Senna didn't camp on a hot evidence trail. They reached Varenholm's east gate at dusk Day 10 — V.E.A. stamps cleared, six souls and one halfling-sized cargo permit logged in.

The Adventurer's Guild's lamps were still lit. Gruff was at the desk. He looked up when Senna walked in, looked at Cookie, looked at Eira (cuffed, escorted), looked at the two villagers (clean and conscious), looked at the evidence-pouch, looked back at Senna, and said only, "Faster than I bet on. Bring it."

The deposition went on the desk. The chain-fragments went on the desk. Eira went into the holding cell behind the desk under formal Guild custody. The villagers were sent to a temple bunk for the night with two coppers each for a meal and a Guild medic in the morning. Gruff stamped three forms — *contract complete,* *evidence chain of custody,* *prisoner intake,* in that order — pushed a small leather purse across the desk to Senna for the Bronze rate plus a five-gold completion bonus, signed the Academy capstone copy of the deposition for Ashworth's file, and looked Cookie in the face for the first time that night.

"Performer. You did the work. Sleep on it. Walk back here Day 11 morning for the academy debrief and the next contract slate."

---

Day 11 morning. Ashworth's lecture hall. The lamps in their iron sconces were still burning low against the grey before-dawn light through the Academy's tall east windows. Cookie was third row from the front, where she'd been sitting all term. Silka was in the second.

Silka turned when Cookie came in.

She didn't say anything. She just turned, and her eyes did something they hadn't done all term — they caught the lamplight and flicked *gold,* a half-second pulse along the iris before the grey returned. She blinked fast like she felt it happen. Her hand went up to her collarbone and then dropped because there was nothing there to touch anymore.

"You came back early." It was all she managed, and it wasn't a question. "I — was going to wait until lunch. I didn't think you'd be here for class. Are you—" She stopped. Tried again. Lowered her voice. "Are you all right."

Two seats over, a Crown Quarter merchant's daughter watched them with too-careful disinterest. Silka registered it the same moment Cookie did and went still. Lyssa's network, presumably, doing what was left of its job.

Ashworth walked in then with her ledger and a coffee cup that was already half-cold, set both down on the lectern, looked once around the room, and her gaze stopped on Cookie for a beat that none of the other students noticed. *I see you. We'll talk after.* She began the lecture without preamble.

Foundations of Performance, Day 11 of term. Two hours of voice technique, breath placement, the difference between projection and amplification in a stone room. Cookie had been good at all of it for months. Silka took notes the entire time without looking up; her hand shook for the first ten minutes and then steadied the way someone's hand steadies when they decide they're going to keep going regardless. The merchant's daughter took notes too. Watched the wrong things.

When Ashworth dismissed the class she didn't dismiss Cookie.

"Aspirant Kettlebrook. Aspirant —" she paused just long enough that Silka looked up — "and Aspirant Mireille. Office, please. Both of you."

---

Ashworth's office was small, book-lined, and warmer than the lecture hall by a careful five degrees. She closed the door behind them. Sat. Gestured at the two chairs across the desk. Folded her hands. Looked at the sealed Guild deposition copy on her desk — Gruff's stamp, Senna's countersignature, the blue-thread evidence-cord still intact.

"I've read this twice. I have one question and then we're done with the formal part of the assessment." She didn't look at Silka when she asked it. She looked at Cookie. "You wrote the structural appendix yourself, on the road back. Is that correct."

Cookie answered.

"Good. Then your capstone is signed." She pulled a stamp from a desk drawer with the calm of someone who has signed a thousand of these and added three impressions to the deposition's cover sheet — *Academy review,* *capstone accepted,* *Aspirant Kettlebrook, course honors.* The motion was so practiced it took maybe four seconds. She slid the stamped folder across the desk to Cookie.

"Recital is Day eighteen. We'll talk piece selection later this week. Off the record now: you found the patron's method but not his name. I want you to leave the name alone for the next ten days. I will not repeat that twice."

Then she did look at Silka. Carefully. The way someone looks at a window that has just begun to crack.

"Aspirant Mireille. I am putting you on a partial sabbatical for the remainder of the term, effective today. Performance theory only — no field assignments, no public performances. You will attend lecture and you will report to my office at the end of every class day. You are not in trouble." She let that sit. "You are *changing.* I would prefer to be the person watching when it happens, rather than someone else."

Silka's hand had gone back to her collarbone. The skin under her shirt collar — Cookie could see it, just barely, where the high collar met the line of her throat — was a faint warm gold that hadn't been there at the start of term. Like ink rising from below.

Silka said, very quietly, "Yes, Professor."

Ashworth nodded once. "Both of you may go. Take the rest of the morning."

---

Outside the office, in the hallway, Silka stopped walking. The merchant's daughter from class was two corners away. The chain-fragments were sealed in Academy archive. The Reader had a method but no name. Lyssa's network was one letter from collapse. Cookie's relationship to the Wardbreakers had been refiled in a single Day-10 nod. And Cookie's half-sister was standing three feet away with gold rising into the visible part of her skin and nothing she could do to stop it, and no one had told her any of what she was yet.

The chapter ends here, in that hallway, with Silka three feet away and a question hanging that nobody has spoken yet.
